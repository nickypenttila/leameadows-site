Mistress of Pemberley

The wedding the book stops just short of — in Elizabeth’s voice.

By eight o'clock on her wedding morning, Elizabeth Bennet had been kissed by her mother four times, wept over twice, prophesied at length, and informed — on the authority of a woman who had been married more than three-and-twenty years and had never once been to Derbyshire — exactly what Derbyshire would require of her. She had also been dressed, undressed half an inch at the shoulder, and dressed again, for Mrs. Bennet had discovered at the last possible moment that the gown which had been entirely perfect at every previous viewing was now, on the only morning it could not be altered, a quarter of an inch too high at the throat.

"You will be looked at, Lizzy," her mother said, as though this were news, and a misfortune. "Ten thousand a year. There will be people who come only to look."

"Then I am glad I shall be too occupied to look back," Elizabeth said, and held still while Jane, with the patience of a sister who had survived the same examination an hour earlier, set the last pin.

In the glass the two of them stood together for a moment — Jane all serene fairness, herself a darker, quicker thing beside her — and Elizabeth thought, not for the first time that week, that the strangeness of the day was not that she was to be married. The strangeness was that she was to be married and happy, and that the two facts, which she had once been quite certain could not be made to share a room, were standing now in the same gown, waiting to be looked at.

"There," Jane said softly. "Now you are exactly yourself."

"Am I to be married as myself? Mama has been at considerable pains to improve me."

"Mama," said Jane, "has been at considerable pains to improve the throat of your gown. You she gave up improving years ago, which is why she loves you."

Below them the house performed its overture. A door; another door; Kitty's voice asking where the white ribbon had gone and Mary's voice answering, with the air of one quoting Scripture, that vanity in the matter of ribbons was the surest sign of a frivolous mind, and then Kitty's voice again, lower, asking Mary to help her find the frivolous ribbon anyway. Somewhere Mrs. Bennet revived herself. She had been reviving herself at intervals all week, and with such regularity that Mr. Bennet had observed she was becoming almost dependable, and that he should be sorry to lose the habit when the wedding was over and there was nothing left to revive from.

Elizabeth went down.

❦

Her father was in the hall, in his good coat, holding his hat in both hands as though it were a thing he did not quite trust himself with. He looked at her coming down the stairs and did not say, at once, anything dry.

That, more than the gown or the ribbons or the four maternal kisses, told Elizabeth what the morning was.

"Well," he managed, after a moment. "You will do."

"High praise, Papa, on such a day."

"You wish me to be eloquent. I have given my eloquence to your mother for three-and-twenty years and find the account overdrawn." He turned his hat once in his hands. "I will say this. I have given a great deal of thought to which of my daughters I could least afford to lose, and I have concluded that you have arranged matters very ill, in being the one I shall miss, and in marrying the one man in England I cannot fault you for preferring."

"You said you would not be eloquent."

"I find," said Mr. Bennet, "that I am not, after all, master even of that." And he offered his arm, and she took it, and they went out to the carriage together while behind them Mrs. Bennet discovered that she had not, after all, finished crying.

❦

The church was cold in the way of old country churches, stone holding the night long after the morning had warmed the lanes — a clean cold that smelled of damp wax and lime-washed walls and the small posy of late roses someone had set, with more love than skill, upon the rail. Elizabeth would remember the cold afterward, and the way the warmth of the gathered people pushed against it, never quite winning.

She would remember, too, that she had braced herself to be looked at, and had forgotten to be, because the only person she found she could look at was at the front, turned, watching her come.

In the hall, with Mrs. Reynolds a step behind, he was Mr. Darcy. Here, before God and the better part of two counties, he was Mr. Darcy still; she would not have his name privately in her mouth until they were alone. And yet what she saw in his face was William — the particular stillness he wore when feeling had gone past the place where his composure could manage it, and he had stopped trying to manage it, and had decided instead simply to look at her and let the rest of the world arrange itself as it could.

He had not, in the end, found a dramatic way to prove himself. He had told her once that he wished he had — that he would have liked there to be some single great act she could point to, some rescue, some sacrifice, some thunderclap of merit that would have settled the question of his worth past all argument. And she had told him that she did not want the thunderclap. She had had enough, in her life, of men who were splendid once and ordinary ever after. What she wanted was the smaller, harder proof, offered again and not regretted: the same man, on an ordinary morning, choosing the right thing without applause.

She walked up the aisle on her father's arm toward a man who had given her a great many ordinary mornings, and meant to give her the rest of them, and she found that her hands, which she had expected to shake, were perfectly steady.

The vows were not spectacular. She had not wanted them to be. Beside her, at the second turning of the service, Jane married Mr. Bingley with a radiance that made the cold church briefly unnecessary; and Mr. Bingley, who had wept at the rehearsal and had sworn he would not weep at the wedding, wept at the wedding. Mr. Bennet gave them both away with the air of a man discharging, in a single morning, a duty he had been quietly dreading for twenty years and was astonished to find he could bear.

When it was done — when she was, in the plain unanswerable language of the register, Elizabeth Darcy, the ink not yet dry — her husband took her hand to lead her out, and she felt, through her glove, that he had taken it bare.

She glanced down. He had removed his own glove. In the cold church, in front of everyone, the master of Pemberley had handed his wife into the rest of her life with his bare hand around her bare fingers, his thumb warm against her knuckles, a small and entirely deliberate impropriety that no one but Elizabeth would ever notice and that she would remember when she had forgotten the gown, the roses, and very nearly the vows.

"You are cold," he said quietly, which was not what he meant.

"I am married," she answered, which was.

The corner of his mouth moved. "Yes," he said. "There is no remedy for it now."

❦

At the church door her father had his moment, and used it as he used everything, sidelong.

"Lizzy," he said, drawing her a half-step aside while Darcy was claimed by Mr. Bingley and the press of well-wishers, "I have a last piece of fatherly counsel, and then I shall trouble you with no more, your husband being now responsible for your improvement and welcome to the labor."

"I am all attention, Papa."

"It is this." He looked at her, and for once let her see the whole of it before he covered it again. "I had thought, when you were small and would not be quiet, that I should be glad on the day some good man took you off my hands. I find the day has come, and the man is better than good, and I am not glad in the least. I am only — " he paused, and chose the smaller word, because the smaller word was the truer one — "pleased. I am very pleased, my dear, and I am going to go and be pleased somewhere your mother cannot see me do it."

He pressed her hand and went. And Elizabeth, who had been steady through the vows and the bare hand and the whole cold beautiful service, was undone in the churchyard by the word pleased, and had to be found a handkerchief, and was, of course, found one by Kitty.

❦

Kitty had been crying since the second hymn, but quietly, which was new. She stood now with Georgiana Darcy at her side — the two of them had stood up together throughout, a matched pair of younger sisters trading the same nervous half-smile — and she put her handkerchief into Elizabeth's hand with the brisk competence of a girl who had recently discovered she was good for something and meant never to forget it.

"Don't tell Mama you cried," Kitty said. "She'll say it was for joy and make a speech."

"It was for joy."

"All the more reason," said Kitty, with a wisdom that had cost her a great deal to acquire, "not to tell Mama." And Georgiana, beside her, laughed — actually laughed, out loud, in a churchyard, a thing she could not have done in May and could do now in October because a summer had happened to her, and to all of them, and none of it would have happened at all if a frightened girl had not, one morning at a breakfast table, found out what her voice was for.

Elizabeth looked at the two of them and thought that of all the proofs she had been given that her judgment, once so sure and so wrong, could after all be trusted — this was the one she would have least predicted and would least give up. She had come into Derbyshire believing she had ruined her own chances by misreading one man. She was leaving it the sister of two girls who had read each other correctly across a crowded country church, and found a friend.

"Come to Pemberley soon," Georgiana said, suddenly shy again, the way she went shy when something mattered. "Both of you. The house is — it is better when there are people in it who laugh."

"Then it shall be very much improved," Elizabeth said, "for I intend to laugh in it daily."

❦

The wedding breakfast was at Longbourn, and Longbourn rose to it.

Mrs. Bennet had ordered, planned, countermanded, and re-ordered the breakfast so many times that the cook had taken to writing down only every third instruction, on the sound principle that two of any three would shortly be reversed; and the result, against all probability, was splendid. There were cold meats and hot, and three kinds of cake, and the good wine Mr. Bennet kept for occasions he privately considered did not exist, and which had therefore been accumulating in the cellar in a state of pleasant surprise for some years.

It was beside her plate that she found it: a small thing, carved in pale wood, set where she could not miss it — one of the little animals from the Lambton fair.

Elizabeth picked it up and had to set down her glass.

She knew it at once. She had watched him buy two of them, that hot afternoon among the stalls, from a boy whose father had carved them through a wet week when the hay could not be cut — had watched him pay without fuss and without flourish, for no better reason than that the work was good and the week had been hard; and she had felt something shift in her then that she had not, that day, been willing to name. He had not bought them to be seen buying them. That was the whole of why she had noticed.

She looked up. Her husband had come to stand at her shoulder.

"I wondered where the other one had got to," she said.

"I have had it since June," said Mr. Darcy. "I found I did not wish to give it away. You will think me sentimental."

"I think," said Elizabeth, "that you bought a poor man's carving on a day you believed I would never have you, and kept it, and have now smuggled it onto my wedding plate like a schoolboy passing a note across a form. Sentimental is not the word I should choose."

"What word should you choose?"

"I am still deciding," she said. "It will take me some years. You must be patient." And he let the smile come, briefly, the way he did only when there was no one near enough to be astonished by it; and she closed her hand around the little carved animal, and kept it.

Across the room Mary, who had been waiting for a lull with the patience of a woman who has prepared remarks, achieved her lull and rose to fill it.

"Marriage," Mary announced, "is a solemn institution, not to be entered into — "

"Mary," said Mr. Bennet, without raising his eyes from his plate, "is going to favor us with her views on the solemnity of the institution, and I have promised her she may, in full, this evening, when the carriages are gone and I am asleep. Before breakfast, however, we will take the solemnity as read, and have the cake."

Mary sat, only slightly wounded, and was consoled — to everyone's surprise, most of all her own — by Kitty, who slipped into the chair beside her and said something low that made the corner of Mary's serious mouth turn up; and Elizabeth, watching her two least-noticed sisters discover, late and quietly, that they need not be alone in a loud family, felt the morning settle in her chest as something she would keep.

There was one empty chair.

It had not been set for anyone; no place had been laid; and yet Elizabeth, looking down the bright crowded length of her mother's table, was aware of it the way one is aware of a single unlit window in a row of lit ones. Lady Catherine de Bourgh had been invited, in terms of perfect correctness, and had declined, in terms of formidable correctness, and was not present at her nephew's wedding.

Elizabeth felt the absence chiefly through Darcy. He gave no sign — he ate, he spoke to her uncle Gardiner about trout, he received Mrs. Bennet's raptures with a patience that three months ago he could not have counterfeited and now, somehow, partly meant. But once, between the meats and the cake, she saw him take a letter from his breast — his aunt's letter, the refusal, three pages of family civility and family correction — and she saw him fold it, once, and then again, until the paper's edges no longer quite met, and put it away.

She said nothing. There would be a time for the empty chair, and it was not the wedding breakfast. But she marked it, and she thought: that is not finished. She will not let it be finished. A woman does not refuse to come to a wedding unless she means, at some later and more inconvenient date, to come to everything else.

She could not have said how she knew it. She only knew, with the same certainty that had once failed her so badly and now, chastened, did not — that a letter would come from Rosings on some morning weeks off, and that Lady Catherine, having declined to witness the marriage, would not for long decline to inspect it.

For now there was cake.

❦

The leave-taking was long, because everything at Longbourn was long, and because Mrs. Bennet, having begun to say goodbye to Elizabeth at half past ten, was determined to extract from the farewell every drop of feeling it could be made to yield.

"Derbyshire," she kept saying, as though the county were a sentence. "So very far. You will write. You will write directly you arrive, and again the next day, and you will tell me everything — the house, the plate, the carriages, whether Mrs. Darcy's apartments face the morning sun — oh, Mrs. Darcy — Lizzy, you are Mrs. Darcy — "

"I am, Mama," Elizabeth said gently, "and I shall write, and the house faces every direction at once, being very large, so you may be easy on the point of sun."

It was her uncle Gardiner who rescued her, as her uncle Gardiner had a way of doing. He drew her aside under the pretense of inspecting the carriage horses — which were Darcy's, and beautiful, and required no inspection — and said, low, watching the grooms rather than her:

"He gave you a fair summer, Lizzy. I watched him do it. A man may be civil to a woman's tradesman uncle for an afternoon; any man may manage that. To be civil all summer, in his own country, to the uncle in Cheapside, with no one of consequence watching to applaud him for it — " Mr. Gardiner shook his head. "That is not manners. That is character. I told your aunt so in June, and she told me I had said it three times already, which I daresay I had."

"You approve of him, then," Elizabeth said, who knew the answer and wanted, on this morning of all mornings, to hear it from this uncle.

"I love him," said Mr. Gardiner simply, "for making you look the way you look. There is no higher reason, and I shall need no other." Then, because feeling embarrassed him as it embarrassed all the best of her family, he added, with a perfectly straight face, "Also his trout are remarkable, and I intend to fish them shamelessly for the rest of my life," and Elizabeth laughed — really laughed, the first full laugh of the whole solemn shining day — and was still laughing when her husband came to hand her into the carriage.

He heard the laugh, and looked at its cause, and looked at her, and she watched him understand that he had married into a family that produced, among its undeniable absurdities, a man like Edward Gardiner; and that this was not a thing to be borne but a thing to be grateful for; and that the gratitude was already, quietly, his.

"Sir," Darcy said to her uncle, "the river is yours whenever you will come."

"You will regret that, nephew."

"I have begun," said Darcy, "to suspect that the things I am told I shall regret are, on the whole, the ones I am gladdest of," and he handed his wife up into the carriage, his hand bare again, and got in after her, and the door was shut on Longbourn, on the noise and the love and the laughter, and on the whole of the life that had made her.

❦

They were quiet on the road north.

It was not the quiet of two people who have nothing to say. It was the quiet of two people who have, at last, the whole of the rest of their lives in which to say it, and need not crowd it all into the first hour. The carriage rocked; the October country went by, gold and rust and the deep wet green of the year turning; and Elizabeth, who had ridden this road before with only his letter for company — had ridden it once in misery, turning his folded pages over and over until she had the cruel kind sentences by heart — rode it now with the man himself beside her, his shoulder warm against hers, the letter long since answered by everything that had happened after.

"You are thinking of something," he said.

"I am thinking that I traveled this road once before, going the other way, and was wretched."

"I know. I have thought of it. I sent you up that road wretched, with a letter, because I had not the courage to do better." His hand found hers on the seat between them. "I have spent the summer trying to deserve the road back."

"You need not have spent the whole summer."

"No?"

"You might have spent rather more," Elizabeth said. "I am an expensive person to deserve. You will be at it for years."

"I am aware," said Mr. Darcy, "of the size of the undertaking. I went into it with my eyes open. I shall require," he added, after a moment, with the particular dryness she had taught him and which he wore now as though he had invented it, "a great many ordinary days. I mean to have them all."

She turned her face into his shoulder so that he should not see how that undid her, and he let her, and put his bare hand over her gloved one, and neither of them said anything else for a long while; and the country went gold and rust and green, and carried them home.

❦

Pemberley received its new mistress at dusk.

She had seen the house in summer, in the full astonishing green of it, and had thought she understood what she was marrying. She had not. In the low gold light of an October evening, with the lamps already lit in the long windows and the smoke standing straight up from the chimneys into the still cold air, the house did not look astonishing. It looked like somewhere people lived. It looked like somewhere she would live. The difference between those two thoughts was the whole distance she had traveled since June, and it closed in her chest as the carriage came up the sweep.

Mrs. Reynolds was at the door, and the household behind her — not bustling, never bustling; Pemberley did not bustle, it arranged — drawn up to receive them with that particular warmth the house kept for the moments that mattered, the warmth that made a guest feel honored without seeming to have exerted itself. Only Elizabeth was not a guest. She understood that, stepping down, with a small interior lurch: the warmth was for her now. She was the one the house had arranged itself around.

"Mrs. Darcy," said Mrs. Reynolds, and curtsied, and in the two words and the curtsy there was — Elizabeth was learning to hear it — a whole sentence: welcome, and well done, and I have run this house through three-and-thirty years and a great many mistresses better and worse, and I have hopes of you. Elizabeth had been mistress of Pemberley for the length of one carriage ride, and already she knew that she would spend years learning to deserve Mrs. Reynolds's good opinion, and that it would be among the better-spent years of her life.

"Thank you, Mrs. Reynolds," she said, and meant, I am terrified, and I am glad, and I will try not to move too much furniture at once. Mrs. Reynolds received the unspoken sentence with a very slight softening about the eyes, which was, in the grammar of Mrs. Reynolds, an embrace.

❦

Later — when the long day had at last let go of them, when the house had quieted into its night sounds of settling timber and far-off doors, when she had been shown her own apartments and had stood a moment in the dressing room not quite believing the looking-glass that gave her back to herself in this place as its mistress — Elizabeth found that she could not yet sleep, and went, in her wrapper, to the window.

Her husband found her there.

She heard him come; she did not turn. "You ought to be asleep," he said, low, behind her. "It has been the longest day of your life."

"It has been the best day of my life," she said. "I am not willing to be done with it. Sleep is a kind of forgetting, and I am not ready to forget any of it yet."

He came and stood with her at the window. The casement was open a little; she had opened it, because the room, lovely as it was, had been shut up warm against her coming, and she had wanted the night air in it — the cold clean Derbyshire dark, smelling of frost not yet fallen and of the leaves going over and of the deep green-black of the grounds rolling away under the rising moon. The room had been closed. She had opened it. There was air in it now.

She had done that, she thought, all summer, without knowing she was doing it. She had come into a house full of closed rooms — a brother who read his steward's letters without seeing them, a shy girl who practiced her Mozart slowly and alone, a proud man shut so tight in his own certainties that he had once proposed to her as though conferring a favor — and she had gone about, room by room, opening windows. It was not a dramatic gift. It was the only one she had. She had used to wish it were a grander one.

She did not wish it any longer.

"My aunt did not come," Darcy said, into the quiet. It was the first he had spoken of it. He had carried the folded letter all day and had set it down, she now understood, only here, only with her, only when the door was shut and the air was clean.

"No," Elizabeth said. "She did not."

"It grieved me. More than I expected. She is — she was the nearest thing to my mother I had left, and she could not — " He stopped.

"She could not come to the wedding," Elizabeth said gently, "because to come would have been to consent, and she is not yet ready to consent. That is not the same as never. People are slower than weddings, William. You of all men know that. You were slow yourself, and look what became of you."

A breath of something that was not quite a laugh. "What became of me?"

"You learned to open a window," she said. "I have great hopes of your aunt. Not soon. But she will come — she will arrive one day, formidably, with a great deal of luggage and a list of grievances and Anne in tow, meaning to inspect us and find us wanting — and we shall give her a room with a fire and air enough to breathe, and we shall see what becomes of her. People surprise you. I have it on the best authority. I married the proof."

He was quiet a moment. Then he said, "You make me believe things I had given up."

"That," said Elizabeth, "is the entire office of a wife. I shall do it daily. You may rely upon me as confidently as upon Mrs. Reynolds, and at considerably less expense to the household."

He laughed then, properly, his head bowed close to hers, his bare hand coming up to rest at the curve of her neck where her pulse was, and the cold air moved through the open casement, and the great house breathed quietly around them in the dark, room after room, all its windows shut for the night but this one — this one open, on purpose, to the turning year.

"Come and sleep," he said at last. "The day will keep. I give you my word. I have become, I am told, a man whose word may be relied upon, and I find I should like to begin proving it tomorrow, and the day after, and on a great many ordinary mornings, for the rest of our lives."

"That," said Elizabeth Darcy, mistress of Pemberley, turning at last from the window into the warm dark of her own house and her own happiness, "is the only proof I ever wanted."

She left the window open.

In the morning the room was cold, and full of light, and entirely habitable; and that, she thought, waking beside him in the house that had decided she belonged in it before she had quite believed it herself, was the whole of what she had come into Derbyshire to learn.

It was enough. It was, she found, exactly enough.
