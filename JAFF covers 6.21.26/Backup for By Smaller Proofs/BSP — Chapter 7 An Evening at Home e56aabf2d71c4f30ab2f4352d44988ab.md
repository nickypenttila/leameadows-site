# BSP — Chapter 7: An Evening at Home

*Two evenings earlier, Grosvenor Street, London*

Darcy had read only to the fifth paragraph of his steward's letter before setting it down on his desk. He had read the first four twice and could not, on request, have given the substance of any of them. He picked it up again. The hedges along the south drive wanted cutting. The east pasture had been let for a month to a neighbor whose Herefords were, the steward reported, in better flesh than had been expected after so dry a spring. Darcy read these sentences in the order they had been written. He read them as a man reads a newspaper he has bought only to hold.

Saturday, they were all to go down to Pemberley. So it had been settled a month ago; so it had been settled again last Tuesday, when Bingley had called, and the road, and the rooms at the Bedford, and the hour of the remount, had all been arranged between them with the cheerful precision of a man who enjoys arranging. The party would be six — himself, Georgiana, Bingley, the Hursts, and Miss Caroline Bingley. Three days on the road; two nights at the posting-inns; one arrival at Pemberley in good order, and the summer to begin.

It was now Wednesday evening. He would see it through.

From the pianoforte in the next room came the Mozart poor Georgiana had been working on for a fortnight. She was playing it slowly. She was making the same mistake at the same bar.

He did not think of Kent. 

They had come straight to town, all late spring bustle and noise. As the weeks passed, the neighborhood had emptied around them by degrees, the usual pilgrimage from Grosvenor Street to the wilds in summer. First the Parliamentary men, as the session closed; then the hunting men, for reasons of their own. Then the rest of the Season — trunks, plants, abigails, spinster aunts — going down by degrees to Hertfordshire and Sussex and Hampshire and the Peak. 

Two doors east, the Lindsays' house had been shuttered a week. Across the way, the Penningtons had closed theirs today. His own household had remained. There had been no reason to leave. The Bingleys could not be ready before Saturday, and Georgiana, he had told himself, was better for the extra week at the London pianoforte with the London music master. She had not yet, she said, made friends with the new instrument at Pemberley.

The room was stifling. He had opened the window onto the street an hour since, and there was no breeze to reward him for having done so. Somewhere below, a servant crossed a tiled floor in soft shoes. The clock on the mantel behind him struck the quarter and seemed to consider the matter closed. He had kept only three candles burning. The sun would be down in an hour. He would light the rest then, or he would not.

On the desk at his elbow, under the steward's letter, lay the ordinary furniture of a gentleman's office: a pen-knife his father had used, a blotter creased at the corner, a ledger of estate accounts he had meant to finish by yesterday and had not, a miniature of his mother in an oval frame that had stood there eleven years. He did not look at the miniature. He did not need to. He had noticed some months ago, and not again until this moment, that his sister's mouth was the same mouth; that she had it from the woman in the frame. He set his eyes back on the steward's letter.

Miss Bingley had left a card. It lay on the tray in the hall when he had come in at five. He supposed it was there still. She had been accompanied, the porter had said, by Mrs. Hurst, and they had remained three-quarters of an hour with Miss Darcy in the small parlor before going away again. Darcy had been at his club. He was sorry that he had missed them. No; he was not sorry. He was glad to have missed them, and glad to have taken no greater trouble to have done so.

A note had come up with the card. It lay at the far corner of the desk, where he had put it on coming in. The hand was Miss Bingley's. He had not read it. He did not mean to read it tonight. He was sure he knew, within a thin margin, what was in it. Some courtesy about his sister; some archness about the upcoming journey; some wish, phrased to resemble her brother's, that the weather would hold. He could answer it without reading it. He would answer it, properly, in the morning.

Or not.

He moved it a half-inch to the left with the edge of his hand.

Bingley had not called today. Nor yesterday. Now Darcy considered it, Bingley had been, for some weeks — for some months — a man who did not call where formerly he had called. Who dined at home, with his sisters, when formerly he would have dined out. A man who had lost, without remarking on the loss, some portion of the openness of manner that had been, in other days, among his most agreeable qualities. 

Darcy had marked the decline in April, when he had come up from Kent. He had marked it again in May. He had, by a habit learned in boyhood, declined to think about it further. Or rather, he had thought about it just so far as comfort permitted, and then laid it aside, as one lays aside a letter one does not mean to answer tonight.

In this case, though, it had not stayed put.

*Do you think that any consideration would tempt me to accept the man who has been the means of ruining, perhaps for ever, the happiness of a most beloved sister?*

April, in a parlor at Hunsford, in a voice that had not trembled.

At the time he had been able to answer it. Of course he had. A man in the habit of arranging other people's happiness is seldom without reasons for the arrangement. The lady had not seemed attached. Bingley had been exposed to an imprudent connection. The family had been — he stopped there, because the next word in his mind had too much of his aunt in it, and because another sentence had followed the first.

*Had you behaved in a more gentlemanlike manner.*

He glanced toward the door. Georgiana was still playing. No one had heard his thoughts, or seen his wince. He could no longer look at Bingley's quietness without seeing his own hand in it. A man who has been shown his own hand in a thing prefers, afterwards, not to look at the thing.

If he can.

He glanced at the steward's letter.

The hedges wanted cutting. Yes. He would speak to the steward on Monday about the hedges. No need to write.

Oh, the south drive at Pemberley. The cool green of it in July. The scent of cut grass at evening, which was definitely not a London scent. The river below the house that would be, at this hour, still bright. The light in his sitting-room there. In summer he kept the north window open and, often, a book on the bench beneath. He had not been in that room for — he could not at once reckon. Since February. Five months.

It was not a longing. It was a preference. He had preferences about where he was on a July evening, as any man did.

He drew his attention back to the desk.

The Mozart stumbled at the same bar again. He did not, this time, let himself attend to it. He had attended to it, fitfully, for an hour. He had not been a help.

Then the lid of the pianoforte closed — not firmly, only firmly enough — and then steps in the hall. Georgiana appeared in the doorway.

Taller than she had been last summer. Darcy shut the door on those thoughts with a slam, but not before a tendril of guilt snuck in.

Georgiana did not, this evening, stand in the doorway, as she had sometimes stood, waiting to be invited in. She crossed the room to him, drew out the chair on the other side of his desk, and seated herself in it. She did this with the composure he had watched her acquire across the past long years. There was, tonight, a small tightness at the corner of her mouth — the set of some feeling not quite patience, caught and held. He had not seen her wear it before. Or had not, perhaps, noticed he was seeing it.

"Brother," she said.

"Georgiana."

"I cannot make it come right," she said. 

"The bar?"

"The bar."

"You will. You make it come right tomorrow. You have made it come right before."

"I did it once," she said, the tightness at her mouth now plainer, "and I have been unable to make it come right since. I begin to suspect that it was chance, and that I have learned nothing about the piece at all."

She was tall for her age — tall enough now that he could no longer, with any honesty, think of her as the child who had once fit beneath his arm on the library sofa at Pemberley. Her figure had taken on, almost without his permission, the full and graceful lines of womanhood; her face, less striking than their mother's had been and less marked than his own, was soft where his was severe, with a sweetness about the mouth that made strangers praise her amiability before they had earned the right to judge it. She was handsome enough to be admired, accomplished enough to be paraded, and shy enough to make both facts a burden to her.

Darcy saw all this, and because he loved her, saw also what others missed: the hesitation before she spoke; the careful lowering of her eyes when attention pressed too hard; the way her hands folded themselves into obedience even when her thoughts had begun, lately, to move past it. She had been praised all her life for gentleness. He had encouraged the praise, because gentleness had seemed safe. Tonight, for the first time, he wondered whether he had mistaken safety for happiness.

"That," he said, after a moment, "is the nature of the instrument."

She did not smile. "It is the nature of practice, I think, more than of the instrument. Mrs. Annesley says one cannot practice what one is not attending to. I have not been attending."

He did not, at once, reply.

She folded her hands in her lap. He had seen her fold her hands in her lap, just so, very often in the last ten months. He had come to regard the gesture as evidence of a patience she was teaching herself. It was not impatience. He had not, until this evening, considered that patience taught to oneself was a kind of weapon, held in reserve.

He thought, for the space of one breath, of what another person in the world now knew — had been told, in writing, and had returned to him no word of since — about the reason this sister had been obliged to teach herself any patience at all. A syllable began to form in the back of his mind; the shape of a letter; the beginning of a name. He did not finish it. He did not permit himself the luxury of even the shape.

He moved his hand, instead, a quarter-inch along the edge of the steward's letter.

"Miss Bingley called today," Georgiana said.

"So I see. I was sorry to have missed her."

She looked at him from under her lashes — quickly, to see whether he had meant it — and then she did something she would not have done three months ago. She said, "I do not think you were, brother."

One of his eyebrows went up of its own accord.

"She brought me a book," Georgiana went on, quite evenly. "She said she thought it would do very well for a young lady of my age. It is a novel I had already read, in May, which Mrs. Annesley thought indifferent and which I thought worse. I did not tell Miss Bingley so."

Something moved at the corner of his mouth — the beginning of a smile, too small to call back. 

"I told her I should be glad to take it down to Pemberley with me."

"Where," Darcy said, "nobody will admire it, and you may read it in peace."

"I do not mean to read it," said Georgiana, "at all."

It was the nearest thing to wit he had ever heard from his sister. He had not thought her capable of that particular edge.

She was growing up.

He did not, at once, know whether to be pleased. He found, on consideration, that he was, on the whole, pleased; and that he was also, in some part that was not wholly creditable to himself, alarmed. A girl who could say that, evenly, at little more than sixteen, was a girl whose attention had sharpened in a direction he had not been at liberty, lately, to supervise.

"Bingley did not come with them?" he said.

"Miss Bingley said he was at the club; but I think she says that when she does not wish to say where her brother is."

Sharp eyes. She had been in company with Miss Bingley, he reminded himself, four and five times a week for three months. It was not unreasonable that she should have formed an opinion on Miss Bingley's habits of speech. It was only that he had, by habit, expected her to form her opinions and keep them; and she was, tonight, putting them on the table between them, and waiting to see what he would do with them.

"He has been quiet," Darcy said.

"Yes."

"For some weeks."

"Yes." She did not, this time, soften the observation with any hope as to Bingley's health. She let the word stand.

The clock marked another half-hour. A door clicked shut somewhere below. A carriage went past in the street, the first in an hour, and the sound of its wheels died away toward the square.

"You have been quiet also, brother," said Georgiana. "Since you came back from Kent."

She was watching him when she said it. She had picked this moment; she had kept it in reserve, perhaps for days, perhaps for longer; and she had delivered it, he understood with an unpleasant clarity, at the one moment when he was least armored against it. The moment his guard had come down, when his attention had strayed from the steward's letter and not yet returned to anything else.

He did not answer at once. He could not. He found, with some dismay, that he had been expecting — for how long? for weeks? for the whole of this quiet July? — some form of this question from someone. He had not expected it from her. He had expected, he now thought, to be asked by a man in a club, to whom he could have given a man's answer; or by his cousin, to whom he could have given no answer at all, and been understood. He had not expected to be asked, across his own desk, by his little bit of a sister.

"Our aunt," he said, at last. "You know how she is. There was some business at Rosings that did not resolve itself. It tires one, more than such things ought. I have been a little —"

He hesitated.

"— melancholy, perhaps. It will pass."

He disliked the word the moment he had used it. *Melancholy* was a word for an idle young man in a novel; for a poet who had mislaid a glove and wished to make something of it. He was neither. What he had been, these months, was not melancholy. He did not know what it was. He had several private names for it, each of which he mistrusted.

"Yes," said Georgiana. She did not, this time, say it in the manner of a young lady who was accepting a displacement. She said it in the manner of a young lady who was registering a reply she did not intend to contradict.

"I am well, Georgiana."

She did not press. She had never, in her life, pressed. But he had the novel sensation, tonight, that she had decided not to press, rather than been unable to.

Past time to return to Pemberley. 

Not a retreat, nor a reprieve. An obligation. A duty.

The carriage was to be made ready on Saturday for all of them and their luggage, and another for the servants and Mr. Hurst's dogs. They would be three days on the road. Bingley was not a man who enjoyed travel, but he was a man who could be cheerful through it. Mrs. Hurst would read, or pretend to read. Mr. Hurst would sleep. Miss Bingley would sit in the forward seat, next to Georgiana, and she would speak.

She would speak for three days.

He considered the sound of her speaking for three days, and of himself sitting through it. He considered the sound of it set against the cool green of the south drive at Pemberley, the pine, the grass, the quiet sun.

"I have been considering," he said, "going on ahead."

Georgiana sat up straighter, if that was possible. "Brother?"

"Tomorrow. I have been some time from Pemberley; there are matters I should be glad to put in hand before — before the party arrives. You will come on Saturday with Bingley and his sisters, as we had arranged. Mrs. Annesley will travel with you."

She did not answer at once. For one instant, he saw her turn over in her mind whether she was being sent away, or being left.

"It is," he added, softly, "nothing to do with you, Georgiana."

A pause.

"I did not think it was," she said.

It struck him, after a half-second, that this was not quite reassurance. It was information.

"I shall be only a few days before you."

"Yes." And then — and here she looked at him, and there was the small true smile he had been waiting for all evening without knowing he had been waiting — "The Derbyshire air has always done you good."

Almost always. "Yes," he said. "It has."

She rose. She crossed to him — it was only a step — and for a moment laid her hand on the back of his, where it rested on the desk. She held it a shade longer than she usually did. Then she straightened, and bade him good night.

The door shut softly behind her. He heard her step on the stair. Mrs. Annesley's voice greeting her on the upstairs landing, and his sister's in reply. 

He sat a moment holding his hand still. Feeling the traces of his sister's touch.

Then he took paper and pen.

*Jenkins — the chaise at six.*

He folded this, directed it, and laid it on the tray at his elbow for the footman to carry down.

He took another sheet.

The candle was low; he did not trim it. He wrote quickly, before the pen could begin to make its own arguments.

*Bingley —*

*I find that I must go on ahead of our party. I shall set out tomorrow morning, and shall look for you, my sister, and the Hursts at Pemberley on Monday. Pray excuse the want of ceremony in this note: I have not time to call on you tonight. All will be in readiness by the time you arrive.*

*I remain, &c.,*

*F. Darcy.*

He had written *I remain* because it was what one wrote. He had written nothing else that was untrue, and he had written nothing, also, that was not true. He did not write *why.* He did not give a reason — not the estate, not the steward, not the hedges. Bingley would not ask for one, tonight or ever. It was a great convenience that Bingley took one's word for things.

He folded the note, sealed it, and laid it with the other. Then he stood, stretched, and went to the window.

Grosvenor Street was lit in squares. The lamps had come up while he had been writing. The pavement was lit in stretches, with the darker spaces between. There were few carriages. A man walked a dog, a well-kept grey terrier of the cheerful, incurable sort. A boy with a broom swept something off the step of a house shuttered for the summer. That was all. Two doors east, the Lindsays' windows were dark. Across the way, the Penningtons'. Further down, a house he did not, at this distance, know, showed a single light in an upper room, where perhaps a servant was closing up. The town had thinned, this past week, to the bone.

He rested his hand on the window frame, the wood still warm from the day.

A word began to form itself in his head. A name, or the beginning of one. 

He did not let it finish. 

He set it down, as he had set down the steward's letter, as he had set down Caroline's unopened note, as he had set down, in April, in a parlor at Hunsford, everything he had thought he understood of himself.

*Melancholy,* he thought, and despised the word.
