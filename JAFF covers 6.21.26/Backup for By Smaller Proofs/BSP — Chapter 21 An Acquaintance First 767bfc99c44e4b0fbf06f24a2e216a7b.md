# BSP — Chapter 21: An Acquaintance First

✅ Revision complete

---

The breakfast-room at Pemberley was at its best before the house had agreed what the day should become. The morning light entered low and pale across the Turkey carpet; the silver on the sideboard gave it back without brilliance; the servants moved with that quiet exactness which made comfort appear almost accidental. There was toast under a cover, coffee steaming in the urn, a dish of eggs before the fire, and no one at the table but Georgiana. Mrs. Annesley had gone to Lambton the evening before to sit with her sister, who was ill.

Darcy stopped on the threshold.

He had not expected her so early. Yesterday — the first full morning with Mrs. Gardiner and Miss Bennet under the roof — Georgiana had come down ten minutes later than her usual hour, glancing toward the door as if expecting someone worth waiting for. She had adjusted her schedule to Caroline's from duty, during the Bingley party's stay. This was different. Georgiana, who had once been punctual from obedience, had become unpunctual from hope.

Yet here she was, seated at the end of the table with a cup of chocolate before her and a shawl of Norwich silk drawn close about her shoulders. She would not wear it when the guests came down — she had taught herself that much, after Mrs. Annesley had gently observed that shawls with fringe were not kind to nervous fingers in company. But at breakfast, before the day required her to be composed, she allowed herself this one soft thing.

"Good morning," he said.

She looked up quickly, and the look had in it the old startle and something newer beneath it. Pleasure, perhaps. Expectation not disappointed.

"Did you sleep badly?"

"No." She considered, as if truth required more exactness than comfort. "Not badly. I was thinking."

He had not slept well himself. Miss Bennet's words from the evening before had followed him upstairs and had not had the grace to leave him at his door.

Darcy took his usual chair. A servant moved at once to attend him; he accepted coffee, refused eggs, accepted toast because refusal would occasion another movement, and waited until the man had withdrawn. The door closed. The room settled.

Georgiana looked at her chocolate.

He had meant to say nothing. He had meant to allow her all the ease she could claim before the guests appeared. But her hands were too still upon the table, and Georgiana at sixteen ought not to have hands so practiced in stillness.

"What were you thinking of?"

She colored at once.

This did not reassure him either.

"Only—" She stopped. Her fingers found the fringe of her shawl and worked it once before letting go. "Only that the house feels different."

Darcy said nothing.

It did. He had known it since the first evening, and more dangerously since yesterday. Pemberley had altered before under company. Bingley brought noise and good humor; Caroline brought arrangement; the Hursts brought trunks, complaints, and a degree of indolence that settled into the furniture before they did. His aunt's visits filled rooms with solemn consequence before she had crossed their thresholds. But this was not that.

Mrs. Gardiner and Miss Bennet had not occupied Pemberley. They had entered it, moved through it, and somehow left it altered.

The breakfast-room knew it. The second cup placed beside Georgiana's was not for Mrs. Annesley. The chair at Darcy's left had not been moved, yet he knew, with mortifying precision, that Miss Bennet had sat there yesterday and had argued with his sister about whether Derbyshire butter deserved its reputation. She had not argued solemnly. Georgiana had laughed over it.

"Different in what way?" he asked.

Georgiana lifted her eyes to him. "Pleasanter."

"Yes," he said, and heard how little the word contained.

"It is pleasant to have someone younger in the house."

Someone whose presence did not turn every sentence into a lesson.

Not a companion. Not a master of music, of classics, of riding. Not Mrs. Annesley, whose kindness was a steady lamp and whose age, though not great, belonged to another country. Not Caroline, whose accomplishments had entered every room before her and left no air for anyone else.

Someone younger.

Darcy sipped his coffee. 

"Miss Bennet has been kind to you."

"Yes." Georgiana's answer came at once. "Kind, but also—" She searched for the word, finally settling on, "She speaks as if I may answer."

The coffee had cooled. He had not tasted it.

"Do others not?"

"They do." She was distressed by the implication at once. "Mrs. Annesley always does. And you do, Fitzwilliam."

"But?" he said.

Georgiana bent her head. "But Miss Bennet seems to expect that I will."

So slight an alteration, and yet the whole balance had shifted.

He had not meant to make a life in which his sister had forgotten to expect anything. He had not meant protection to become a room so carefully furnished that its occupant did not know whether any chair was meant to be used. He had given her masters, a companion, music, books, even London — as if noise and crowds could cure what his caution had made. Every attention he could conceive and every caution he could afford.

He turned his cup a quarter-inch upon its saucer.

*Attention is not the same as company.*

Miss Bennet's voice, returning with unwelcome clarity.

He broke the edge of his toast with his knife.

"That is a valuable quality in a friend," he said.

Georgiana looked up.

"A friend," she repeated.

"An acquaintance," he corrected, too quickly. "At first."

Her eyes moved back to her plate, but not before he had seen the hope there. It was small, almost ashamed of itself.

"Of course."

He had done it again. Given the gift with one hand and measured the danger of it with the other.

Darcy pushed his toast aside.

"And Mrs. Gardiner?”

"Oh, Mrs. Gardiner is so easy. She is never—" Georgiana stopped — the same abrupt retreat he had seen a hundred times this past year when she feared she had spoken past her allowance. "Never waiting to be pleased,” she finished.

Darcy was obliged to look away for a moment. Through the window, the prospect was fair and dry. Another hot day.

"Mrs. Gardiner has much sense," he said.

"And Miss Bennet has spirits." Georgiana considered this, then added conscientiously, "Not noisy spirits. Not like—" She stopped again.

Darcy knew who had almost been named. Liveliness, in Georgiana's experience, had worn a handsome face and an easy laugh, and it had nearly destroyed her. The terror of Ramsgate had not faded; it had only learned to be quiet.

But not all liveliness was ruin.

The sentence sounded like Miss Bennet. He was not pleased to discover how thoroughly her opinions had colonized his own.

Georgiana's fingers returned to the fringe of her shawl.

Darcy set down his knife.

He had not meant to raise this at breakfast. He had not meant to raise it at all until he had spoken with Mrs. Gardiner. But Georgiana's hands were still, and her hope was visible, and the morning would not wait for a better-furnished intention. 

"What if we invited a young lady," he said. 

Georgia gave him all her attention

"Someone nearer your own age. She might visit for a time. Some time, if the arrangement proved satisfactory."

Her eyes widened. "Who?"

He had not expected the question so soon. Foolishly.

"A daughter of one of your friends?” Eagerness quickened her voice, and sharpened it. "Or—someone from Lambton?"

"No."

"Then—" She stopped, uncertain.

Darcy's left hand gripped the side seam of his trousers, beneath the edge of the table, where she could not see it.

"Miss Catherine Bennet," he said.

Georgiana's lips parted.

"Kitty?" She leaned back a shade, but did not retreat. "Miss Elizabeth called her Kitty when she told us of Jane's letter yesterday afternoon. She said no one at Longbourn could remain Miss Catherine for above four minutes togethe."

Darcy should not have found that pleasing.

He did.

"What else did Miss Bennet tell you?"

Georgiana's face brightened. "Just in the past week, Kitty had devised a way of keeping Mrs. Gardiner's two eldest children from quarreling over battledore and shuttlecock. They would not obey the nurse, because each believed the other had begun it. Miss Catherine told them there must be a court."

His brows rose.

"Yes, a court. She made the youngest child the judge, because he could not understand the matter and would therefore be impartial. The others were obliged to argue their case before him, and every time they interrupted one another she made them bow to the judge and begin again."

He could just picture it. Despite himself, Darcy laughed.

Georgiana laughed too, softly and with relief, as if his amusement had given her permission for her own.

"And did this produce justice?"

"I do not think so. Miss Elizabeth said the judge ate part of the evidence."

"The evidence?"

"A piece of cake. It had some bearing on the quarrel. It wasn’t clear how."

"Then he was well chosen. Many judges have disposed of evidence less agreeably."

Georgiana smiled down at her chocolate. "Miss Catherine made them all laugh, at least. And then they forgot to quarrel."

There was the point, as plainly as if Elizabeth had placed it before him in writing: He had been guarding against the wrong peril.

Kitty Bennet, whom he had not troubled himself to distinguish from her youngest sister, had managed to keep children from discord with nothing but invention and a piece of cake. She had governed, not by authority, but by wit. It was not polish. It was not a quality Caroline Bingley would have praised, or, having recognized it, have thought worth praising. It was something less safe on paper and more valuable in a room.

"And Miss Elizabeth Bennet thought well of it?" he asked.

"She did. Though she did say that Kitty was much improved by having persons smaller than herself to command, for it prevented her from being the smallest person in every argument."

He could hear Elizabeth in the line — the tenderness under the wit and the apology under the tenderness.

*My sister Catherine.*

Miss Bennet's chin had lifted when she said it. Not *a Bennet*.  Not one of the family he had judged and written of and judged again.

He had thought only of the dangers.

"Would you like such a person to visit?" he asked.

Georgiana's smile vanished. Not from reluctance. From astonishment.

"Here?"

"If it could be arranged properly."

"At Pemberley?"

"Yes."

"To visit me?"

The last words were very quiet.

Darcy had thought himself prepared. He was not.

"Yes," he said. "If you wished it."

Her eyes dropped. She pressed her lips together. "Miss Catherine might expect—"

"What?"

"That I should be entertaining. Or accomplished. Or very grand."

"I do not think Miss Catherine sounds very grand herself."

He heard his own answer. Too prompt. Too grim.

Georgina glanced up at him. "Is she very improper?"

The question was so direct that he could not protect himself with formality.

"I do not know her well."

"But you are afraid she may be."

He did not answer.

"Because of what happened before," she said.

The urn hissed softly. A footman passed somewhere beyond the door. The house continued. Darcy did not.

They did not speak of Ramsgate. They had spoken of it at first in broken necessities: arrangements, dismissals, letters, what must be done, what must never be said. Then silence had become mercy, and mercy had hardened into law.

Georgiana's hands folded together in her lap.

"I know you are afraid for me," she said. There was no reproach in it. That was worse.

Darcy rose and went to the sideboard, though he wanted nothing there. He stood with his back to her long enough to master his face.

*There is a kind of ease that cannot be taught by older people, however much they love us.*

He returned to his chair.

"Miss Catherine Bennet is young," he said. "Her manner has not always recommended her — though I cannot name a particular fault beyond it. But Miss Bennet believes she has warmth and better sense than has always been allowed to show."

"Miss Bennet would know."

The simple trust of it pierced him.

"She is not infallible," he said, as though a man who had misjudged every Bennet in the county were qualified to say so.

"No." Georgiana hesitated. "But she sees people."

He had no answer to that.

Georgiana pondered a moment. Her hand rose to the table to reach for her cocoa. It paused. She tilted her head in that way women do when they are calculating.

"If Miss Catherine came, Mrs. Gardiner and Miss Elizabeth might stay longer."

Darcy did not move. He was aware, with perfect clarity, that his sister had just said aloud what he had not permitted himself even to think.

"I did not mean—that is, Mrs. Gardiner would be less solitary. And Miss Bennet would have her sister. And if Miss Catherine were uncomfortable, Miss Bennet could help her."

"Georgiana," he said carefully, "Miss Bennet's remaining at Pemberley cannot be the object of any invitation."

"I know." Her voice was small.

"But if Miss Catherine were invited for your sake, and if Mrs. Gardiner approved the arrangement, and if Mr. Bennet consented — then it might be considered."

"Considered," she repeated. Then, more quietly, "But do you wish it?"

He could not refuse her that.

"Yes."

She looked toward the windows, where the morning had strengthened by degrees and now reached clear across the carpet. The light brightened the silk fringe where it lay across her arm.

"A friend," she said, almost to herself.

The color in her face. The smile she was trying to restrain. He thought of all the years in which he had congratulated himself on keeping danger at a distance. He thought of the distance itself.

"Mrs. Gardiner must be consulted," he said.

Georgiana sat straighter. "This morning?"

"If the opportunity offers."

"And if she thinks it unwise?"

"Then we shall attend to her judgment."

Georgiana nodded. It was a reasonable answer. It was also a blow, and he saw her receive it with all the gentleness he had taught her.

He could not bear the success of his own instruction.

"But if she sees no objection," he said, "you may write a note to Miss Catherine — to be enclosed with mine to Mr. Bennet."

Georgiana's breath caught.

Her eyes shone. "Today?"

"Today, if Mrs. Gardiner agrees."

The door opened before she could answer. A servant entered to replenish the coffee, and the breakfast-room became, in an instant, a public place again. Georgiana lowered her eyes to her plate. Darcy took up his cup. 

Mrs. Gardiner would come down soon. Miss Bennet would come with her, perhaps laughing at some private exchange upon the stairs, perhaps grave from a letter, perhaps entirely herself in a way that continued to undo every defense he had arranged against her.

He would not speak to Miss Bennet first. He would not turn this into a triumph for her argument, nor into a confession of his own. He would do the thing properly, if it was to be done at all.

Mrs. Gardiner first. Then his own letter to Mr. Bennet, and Miss Bennet's — with Georgiana's note folded inside hers.

Pemberley had admitted one Bennet under its roof and been altered by it.

He was about to invite another.
