# BSP — Chapter 5: A Carriage at the Door

✅ Chapter complete

---

Elizabeth had folded the same two stockings twice before she gave up on orderliness and began putting things into the trunk in the order they came to hand. The trunk stood open at the foot of her bed, a small wooden sea into which she was pouring — without ceremony, without much accuracy — the furniture of a month's absence: gloves, a writing case, three books, the sturdiest of her walking boots, a spare pair of slippers, two caps she would certainly not wear, a length of lace she had no use for and had packed only because the drawer was open. Her method, she reflected, was her mother's.

The thought did not improve her mood.

From the passage came the sound of Mrs. Bennet's voice. "Not *that* cloth, Hill — what can you be thinking? It is stained, anyone can see it is stained; my sister Gardiner shall think we have nothing better. The Holland cloth. The Holland! Have I not said so three times? And the cold fowl — is Cook attending to the cold fowl — and the ratafia cakes, they must be fresh, not yesterday's, Hill, not yesterday's — Hill. *Hill.* The Holland."

Elizabeth heard, with what she recognized as a small and not quite kind satisfaction, the silence that followed: the silence of Hill moving in the direction of the Holland cloth, which had already been set out an hour ago because Hill had been planning for the Gardiners' arrival since last Tuesday.

"And fresh flowers in the hall. *Fresh.* Not those — Hill, those are faded — and is someone seeing to the horses? They shall want feeding before they go on — Hill!"

A door closed on the floor below. Hill had contrived an escape.

Elizabeth abandoned her trunk and sat on the edge of her bed.

The house this morning felt close, overheated. Too many people being too loud in too many different keys. Mrs. Bennet from the stairs. Mary from the back parlor, working through a set of variations at the pianoforte that did not improve on repetition. And from the small parlor below, Lydia: audibly not reading the book she had taken up, audibly turning pages at a rate that meant nothing, audibly refusing to come and help, or to answer her mother, or to be anywhere but exactly where she had chosen to sulk.

She and Lydia had not spoken in two days. They had sat at meals, Lydia radiating such palpable indignation across the table that conversation around her had the quality of weather discussed near a distant storm. But the ordinary business of sisters — a borrowed ribbon, a shared complaint, an observation tossed from one side of the room to the other — had gone unconducted. It was like watching a clock tick without the hands moving: something one had always taken for mechanical was revealed to be a matter of choice, and the choice, for now, was being withheld.

Jane came in with a stack of clean linen folded against her waist and the look of a woman who had been carrying it for some minutes before finding a destination. It was her room as much as Elizabeth's; none of the disorder was her doing.

"Hill has been driven out of the linen cupboard," Jane said. "I offered to carry these."

"Offered is a polite word for it, I think."

Jane's mouth did the small thing it did when she would not quite admit to a joke. She set the linen on the chair by the window. "Have you nearly done?"

"I have nearly *begun*. I have been packing for two hours and I have packed approximately four items. I believe at this rate I shall depart in November."

"You shall depart in the morning." Jane opened the trunk, surveyed the contents of the trunk with a calm that Elizabeth considered almost offensive, and began, without asking, to rearrange. The stockings went to one side. The books went in as ballast. The length of lace, which had no business in the trunk at all, went back into its drawer. "The Gardiners will be here by tea, and my uncle means to be on the road with you at first light."

"I know."

"Then here is the dressing case. And you will want —"

Elizabeth put her hand on Jane's wrist. It was the smallest gesture; she could not afterward have said why she had made it. Jane's hand stilled under hers. Neither of them looked at the other, and neither of them moved, and for half a minute there was only the distant, persistent music from the back parlor and Mrs. Bennet on the stairs.

"I shall be all right," Jane said.

Elizabeth let go. Jane took up the folding where she had left it.

"But do write," Jane said. “Please.”

Elizabeth was going to say *of course* and did not. The word *write* had arrived in the middle of what was not quite a silence, with the quiet weight of a word standing in for a longer sentence, and Elizabeth heard, in the shape of it, the rest. 

Jane would not ask what was in Hertfordshire now that Elizabeth was leaving it; Jane would not ask whether any particular name arose in conversation where Elizabeth was going; Jane would not ask anything that required Elizabeth to be careful in answering. Jane asked *write.* And Elizabeth heard: *tell me about the hills and the cathedrals and the people at your aunt's table, and in telling me those things, tell me whether anyone mentions the one thing I cannot name, and in not telling me whether they do, tell me all the same.*

"I shall write often," Elizabeth said. "I shall bore you with cathedrals."

"Please do."

Jane closed the trunk. She did not look up from its latch as she spoke again.

"And Lizzy. If the cathedrals are not interesting, any other news will do."

Elizabeth was saved from having to reply by the sound of wheels on the drive.

---

The Gardiners arrived with the quality of weather breaking. For a long moment at the front of the house there was only the crunch of gravel and the murmur of the horses; and then, when the carriage door opened, the scene was populated all at once with a quantity of children.

Four of them, none above the age of ten.  Elizabeth, descending the stairs behind Jane, had time to register them only as a collective phenomenon — a quantity of bonnets and boots and a hat detaching itself from a small head and rolling along the drive — before the eldest, a girl of perhaps eight, captured the hat with an efficiency that suggested considerable practice.

Mr. Gardiner came down last: a square, comfortable man in plain brown, not tall, with the same dark eyes as his sister and none of her nerves. He paused on the drive, said something quiet to the boy who had run out to the horses — Elizabeth could not hear it, but the boy's face lifted in the way of a person who has been spoken to rather than spoken at — and turned toward the house.

Mrs. Gardiner’s attention was elsewhere. "Maria, not the flower bed. *Not the flower bed,* Maria. William, you may take your cousin Jane’s hand and you may walk to the front door like a gentleman, and if you do, I shall see that you are given a ginger biscuit, and if you do not, the biscuit shall be eaten by someone else and you shall look on. Those are the terms."

William, a fair-haired child of about six, considered the terms with the expression of a man weighing principle against interest, and chose interest.

"Elizabeth," Mrs. Gardiner said, coming up the steps. She was a small woman, not handsome, with a round intelligent face under a plain bonnet — the sort of face one would trust to have read a letter carefully before replying. It softened, now, into a warmth Elizabeth had been, without admitting it, waiting for. "Oh my dear. How you look."

"How do I look?"

"Like a woman who needs a change of scene. Come, kiss me."

Elizabeth did. She kissed her aunt and then her uncle — who smelled, comfortingly, of the road and of his own unostentatious cleanliness — and then she was engulfed, briefly, by children. Maria offered her a curtsey and a grave observation about the length of the journey — she had her father's face and her mother's composure, and the air of a child who had been the eldest long enough to know it. William demanded a biscuit. The other two — a girl of perhaps four and the smallest boy, not above three — pressed themselves close to Jane on either side and took stock of Elizabeth with the steady scrutiny of very small strangers.

"My dear Mrs. Bennet," Mrs. Gardiner said, turning, for Mrs. Bennet had achieved the bottom of the stairs at this point, in a lace cap and a high color — "how kind of you to receive us with such — yes, Edward has been speaking of nothing but Longbourn for a week, the children have been in an agony of anticipation, yes, indeed the roads were excellent — no, I thank you, we have eaten on the way — yes, a little tea presently would be very welcome, if it is no trouble, though please, Mrs. Bennet, do not put yourself to —"

It was not, Elizabeth understood, that her aunt prevented Mrs. Bennet from speaking. Mrs. Bennet spoke, and spoke a great deal; but Mrs. Gardiner's replies had the quality of a person laying stones across a stream, stepping lightly from one to the next, and by the time Mrs. Bennet had achieved the drawing room her speech had been somehow converted, by these calm interjections, from lamentation into information, and from information into instruction for the tea tray, and the tea tray was being produced by Hill at precisely the moment required.

Mr. Gardiner, in the hall, was being spoken to by Mary. Mary had emerged from the back parlor with an air of having been summoned by a private signal, and was informing him, in the voice she reserved for pronouncements, that the principle of hospitality as laid down by the ancients required —

"Yes, quite so, Mary, quite so," Mr. Gardiner said, with the grave courtesy he would have extended to the Lord Chancellor. "I have long thought so myself. Shall we go through?"

Mary went through, almost visibly satisfied — a state Elizabeth had seldom observed in her before, and which had, she suspected, to do with being answered rather than interrupted.

Kitty was already there. She had, it transpired, been out in the sweep almost before the carriage stopped — fetched by the sound of wheels before anyone else had rallied — and she was in the hall now, hatless, a little breathless, bent over the smallest boy to inspect whatever he was producing from his pocket. She straightened at her aunt's greeting and submitted to being kissed with a flushed, too-bright smile; then, almost before the kiss was done, her attention was back among the children, and when the middle child embarked on a long and not quite coherent history of a bird seen from the carriage, Kitty attended as though the bird were the only matter of consequence in the hall.

She was saved — or more properly, given her opening — by a sound Elizabeth had been half expecting and more than half dreading.

Lydia had come out into the hall.

She had emerged from the small parlor, a book still held shut over one finger, her hair dressed but her bonnet pointedly absent. She did not cross to her aunt; she stopped where she stood. Her face was so empty of expression that Elizabeth understood she had rehearsed this.

"Aunt. Uncle."

It was not quite rude. The words were the correct ones. It was the emptiness of the voice that was rude — and the way she did not come closer to kiss her aunt. She nodded, once, and turned her attention to Elizabeth with the same level gaze.

"I hope you enjoy your journey."

"Lydia —"

"I am sure you will."

She turned and slowly stepped up the stairs. The words had cost her more than Lydia had planned to pay. At the landing she paused briefly, one hand on the banister, and then she was gone, and a door above closed with the soft finality of a door shut by someone determined not to slam it.

Mrs. Bennet, from the drawing room, half-noticed and waved it off — *Lydia is so vexed, poor child, she feels things* — and returned, at length, to the unsatisfactoriness of Hertfordshire weather.

Mrs. Gardiner laid a hand on Elizabeth's arm, very lightly, for no longer than it took Elizabeth to register the touch; and then she turned to the children, who had been patient beyond what was reasonable, and said, "Now. You have all been good. Who should like a biscuit?"

William gave it as his opinion that he should like his biscuit now, thank you, and not later, and with sugar if convenient.

They had begun to move toward the drawing room — the whole mass of them, children and tea tray and Mrs. Bennet's commentary — when the smallest Gardiner, the boy not above three, stopped where he was. It was the stopping of a child who has arrived at the furthest edge of his patience. He did not scream. He did not throw himself down. He simply stood in the hall, just where the runner met the stone, and began to sob: a small, defeated crying, the kind produced by a person who has sat very still in a carriage for many hours and has now been asked to be civil in a strange house.

Mrs. Gardiner turned; but she was already burdened with the hand Mrs. Bennet had pressed upon her arm, and with Mrs. Bennet herself, who was three sentences into an explanation of the tea.

It was Kitty who crossed the hall.

She did not fuss. She did not stoop and coo. She simply sat down on the second-to-bottom step of the staircase, so that her face was level with the child's, and said, in a voice Elizabeth had not heard her use before — practical, unhurried — "Carriages can be so tiring. Should you like to sit beside me for a moment?"

The child considered. He walked, on uncertain legs, across the distance between them, and allowed himself to be received onto Kitty's lap. She did not embrace him. She held him in the unassuming manner of a chair. After a moment she took, from her own pocket, a small sugared biscuit that had no business being there — evidence, perhaps, of some private negotiation with Hill earlier in the morning — and broke it in half, and gave him one half without comment.

The crying stopped. The child chewed.

Mrs. Gardiner had paused in the drawing-room doorway. She took a careful inventory of her niece; and then she went in to her tea.

Kitty said, to the top of the child's head, "When you have finished, we might go out of doors. The lawn is very good for hoops, and Maria shall show you. Should that do?"

The child nodded with the solemnity of one concluding a treaty.

Kitty rose — carefully, so as not to disturb him — and carried him into the drawing room to be restored to his mother. Elizabeth, finding herself no longer required, slipped back upstairs to the unfinished trunk.

The tea things were handed out. Hill, released from Mrs. Bennet's surveillance by the consuming interest of the tea, slipped past with an armful of towels and the face of a woman who had been reprieved. 

Soon after, Kitty, with Maria at her shoulder, marshaled the smaller children toward the garden door; there was laughter from the lawn for the better part of an hour. 

Elizabeth did not go down. She closed her trunk at last, and turned the key. In the hall below, Mrs. Gardiner was heard to say — *yes, Mrs. Bennet, I quite agree, the children are far better aired. Kitty is very good with them. I had not known* — and Mrs. Bennet, on a note of discovery, rallied, and found other qualities in Kitty at some length; but Elizabeth had stopped listening.

---

Dinner passed. Mrs. Bennet, with five guests and a fresh audience, was in her element. The children ate with the seriousness of small soldiers. Mr. Gardiner and Mr. Bennet spoke, at their end of the table, of mills and of roads, and afterwards retired with the brandy to the study. Lydia did not come down. Her chair stayed empty, and no one remarked upon it, which was itself a kind of remarking.

The children went up to ready for bed. The ladies withdrew.

The drawing-room fire had been built up against the evening cool. Mrs. Bennet occupied the sofa and the greater part of the conversation, having taken up a grievance concerning a neighbor and a dish not yet returned. Mary, at the pianoforte but not playing, was prepared to supply, at the first opening, an observation upon the obligations of borrowing. Mrs. Gardiner had taken the chair by the fire. Jane sat close beside her — very nearly too close — the way one sits when one has been waiting for a particular listener for a particular purpose.

Elizabeth took up a book and did not read it. She turned a page at reasonable intervals. From the sofa came her mother, clear and uninterrupted, with only poor Kitty forced to respond. From the fire came only fragments, low and pitched for two.

"— all the way to Gracechurch Street —"

"— and to stay not above three minutes —"

"— but Miss Bingley meant well —"

It was not eavesdropping; eavesdropping required an object. Elizabeth only knew what the fragments meant because she knew who they were about; and she knew who they were about because Jane, who had not been able to speak of Mr. Bingley to her in eight days, was able, now, in a low voice by the fire, to speak to their aunt of his sister.

Mrs. Gardiner had been there. She had received the call — or the insufficiency of it — in her own parlor in Gracechurch Street, and had drawn her own conclusions long before tonight. Jane was not telling her anything she did not already know. She was going over it again, the way one returns to a bruise not to learn its shape but to have someone else confirm it is still there. Elizabeth had not known that Miss Bingley had called. She had not known the visit had lasted three minutes or less. Jane had carried all of it, and had not laid it down until she was given a pair of hands that already knew its weight.

Elizabeth sightlessly turned another page.

The sting was small but sharp. She was not owed Jane's confidences; she had been the confidante for a long time, and would be again. But tonight Jane had looked around her own house and had chosen to speak, not to her nearest sister, but to the aunt who had been at Longbourn eight hours. 

She, of all people, had no claim to make one. Elizabeth had a letter folded in her writing-case that Jane had never seen, and could never see, and about which Elizabeth had never spoken.

The fragments at the fire settled into silence; Jane's hand had found Mrs. Gardiner's on the arm of the chair. Mrs. Bennet, at length, discovered the hour, and discovered that she was tired, and delivered both discoveries to the room. They went up.

Jane, by the fire, had spoken of a man who had disappointed her by not coming back. He had not written; he had not called; there was no letter in her writing-case to explain him. Jane's grief was simple. Clean. It asked no questions of her own conduct. It did not require her to reconsider what she had said, at Hunsford, or to whom, or why.

Elizabeth envied her, briefly, the decisiveness of it.

---

Next morning, they all stood on the drive in the pale early light before Elizabeth had quite managed to believe that they were leaving.

The trunks were on; the horses had been brought round; her bonnet had been tied and retied by Jane, who had a better hand for ribbons. Her mother had kissed her three times, each kiss accompanied by a separate instruction concerning her health, her letters, and the folly of eating the food of inns. Lydia had not come down. No one had sent for her, and no one had suggested that anyone send for her.

Mr. Gardiner was settling his wife into the carriage with the small practical attentions of a man who had been handing his wife into carriages for fifteen years and intended to hand her into carriages for fifty more. Mrs. Gardiner arranged the rug over her knees and looked out at Elizabeth with a face that said *come.*

Jane was beside her at the carriage door.

"I shall write," Elizabeth said.

"I know."

"Often."

"I know."

Jane pressed her hand. Then she stepped back, and Elizabeth went up into the carriage, and the door was closed, and the horses shifted in their harness, and the carriage began to move.

The house slid past the window: door, step, the hedge by the gravel sweep. Elizabeth had promised herself she would not seek her father's study window, and she did not. Beyond the hedge lay the front lawn — a pale green square in the early-morning sun.

Kitty was there.

She had tucked up her skirt and was running, in a half-remembered way that suggested she had not run for some years, pacing a hoop across the grass with the stick held out before her like an ensign of office. Maria was shouting direction. William had seated himself on the grass and was offering his observations upon the business. The middle child was trying to overtake Kitty, and was failing. The smallest child stood at the edge of the lawn with his hands in the air and his face tipped back, laughing.

The carriage turned past the hedge, and they were gone — the lawn, the children, Kitty — and Elizabeth sat forward against the cushion and closed her eyes for the length of a breath.

"Well, my dear," said Mr. Gardiner from the opposite seat, mildly. "And now we are away."

"Yes," Elizabeth said.

"A long day's drive," Mr. Gardiner said, opposite. "The Swan tonight, and then north in earnest."

Elizabeth opened her eyes. The hedgerows of Hertfordshire were already beginning to pass. She had not known how much of herself she had mistaken for the house she was leaving; what else she had mistaken, she would have a month to find out.

The carriage bowled on toward the Meryton road.
