# BSP — Chapter 32: Recognition

The morning had made no promise of coolness. It came in pale and already warm at the edges, lying across the curtains in heavy sheets and turning the white counterpane into something too bright for comfort. Elizabeth had slept little, and had waked with that peculiar conviction, produced by a restless night, that every thought which had visited her at two o’clock must be sensible because it had been suffered through.

Her gown for traveling was not laid out; she had not gone so far. She had swept up the papers, pens and books and collected them on the small table by the window. These were not decisions. They were merely preparations of a sort too small to accuse her.

Mrs. Gardiner must depart. That fact had stood beside Elizabeth’s bed all night with more authority than any argument. Kitty might remain. Georgiana would wish it; Mrs. Annesley would make it proper; Mr. Bennet had already sanctioned what was necessary, and Kitty, now among kittens, music, and a young lady who cared what she became would not easily be restored to Longbourn without feeling herself injured by Providence.

But Elizabeth was not Kitty.

Her aunt's voice returned, dry and exact: *Do not dress it entirely in Kitty's frock.* She had been cutting the cloth all night.

She tied one ribbon, untied it, and let the bonnet fall back upon the chair. She had not decided to walk. Her hands had.

To remain at Pemberley after her aunt’s departure would be no longer the consequence of road, weather, invitation, sisterly convenience, or any of those useful circumstances by which inclination may be disguised from its owner. It would be choice. It would be declaration before declaration had been offered. It would say, to every sensible observer in Pemberley, and most cruelly to herself, that she had preferred uncertainty near Mr. Darcy to safety at a distance.

And if she were wrong?

The question had worn itself smooth by repetition. It no longer startled; it occupied. She had been wrong once, and wrong with such confidence that confidence itself had become suspect. To go away would be painful. To stay might be presumption. Pain, at least, was respectable. Presumption had no proper defense.

She opened the drawer wider and took out her gloves.

A knock came at the door. A maid entered, made her curtsey, and held out a folded letter on a small salver.

"A letter for you, miss."

She took it and did not move. The gloves remained in her left hand. The letter lay in her right.

She knew the hand.

There was no reason the room should alter; it did not. The bed stood unmade only by her own restlessness. The washstand, the chair, the open drawer, the offending bonnet — all continued in their places with admirable indifference.

The paper was folded with exactness. The seal bore no extravagance. Her name was written in a form neither intimate nor cold.

*Miss Bennet.*

Not *Madam*.

The absurd relief of that first observation nearly overcame her. She set the gloves down, then picked them up again because she had set them upon the wrong chair, though no one in the world could have cared. Her fingers found the edge of the seal, and stopped.

No.

Not here. Not inside his walls, with the house around her and Pemberley itself seemingly built into the very paper. If he meant to wound, she would not be wounded under his roof. If he meant to explain, she would not receive explanation where the house itself might do half her feeling for her.

And if he meant neither?

That possibility she could not examine indoors.

Elizabeth put on her bonnet. The ribbons would not tie properly; she tied them badly and let them be. She took her gloves because her hands required employment, not because she intended to wear them. The letter she slipped into a pocket. She crossed the room, descended the stairs, and passed through a side door that a footman opened before she had quite reached it.

No one stopped her. That was Pemberley too: a house in which attention did not become interference.

She went first to the formal garden, because it was nearest and because a gravel path offered the comfort of direction. The beds lay in full summer order, their edges clipped, their colors arranged with that art which made itself invisible. Beyond them the land fell away, and farther the trees gathered in darker ranks, their shade still some distance off.

She walked until the house was behind her shoulder and the windows no longer seemed capable of seeing. A stone bench stood near the turn of the path. Sitting was impossible. She drew on one glove, then took it off — the paper would not feel right through kid — and pressed the letter flat against her skirt.

She broke the seal.

The first line was simple.

*I cannot allow my silence to be mistaken for indifference.*

Elizabeth read it once without breathing, and then again with such attention that the words ceased for a moment to be words and became only marks, black and exact upon the page.

Not indifference.

She had expected, though she had not admitted it, some reference to her manner at dinner, or to what she had asked under the stars, or to the sharpness with which she had told him that his interference had cost Jane a year of her happiness. She had expected pain to answer pain. 

She had expected dignity. She had expected, if not accusation, then at least the proud suffering of a man too honorable to complain and too injured to be easy.

This was none of those things.

She walked on.

The gravel gave way to a narrower packed-earth path, still kept, but less commanded. Trees stood nearer here, starting to crowd. The grass beyond them was not trimmed into obedience. Elizabeth walked, and stopped to read, and walked again.

He wrote that his feelings were unchanged.

Here she stopped.

The word did not strike as Hunsford had struck. There was no violence in it, no demand entering before permission, no expectation that she must hear because he had resolved to speak. The sentence stood with a gravity that made no advance. His feelings were unchanged — except, he wrote, that he knew better than he once had how little right they gave him to disturb her peace.

Elizabeth pressed the page flatter against the palm of her glove.

At Hunsford, love had come armed with every objection to herself, her family, and her connections, and had seemed to expect gratitude for surviving them. Here, love stood at a distance and named the distance as her due.

She had been prepared to defend herself. It was difficult to defend oneself against a man who had made no attack.

She walked farther down the path.

The next paragraph she read too quickly and was obliged to return to it, angry at herself for cowardice. It concerned Jane. Not much — less than she had feared, less than another man might have claimed — but enough.

He had been wrong. That was how he put it. Wrong in judging Jane’s heart; wrong in allowing that judgment to govern another’s happiness. Mr. Bingley had known the truth before he left Pemberley. What followed was Mr. Bingley’s own act, as it ought always to have been.

No parade of usefulness. No account of difficulty. No description of Bingley’s distress, or his own sacrifice, or the delicacy of restoring what he had injured. Jane's happiness entered the letter only to be freed from any claim he might make on it.

Elizabeth folded the sheet along its crease, opened it again, and read the paragraph a third time.

*As it ought always to have been.*

The words found an older wound but did not press it. They only showed where it had been.

Jane's letter, all restrained joy and tremulous certainty. Bingley's easy warmth at Pemberley, his readiness to be pleased, his heart so much more transparent than the people nearest him had honored. Mr. Darcy at dinner saying, with care enough to make the sentence almost painful, that Bingley had acted freely.

He had been telling the truth then. More than the truth, she thought. He had been refusing to use it.

The path curved toward a little rise where the trees thinned. From there she could see, not the whole house, but part of it: one long pale front through branches, steady in the morning light, without triumph. Pemberley did not look like a trap. That was inconvenient.

She read on.

He had not spoken because she was his guest. He had not wished that any kindness shown under his roof should seem to gather weight against her. He had erred, he wrote, if his silence had caused pain; but he hoped she would believe that pain to be the result of restraint, not indifference. He asked for no answer now, nor for any kindness she did not freely wish to give.

*Freely.* The word was not ornament. At Hunsford, any kindness she might have given would have been claimed rather than received. Here he asked only for what she chose to give.

Elizabeth sat down at last, not upon a bench but upon a low stone edging half lost in moss. The impropriety of the seat would have interested her on another morning. On this one, it only placed her closer to the ground.

*No answer now.*

Not *God bless you*. Not farewell. He had told her what he felt and required nothing in return.

His first letter returned so clearly that for a moment she was again in the lane at Hunsford, damp grass under her shoes and mortification in every line.

The first had said, in effect: You have been wrong, and I will show you where.

The second: I have been wrong, and I will not use my amendment to claim you.

Elizabeth bent over the page. A breeze moved one corner of it; she held it down with her thumb.

She had asked silently and often for proof. She had imagined proof, perhaps, as something large enough to relieve her of the work of choosing — a single act so unmistakable that doubt would become ungrateful and acceptance merely sensible. She had wanted, if she was honest, to be spared the harder business of believing her own eyes.

No such rescue had come.

There had been only a man who bowed to her uncle without condescension. Who had stood in a stable doorway and watched Kitty Bennet put a kitten into Georgiana's uncertain hands, and had not moved to prevent it, though every cautious instinct must have told him to — and had heard Georgiana laugh. Whose face had done something Elizabeth could not name at the time but could name now. 

Who stood under the stars and did not defend himself when defense would have been easier than silence.

Proof had not arrived like thunder. It had accumulated like daylight.

And she had seen it.

That was the thought from which she had been hiding. She had known he was not what she had once believed him. She had known it in pieces long before this morning. And the word *love*, though powerful, explained less than she had once believed. She had loved Wickham’s story and found it false. She had loved her own cleverness and found it insufficient.

But she had seen Mr. Darcy, day after day, in particulars too small and too undramatic to have been wished for. She had seen him when he was not speaking to her. She had seen him as master, brother, friend, host, man corrected. She had seen him refuse triumph.

If she could not trust that, what could she trust?

In the gallery, weeks ago, she had stood before his portrait and felt something she could not name. Now she could. It was recognition: the slow, costly business of looking long enough to see truly.

Elizabeth stood.

She had expected, perhaps, that certainty would feel like happiness. What came first was steadiness — serious, and a little frightening. And then, after a few steps, something very close to relief.

She folded the letter carefully.

For several minutes she walked without purpose except movement. The path had carried her farther than she intended. A field opened to her right; the formal garden was out of sight; the house had vanished behind trees. She might have laughed at herself then, had she been less near tears. She had come out to be free of Pemberley and had walked deeper into it.

No. Not into Pemberley.

Into truth.

A dangerously solemn phrase, and she objected to it at once. Truth, in her experience, was seldom so grand. It was more often a maid with a salver; a folded sheet; a gentleman not saying what he might have said; a young girl laughing over kittens; an aunt laying one hand briefly upon one’s arm and asking a question too kind to evade.

She turned back.

The return path seemed shorter, as return paths often do when one walks them willingly. The trees loosened. The house reappeared in fragments — a chimney, a pale line of stone, a window flashed by sun.

At the edge of the formal garden she stopped.

Mr. Darcy stood near the turn of the walk.

He was not posed for waiting. That was her first absurd, exact thought. He had no hat in his hand, no attitude of picturesque suffering, no appearance of a gentleman arranged by Providence beneath a tree for the convenience of a lady’s altered feelings. He stood quite still, as a man stands who has walked from one errand and failed to proceed to the next. His attention was on the path by which she had come; when he saw her, he did not advance.

She crossed the last of the gravel toward him. He did not move. He did not look away, but stood with that serious, still attention that asked nothing and concealed nothing. She was aware of the letter in her hand. She was aware of being seen. She walked anyway.

He bowed. His attention, after the bow, went to the middle distance — giving her, she understood, whatever space the letter in her hand required.

"Miss Bennet."

"Mr. Darcy."

The forms had never sounded so thin, nor so necessary.

His eyes dropped once to the letter, then returned to her face. "I had not meant to wait," he said. "And then I did.”

“Yes,” she said. “I know.”

The answer moved him more than accusation might have done. She saw it in the care with which he held himself still.

"I was going to the stables," he said, which was so evidently true and so entirely incomplete that, on any other morning, she might have enjoyed it.

There was her Mr. Darcy — not hers, not yet, and the presumption of the word nearly undid her — but there was the man himself: precise enough to be honest about even the failure of an errand.

She enjoyed it. She would not have thought herself capable of enjoying anything, an hour ago.

A little courage returned with a shape she recognized. "And have the stables offended you, sir, or only failed to be sufficiently absorbing?"

His gaze changed. Only just.

"They are blameless.” He looked at her then, rather than past her. “The fault is mine."

"A generous confession. Stables are seldom in a position to defend themselves."

For one moment, almost nothing stood between them but summer air and the foolishness she had offered him. He received it — not with laughter, but with the expression of a man who had not expected to be given anything at all. Then he looked again at the letter in her hand, and the moment deepened.

"If my letter has distressed you," he said, "I am sorry for it.” He took a step back. “If you would prefer that I leave you—"

"No."

It was too quick. It was also true.

He stopped. His hand, at his side, closed and opened. 

She had meant to proceed with dignity. She had meant to say, with all the elegance proper to a woman who had been given the rare opportunity of correcting a former error, that his letter had affected her, that she valued his confidence, that her sentiments had undergone so material a change. 

No. She would not borrow from old speeches and dress them in new sincerity.

What she wanted to say was this: I have been afraid of my own judgment, and you have given it back to me — and asked nothing for the giving.

What she said was, "You were right to write first.”

His brows drew together. 

"At Hunsford," she said, "the letter came after. This time it came before.”

His composure suffered then. His breath changed, and his eyes, fixed on her with a steadiness that did not presume, made speech more difficult than silence.

"I hoped only that it would not add to your uneasiness."

"It did."

He flinched before he could prevent it.

Elizabeth shook her head. "No — not in that way. It made me uneasy because I had been trying very hard to be sensible, and your letter gave me no assistance at all in continuing so."

"I am sorry."

"You need not be. I have been sensible in several directions this morning and liked none of them."

A sound escaped him, too brief for laughter and too warm for anything else.

She took a step nearer. The gravel shifted under her shoe. It seemed an impertinent sound for so grave a moment.

"You said you asked for no answer now," she said.

"I did."

"And for no kindness I did not freely wish to give."

"Yes."

"Then you must allow me the same freedom in answering as you allowed me in reading."

He bowed his head. "Always."

Always. He had no right to say it, and every right; or perhaps she had given him the right by wishing to hear it.

Elizabeth looked toward the house. Its windows caught the sun; behind one of them, Mrs. Gardiner might be folding a letter, or Kitty might be singing as Georgiana played, or Mrs. Reynolds might be directing linen to rooms that had no notion how many futures they contained. Ordinary life continued with indecent calm.

She turned back to him.

"I cannot thank you for Jane’s happiness," she said. "That belongs to Jane and Mr. Bingley."

His mouth tightened once, briefly. Accepted and deserved. "It does."

"But I can thank you for knowing it."

He closed his eyes. When he opened them, she had the oddest sensation that he had expected refusal in so many forms that gratitude, properly limited, had become the most dangerous kindness.

"You were wrong about her," Elizabeth said.

"I was."

"And I was wrong about you."

He said nothing.

"I have had more practice admitting it," she added, because gravity without a little rescue was intolerable. "Though I cannot claim to have improved in grace."

"Your grace has never been the quality I most admired."

"No? Then I am spared the effort of cultivating it."

This time he did smile. Slight, unguarded, and gone too soon.

"Miss Bennet," he said, and the name, which had been safe upon the page, trembled at the edge of something unsafe aloud. "Elizabeth. If I may."

She did not answer with words. She did not look away.

"My feelings are as I wrote them," he said. "They have not changed, except as correction and time have taught me how little they can deserve by merely existing. I love you. I have loved you badly, proudly, and in ignorance. I hope I may love you better. If you cannot return it, say so, and I will not trouble you again. But if there is any hope—"

"There is more than hope."

The sentence came before prudence had dressed it. Elizabeth heard herself, understood herself, and for once did not repent the speed of either.

"Elizabeth," he said again.

"Yes," she said. "If that was the question. Yes."

He took one step, then stopped, asking still without words.

She held out her hand.

His closed around it. His hand was bare — he had been going to the stables, she remembered, or had said so — and warm from the sun. Her own hand was ungloved because she had carried her gloves all morning as props for a composure she had never achieved. The impropriety was slight. 

The sensation was not.

She was conscious of the pressure of his hand around hers — around her — and of the sun on her back, and of Pemberley behind him, waiting.

For several moments they stood so. 

At last she said, because someone must rescue them from perfect happiness before it became embarrassing, "We should go in."

"We should."

"Mrs. Gardiner will be wondering."

"I doubt Mrs. Gardiner wonders without profit."

"Usefully, I expect."

He offered his arm.

Elizabeth looked at it, then at him. "You are certain this is not gratitude?"

His face grew serious at once. "Are you?"

She considered the question. Then she placed her hand upon his arm.

"Quite certain. Gratitude would have made me more solemn."

Mr. Darcy laughed then — not much, but enough; enough that the sound belonged to the morning and not to memory.

She had left the house alone to read the truth.

They turned toward the house together.
