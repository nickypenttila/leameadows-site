# BSP — Chapter 17: Pemberley Goes Quiet

By nine the next morning, Pemberley had arranged itself around departure.

Darcy had always disliked the particular confusion of guests leaving a house. Arrival could be made handsome: horses checked at the sweep, servants moving with clear purpose, greetings exchanged beneath the shelter of expectation. 

Departure exposed everything arrival had concealed. Trunks loitered in passages. Footmen crossed the hall carrying rugs, small boxes, books forgotten in bedrooms, bottles of smelling salts, a shawl Miss Bingley had declared lost and then discovered to have been under her own hand. A lady’s maid came down twice to ask whether Mrs. Hurst’s cordial had been packed, and Mr. Hurst, emerging from the breakfast room with his coffee unfinished, expressed his belief that if the cordial had not been packed, it deserved to remain in Derbyshire and recover itself.

Mrs. Hurst did not laugh. She was pale enough to make the journey seem reasonable and languid enough to make every stair in the house an accusation. She had spent ten minutes over dry toast, taken nothing else, and afterward declared, with a hand to her temple, that she should never be well until she was again in her own rooms.

Mr. Hurst looked ready to agree with any opinion that conveyed him nearer London, or nearer a sofa that expected nothing of him.

Miss Bingley was all solicitude.

Darcy had seen Caroline Bingley perform many social duties with skill: admiration, regret, civility, the appearance of interest in matters that did not interest her in the least. Solicitude, this morning, had a brightness at the edges. She was very sorry to leave Pemberley. She could not bear that Louisa should suffer. She hoped dear Georgiana would forgive the abruptness. She must believe Mr. Darcy would understand that a sister’s health admitted of no delay.

He understood several things.

He understood that Louisa Hurst was genuinely unwell, though perhaps less endangered than Caroline’s manner suggested. He understood that Mr. Hurst had no wish to pass another day in a house where he might be expected to admire music, walking, or conversation. He understood that Bingley would have remained, had his family allowed it.

He had planned for that, though he had disguised the plan from himself as prudence. Beneath all his proper intentions had lived the assumption that Bingley would remain another month, perhaps two; that there would be a walk, a quiet hour, some decent interval in which a confession might be approached by degrees.

Louisa’s illness had not granted him that. He could not regret Miss Bingley’s removal; he would not leave Georgiana in her orbit a moment longer than necessity required. But Caroline’s retreat drew Bingley with it, and that meant a carriage ten minutes from the door.

Georgiana stood near the foot of the stairs, composed, correct, and too still. She had come down early, dressed in a pale morning gown with a ribbon at the waist and her hair arranged with more care than the hour required. 

“Dear Georgiana,” Miss Bingley said, taking both her hands. “You must not be dull when we are gone. I depend upon your writing to me — long letters, mind. You must tell me all your improvements, and whether you have conquered that Mozart. I shall be most severe if you neglect your practice.”

Georgiana’s fingers moved once within Caroline’s. “I shall write.”

“And do not over-fatigue yourself with company.” Miss Bingley smiled. “You are not yet used to being so much admired.”

The words were light. Another small wound.

Darcy had made one correction the night before. It had been public, necessary, and, apparently, insufficient. To make another now, in the hall, before servants and trunks and a sister already humiliated by too much notice, would only turn Georgiana’s mortification into a scene and give Caroline the advantage of appearing injured.

“Georgiana,” he said, “Mrs. Reynolds wished to consult you about the blue room flowers before they are sent up.”

His sister looked at him. Relief entered her face and was disciplined almost at once. “Yes. Of course.”

She withdrew with a curtsey that did her credit and made Darcy angrier than clumsiness would have done.

Miss Bingley watched her go. “How sweet she is.”

Darcy did not answer.

Caroline’s smile faltered by no more than a thread. “And so young, after all. One forgets it, seeing her so accomplished.”

“One should not,” Darcy said.

It was not severe. It was also not an invitation to continue.

Bingley, who had been speaking to Mr. Hurst near the door, looked from one to the other with the quick discomfort of a man who had smoothed his sisters’ edges more than once. “Darcy, would you walk with me a moment before we set off? Hurst says the horses will not be ready for ten minutes, which in posting language means either five or twenty.”

Mr. Hurst said, “It simply means they are not ready,” and took another swallow of coffee.

Darcy inclined his head. “Certainly.”

They exited through the side door to the west terrace, its pale stone still cool from the night. Beyond the balustrade, the lawn ran bright and wet toward the trees; cut grass and watered gravel scented the air. A groom crossed the sweep at a distance, leading one of Bingley’s horses. From the open window behind them came Caroline’s voice, lowered but distinct enough to carry the shape of complaint.

Bingley stood with his hands behind his back and looked out across the lawn. He had not, Darcy thought, slept well. His good humor remained, but it sat nearer the surface, ready for use and not quite natural.

“I am sorry to go,” Bingley said. “It is absurd. I have been here only a few days, and half the house has been unwell for most of them, but I am sorry all the same.”

“You must attend your sister.”

“Yes. Louisa will be more comfortable in town. Hurst will be more comfortable when Louisa is comfortable.” He smiled, then lost the smile. “Caroline is determined that her usefulness shall be observed.”

Darcy let that pass.

Bingley turned a pebble under the toe of his boot. “I had hoped to see the Gardiners again before leaving. And Miss Elizabeth.”

Darcy’s hand tightened on the balustrade.

“And Miss Bennet,” Bingley added, too quickly. “That is — to hear of Miss Bennet. Her sister said she was well.”

“Yes.”

A cart sounded somewhere beyond the stable-yard. A servant laughed. Darcy took a deep breath.

He had intended to speak later. He had intended some decent order: a word first, perhaps; then a fuller account; then, if Bingley wished it, every mortifying particular Darcy could honorably give.

There was no time now.

He could write. Wait until Bingley was settled in London, when a friend’s confession might be delivered at a distance.

But truth had already suffered too much from his arrangements.

“Bingley,” he said. “There is something I must tell you.”

Bingley looked at him at once. “Is anything wrong?”

“Yes.”

The word came more easily than the explanation.

Darcy moved a little farther along the terrace, beyond the open window’s easy reach. There was no merit in looking at the view. He did it anyway.

“When you left Netherfield last November, I believed Miss Bennet did not return your regard.”

Bingley’s hands, which had been clasped behind him, shifted once. For a moment he looked only puzzled, as if Darcy had named a subject from another room entirely.

Darcy had known Bingley in spirits, in embarrassment, in disappointment, in all the soft weather of his temper. He had not known him quite so still.

“I told you so,” he continued. “More than once. I used my influence with you — and with your sisters — to prevent your returning to Hertfordshire. I thought I was acting for your benefit.”

The puzzlement left Bingley’s face. Pain started to spread across his countenance.

Darcy turned. His friend had put one hand on the balustrade. Not heavily; only enough that the knuckles showed pale for an instant before he loosened them.

“I was wrong,” Darcy said.

Bingley drew his hand from the stone, then set it back again. “Wrong in what part?”

“In the judgment. In the interference. In the confidence with which I mistook Miss Bennet’s manners.”

The stillness altered. Bingley looked away, across the lawn. “You said you thought she did not care for me.”

“I did.”

“And do you no longer think so?”

“No.”

Bingley turned back to him. He searched Darcy’s face with an expression almost comical in its astonishment, had there been any comedy in the occasion. “But you are an excellent judge of everyone.”

“No.”

The answer was so immediate that Bingley blinked.

Darcy pushed himself to continue. “I have often been a confident judge. Not the same thing.”

Bingley looked down. The toe of his boot moved against the pebble again, then stopped. “What has altered your opinion?”

Darcy did not answer quickly enough.

Bingley looked up. “Miss Elizabeth?”

Darcy let out a breath. “Miss Elizabeth Bennet gave me reason to understand that I had been mistaken.”

That was too little. It was also all he could say. Hunsford, the proposal, the letter, Elizabeth’s accusations — none of it belonged on the terrace at Pemberley with Bingley pale in the morning light and the carriages waiting.

Bingley’s face changed at Elizabeth’s name, but not toward suspicion. Hope, when it came too fast, could look almost like pain.

“Miss Elizabeth would know,” he said.

“Yes.”

“And Miss Bennet — Jane — may have cared.”

Darcy heard the name and accepted the rebuke of it. Bingley had earned the right to say what Darcy had avoided saying for all these months.

“I now believe she did.”

Bingley turned away. His arms came across his chest, and he looked out again over the dew-bright lawn. For once, Darcy did not attempt to read him. He had read Jane Bennet too neatly, too coldly, and had congratulated himself on penetration. He would not now name every movement in his friend’s face and call the naming understanding.

“I thought myself a fool,” Bingley said at last. His voice low, but steady. “At first, when we came away, I thought — well, no matter what I thought. Caroline was so certain. Louisa too. You, as well. I convinced myself I had imagined more than existed, and how could I not? You are all so much cleverer than I.”

“Bingley—”

“No, it is true in many particulars.” He gave a small, unhappy laugh. “I do not mean I am an idiot. I hope not, at least. But you see further than I do. You always have. I thought, if Darcy says there was nothing, then I have been making a romance of ordinary kindness. A man may survive being made a fool of. He ought not, perhaps, arrange his whole life around never being made one again.”

Darcy had no answer worthy of him.

Bingley uncrossed his arms and leaned both hands on the balustrade. “And now you tell me there may have been something.”

“Yes.”

“After all this time.”

“Yes.”

“Good God, Darcy.”

It was not anger, or not only anger. There was injury in it, and bewilderment, and a dawning impatience.

“I had no right,” Darcy said. “I cannot repair the months you have lost. I cannot know what Miss Bennet now feels. I can only tell you that my former judgment should no longer govern yours. If you still wish to know her mind, you must act upon your own judgment.”

Bingley gazed steadily at him then, and the injury sharpened. “My own. Yes. I suppose I ought to have done that before.”

Darcy inclined his head once. It was all he trusted himself to do.

For half a minute they stood with the lawn before them and the open house to their side. Darcy heard the faint sound of wheels on gravel as another carriage was brought round.

Then Bingley said, “Did you mean to tell me before today?”

Darcy’s throat worked against the answer. “Yes.”

“Had you?”

“No,” Darcy said. “Not today. I meant to wait until it could be done better.”

Bingley gaped at him, astonishment returning in spite of everything. “Better?”

“More privately. More completely. Less—”

“Less at the very moment my carriage is being brought round?”

“Yes.”

Bingley considered him for one grave second.

Darcy waited for the anger he deserved. He had imagined Bingley injured; he had imagined him wounded into silence; he had not, until that instant, imagined him raising his voice. The possibility entered him ice-cold and well deserved.

Bingley laughed.

The laugh had no gaiety in it at first. Then, because he was Bingley, some gaiety entered despite everything. “You are the most extraordinary man. You have ruined my peace since last winter, and now you dislike the timing.”

Darcy found himself almost smiling. It hurt.

“I dislike many things about my conduct.”

“That is just as well, for I dislike some of them too.”

“You have every right.”

“I know.” Bingley straightened. “That is new. I generally leave rights to other people; they manage them with more confidence.”

The carriage rolled into view beyond the corner of the house. A footman came down the steps with a rug over one arm.

Bingley watched it, then looked past it, as if the road visible from the sweep might already be the Hertfordshire road. “I must take Louisa to town.”

“Yes.”

“And Caroline.”

“Yes.”

“And Hurst, unless we abandon him by accident at the first inn, which would be unkind to the inn.”

Despite himself, Darcy said, “Very.”

Bingley’s mouth curved and fell again. “But I need not remain in town.”

“No.”

“I may go to Netherfield.”

“If you choose.”

“If *I* choose,” Bingley repeated. The phrase seemed to interest him, as a door might interest a man who has always supposed the wall solid.

Darcy said nothing.

Bingley turned to him. “Does she know? Miss Elizabeth. Does she know you have told me?”

“No.”

“Will you tell her?”

“I do not know.”

Bingley studied him then with more perception than Darcy had ever allowed him credit for, though that too was an old injustice. Bingley had never been stupid. He had only been willing to believe those he loved.

“At some point,” Bingley said, “you may find that a lady likes to be trusted with what so nearly concerns her.”

Darcy looked at him.

Bingley colored. “That sounded wiser than I intended.”

“I believe it was.”

“Then I retract it. I am not fit for wisdom this morning.” He took one breath, then another. “I am angry with you, I think.”

“Yes.”

“And grateful.”

“You should not be.”

“I daresay I shall decide that for myself too.”

There was nothing to say to that.

Bingley held out his hand.

For a moment Darcy only looked at it. Then he took it. Bingley’s grip was warm, brief, and firmer than usual.

“I wish you had been wrong less cleverly,” Bingley said.

“As do I.”

“And I wish I had loved her less obediently.”

Darcy had to look away.

Caroline’s voice reached them from the steps. “Charles! Louisa is in the carriage.”

Bingley released his hand. “Coming.”

They returned together to the portico, where the whole business of leave-taking had arranged itself into public order. Georgiana stood at Darcy’s side. Miss Bingley expressed regret enough for three households; Mrs. Hurst was already settled with cushions; Mr. Hurst entered the carriage with an expression of martyrdom too indolent to be theatrical. Bingley stood last on the step.

He bent over Georgiana’s hand with his old kindness. “You must write to me about the German fellow,” he said. “Mozart. I shall rely upon you to make me seem informed in conversation.”

Georgiana smiled. “I do not think that is possible by letter.”

“Then I am lost. But write anyway.”

She laughed, a little. Darcy was grateful for it.

Soon, the carriages went down the sweep, wheels loud on gravel, then softer past the bend, then gone.

For some time after, Pemberley was full of the noises that follow departure: doors closing, servants carrying away the last signs of occupation, Mrs. Reynolds giving instructions in the hall. The house resumed itself room by room. Cushions were restored. Fires were mended. Curtains drawn back. A maid crossed the music room with the brass candle shields from the pianoforte, and Darcy, passing the door, stopped.

The room looked harmless by daylight.

The mahogany instrument stood in its proper place. The sheets had been stacked. No circle of chairs compelled anyone to sit and admire. The harp threw a narrow line of gold against the wall. Last night’s unhappiness had left no mark upon the furniture.

Georgiana was not in the room.

He found her in the small morning room, seated at the table with a note open before her. She looked up when he entered and folded the paper too quickly.

“From Mrs. Gardiner?” he asked.

“Yes.” She smoothed the crease. “They cannot come today. Mr. Gardiner has letters to answer, and Mrs. Gardiner thinks Miss Bennet a little fatigued after last evening. They send their compliments and thanks.”

Miss Bennet, fatigued. Perhaps she was. Perhaps the fatigue was his doing, or Caroline’s, or Pemberley’s, or all of them combined. The thought lodged where no useful action could reach it.

Georgiana looked down at the folded note. “She says they may be obliged to return to London sooner than they expected.”

Georgiana kept her attention on the paper. “Not certainly. Only perhaps. Mr. Gardiner has received a great many letters from London.”

The morning had already carried the Bingleys away. Now it threatened the Gardiners too.

Darcy had known, of course, that the Gardiners were travelers. Their presence in Derbyshire had never belonged to him, nor even to Pemberley. They had an inn, a carriage, children in Hertfordshire, business in London, and every claim upon their time that propriety could respect. He had known it; but knowledge, while Miss Bennet stood beside Georgiana at the instrument, had been a fact put away for later.

Later had arrived before noon.

Georgiana folded the note again. “If they are to leave, they will have much to arrange.” Meaning no time for tea, or dinner, or music, or grace.

She said it reasonably. That was the worst of it. She had already begun to make herself equal to the disappointment.

The morning room had always been sufficient for letters, breakfast, estate business, and the small civilities of a quiet house. It had never before been asked to hold a girl’s disappointment and do anything useful with it.

“Did you wish to ask them again?” he said.

Georgiana’s eyes lifted at once, then lowered. “I do not wish to trouble them.”

“That is not what I asked.”

Her fingers slid along the edge of the note. “I did like having them here.”

Darcy gazed past his sister, toward the window.

Georgiana’s voice grew softer. “I thought perhaps — only if Mrs. Gardiner wished it — they might come to tea another day. Or we might call. But if they are leaving tomorrow, perhaps there is no another day.”

There it was: the careful retreat before refusal, the apology built into desire. He had taught her that, not by instruction but by habit. Caution had been the language of their household for years. He had spoken it fluently, and Georgiana had learned to make even pleasure conditional.

“We have not asked a great deal,” he said.

She looked at him then.

“We shall call at Lambton this afternoon. See if Mrs. Gardiner is at home to us.”

Her hand moved across the table and came to rest, lightly, on his.

“Yes,” she said.

The touch, brief and unlooked-for, struck him more sharply than any thanks.

Bingley was not the only person to whom he had given too much guidance and too little trust.

With Bingley he had called it friendship. With Georgiana, protection. The habit was the same. If it was to alter, it would not do so by resolution alone.

It would begin with Lambton.
