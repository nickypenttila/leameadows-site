# BSP — Chapter 23: The Carriage from Longbourn

By noon Pemberley had reached that condition of readiness which, in a smaller house, would have passed for agitation and, in this one, expressed itself chiefly through the absence of any visible disturbance.

Elizabeth, who had been learning Pemberley’s habits by degrees, found the effect more oppressive than if half the servants had been running through the passages with linens under their arms. At Longbourn, an expected arrival announced itself everywhere: in her mother’s voice on the stairs, in Hill’s compressed lips, in the wrong cloth laid and removed and laid again, in some younger sister sent on three errands and completing none of them. Here, a chamber was aired, hot water ordered, a fire laid and not lit, a tray adjusted by half an inch, and the house went on as if preparation were a natural property of polished wood and well-trained feet.

Kitty was coming to Pemberley.

The words had looked improbable in a letter. They had become no more manageable in expectation.

In the music room, Mrs. Gardiner sat near the garden-side window with her work in her lap, making a tolerable show of attending to it. Georgiana occupied the chair nearest her, a square of embroidery in her lap that had not advanced by a single stitch since Elizabeth entered the room. Mr. Darcy stood near the mantel with the air of a man who had been placed there for composure and intended to remain until composure had served its sentence.

He had already said, twice, that the carriage could not be expected before one. He had asked Mrs. Reynolds whether the blue room had been aired, though Elizabeth was certain Mrs. Reynolds had aired it already yesterday. He had inquired whether Miss Catherine would prefer tea upstairs on arrival, and had accepted Mrs. Gardiner’s assurance that no young lady, after so long a journey, should be required to know what she preferred until she had washed her face.

“The first kindness to a traveler,” Mrs. Gardiner had said, “is to ask nothing of her for twenty minutes.”

Mr. Darcy had bowed as if she had settled a point of estate law.

Elizabeth had been tempted to laugh. But she could not. She had written the letter that was bringing Kitty here; she had argued, persuaded, promised. If the day went wrong, it would not be Pemberley that had failed, or Georgiana, or even Kitty. It would be Elizabeth, who had asked too much of all of them.

Georgiana's hands had left her needle and lay folded in her lap with that unnatural stillness. A month ago Elizabeth might have taken that stillness for composure. Pemberley had taught her better. Georgiana could arrange herself beautifully; she could sit upright, answer correctly, and keep every finger in its proper place; but hope without occupation looked, Elizabeth thought, very like dread.

“You need not look so solemn, my dear,” Mrs. Gardiner said to Georgiana, without lifting her eyes from her work. “Catherine will be more frightened of you than you can possibly be of her.”

Georgiana lifted her chin — a small, brief motion so like her brother that Elizabeth nearly did laugh. "I am not frightened,” she said.

“No. Of course not. I should have said: our Kitty has had two days in a carriage to imagine Pemberley, and no one imagines sensibly after the first forty miles.”

Elizabeth kept her expression in place by main force. Her aunt was right. Kitty had had two days to imagine Pemberley, and imagination, left to itself, could build something terrifying. She had written, in her round uncertain hand, that she hoped she should not be too noisy for Pemberley.

Too noisy for Pemberley.

Elizabeth had not known whether to laugh or weep over that line. Since then, the words had followed her through two mornings and one very unsatisfactory night.

The door opened. 

It wasn’t the carriage. A servant entered and crossed at once to Mr. Darcy, who turned before the man had spoken, as if he had been expecting not this servant, exactly, but this sort of interruption.

"From Robinson, sir," the man said, low, and held out a folded slip.

Mr. Darcy took it. His eyes moved over it; the paper did not require much reading. "How long ago?"

"Ten minutes, sir."

"The west stall?"

"Yes, sir."

"Tell him I shall come down if there is any change. And send Tom to the lower drive. He is to watch for the carriage and bring word before it reaches the sweep."

"Yes, sir."

The servant withdrew.

Mrs. Gardiner looked up then. “Nothing amiss, I hope?”

“No, indeed. One of the mares is near her time.” Mr. Darcy folded the note with more precision than so small a paper deserved. “My stablemaster is cautious.”

“And you are more so,” said Mrs. Gardiner.

He inclined his head, accepting the charge without contest. “Where livestock is concerned, caution seldom offends me.”

It was said to the room; nevertheless Elizabeth heard in it something of the man she had seen in his office. His responsibility had a shape: messages carried to him softly; decisions made at once; comfort ordered for others; an eye kept upon the drive.

Mr. Darcy slipped the note into his coat pocket and seemed, for the first time that morning, not master of the whole of his countenance. He did not pace. He did not fidget. He only looked once toward the window, though the stables were not visible from it, and then back toward his sister.

“If you wish to play, Georgiana, pray do not wait on our account.”

Georgiana started. “I had not meant—”

“I know.”

Something in the brevity of it released her. She rose, crossed to the instrument, and opened it with the care of a person lifting a lid from water that might reflect too much. She started with a few scales and broken chords, and then began playing a piece Elizabeth had heard before but could not name. Georgiana stopped after six bars and returned to the beginning. Her hands settled as the music took them.

Mr. Darcy listened for half a minute; then, as if decision were a relief in itself, he stepped away from the mantel.

“Mrs. Gardiner—if you will excuse me. I shall go to the west stable.”

Mrs. Gardiner looked up with a smile and a little inclination of the head.

Elizabeth remained only a few minutes longer, till she could not but feel that every moment added to her awkwardness. Mrs. Gardiner’s needle moved steadily. Georgiana repeated the troublesome passage, not badly, but with the fixed attention of one who had given her nerves a task and expected obedience from them.

Elizabeth rose. “I shall take a turn,” she said to the room.

Mrs. Gardiner lifted her gaze to Elizabeth and dropped it again, smiling to herself over her work. Georgiana, at the pianoforte, took no notice.

Elizabeth went out through the glass door to the veranda.

The air held the dry warmth of a summer day not yet resolved into heat. Beyond the terrace, the lawn fell away toward trees, their leaves dappled and moving in a light wind. As she paced, the music inside faded, and the sounds without came forward. At the far edge of the veranda she could see the edge of the drive, a pale curve appearing between the limes, vanishing behind a thicker stand of beeches, then emerging lower down where the land opened.

Below, near the turn where the trees thickened, a boy in short trousers came running into view, stopped, shaded his eyes, then turned toward the house. He saw Elizabeth on the veranda and altered course at once.

“Miss Bennet,” he called, checking himself into something like decorum when he reached the steps. “Beg pardon, miss. The carriage is on the lower drive.”

Elizabeth’s hand, which had been resting on the stone balustrade, closed once. Something tightened below her ribs, brief and unreasonable, as if the news had pressed the air from a room that had no walls.

“Thank you.”

The boy bobbed and ran on toward the side entrance, where messages entered Pemberley as cleanly as letters on a tray.

Elizabeth did not go back to the music room.

She ran down the steps.

The carriage came on through the trees: a dark gloss between the limes, a flash of wheel, the lifted heads of horses that had come a long way and knew they were nearly done. Then it cleared the beeches and was fully before her — Pemberley's own carriage returning with Longbourn inside it.

Elizabeth had thought she was prepared for the sight. She was not.

The horses held a controlled trot. A postilion sat straight. Road dust lay along the lower panels and had gathered in the creases and even touched the Darcy crest on the door. The carriage was elegant, well-sprung, and far too composed for the cargo it carried. It stopped before the steps with a final crunch of gravel that sounded, to Elizabeth's foolish ear, like the end of one arrangement and the beginning of another.

She was at the step before the footman reached the door.

He opened it. Mr. Bennet descended first.

He had traveled better than Elizabeth expected and worse than he would have admitted. His coat was brushed, his neckcloth tolerable, his hat at the correct angle. But there was road dust on one cuff, and his face, when he turned toward the house, had the faintly narrowed look of a man who had been jostled for too many miles and was determined to make amusement pay for the inconvenience.

“Well, Lizzy,” he said, looking up at Pemberley, “I begin to perceive that Derbyshire has arguments.”

“Only now, Papa?”

“I reserved judgment. It is the privilege of age to arrive late at conclusions and call it prudence.”

He kissed her cheek. The kiss was brief, dry, and familiar enough to undo her more than tenderness would have done. 

Then Kitty appeared at the carriage door.

For a full second she did not move.

She had both hands on the frame, one booted foot extended toward the step, her eyes fixed upon the house with a kind of helpless attention. A ribbon had worked loose from her bonnet. Her traveling gown, though neat, bore every mile between Hertfordshire and Derbyshire. She looked like a girl who had been told she was wanted and had spent the whole road north preparing to be found wanting.

Elizabeth went to her, and reached up and caught her sleeve.

“Come down, Kitty. The gravel will not examine you.”

Kitty’s gaze snapped to her. Relief crossed her face so plainly that Elizabeth felt the force of it in her own throat.

“Oh, Lizzy.”

It was not a greeting. It was an appeal.

On the other side of the step, the coachman held out his hand. Kitty took it and came down with more haste than grace, then stood beside her on the sweep, clutching a small reticule as if it contained instructions for survival.

At the back of the carriage, footmen began to remove the luggage. A groom moved to the horses’ heads. Somewhere behind them, the house door opened and steps sounded in the hall, but no one came out in a rush

Kitty’s gaze moved from the open doorway to the windows, then back to Elizabeth. “Is Mr. Darcy not at home?”

“He is, and Miss Darcy, as well.”

Kitty looked more frightened, not less. “Have we come at a bad time? I thought — I do not mean that anyone should have — only, I thought perhaps—”

“That the whole house would come out upon the gravel to receive you?” Mr. Bennet supplied, removing his gloves finger by finger. “In great houses, Kitty, affection is permitted to wait upon hot water.”

Kitty stared at him.

Elizabeth laid a hand very lightly on her sister’s sleeve. “They are being considerate. No one expects you to be received formally with the dust of the road still on your face. Here’s Mrs. Reynolds, who will show you up; Aunt Gardiner is waiting; and when you are comfortable, Miss Darcy will be very glad to see you.”

Kitty drew one breath, then another. “So they are not — that is, she is not disappointed?”

“She has not had time to be disappointed,” said Mr. Bennet. “You have not yet spoken.”

“Papa,” Kitty said—her usual intonation of affront, but fainter.

“An advantage, my dear. Preserve it until you have washed.”

Kitty almost smiled. Not quite, but at least she was less near tears than she had been a moment before.

At that instant, Mrs. Reynolds arrived, composed, capable, as unperturbed as if she greeted dusty Bennets every Tuesday.

“Mr. Bennet. Miss Catherine. Welcome to Pemberley.” Her curtsey was perfect. “Your rooms are quite ready. Mrs. Gardiner is upstairs and asked to be told the moment you arrived.”

“Thank you,” Kitty said quickly; then, as if the words had escaped too soon and required improvement, “I am very much obliged — I mean, we are — Papa, are we?”

“We are in a state of obligation so extensive I hardly know where to begin,” said Mr. Bennet. “Lead on, Mrs. Reynolds. My daughter is anxious to become presentable before anyone of consequence discovers her.”

“Papa,” Kitty whispered, mortified.

But Mrs. Reynolds’s mouth had softened at the corner. “Miss Catherine has had a long journey, sir. Pemberley does not stand on ceremony with road dust.”

This was so exactly the right sentence that Elizabeth could have embraced her, had Mrs. Reynolds been an embraceable woman, which she very much was not.

They crossed the threshold.

The hall had altered since Elizabeth first entered it and not altered at all. Beeswax, cool stone, flowers on the side table, the round oak table at the center. Kitty’s step faltered. Elizabeth slowed with her.

“There — we are in,” she murmured.

Kitty looked at the ceiling. “A hall like a church.”

“But it cannot hear you.”

“That you know of.”

The answer was so quick, and so nearly Kitty herself, that Elizabeth turned to look at her. Kitty had spoken without thinking. Elizabeth could not help smiling. Kitty saw it and looked alarmed at her own success.

Before Elizabeth could answer, Mr. Darcy appeared from the passage leading toward the back of the house.

He came in without haste, but Elizabeth saw at once that he had not been waiting idly. There was a trace of the outdoors about him — a faint darkening of dust at the edge of one boot and the look of a man whose attention had been recalled from one duty and set upon another before the first had entirely released him.

“Mr. Bennet,” he said, bowing. “Miss Catherine. I am very glad to see you safely arrived.”

Mr. Bennet returned the bow with all necessary gravity. “You are very good, sir. Your carriage has conveyed us in such comfort that I begin to fear I shall be unfit for common roads.”

“I hope not. I should be sorry to have done Hertfordshire an injury.”

Mr. Bennet’s brows lifted by the smallest measure. The first probe had been answered.

Mr. Darcy turned to Kitty. “Mrs. Reynolds will show you to your room. My sister will be happy to receive you when you are rested from the journey.”

Kitty, who had curtseyed with tolerable composure, lifted her eyes to him and dropped them again. "Thank you, sir. I am — very grateful for the invitation.”

“The kindness is yours,” he said.

Not mere civility, but a sentence placed where she could stand upon it.

Something in Kitty steadied. A small settling, like a breath completed.

Mr. Darcy stepped back, giving Mrs. Reynolds the way. He did not look at Elizabeth until Mr. Bennet and Kitty had begun up the stairs. When he did, the look was brief enough to be nothing and direct enough to be impossible to mistake.

Elizabeth felt it like a hand laid, without warning, upon the very thought she had been striving to keep silent. To be seen so plainly was not comfort; it was exposure.

She had meant only to bring Kitty into safety, and to bring Georgiana some comfort. She had not meant to bring her there—to Pemberley, to him, under his eye—as if Longbourn were a thing to be weighed and found wanting.

Her pride rose first; then, more unwillingly, a sharper sensation behind it. What if, in fact, he looked not to judge, but to understand?

Then he turned, because another servant had appeared at the far end of the hall, and Pemberley, having admitted Longbourn, resumed all its other claims upon him.

Three-quarters of an hour later, Kitty was ready to come down, her gown changed, her hair re-pinned, and her face scrubbed into greater anxiety.

Elizabeth could not blame her. Washing removed dust, not expectation.

The smaller drawing room on the garden side was familiar by now: the pale green silk, the cream curtains drawn back from the high windows, the polished table set near the chairs by the light. The first time, she had entered it wondering what countenance a woman ought to wear when visiting the house of a man whose proposal she had refused. Today, she entered beside a sister who was likely wondering, with equal urgency, what countenance a girl ought to wear when invited to be useful in a house that seemed to require nothing.

The tea things were already laid.

Same as before: the thin porcelain, the bread and butter, the cakes, the silver tongs, the shell-shaped dish with preserved fruit, all the careful plenty of Pemberley’s version of simplicity. But today, place for another cup had been set where Kitty would not have to reach across anyone. The sofa nearest the windows had been drawn a little forward, as if to make room for two sisters to sit together without appearing to have arranged themselves for mutual defense.

Mr. Darcy stood behind his sister and a little to the side, exactly as he had stood on that first afternoon, near enough to steady, far enough to let her be seen. But this time Elizabeth understood the position. She had perceived something of it then; today, with Kitty trembling beside her, she saw how much labor such stillness concealed.

Mr. Darcy took a step forward. “Georgiana—allow me to present Mr. Bennet and Miss Catherine Bennet.”

Georgiana came forward, made a slight curtsey, and said, “Mr. Bennet.” Then she turned to Kitty. “Miss Catherine,” she said, and stopped.

Kitty curtseyed. “Miss Darcy.”

They looked at one another with all the solemnity of two young ladies who had each been told the other was very much to be liked and found the instruction too important for ease. Elizabeth remained at Kitty’s side, near enough that her sister’s skirt brushed hers.

“I am very glad you are come,” said Miss Darcy.

“I am very much obliged to you for inviting me.”

Georgiana’s hands clasped once, then released. “I hope your journey was not too fatiguing.”

“No — yes — I mean, it was long, but the carriage was very comfortable, and Papa says I must never complain of a road after being carried over one in such a manner, because it would show ingratitude and poor taste both.”

Mr. Bennet, who had allowed himself to be directed to the chair near Mr. Darcy with the air of a man accepting the least uncomfortable position in an experiment, said, “I stand by the principle.”

Georgiana smiled. It was small, but it was real enough to reach Kitty, who looked quickly at Elizabeth as if to ask whether anything had gone wrong.

As they were seated, Elizabeth took the place beside Kitty on the sofa, near enough for support and not so near as to appear to offer it. Miss Darcy returned to the tea table with the gravity of one undertaking a serious public office, and Mrs. Gardiner placed herself near enough to be useful without seeming necessary.

Elizabeth shifted nearer Kitty on the sofa. “You are doing very well,” she said under the cover of Mrs. Gardiner asking after Mr. Bennet’s journey.

“I have only spoken nonsense.”

“No more than is customary in drawing rooms.”

Kitty's breath hitched, not quite a laugh.

Mr. Darcy did not take his own cup at once; instead, he turned to Mr. Bennet with the plate of cakes and a gravity which made pastry appear a matter of hospitality rather than appetite.

“I understand you are fond of reading, sir. The library is at your disposal while you remain. Though I should warn you, the catalogue has lately fallen into a state my steward calls temporary and Mrs. Reynolds calls provoking.”

Mr. Bennet regarded him over the rim of his cup.

“A gentleman who offers another gentleman a library before asking his opinion of the weather has made a promising beginning.”

Mr. Darcy’s answer came after precisely the right interval. “Then I am fortunate to have begun correctly.”

“Do not be too confident. The catalogue may yet condemn you.”

“I shall submit to judgment.”

“An admirable resolution. Few men do, when books are involved.”

Elizabeth took her cup because Miss Darcy offered it; otherwise she might have forgotten that tea existed.

He had prepared.

He had thought of her father before her father entered the room. He had considered what might make him comfortable, what might turn his wit toward pleasure rather than injury, what might occupy him without inviting him to make sport of anyone weaker. He had prepared for Mr. Bennet as he had prepared occupation for Georgiana, sport for Mr. Gardiner, and likely even shade for the lower walk.

The thought ought to have disturbed her. Instead, finding no argument ready to her hand, Elizabeth turned to Kitty and chose the smaller duty before her.

Kitty had taken one of the smaller cakes and was holding it in both hands without eating. Georgiana, seated now with her own cake, seemed to be searching among possible subjects and finding each too delicate to introduce.

Elizabeth gave Kitty the slightest nudge with her knee. “The kittens,” she murmured.

Kitty’s eyes widened — not in alarm this time, but recollection. She turned back to Georgiana with such earnestness that the cake in her hand was forgotten entirely.

“I have thought of the kittens nearly the whole way north,” she said. “I hope that is not very foolish.”

Georgiana’s face altered. The careful hostess vanished for half a second, and the girl who had written the invitation looked out.

“No,” she said. “I have been waiting to show them to you. Robinson says they may be handled soon.”

Kitty’s shoulders lowered by an inch. “Are they very small?”

“Four are, Robinson says. One is larger and thinks himself above the others.”

“That is often the way in families,” said Mr. Bennet.

“Papa,” said Kitty, before remembering where she was.

Mr. Bennet looked entirely unrepentant. Georgiana’s mouth curved — not quite a laugh, more the beginning of one — and her eyes went to her brother. His face softened by the smallest degree. The laugh escaped her after all, quickly checked, but enough that Mrs. Gardiner looked down into her tea to conceal a smile.

Mr. Bennet raised his cup. Over its rim, his eyes found Kitty — and stayed, with something quieter than wit. The steady look of a father who had noticed his child doing well.

Kitty’s shoulders settled. The cake, forgotten until now, received its first small bite.

A servant appeared in the doorway.

Mr. Darcy turned at once.

The man did not enter fully. “Beg pardon, sir. Robinson asks whether you would come to the west stable when convenient.”

“Is anything amiss?”

“No, sir. Only what’s expected. He says it may be soon.”

Mr. Darcy set down his untouched cup. “One of the mares is near her time,” he said to Mr. Bennet. “Pray excuse me.”

He bowed and crossed toward the door.

Kitty had leaned closer to Elizabeth, her cake plate empty, her face turned toward Miss Darcy as the two of them considered the relative dignity of kittens. Elizabeth, glancing down, saw her sister’s fingers loosening at last around the plate.

Then she looked up.

Mr. Darcy had paused at the door. He had turned back.

His gaze rested for a moment on Kitty, then on Elizabeth beside her. For one startling instant, Elizabeth understood them as he might: the woman he had once asked to enter his life, and the sister attached to every objection he had once laid before her, both admitted now beneath his roof.

There was no triumph in his face. No surprise. No trace of the judgment she had feared.

Elizabeth was the first to lower her eyes.
