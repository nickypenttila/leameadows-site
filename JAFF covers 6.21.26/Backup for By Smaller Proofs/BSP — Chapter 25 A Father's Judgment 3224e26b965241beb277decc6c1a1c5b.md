# BSP — Chapter 25: A Father's Judgment

✅ Chapter complete

---

By the time the ladies withdrew after dinner, Pemberley had begun to receive Kitty Bennet without visible alarm.

This was either a compliment or a capitulation.

For her second dinner in the great house, Elizabeth’s sister had come down with her ribbons ironed, her hair arranged with more patience than usual, and an expression of determined propriety. She had taken her place beside Georgiana without knocking the chair, the table, or her own courage out of order. She had answered Mr. Darcy when he spoke to her.

During the meal, the conversation had turned, by Mrs. Gardiner's gentle management, to the country around Lambton; and Georgiana, who had been quiet for almost three minutes, asked whether Longbourn had any hills of consequence. Kitty opened her mouth, shut it, considered, and said that Hertfordshire had some slopes which Mary called improving and Lydia called inconvenient. Georgiana's smile appeared at once. Kitty saw it and grew, not louder, but steadier.

Mr. Bennet, at Mr. Darcy’s right hand, observed this through the mild, half-absent air by which he generally concealed attention. He had spoken with Kitty before dinner. Kitty had found Elizabeth while she was fastening her necklace.

"Papa asked whether I liked it here. Not liked it, exactly. Whether I was comfortable. And whether Miss Darcy — Georgiana — was kind. And whether I understood that I was not to be troublesome. Only he did not say troublesome. He said — I forget what he said, but I knew what he meant." Kitty's fingers had gone to the ribbon in her hair. "And then he asked whether I wished to stay."

"And what did you say?"

"I said I did. If I might. If you stayed too. Or Aunt Gardiner. Or Mrs. Annesley, but she’s always here, I suppose. I did not mean I should be wild." She had colored. "I told him Georgiana wished me to see whether the gray kitten is still a tyrant tomorrow."

"And did Papa consider that a sufficient reason?"

Kitty's chin had come up. "It is a very good reason. Papa said it was the first political question in which I had shown any steadiness."

Elizabeth had laughed then, but the answer had remained with her through dinner, settling somewhere beneath the brighter noise of knives, glass, and civil conversation.

Her father had asked Kitty what she wanted.

A daughter might reasonably be asked whether she wished to remain in a house not her own. Yet Elizabeth knew too well how often decisions at Longbourn were made around Kitty: to silence Lydia, to soothe Mrs. Bennet, to preserve peace for an hour, to prevent some greater folly. Kitty had been carried by stronger wills, louder wishes, nearer tempers. Tonight, she had been asked.

And having been asked, she sat beside Miss Darcy with the dignity of a person who knew her answer mattered.

When the ladies rose to take their leave, Mr. Darcy held the drawing-room door for them as they passed. Elizabeth, last but one, found herself obliged to go near him. He was all due attention: the hand upon the door, the slight inclination, the controlled serenity of a host. But his eyes, when they met hers, carried something stronger than courtesy. The stable had not been forgotten. Nor the moment beside her, not quite near enough to be improper, not far enough to be safe.

She passed him with a step that was nearly steady.

In the drawing room, Kitty and Georgiana took up a place near the table, where a work-box had been set out and abandoned earlier. Kitty picked up a length of ribbon, then put it down again, perhaps remembering the tyrant kitten's claims upon property. Georgiana bent over some piece of sewing with great seriousness and immediately threaded the wrong size of needle. Kitty glanced once toward Mrs. Annesley.

Mrs. Annesley, at the far end of the table, smiled and returned to her own work.

No permission was spoken. None was needed. Kitty leaned closer and murmured something Elizabeth could not hear. Georgiana listened as if the management of a needle had become the most interesting subject in Derbyshire.

Mrs. Gardiner, seated beside Elizabeth, said very softly, "Your sister seems happier tonight."

"Yes."

"And less careful about it."

Elizabeth looked down at her hands. She had found no occupation for them and did not quite know what to do with them. "My father spoke with her before dinner."

"I know."

"Did he tell you?"

"Kitty did, in effect. She has the air of one who has passed an examination and cannot be persuaded not to mention the school."

Across the room, Kitty was choosing thread from the work-box, an embroidery frame newly set up beside her.

The catch beneath Elizabeth’s ribs returned — the same she had felt in the stable. "I feared she could not be steady."

Mrs. Gardiner's reply was gentle. "She has not had much occasion to try."

There was nothing to answer to that.

The fire had settled into its coals, and the candles nearest the work-table threw the girls' shadows long across the carpet. Georgiana paused over a stitch. Kitty glanced at her work.

"It is a common rebellion," Kitty said. "Thread never wishes to go where it is told."

Georgiana turned the needle between her fingers. "Does anything?"

Kitty considered this. "Dogs. Sometimes."

"Not kittens."

"Never kittens."

The exchange steadied them both.

It did not steady Elizabeth. She reached for the nearest book upon the table and discovered it was a volume of sermons. The first sentence informed her that the discipline of the mind began with command of wandering thoughts. She snapped the book shut.

Mrs. Gardiner did not laugh. That was a kindness.

The gentlemen were still at table. There were many subjects upon which two gentlemen might speak over port: estates, roads, the weather, Mr. Gardiner's absence, the merits of Derbyshire stone, the general inconvenience of daughters. Her father could spend ten minutes on a label if the vintage displeased him. Mr. Darcy, she was certain, could discuss a farm boundary or a tenant's roof with such precision that time itself would feel obliged to stand properly measured.

That was not, she was sure, what they were speaking about.

She did not fear that her father would be rude. He could be provoking, indolent, negligent, and, upon occasion, unfair; but he was not a fool. Nor did she fear that Mr. Darcy would be offended by a father's questions. He had borne worse from her.

No. What unsettled her was the knowledge that they might be speaking of Kitty — and perhaps, without saying her name, of Elizabeth. Of leaving two daughters under Mr. Darcy's roof. Of trust, which could be made to sound like household management if gentlemen tried very hard.

And beneath all that, the thought she did not permit to stand upright: that her father was judging the man she had judged, misjudged, resisted, watched, defended, and begun — begun, only begun — to need near her.

The door opened. Mr. Bennet entered first, Mr. Darcy behind him, both perfectly composed. Too composed. Elizabeth knew her father well enough to understand that idleness did not always mean inattention, and Mr. Darcy well enough, now, to know that restraint could conceal more movement than another man's speech.

Coffee was brought. Cups were distributed. Mr. Bennet waved his away with thanks. Mr. Darcy spoke to Mrs. Gardiner about the roads near Lambton. Georgiana showed Kitty a stitch; Kitty corrected it with care. Elizabeth's cup grew cold between her hands.

At length her father turned to her. "Have you finished your coffee, Lizzy?"

"I have not started it."

"Then you will not miss it." He rose. "Mr. Darcy has been good enough to lend me his library for a time. Come and keep me company."

Kitty, startled, looked from her father to Elizabeth and then back again.

Elizabeth set down the untouched cup and rose. Mr. Darcy rose as they did. She was aware of Mrs. Gardiner's eyes upon her, of Georgiana's anxious stillness, of Kitty's parted lips. Mr. Bennet went ahead. As Elizabeth passed near Mr. Darcy, she could hear the slight shift of his breath before he spoke.

"My father has not exhausted you, I hope," she said quickly, because she could not afford to hear whatever he might say first.

"No," he said. Then, after a pause, "He is very much your father."

It should have amused her. It did. It also made her heart behave foolishly.

"That is not always taken as praise."

"In this instance, it was intended as such."

His face gave little away, but not nothing. The warmth was there, held carefully back — not, she thought, because her father was in the corridor, but, perhaps, because he was not yet certain she would welcome it.

She had not, before.

"Thank you," she said.

Mr. Darcy said nothing, and she went past him.

The library was near — only the next room but one. Its door stood half open. She knew the room. She had walked through it with Mrs. Reynolds on that first morning, when the books had stood at odd angles, as if lately read, the window-seat cushion had been sat on, and the owner had been thought to be miles and miles away. She had been in it since — once, when Mr. Darcy had shown her uncle a set of county maps. But she had not been in it like this, at night, with the candles lit and her father waiting. Tonight it seemed less a library than a witness.

Her father crossed to the hearth, picked up a book from the table, and turned it over without reading its title. "Shut the door, if you please."

Elizabeth did as he asked, and then moved to the chair opposite his, in front of the banked fireplace. He took his spectacles off, and turned them over in his hand.

"I have spoken with Kitty."

"So she told me."

"Did she give a faithful report?"

"That depends upon what you asked."

"Then no." He leaned back, with the slow satisfaction of a man who had found the chair adequate to his need. "She informed me, among other things, that Miss Darcy's cat is a monarch, that one kitten is destined for tyranny, and that she herself may be of some use in preventing civil disorder."

"So I observed."

"I feared as much." His mouth twitched. "She also told me that Miss Darcy is kind, that Mrs. Annesley is always near without being tiresome, that Mrs. Gardiner knows everything before anyone says it, and that you smile more here than lately at Longbourn."

Elizabeth's color rose. "Kitty is impertinent."

"Frequently. In this case, she may also be accurate."

"Papa."

"Yes, yes. We shall return to your crimes in a moment." He leaned back. "First, Kitty. She wishes to stay."

"Yes."

"She does not wish it merely because she has her own room here, or because there are kittens, or because Miss Darcy owns more ribbons than her sisters?"

"I do not think so."

"No." He tapped the spectacles lightly against his palm. "Nor do I. She spoke of Miss Darcy before she spoke of the kittens. That is either a good sign or a severe indictment of the kittens."

Elizabeth smiled despite herself.

"I have also spoken with Mr. Darcy."

"So I gathered."

"A composed young man. Difficult to provoke. I was obliged to work harder than is good for my health."

"What did you ask him?"

"What a father ought to ask when he proposes to leave one daughter in another gentleman's house and finds another daughter already far too much at ease there."

The words were lightly spoken. They struck with inconvenient force.

Elizabeth folded her hands in her lap. "And were you satisfied?"

"As far as any father may be satisfied." He looked about the room. "Mrs. Annesley appears to be a sensible woman. Your aunt is satisfied for the present. Miss Darcy's desire for Kitty's company is plain enough, and Mr. Darcy's understanding of the arrangement is neither careless nor obscure. Kitty will not be left to govern herself." He paused. "Your aunt, of course, cannot remain indefinitely.”

Elizabeth could not deny it. "Aunt Gardiner is still within the time she expected to be away."

"For the present."

"Yes."

"When your aunt must leave, the matter will want settling again. I will not have it drift." He tapped the spectacles lightly against his knee. "But while she remains, I see no reason to drag Kitty south tomorrow simply to prove that I can." He paused. "She may remain."

The relief came at once, and too strongly. It was not only for Kitty.

"She will be very happy."

"So I believe. I shall attempt not to resent Derbyshire for succeeding where Hertfordshire has often failed." He put on his spectacles, sighed, and removed them again. He pulled out his handkerchief and started wiping the glass. "You may remain as well, while your aunt does."

“Was this in question?”

"Was it not?"

"I am here with Aunt Gardiner."

"An admirable answer to a question no one asked." His voice gentled, though the sharp edge remained. "Lizzy, do you wish to return with me?"

She had expected the question. She had not expected the room to go so still around it.

"Kitty should not be unsettled again so soon."

"Kitty has been discussed."

"Miss Darcy depends upon the arrangement."

"Miss Darcy has a brother, a companion, a house full of servants, and, for the present, your aunt."

"But Kitty—"

"Will remain." He held her gaze. "I asked whether you wished to return."

Elizabeth looked away first. The library shelves rose dark and orderly beyond him. By candlelight the spines looked different — warmer, closer, the gilt worn soft on the volumes nearest the hearth. A book lay face-down on the arm of the chair she had not taken, its place held without a marker, as if the reader meant to return before the candle burned out. 

"I am comfortable here," she said.

"A dangerous confession. Pemberley has undone stronger minds."

"It has done no such thing."

"No? Then I must have mistaken the evidence."

She stood, because sitting had become intolerable, and went to the nearest shelf. The books there were bound in calf and arranged by a logic she could not immediately discern. Her finger touched one spine and withdrew. "You have been speaking with Mr. Darcy too long. You are both becoming fond of evidence."

"Mr. Darcy is fond of evidence. I am fond of conclusions reached with as little exertion as possible."

"Then you may conclude that I am useful here."

"I have." His answer was low enough that she turned. "You are useful to Kitty. You are useful to Miss Darcy. You are useful, I suspect, to Mr. Darcy, though he did not have the presumption to say so."

Heat came into her face at once. "Papa."

"Do not look so severe, Lizzy. I have not accused you of a crime."

"You imply—"

"I imply that a father may observe his favorite daughter without immediately being charged with imagination."

She tried to answer. Wit came, saw the room, and retreated.

"I have no wish to carry you off, if you are truly easy here. But I have still less wish to leave you here merely because you think everyone else requires it."

"I do not."

"Very good. Then say so."

Elizabeth looked at him helplessly. It was an old look they shared, though the cause was new. At Longbourn, he had often invited her to share his amusement and left her to carry what amusement did not mend. She had loved him for his wit and resented him for its uses, sometimes in the same hour. Tonight he was trying to do something more difficult than laugh at the world.

She could not punish him for being late when he had finally come.

"I do not wish to leave tomorrow," she said.

Not all the truth, but enough of it to stand.

Mr. Bennet nodded once. "Then you shall not."

"Thank you."

"Do not thank me too warmly. I am leaving one daughter in Derbyshire nearly smitten by the livestock and permitting another to remain in the house of a man whose library has already led her into dangerous partiality."

"Partiality for books?"

"Among other hazards."

"Papa."

"Ah, that familiar tone. I am reassured. You are still my Lizzy." He rose and came to stand beside her at the shelf. For a moment they both looked at the books and not at each other. "Mr. Darcy spoke well."

Elizabeth's throat tightened for no reason she was willing to examine. "Did he?"

"He spoke plainly. That is rare." Mr. Bennet took down a volume, glanced at the title, and replaced it. "He made no display of generosity. He did not pretend there was no inconvenience. He described the arrangement, the limits of it, and the persons responsible for it. A dull performance, in the best possible sense."

Elizabeth smiled faintly. "You approve of dullness?"

"In young men, I approve of it exceedingly when the alternative is charm."

The word *charm* landed between them with another name beneath it. Wickham was not spoken.

Mr. Bennet looked at her over the rims of spectacles he had finally put on. "I have not forgotten, Lizzy, that our family has lately had cause to distrust charming young men and careless fathers."

She went still.

He said it dryly, almost lightly, but not quite. The confession in it was small, and very like him. He would not unfold guilt upon the table. He would set down a corner of it and pretend it was a bookmark.

"Nor have I," she said.

"Then I shall try, for once, to be neither charming nor careless." He drew a breath, seeming mildly displeased by the exertion of sincerity. "Kitty may remain because she wishes it, because she is properly attended, and because I believe it may do her good. You may remain because your aunt remains, because the arrangement is respectable, and because you choose it."

The last words found her.

*Because you choose it.*

Her fingers found the cuff of her sleeve. Every reason she had given herself for remaining was true, and together they made a wall she could stand behind that she had constructed herself.

Her father had opened a door in it.

"I do choose it," she said, too softly.

Mr. Bennet heard. Of course he heard; he had always heard what amused him. Tonight he heard what mattered.

"Then stay, Lizzy," he said. "Only know why you stay."

The sentence should have relieved her. It did not. It followed her when they left the library and returned to the drawing room, where Kitty looked up with all her future held anxiously in her face.

"Well?" Kitty said, forgetting every lesson in composure at once.

Mr. Bennet folded his spectacles and put them away. "I have examined the evidence and find that Derbyshire may endure you a little longer."

Kitty rose so quickly that her needle rolled off the table. "Papa!"

"Do not embrace me with a needle in the neighborhood."

"I have not got it."

"That is even less comforting."

Georgiana had gone pink with pleasure. Mrs. Annesley retrieved the needle; Mrs. Gardiner looked from Mr. Bennet to Elizabeth and asked nothing. 

Mr. Darcy, who had stood on Elizabeth’s entrance, folded himself back into the wingback chair, held in that careful way she had begun to recognize — the restraint that meant he was feeling more than he intended to show.

Kitty turned at once to Georgiana. "Then we may see whether Mr. Grayfur is still a tyrant tomorrow."

"If the queen permits it," Georgiana said.

"She must. Papa has examined the evidence."

Mr. Bennet bowed slightly toward Miss Darcy. "I leave my daughter in your care, Miss Darcy, and your cat's governance. I trust one of you will prove equal to the charge."

Georgiana, startled and pleased into courage, answered, "We shall try, sir."

Mr. Darcy's eyes moved to his sister. His face did not soften as openly as it had in the stable, but Elizabeth saw the effort it cost him not to show too much before them all. Then his gaze came to her, and something in his face gave way. Not much. The corner of his mouth, a softening he did not catch in time.
