# BSP — Chapter 26: The Fair at Lambton

The next morning, Mr. Bennet departed after an early breakfast, before the dew was off the lawns. At late breakfast, the table had not changed, but everyone seemed aware of the chair he no longer occupied — Kitty most of all. 

Determined not to be low, she praised the marmalade. Georgiana asked whether marmalade was always so highly considered in Hertfordshire. Mrs. Gardiner, with admirable gravity, gave a short account of orange preserves, London tradesmen, and Kitty’s mother’s opinion that no breakfast could be respectable without fruit.

Mr. Darcy listened as if breakfast talk, once admitted to his table, deserved the courtesy of being heard. Elizabeth had learned not to mistake it for dullness. He did not always speak; he registered. Georgiana's guarded eagerness, Kitty's determined cheerfulness, Mrs. Gardiner's careful effort to keep the talk easy — he seemed to see them all and interfere with none until interference would serve.

"There is a fair at Lambton today," he said when Mrs. Gardiner had finished.

He had mentioned it before, at the stables while Kitty and Georgiana were occupied with the kittens. He had described it then as if testing whether ordinary pleasure might be permitted.

Kitty set down her knife. "A fair?"

"A small one."

"How small?"

"Large enough to be called a fair by those who wish to attend it, and a nuisance by those who do not."

Georgiana's eyes went at once to Kitty, whose face had brightened in the most complete betrayal of all resolutions to be composed.

"Is it like Meryton market?" Kitty asked.

"Less military, I expect,” said Elizabeth.

"Less dominated by officers," Mrs. Gardiner added, with a glance at Elizabeth that carried more history than her words.

Kitty colored, but did not retreat. "Then it may be better."

Mr. Darcy's gaze moved from Kitty to Georgiana. "There are stalls in the market-place and along the green; some farmers bring stock, though not in any great number; there are sellers from Bakewell and Matlock, ribbons and straw work, gingerbread, tools, harness, toy-sellers, printed ballads, a fiddler by the inn, and games for children on the green. I have known it since I was a boy."

The last sentence altered the table. It was not said to invite tenderness; perhaps that was why it invited it. Elizabeth looked at him before she could prevent herself. He had not been describing amusement only, but a piece of the estate's life, one of those small public observances by which a great house acknowledged the country around it.

Kitty asked him, "And do you still like it?"

Mr. Darcy considered his answer with unnecessary seriousness. "In moderation."

Kitty considered this. "That is how Mary likes everything."

"Then Miss Mary Bennet and I are of one mind on more subjects than I had supposed."

How different such a sentence would once have sounded from him; how impossible it would once have been for him to say it at all.

Mrs. Gardiner's expression held a little of memory and a little of caution. "When I attended the Lambton fair, it was not always the quietest of places. Has it changed much?"

"In some respects, not much," said Mr. Darcy. "There is still a part of it best left to those who enjoy noise for its own sake. But in the earlier hours, and along the market-place and green, it is orderly enough. Mrs. Annesley means to walk there later with her sister's family, I believe, so we must not claim her attendance."

Georgiana's disappointment showed before she corrected it. "Her sister's youngest child has had a fever."

“And is now recovered enough to be shown the fair,” said Mr. Darcy. “Mrs. Annesley has been promised that privilege.”

Mrs. Gardiner smiled. "Then we must not rob her of it."

"We need not go," Georgiana said at once.

Kitty looked from Georgiana to Mr. Darcy, and then, with an effort which did her credit, looked down at her plate. The whole little battle was there: the wish to plead, the memory that pleading was Lydia's province, and the choice of silence because she had begun to value being trusted.

Mr. Darcy's gaze moved from Kitty's lowered eyes to Georgiana's face.

"If Mrs. Gardiner approves," he said, "and if you would like to see it, I can take you for an hour after luncheon."

Georgiana's answer came only through the lifting of her head.

Kitty forgot caution. "Oh, yes — if Aunt Gardiner approves. I mean, if Miss Darcy wishes it."

Georgiana smiled. "I should like it."

Elizabeth turned her cup a little in its saucer. She had no reason to be moved by so small a concession. Young people went to fairs; guardians permitted it; gentlemen accompanied sisters. Yet nothing in Mr. Darcy's manner suggested he thought it small. He had measured the noise, the crowd, Kitty's eagerness, Georgiana's shyness, Mrs. Annesley's absence, and his own authority — and chosen liberty.

It was not the kind of proof which announced itself. It merely changed what the day would be.

“Elizabeth?” her aunt asked. “What do you say?”

Elizabeth glanced up from the cup she had been turning. “Yes,” she said, and found the word less steady than she meant it to be. “If we keep to the earlier part, I think it may do very well.”

"Then we shall all go," said Mrs. Gardiner.

Kitty smiled as if Derbyshire had justified itself at last.

---

The road into the village was busier than Elizabeth had yet seen it: carts drawn to the side; farmers in their better coats; children darting where children ought not; women pausing in pairs to inspect goods and each other with equal care.

The green had been given over to booths, trestles, and small temporary wonders. A fiddler stood near the inn door, tightening a peg while a few children waited hopefully. From another direction came the smell of gingerbread, horses, damp straw, and hot metal from a brazier where a man repaired pots.

Mr. Darcy, who had ridden beside the carriage, dismounted before the servant had fully lowered the step.

Kitty came down from the carriage carefully, though delight kept breaking through. Georgiana landed beside her, gloved hands folded, looking not frightened exactly, but aware of every motion around her.

"It is very busy," she said.

"It is," Kitty answered. "But not all at once, if one looks at one thing at a time."

Elizabeth turned, surprised and pleased.

Georgiana considered this. "Then what should we look at first?"

"Something that cannot run away."

"The ribbons, then," said Elizabeth.

"Or the gingerbread," said Kitty.

“The gingerbread seller has too many admirers already,” Georgiana said.

"True. Ribbons are steadier."

Mr. Darcy, who had come to Georgiana's other side, did not smile openly. "Then we begin with ribbons."

They did not begin with ribbons. They began with being stopped three times before they reached the green. Mr. Darcy was greeted first by a farmer whose lease, Elizabeth gathered, had lately been renewed. A miller's wife wished him to know how proud she was that her eldest boy had been taken on at the stables. Then an older man, his back bent nearly double, removed his hat and forgot whatever he had meant to say.

Mr. Darcy supplied it for him.

"Your granddaughter, she is recovered?"

"Aye, sir. That she is. Running about again, and her mother says more trouble than the fever."

"I am glad to hear it."

"Aye, sir. We are much beholden."

Mr. Darcy inclined his head. “A promising sign.”

Elizabeth walked on with Mrs. Gardiner.

"Lizzy," her aunt said in a low voice, "you are very silent."

"I am observing."

"That is often the most dangerous form of silence."

Elizabeth might have answered, but Kitty called to them from a stall where lengths of ribbon were displayed with all the triumph of scarlet, blue, green, and yellow that sun and cheap dye could produce. Georgiana stood beside her, looking at the happy confusion of colors with the solemnity of a person choosing between moral principles.

"This one," Kitty said, holding up a blue ribbon, "is nearly the color of the gray kitten's eyes."

Georgiana touched the ribbon. "It is pretty."

"Too bright for a tyrant," Elizabeth said. "He would require something sterner."

"Black velvet," Georgiana said, "if his government is to continue."

Kitty laughed; Georgiana pressed her lips together, but the laugh escaped all the same. The woman behind the stall, a broad-faced person with sharp eyes and a warm manner, looked from one young lady to the other with satisfaction.

"Miss Darcy has found merry company today," she said.

Georgiana’s color rose, but she answered with proper composure. “Yes. Miss Catherine is very kind.”

Kitty, perhaps to spare her any further notice, held up another ribbon. “This darker blue, perhaps?”

After the young ladies had obtained both the blue and the purple, they moved to the next stall, stuffed with well-made but poorly painted wooden toys.

"That was well done," Mrs. Gardiner said.

Elizabeth looked after Kitty and Georgiana. “It was,” she said, softer than she intended.

As they moved on, Mr. Darcy rejoined them. Elizabeth was conscious of the arrangement at once: Kitty and Georgiana ahead under Mrs. Gardiner's eye, herself and Mr. Darcy following at a proper distance. The world around them supplied witnesses by the dozen.

Every few steps someone greeted him. Not with the boldness of intimacy, nor with fear. Not with the formal respect his consequence always commanded, but with a confidence that seemed founded on long experience.

He knew names — not every name, but enough, and the names he did not know he did not pretend to know. He asked one woman whether her husband had found the new gate easier for the cart. He told a boy not to lead a pony through the thickest part of the crowd and was obeyed after only a little resentment. He stood aside for an older woman with a basket before she had to ask. He refused a painted wooden whistle Georgiana could not possibly want, but bought two small carved animals from the same boy after learning the boy's father had made them during a wet week when the hay could not be cut.

One went to Georgiana, a stiff-legged horse. The other he gave to Kitty, after a pause, with such formality that Kitty received it as if it had been a royal appointment.

"For me?"

"If you will accept it."

"It is a cow," Kitty said, delighted.

"So I believe."

"I shall call it Mr. Bennet."

Elizabeth laughed before she could help it. Mr. Darcy looked away for a moment, but the corner of his mouth betrayed him.

"An honor I shall not report to your father unless compelled," he said.

Kitty tucked the wooden cow into her reticule, where it made a most inconvenient bulge.

They reached a stall set a little back from the busiest part of the green, shaded by a canvas awning and crowded with straw bonnets, chip hats, ribbons, and artificial flowers. Several women were already there, turning brims, comparing trims, and discovering defects with the air of those who expected defects to lower the price. Georgiana came to a stop before a plain straw bonnet with a narrow brim and a pale ribbon about the crown.

Kitty noticed at once. "That one would suit you." She lifted it by the brim.

"Would it?"

"Yes. It is not too smart."

Elizabeth, drawn by a display of printed cottons at the next stall, had moved a little down the row. The two stalls stood almost together, separated only by a hanging length of muslin and a narrow passage; she could still see the girls when she turned her head, but Kitty, intent upon the bonnet, had lost sight of her.

"I mean, it would not look as if you had tried to be someone else,” Kitty went on. “It would look like you, only at a fair."

Elizabeth looked down at the printed cotton before her and pressed her lips together. It was affection, she reminded herself, and nearly a compliment.

The shopkeeper chuckled. "There's a recommendation I have not heard before. 'Like yourself, only at a fair.' I shall put it on a sign."

Elizabeth glanced back in time to see Georgiana smile, though her hand still hovered short of the bonnet. Mrs. Gardiner had stepped aside to admire a length of lace, and Mr. Darcy, a few steps beyond the hats, had been claimed by a tenant who wished to present a niece visiting from Chesterfield. The introduction was performed with the gravity of a court ceremony: the man beaming, the young woman curtseying once and then hovering as if uncertain whether a second curtsey might be required.

Georgiana looked behind her. "Where is Miss Elizabeth?"

Kitty answered without thought, turning the bonnet this way and that.

"With Mr. Darcy, I believe. You’re right, she will know."

The words were nothing. They were everything. They came with no emphasis, no arch look, no awareness that they might be heard by anyone beyond Georgiana. Kitty spoke as she might have said Elizabeth was with Mrs. Gardiner, or at the bookstall, or near the carriage. *With Mr. Darcy* had become, in Kitty's mouth, a location.

The shopkeeper’s attention fixed on Kitty. At the far end of the stall, another woman, who had been comparing artificial flowers, lifted her eyes and then lowered them again with an expression of sudden industry.

"Is that so?" said the shopkeeper, not quite to Kitty.

Kitty, now attending only to the brim, said, "Yes, I think so. They were just behind us."

Georgiana, catching more in the shopkeeper's tone than in the words, lowered her hand.

At the far end of the stall, the other woman gave a small, satisfied sound. "A fine thing," she said, "if it were so."

Kitty looked up. "If what were so?"

"Nothing, miss," said the shopkeeper, with the practiced innocence of one who had meant everything. "Only that Pemberley has been long without a lady to choose bonnets for young ladies. Miss Darcy is as sweet as May, but a house wants more than sweetness, does it not?"

"Aye," said the other woman. "And Mr. Darcy wants—"

She stopped.

Elizabeth, from the neighboring stall, had heard every word. She turned back at once; she could not let this continue.

"Indeed," she said, because civility demanded a tone and her own pride supplied the steadiness of it, "we are only choosing a bonnet."

"To be sure, miss," said the shopkeeper.

The woman at the flowers, less easily silenced, smiled at Elizabeth with a warmth that was almost maternal. "Only that it does a place good to see young ladies about it. Miss Darcy looks well today. Some company suits a house."

Elizabeth's gaze moved to Georgiana, then to Kitty, then — fatally — to Mr. Darcy, who had now entered the stall.

He had heard enough—of that she was certain. All expression left him except courtesy. It was done so quickly, so cleanly that no one unaccustomed to him would have detected a change. His posture did not stiffen; his voice did not cool by any degree that could be measured; he merely became the gentleman of Pemberley in public, exact in attention and inaccessible behind it.

"Georgiana," he said, "if you like the bonnet, you should have it."

Georgiana looked uncertain. "I wanted Miss Elizabeth's opinion."

Mr. Darcy turned to Elizabeth. "What do you advise?"

There was no impropriety in the request. There was no warmth in it either — not that anyone else could claim. He gave her the safest possible place in the exchange and removed himself from it.

Elizabeth took the bonnet from Kitty because her hands required employment. She examined the straw, the ribbon, the proportion of brim to crown, as if bonnets had become the single test of judgment left in the world.

"It is well chosen," she said. "Kitty is right. A very fair bonnet."

Georgiana laughed, relieved. Kitty laughed too, and the shopkeeper declared she would shave off an extra shilling for so philosophical a recommendation.

Mr. Darcy paid for the bonnet. He paid what was asked. Elizabeth noticed that absurd detail and hated herself for noticing. Had he paid more, she might have called it display. Paying exactly, she called distance. Every action offered two meanings, and the one she chose revealed too much of her own heart.

Mrs. Gardiner returned from the lace with her acquaintance and saw enough in Elizabeth's face to look twice, but not enough to understand. Georgiana thanked everyone, and, with a quiet decisiveness, put it on at once, handing her own bonnet to Mrs. Gardiner. 

“The crowd is increasing,” Mr. Darcy said. “We had better return toward the carriage.”

It was perfectly sensible. Elizabeth heard retreat.

They went on. The fair had not altered. The fiddler had begun to play. Children still ran; women still bargained; two dogs engaged in a disagreement beneath a cart and were scolded by three people who owned neither of them. Georgiana touched the ribbon of her new bonnet when she thought no one observed, and when Kitty declared it a triumph, she smiled with real pleasure.

Elizabeth saw all of it at a distance of three feet and several miles.

Mr. Darcy did not walk beside her again. He took Mrs. Gardiner’s arm instead. Once, when a boy carrying a tray of gingerbread came too near, Mr. Darcy checked his own step and Mrs. Gardiner’s, leaving Elizabeth room to pass untouched. Once, when a woman at the next booth looked from Mr. Darcy to Elizabeth with the beginning of a smile, he turned away before the smile could finish. Once his gaze came to Elizabeth's face and glanced off again, as if he dared not know her there.

He might be shielding her from public presumption, sparing her the mortification of an assumption she had not invited. Or he might be withdrawing because the assumption itself was unwelcome. She could build either reading from the same evidence.

That was the terror.

She walked on. At Hunsford she had been so certain. She had read his pride, his contempt, his injury to Jane with such confidence. Then his letter had exposed the certainty itself as the deepest error. She had believed herself humbled enough, since then, to be fair.

But fairness had become desire without asking leave.

The Lambton people, with their half-finished sentences and hopeful looks, had placed her where she had not dared place herself. They had imagined her into Pemberley. They had given Mr. Darcy a happiness and made her the instrument of it. And some unguarded part of her had answered *yes* before judgment could object.

And now Mr. Darcy had become correct — impeccably, impenetrably correct.

A man may be correct from kindness. A man may be correct from embarrassment. A man may be correct from regret that others have presumed too much.

Which man was Mr. Darcy? And what was Elizabeth, if she could be so uncertain of him — and so little certain of herself?

They returned to the carriage before the hour was quite gone. Mr. Darcy handed them all in. His manner was all that it ought to be. No one could have accused him of coldness. No one could have claimed warmth.

Kitty carried the carved cow, a paper of gingerbread, and enough observations to sustain the whole road back. Georgiana wore the straw bonnet and touched its ribbon more than once. Mrs. Gardiner was pleased with the outing, with Georgiana, with Kitty, and with Derbyshire in general.

Elizabeth sat opposite Georgiana. Kitty, beside her, began at once to argue that Mr. Bennet the cow had a statesmanlike countenance. Georgiana defended her carved horse from charges of excessive dignity. Mrs. Gardiner smiled indulgently and said both animals must be judged when they had been longer acquainted with Pemberley.

Elizabeth responded only when required. She answered once when Kitty appealed to her. Mrs. Gardiner, under cover of adjusting her glove, studied her niece's profile and said nothing. Elizabeth looked out as Lambton drew back from the carriage windows and the road rose toward the park.

Only one person did not accept her performance.

Mr. Darcy rode beside them for part of the way, close enough that when the road narrowed and the carriage slowed, Elizabeth could see him through the open window. He was not looking at Georgiana's bonnet or Kitty's animated hands or Mrs. Gardiner's amused face. He was looking at her.

She turned away, but already the concern on his face had become another piece of evidence she did not know how to read.

By the time Pemberley's trees closed around the road, Elizabeth had arranged her countenance into something nearly like ease. It cost her more than she had expected.

He had done nothing wrong, and she was less certain than ever.

The carriage stopped. Mr. Darcy again was at the door before the servant had fully lowered the step. He handed down Mrs. Gardiner, then Georgiana, then Kitty. When Elizabeth's turn came, his hand was steady beneath hers.

"Miss Bennet," he said. Only that.

She looked at him. What she wanted to see and what was true had become, at some point during the afternoon, indistinguishable. She had been wrong about this man once with total confidence. She could not afford to be wrong again. And she could no longer tell which error she was making.

"Mr. Darcy."

She withdrew her hand and went inside with the others.
