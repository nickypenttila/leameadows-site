# BSP — Chapter 20: Proper Company

The morning after their removal to Pemberley found Elizabeth in possession of several privileges to which no young woman of sense could reasonably object: a chamber near her aunt’s, a bell she did not mean to ring, a writing table placed exactly where the light was best. She had only to rise from a bed whose smooth linen still seemed an extravagance, and she was already at Pemberley. No horses to order, no carriage to endure, no road between anticipation and arrival.

This last circumstance was the most difficult to grasp. Pemberley had, until now, required preparation. It had required a bonnet, a road, a turn in the drive, the gathering of composure while stone and glass rose into view, the knowledge that one must enter, admire, behave. This morning there had been no approach. She had opened her eyes, and Pemberley was before her.

Her chamber was sizable and airy, but not one of the grand rooms. Grandeur might have been resisted. A state bed, a painted ceiling, a prospect arranged for admiration — all these would have reminded her that she was temporary, insignificant, and very properly overwhelmed. But this room, with its pale blue hangings, sober rug, small fire-screen, and view over the rising ground, seemed arranged to put a guest at ease rather than overpower her. 

Her trunk had stood at the foot of the bed for all of half a day. Some quiet and very efficient person had carried it away, after distributing her clothing into drawers and presses that opened smoothly, setting her brushes where they ought to be, and leaving no sign that arrival had required effort at all. Her gloves lay upon the chair where she had left them. A volume, placed there by some considerate hand, waited upon the little table as if young ladies had always been expected to read at Pemberley before breakfast.

Mr. Gardiner had accompanied them to the great house. He had kissed his wife with tenderness, charged Elizabeth to prevent her aunt from climbing every hill in Derbyshire merely because she had climbed it twenty years before, and thanked Mr. Darcy with a frankness which, had it been less sincere, might have been easier for Elizabeth to bear. Mr. Darcy received it as he received everything that morning: with exact attention, no flourish, and an absence of condescension which made gratitude harder to dismiss.

There had been no officious display of rooms, servants, carriages, views, advantages, or obligations. Mrs. Gardiner’s comfort had been consulted before she had time to request it. Elizabeth’s chamber sat very appropriately beside her aunt’s. Miss Darcy had shown them the way herself, shyly pleased and more animated with every door she opened.

By the next morning, Mrs. Gardiner had accepted their situation with a composure Elizabeth envied. Her aunt had written to Longbourn, arranged that Mr. Gardiner should send word from London on his arrival, praised the linen, inquired whether Miss Darcy walked before or after luncheon, and displayed no uneasiness beyond that of a woman who suspected Derbyshire weather of plotting against her bonnet.

Elizabeth, less usefully employed, found herself wandering after breakfast with no better purpose than to discover what sort of place Pemberley became when it was at ease.

As a visitor, she had been conducted. As a caller, she had been received. As a guest, it appeared, she might move freely, quietly, at whatever pace she chose, without being summoned, guided, or corrected. It was a dangerous degree of liberty; she had already proved unequal to it by leaving her gloves upon a chair.

The house altered under such liberties. Rooms she had admired three days ago were now simply in use. A door stood open where a servant had passed. A vase had been moved since yesterday. A chair near a window bore the slight displacement of recent occupation. In an upstairs sitting room, a book lay open upon a table with a paper-knife beside it, abandoned mid-chapter. Elizabeth did not take it up. That would have been too much like confidence. But she read the title as she passed, and touched the margin with one finger before she knew she had done it.

Pemberley was not less grand for these proofs of life. Instead, it was more difficult to resist. For one foolish instant she could imagine going on in just such a manner: passing through halls without being conducted, knowing which room held the morning sun, leaving gloves where some silent hand would restore them, finding windows open and fires laid and every convenience arranged before she had thought to desire it.

The impertinence of the fancy stopped her where she stood.

To imagine admiring Pemberley had once been dangerous enough. To imagine being at ease in it was worse. She could hear her mother’s voice in the very next breath — *Oh, Lizzy! only think what a house!* — and the mortification was so complete that Elizabeth almost laughed aloud. Nothing could more effectually cure a young woman of presumption than discovering Mrs. Bennet already at the end of it, arranging the drawing-room chairs and asking whether the plate was all real silver.

She had meant only to look for Mrs. Gardiner, who had been drawn away by Miss Darcy to consult over some music. Instead, Elizabeth took a wrong turning — or persuaded herself she had — and found herself in the portrait gallery.

Unlike the first time she’d seen it, the gallery was empty, and the Darcys on the walls appeared to have completely recovered from the inconvenience of being exhibited to strangers.

Elizabeth strolled slowly beneath them.

These silent sitters could not object to her. They could not know of Meryton assemblies, ill-judged speeches, mortifying mothers, flirtations, refusals, letters, or younger sisters tempting fate and folly . The living Mr. Darcy knew much of it.

She stopped before a portrait of a young woman in pale silk, one hand resting on the shoulder of a little girl who looked out with solemn eyes. The lady’s expression had that composed sweetness which painters bestowed upon women of rank, whether they possessed it or not. The child, however, had escaped into something nearer truth. She appeared not unhappy, exactly, but watchful; loved, perhaps, and instructed; cherished and alone.

Another Miss Darcy.

No — Georgiana. The correction mattered. The permission had been given after dinner the previous evening, with much color and a courage so evidently gathered for the purpose that Mrs. Gardiner had accepted it as gravely as if it were a treaty. Elizabeth had said the name only once since, and had felt, absurdly, as if she had been trusted with a key.

Not another composed young lady arranged for admiration; not the accomplished musician, not the heiress, not merely the sister guarded by a brother who would have fenced the world out — and had learned, once, how easily a fence might be climbed. But Georgiana: the young woman who had looked almost startled when Mrs. Gardiner praised a country tune; who had brightened at the prospect of kittens; who had admitted, with all the courage of a much bolder creature, that Pemberley was quiet now.

In the distance came the sound of a pianoforte. A phrase began, faltered, and was taken up again from the beginning. A bar was repeated with conscientious care, as if the player had been accused by no one and had nevertheless found herself guilty. 

Elizabeth left the portraits to their propriety and followed the music.

The music-room door stood partly open. She paused before entering, less from hesitation than from an unwillingness to disturb what she had found.

Georgiana sat at the instrument, her profile bent over the music. Mrs. Gardiner stood beside her, one hand resting lightly on the top of the piano, following the troublesome passage as if her attention alone might help it into order. As always, her aunt had found a way to make herself useful.

Elizabeth entered quietly. Georgiana did not seem to notice her, but Aunt Gardiner caught her gaze and smiled a welcome.

Elizabeth settled herself in the deep embrasure of the east window, where a cushioned bench and two pillows made comfort almost indecorously easy. Looking out, she could see the sweep of the hill down to a small stream; looking in, she could see the player and her aunt, both with the glow of morning light around them. 

The gentle scene pressed upon her more than a formal entertainment could have done. No one asked Georgiana to astonish them. No one praised her into discomfort. No one made her accomplishments a tax she owed to the room. Elizabeth took up neither work nor book. For once, idle hands did not feel like negligence; they felt like permission.

When Georgiana recommenced the piece, Mr. Darcy appeared at the door.

Elizabeth knew he had entered before she turned and saw him, for Georgiana’s playing altered by the smallest degree — not worse, but perhaps a bit more careful. He seemed to perceive it too. He did not advance into the room, but remained near the door, listening with an expression Elizabeth could not readily name. Pride was there, certainly. Affection also. But beneath both lay a vigilance which had begun to trouble her.

A brother might delight in a sister’s music. Mr. Darcy seemed always prepared to defend his sister against censure, however slight, and from quarters Elizabeth could not yet see.

Mrs. Gardiner turned the page. Georgiana crossed one of the difficult passages neatly this time, and Mr. Darcy’s face changed. The change would have escaped anyone not already in the habit of watching him; Elizabeth was vexed to discover that the habit had formed without her consent.

When the piece ended, Georgiana looked first to Mrs. Gardiner.

“There,” said Mrs. Gardiner. “Mozart deserves you.”

Georgiana shook her head, but smiled. “Shall I try the sonata next?”

“By all means.”

They looked round as Mr. Darcy entered the room. He bowed to Mrs. Gardiner. “Ladies,” he said. “Pray, continue.” 

Georgiana turned back to the music, sifting the sheets with Mrs. Gardiner to find the new piece. 

Mr. Darcy held a book in one hand, and moved at once toward the chair nearest the east windows, its little round table set beside it as if for his habitual use. Then he saw (too late to pretend he had not seen) that Elizabeth already occupied the window-seat nearest the chair. He paused, no longer than a breath, but it made his decision visible. He came on nevertheless. 

His eyes met Elizabeth’s only long enough to acknowledge her presence. She looked down at her hands and found nothing in them to distract her.

When he sat, the chair placed him at an angle to the window, near enough for speech but turned sufficiently toward the instrument to make silence proper. The light fell cleanly on the open pages, and his profile was presented to Elizabeth as if the arrangement had been intended for his reading alone.

For a little while they attended the music. Then silence, instead of remaining silence, began to acquire weight.

“Miss Darcy seems happy this morning,” Elizabeth said, keeping her voice low enough not to travel beyond him.

Mr. Darcy’s attention did not leave his book. “You are kind to think so.”

“No—kindness is easy to grant; I am not in the habit of granting what is easy.”

He turned then, enough to show that he had understood more than the surface of her remark. Elizabeth wished, too late, that she had not begun so near the heart of the matter. But Georgiana was bending over the music with her aunt, pointing to some favorite passage, and the sight gave courage to what had only been thought.

“Miss Darcy must have had many excellent instructors,” Elizabeth said.

“She has been fortunate in them.”

“And in her brother.”

His expression closed by a degree. “I hope she has not been unfortunate.”

“No,” said Elizabeth. “No one could think that.”

The correction was too quick. Mr. Darcy’s eyes lifted from the page to her face, and his hand stilled upon the book, as if the answer had struck him more forcibly than praise. 

Elizabeth looked away, at Georgiana. “But excellent instructors cannot be everything.”

Mr. Darcy did not answer at once.

Georgiana began another piece: something lighter than the last, with a turn in it that seemed to please her, though she played it twice before she trusted it. The morning had arranged itself into a picture of perfect propriety: music, open door, Mrs. Gardiner within easy hearing, and Mr. Darcy no nearer than courtesy allowed. Nothing in the world could be safer, yet Elizabeth felt strangely without defence.

“No,” Mr. Darcy said at last. “They cannot.”

The answer was too measured to be agreement.

Elizabeth glanced at him, and away again. “You think I am presuming.”

“I think,” he said, and stopped, as if rejecting the first answer that had offered itself. “My sister has been fortunate in Mrs. Annesley.” The lady sat happily knitting by the little fire, the sort of presence one ceased to notice until one required it.

“I do not doubt it. Mrs. Annesley appears all kindness and sense.”

“She is both.”

“And yet she cannot be sixteen for Miss Darcy.”

He looked at her then—quite at her; and she felt, at once, that she had drawn him from every other thought.

Elizabeth had not meant to say it so plainly. The sentence had come while she was watching Georgiana’s hands: so accomplished, so careful, so young. But having said it, she would not recall it.

Mr. Darcy’s face altered very little, one of his most inconvenient habits.

“She is not much alone,” he said.

Elizabeth felt the justice of the answer, and its insufficiency. “That is not quite what I meant.”

At the pianoforte Georgiana paused. Mrs. Gardiner turned the page back, and the passage began again. Elizabeth waited until the music covered them.

“I am sure she has every attention,” she said. “More than most young women could hope for. But attention is not the same thing as company.”

Mr. Darcy’s hand, which had rested beside the book, closed on the carved arm of the chair.

Elizabeth marked it at once, and knew she ought to stop. She continued.

“When I was a girl,” she said, “I was improved by the society of my sisters, though not, perhaps, in any way a governess would have thought worth praising. I learned little method from them, and less moderation. But I was made less solemn by it. Less watched. There is a kind of ease that cannot be taught by older people, however much they love us.”

He looked away from her, toward Georgiana.

For several bars there was no answer. Elizabeth began to think he would not give one. Then he said, “Ease is desirable. It is not always safe.”

So that was his first principle: not pride, but fear.

Elizabeth might have pitied him for it, had fear been less tyrannical in its conclusions. Instead it irritated her, because it was too reasonable to be dismissed and too narrow to be accepted.

“No,” she said. “Not always.”

“You will understand,” he said, with a formality that did not quite hide the effort beneath it, “that my sister’s situation requires some caution.”

“I do understand it.”

“Do you?”

The question was low, yet it struck more sharply for its quietness.

Georgiana had bent closer to the music; a curl had slipped against her cheek. Her whole attention seemed fixed upon the keys. Elizabeth understood then that Mr. Darcy’s caution did not begin with rank, or fortune, or the ordinary hazards of a young heiress. It began with one man’s name.

*Mr. Wickham.*

The name entered the room as surely as if it had been spoken.

Elizabeth clasped her hands tightly. What if it had been Lydia in Wickham’s sights? 

Perhaps it had been.

Brighton.

“I understand more than I once did,” she said.

He turned back to her. Something crossed his face. Pain, quickly restrained.

“I beg your pardon,” he said. “My question was ungenerous.”

“It was not unjust.”

“I did not mean to imply—”

“That I have no right to speak? No. I did not take it so.”

This was not wholly true. It had been exactly how she had taken it for the first half-second. The second half had been less comfortable.

Mr. Darcy seemed to hear both halves.

“I know you speak from kindness to her,” he said.

The words were civil, and therefore more provoking. They granted motive and refused judgment.

Elizabeth drew a breath and let her eyes return to Georgiana, who was trying the same difficult turn once more with patient obedience. “Then let me speak from kindness a little further, and you may think me impertinent after.”

His jaw tightened once, and settled again. “I shall endeavor to postpone it.”

It was so unexpected, and so grave in delivery, that Elizabeth nearly smiled. 

“Miss Darcy might benefit from a companion nearer her own age.”

There. The thought, once a half-formed conviction, had become a sentence. It stood between them with shocking solidity.

Mr. Darcy said nothing.

Elizabeth wished he would object at once. Silence gave too much room to imagination. It allowed her to hear every possible answer: *Impossible. Unsuitable. Presumptuous. A Bennet? Untenable.*

“A companion,” he said.

“Yes.”

“Young ladies of her age are not always—” He stopped again, but this time the word he had withheld was plain enough.

Safe.

Elizabeth heard it and could not entirely condemn him for it.

“Not always,” she said. “But neither are older ladies always perceptive. Nor accomplished ones always kind. Miss Bingley was very proper company.”

Mr. Darcy’s head turned a fraction, a small, involuntary recoil, checked almost at once.

Elizabeth had not meant to use Miss Bingley as a weapon. Or perhaps she had. It was difficult to be certain of one’s virtue in the middle of an argument.

“She is not an example I would choose for my sister,” he said.

“No. But she is an example the world would choose for her.”

His answer did not come.

Elizabeth pressed on before courage could cool into prudence.

“A young woman may be guarded from impropriety and still be made miserable by what everyone calls suitable. I do not say Miss Darcy is miserable. I only say—” She stopped, because only say had become the refuge of people saying too much. “I only wonder whether she has enough easy society.”

Georgiana started the passage over again.

Mr. Darcy’s expression turned severe; his eyes remained elsewhere. “You have someone in mind.”

It was not a question.

Elizabeth could have retreated there. She could have said only that the principle seemed worth considering. She could have left Kitty unnamed and returned to Mrs. Gardiner with her dignity less endangered.

But Kitty’s face came to her with painful vividness: Kitty sulking in the parlor, Kitty beginning three sentences before finishing the fourth, Kitty standing in her father’s library with courage enough to be frightened, Kitty laughing too loudly over something foolish because no one had taught her how to be pleased in moderation.

Kitty, who was not Lydia.

“My sister Catherine,” Elizabeth said.

Mr. Darcy’s hand left the chair.

It was not a large movement. It did not need to be.

Elizabeth felt the old mortification rise at once, hot and familiar. There was the answer before he had spoken it. Not Miss Catherine. Not a Bennet. Not one of those younger sisters whose want of propriety had once been written of with such pitiless justice.

She lifted her chin by a degree. “You need not look so alarmed, sir. I have not ordered her trunk.”

His eyes returned to hers. “I did not mean—”

“No. Of course not.”

That was unfair, and she knew it while saying it. The knowledge did not improve her temper.

Georgiana stumbled over a note. Both Elizabeth and Mr. Darcy turned toward her at once. Mrs. Gardiner murmured something kind; Georgiana gave a little laugh of apology and began the measure again.

The shared motion had checked them more effectively than politeness.

When Mr. Darcy spoke again, his voice was lower. “Miss Catherine is very young.”

“She is the same age as Miss Darcy.”

He let out a short, impatient breath.

“I do not claim she is wise,” Elizabeth pushed on. “I do not claim she is accomplished. I do not even claim she would not say something foolish before the first hour was over. But she has warmth. She has spirits. And she is not—”

She stopped.

She had nearly said *she is not Lydia*. To speak the sentence aloud would be to injure Kitty in defending her and Lydia in condemning her. It would also confess too much to Mr. Darcy of Longbourn’s inward divisions.

Elizabeth tried again. “She might like Miss Darcy for herself.”

The words, once spoken, revealed the true center of her argument.

Mr. Darcy’s gaze moved to Georgiana, who was nodding agreement at something Mrs. Gardiner had said over the music.

“For herself,” he repeated, almost too soft for Elizabeth to hear.

His expression altered—not enough for triumph, but enough to show that the argument had found its mark.

Elizabeth saw it and was foolish enough to hope.

Then he said, “And if Miss Catherine should be led again by livelier spirits than her own?”

Hope withdrew.

Elizabeth tilted her head, and looked directly at him. “At Pemberley?”

“At Pemberley, in Lambton, at Longbourn, anywhere. Influence is not confined to the house in which it begins.”

“That is a very comprehensive objection.”

“It is an honest one.”

The civility of that answer cost her more than a sharper one would have done.

Mr. Darcy’s face tightened. “Miss Bennet—”

“No, pray do not apologize. You are responsible for your sister. You must consider what is best for her.”

“I must.”

“And I am responsible for thinking well of mine where I can.”

The music stopped.

It was not the end of the piece. Georgiana had only paused to ask Mrs. Gardiner something about the fingering, but the break came at the worst possible instant. Elizabeth’s last sentence seemed to remain in the open air, where anyone might have collected it.

Mrs. Gardiner turned another page with admirable composure. “I think here, my dear. Though you must not trust me beyond counting to four.”

Miss Darcy laughed. 

The piece began again.

Elizabeth had time to recover herself. She did not recover.

Mr. Darcy, for his part, looked as if he had found himself in the wrong and could not yet discover the honorable path out of it. The acknowledgment checked her resentment, though it did not wholly disarm it.

“If not Kitty,” Elizabeth said, surprising herself by the steadiness of her voice, “then someone else. That was my true meaning. A respectable young woman from Lambton, perhaps. Someone known to Mrs. Reynolds. Someone whose family is steady, whose manners are modest, whose situation would make the invitation a kindness rather than a condescension.”

Mr. Darcy looked more alarmed at this than he had at Kitty.

Elizabeth stared at him. “Worse?”

The word escaped before she could dress it properly.

His brows drew together. “Not worse.”

“Only impossible in a different direction.”

“That is not what I said.”

“It is what you looked.”

He took that with more justice than most men would have done.

“A local girl,” he said carefully, “would be known here.”

Elizabeth waited.

“Known by the servants. By every family in Lambton. Her father’s shop, or farm, or connexions — whatever they were — would enter with her before she had crossed the threshold.”

“That need not make her wretched.”

“It might.” His eyes remained on Georgiana. “If she were treated as companion, she might be thought beneath friendship. If treated as friend, she might become an object of envy before she had done anything to deserve it.”

Elizabeth had not expected that answer.

It was not contempt. It was worse. It was consideration.

“You have thought of everything,” she said.

“No.”

The word was immediate.

He looked at Georgiana.

“I have thought of too many dangers,” he said. “That is not the same thing.”

Elizabeth’s anger lost its footing.

She did not know what to do with an honest man.

Another man might have made more of it. Mr. Darcy seemed almost displeased to have allowed the words out. But they changed the air between them more than any apology could have done.

Elizabeth found she could not answer at once.

In the silence, Georgiana finished the piece. Mrs. Gardiner praised her with exactly enough warmth. Georgiana rose from the bench and turned toward them, her face brighter than it had been when Elizabeth entered.

“Brother,” she said, “did you hear? I did not lose myself at the second turn.”

“Very good.”

The two simple words transformed Georgiana. She looked younger, shone brighter.

She laughed and came away from the instrument with the music still in her hand. “I am afraid you have discovered that I possess more courage than command,” she said to Mrs. Gardiner.

“Then I have discovered a very common accomplishment. My dear Georgiana, if you truly wish to hear young ladies perform with more courage than command, you need only invite Lizzy to sing before dinner.”

“Aunt!” cried Elizabeth; such an attack could not go unanswered.

“I have said nothing against your singing. I have only described it with family accuracy.”

Georgiana’s giggle came freer this time. Even Mr. Darcy made a sound that might have been interpreted as a chuckle.

Elizabeth received the attack with all the dignity available. “You see how I am treated in Derbyshire, Georgiana. I had hoped Pemberley might be a refuge from family truth.”

“Pemberley has never been secure against truth,” said Mr. Darcy.

The words were mild. They might have meant nothing. But Elizabeth made the mistake of looking at him. He was not smiling now, nor was he stern; his countenance had settled into something thoughtful and proud and uneasy. 

He had not yielded. Still, she had the mortifying comfort of believing that he had heard her.
