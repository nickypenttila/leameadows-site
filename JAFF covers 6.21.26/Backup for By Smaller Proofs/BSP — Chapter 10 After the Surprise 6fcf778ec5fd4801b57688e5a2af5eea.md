# BSP — Chapter 10: After the Surprise

✅ Chapter complete

---

The chaise passed the beech turn and was gone.

For half a minute the road still kept the noise of it — wheels over dry gravel, the creak of leather and jingle of harness, the postilion's call to the near horse — and then the wood took that too. The dust hung where the carriage had been. Then the dust settled.

A beautiful day. 

And now empty.

Darcy stood at the foot of his own steps with one glove in his hand and no recollection of having taken it off.

The boy had the bay by the bridle and was leading him round toward the stable road. Darcy rolled his shoulder and turned to mount the steps. Mrs. Reynolds was already emerging from the doorway.

She did not run. Mrs. Reynolds had not run in this century, and would have considered any report to the contrary an impertinence. But she crossed the portico and came down the first two steps faster than dignity strictly required. She caught herself on the third, and travelled with him back to the landing with her cap and her keys and her countenance all restored to office.

She was a small, upright woman, near sixty. She had been at Pemberley nearly thirty years. She had taken his first fall from the great chestnut as a private grievance against the chestnut, and held it still. In the year after his mother died, he had known Mrs. Reynolds's face better than almost any woman's. The same brown eyes — quick, judicious, devoted — held him pinned now.

"I beg your pardon, sir." She folded her hands at her waist. "Had I known you were so near arriving, I should never have taken a party through the house this morning."

She glanced down the long drive, squinting. "And the others?”

"Monday."

"Of course." She glanced back at him, measuring more than his answer.

"You could not know," he said. "No harm was done."

Her face eased. There was the matter of the Monday arrivals to dispose of, then. She named them as they crossed the portico: the blue chamber for Miss Bingley, the Hursts' old rooms, the chamber over the south door for Mr. Bingley, Mrs. Annesley's room aired and Miss Darcy's apartments prepared. He gave the briefest assent he could give without seeming inattentive.

He knew he did not fool her.

Having recovered her composure, she turned briskly to the business of the house.

"The younger lady was much taken with the library, sir. Which puts me in mind — those books you sent for last spring have come in. There were so many parcels I had them set in the library rather than carried up. I did not think you would wish your desk so heaped."

The younger lady.

His mind supplied the name before he could prevent it. Elizabeth.

He shut it away at once, but not before Mrs. Reynolds paused half a breath — not long enough to be impertinent — and then went on as if she had seen nothing.

"Thank you, Mrs. Reynolds."

"Very good, sir."

Inside, the hall held the cool of the stone and a faint beeswax scent. Mrs. Reynolds walked half a pace behind him, still a little out of breath and too well trained to admit it.

"Mr. Greaves will be glad to hear you are come down early, sir," she said. "He had wondered, with so large a party expected, when he should have his half-hour with you. The estate papers are already in your office."

Darcy glanced at the old walnut longcase clock at the far end of the hall. "Send to him for three o'clock."

"Very good, sir." She paused another half a breath, taking in what he had not said, and then moved on. "A cold tray in the small parlor, then. I’ll see to it now."

The principal rooms were familiar as the order of his own name: gallery, library, morning room, the private stair that led toward the family apartments.

Miss Elizabeth Bennet had passed through some of those rooms not two hours before. She had stood where his mother had stood. She had looked at his books. She had heard his housekeeper speak of him.

He did not turn his head toward any of it. He went directly through to his dressing-room.

Already, someone had set out water, towels, a fresh shirt. Hodges, an under-butler doing the office of valet. Watkins had remained with the rest of the household and would not reach Pemberley until Monday. Darcy let himself be unbuttoned and helped out of his coat. He sluiced cold water across his face and the back of his neck. The gravel-smell of the road slid off in a single grey rinse. He scrubbed the dust from his hands. He scrubbed his right hand twice.

It was not the dust.

A lady accepting assistance into a carriage; a gentleman giving it. An interval so exact that no bystander could have put a name to it. No gentleman, afterwards, had the right to do so.

His palm remembered it.

Inconvenient. He had spent months teaching himself not to think of her. Reinforced the lesson over the past long days on the road. The lesson had gone to nothing in five minutes on the gravel.

Hodges had a clean shirt over his head before he had finished considering the inconvenience. By the time he was buttoned again, a stranger looking at him would have seen only a gentleman newly come in from a short ride; nothing of three days' travel, and nothing of the catastrophe five minutes on the gravel had made of him.

He did not look in the glass.

The estate office was on the ground floor near the steward's door, a small paneled room with shutters half-drawn against the heat. He took the back steps down. Mr. Greaves's first batch of papers waited on the blotter, held by the silver weight. Hedges, leases, repairs, accounts. 

This was work he could set his mind to.

Darcy sat in his father's chair — broad, square, and uncushioned, the arms worn smooth where three generations of Darcys had set their hands. The top sheet concerned the east pasture and the man with the Herefords.

He read the first paragraph. He read the second. Mr. Greaves's dry, slanting hand had not altered in twenty years: *The tenant being desirous of extending the let to Michaelmas…*

He knew the sentence. He had read its brothers all his adult life.

He took up the pen and wrote beneath the line: *Renewed to Michaelmas.*

Three words. A black line. A decision in the world. Easy.

He set the pen down and drew the next sheet toward him.

At three, Mr. Greaves came in with a folio under his arm. A thin, grey man in a perfectly brushed coat and immaculate linen, he had been steward at Pemberley for nearly all of Darcy's father's time. Twelve years a widower. Not a sentimental man, but an exact one. Darcy had inherited him as he had inherited the house and the silver. As with the house and the silver, he was sometimes uncertain whether Greaves served him, or only continued, as the rest of the furniture continued, in his service.

If Greaves had heard there had been visitors in the house that morning, he gave no sign of it. Visitors belonged to Mrs. Reynolds's province; hedges and leases belonged to his.

They worked through the papers.

The Hartside boundary, where an old hedge had been permitted to argue with a new ditch. The mortar wanting work on the stable wall. Adamson leaving the home farm for his brother's place near Sheffield, and deserving, Greaves thought, a recommendation if Mr. Darcy saw no objection. The east pasture. More about the Herefords. The south drive.

Darcy answered. He signed where he was to sign. He gave leave where leave was wanted. He wrote Adamson's character himself, refused the proposed delay on the stable wall, and approved the new gate at the Hartside boundary, which had been wanting these three years.

How far was it to Lambton?

The thought came in between one sheet and the next, with no obvious provocation beyond Greaves's mentioning the Lambton road in another connection. Five miles, give or take. He had ridden it as a boy, as a young man, as an adult last summer with his sister. Five miles was nothing on horseback; a small excursion by carriage. It was not so far that one could not call. It was not so near that calling was inevitable.

Did he dare?

He set his hand flat on the desk while Greaves moved to the next sheet. The signet ring caught the light.

She had no affection for him. He had her own word for that, given in April, in a parlor in Kent, in language he could repeat to himself word by knife-edged word. To present himself at the inn at Lambton merely because she happened to be staying there — to send a note, to send a basket, to send anything — was to require her to receive him with a civility she might not feel. It was the imposition of an acquaintance she had renounced. 

It approached, at least in spirit, the offense she had named at Hunsford.

But that touch.

Her hand had rested in his for the space required by courtesy. Perhaps a shade longer. He could not, in justice, build a single thing upon it. A lady did not refuse the assistance of a gentleman handing her into a carriage. She could not, without insult to him and a public scene, have done other than place her hand where she had placed it. The interval had been the interval prescribed by good manners. It signified nothing.

It signified only that she had not drawn back. It was nothing. It was less than that. 

But it was more than he had had a month ago.

He permitted himself the single word, *hope*. And then took it back, because he had no right to it.

Mr. Gardiner, though, he might write to at once. The freedom of the Pemberley water was a courtesy from gentleman to gentleman, and required no sister's countenance. 

The ladies were another matter. He could not invite Mrs. Gardiner and her niece to so much as a luncheon under his own roof. Georgiana must come down on Monday to issue the invitation in her own person. That was the form. He would observe it, even if it cost him three days.

But what if the Gardiners planned to depart before Monday?

"Sir?" said Mr. Greaves.

Darcy looked up. Greaves had a sheet held out, waiting.

"I beg your pardon.” He touched his temple. “The road, I think. Read it again."

Greaves read it. Darcy heard one sentence in three. He nodded where Greaves's voice paused for nodding, and said *yes* twice, and was, by some grace of inheritance, not understood to have been elsewhere.

The meeting ran past its half-hour. Darcy did not draw it to a close. He was grateful for each new sheet. Each sheet asked a question with a possible answer.

And not one of them required courage.

Mr. Greaves closed the folio at last. He stood, with the aid of the chair’s armrest. "I shall have the men on the stable wall Monday, sir, unless you prefer it delayed while the family is in the house."

"Monday. The wall will not mend itself out of respect for visitors."

Greaves's lips pursed, which in Greaves was laughter. "Very good, sir."

When he had gone, the room enlarged around the empty chair.

On the desk, a blank sheet lay before him. He drew it nearer. *Mr. Gardiner — the freedom of the Pemberley water — tackle to be sent down — a basket from the gardens, with the housekeeper's compliments to the ladies.* *Surely, you are staying the week?* He composed it three times in his head. He did not yet take up the pen.

Tomorrow would do better. Tomorrow the note would have the look of intention rather than impulse. He would not go himself. Nor Monday. On Tuesday he would call at the inn with Georgiana and Mrs. Annesley, precisely as form required. His sister would be introduced to Miss Bennet. Mrs. Annesley would sit beside her. The visit would last the proper length of time. He would say what civility required and not one word beyond his command.

He arranged these determinations in his mind as a man sets stones across a stream in flood.

The absurdity was that it was he who had built the stream.

By evening, the heat had gone out of the glass and nearly out of the wood. The house settled and the sounds softened. A tray set down below. A soft step in the passage. A door latched at the far end of the corridor. The clock on the mantel performing its office with the same grave indifference it had shown to every private disaster since his grandfather's day.

He ate again in the small parlor because Mrs. Reynolds had arranged it, and because not eating would have set the household worrying. She brought in a plain dinner herself, saw that the wine was what it should be, and stood a moment longer than the placing of dishes required.

"Mrs. Annesley's room is aired, sir, and the linen done. The green chamber is in readiness, should Mr. Bingley come up before Monday. Robert has set out the silver as you prefer it."

"Thank you."

"Will you take coffee in the library, sir?"

"No, brandy in the blue sitting-room, thank you."

She inclined her head and left.

He had been listening for the thing she did not say. Whether the young lady had frowned at his portrait, laughed at Mrs. Reynolds's praise, looked bored among his books, looked relieved to leave. Mrs. Reynolds had told him none of it. Of course not.

That was the part he could not pardon himself — that he had wanted to ask.

He did not, at once, go up to his sitting-room. He turned the other way, through the hall and up to the gallery.

Beyond the branched candlestick he carried, the long room was swathed in summer dusk. The portraits hung as they had hung all his life. His grandfather, in court dress, glowering at the painter. His great-aunt with her spaniel, painted twice because the first attempt had not flattered the dog. His mother in white, twenty and not yet anyone's mother. So like Georgiana. His father, three years before he died, the new hunting dog at his feet and a younger man's face still upon him. Darcy paused before each.

He came at last to himself.

He had not chosen to stand for it. He had thought it absurd at the time and thought so still. He had been merely five-and-twenty. The painter had been an obligation acquired through his aunt. The sittings had cost him three entire afternoons.

He had grudged them — until the third. That afternoon, Georgiana had come in, mid-sitting, on some small errand of her own, and stood at his shoulder to ask the painter a serious question about the brush. He had turned his head to her and answered her, what, he could not now recall. Only that she had laughed, and he had felt his own mouth move with hers.

The painter had been quick.

There it was now, by candlelight: a quirk, asmall expression at the corner of the mouth that did not belong to the rest of the face. The likeness was respectable. The expression was not for company.

Mrs. Reynolds was fond of the picture. She must have brought Miss Bennet up before it this morning. He could imagine, with painful distinctness, what the housekeeper had said: that he was the sweetest-tempered, the most generous-hearted master in the world. He could imagine, with painful distinctness, what Miss Bennet had not said. *I have known this gentleman in two aspects, and neither of them was this one.*

Mrs. Reynolds was fond of the picture. She must have brought Miss Bennet up before it this morning. He could imagine, with painful distinctness, what the housekeeper had said: that he was the sweetest-tempered, the most generous-hearted master in the world. He could imagine, with painful distinctness, what Miss Bennet had not said. *That is not the gentleman I knew.*

He turned away.

He left the gallery and went down to the library. The shutters were not closed; the tall west windows held the last of the light. The shelves stood as he had arranged them himself, two summers ago. On the second shelf of the south wall, the new books — perhaps thirty of them — lay flat in neat stacks, still in their paper boards, some with their pages uncut.

He set his hand on the topmost stack and let it rest there. She had stood in this room. She had, surely, looked over the titles. What had she seen?

He would not imagine it.

In the blue sitting-room, the lamp on the small writing desk illuminated the tray with the brandy and a small plate covered with a white napkin. The rest of the room stood in the half-dark, too familiar to distract him and too quiet to be merciful.

He crossed to the window that overlooked the sweep and the beginning of the south drive. Outside, the gravel sweep had faded to a pale blur, and the beginning of the south drive disappeared into trees. 

The lamp behind him transformed the glass into a poor mirror. His face, thinly imposed on Pemberley: brow, mouth, the line beside the mouth deeper than it had been in April. Giving nothing away.

So he hoped.

He set his hand on the window-frame.

Under his palm, beneath the warmth of polished oak, a whisper of the other pressure remained: exact, light, accusing. *Hers.*

He would write to Mr. Gardiner in the morning. He would offer the freedom of the water and send tackle if Mr. Gardiner wished it. He would send the basket. He would not ride to Lambton.

On Monday, the house would fill. On Tuesday, he would call at the inn with his sister. He would introduce them. He would conduct himself as a gentleman conducts himself toward the visiting relations of an acquaintance.

After Tuesday, he would not seek her out.
