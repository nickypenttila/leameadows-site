# BSP — Chapter 24: The West Stable

✅ Chapter complete

---

Pemberley had survived the arrival of Mr. Bennet and Kitty without any outward sign of distress. No servant looked alarmed; no chair had been displaced; no ancient Darcy had stepped from a portrait to object. Yet by ten o'clock the next morning, Elizabeth suspected the house had begun, in its quiet Derbyshire way, to reconsider some of its arrangements.

Breakfast did not break up so much as divide itself. Georgiana had spoken of the foal and the kittens with a banked excitement that made refusal impossible; Kitty had received the intelligence as if Pemberley had been built for no other purpose; and Mrs. Gardiner, after one glance at Mr. Bennet and another at Mrs. Annesley, had declared that the young ladies might surely be trusted to admire young animals under suitable guidance.

Suitable guidance, Elizabeth soon discovered, meant herself.

Her father, Mrs. Gardiner, and Mrs. Annesley remained at the table over coffee, the cups drawn nearer, their voices lowered into that tone by which grown people pretend they are discussing nothing of consequence. Kitty and Georgiana had gone to exchange slippers for half-boots; Elizabeth, detained by the last of her coffee and already clad appropriately, heard only enough to understand the subject — *comfortable arrangements*, *a few days more*, *young ladies together*. At Longbourn, such a discussion would have been shouted through three doors and answered by the person least concerned. At Pemberley, it advanced by degrees, folded into civil sentences and set down beside the coffee-pot.

Kitty knew nothing of this. Kitty knew only that Miss Darcy had said the kittens might be seen after breakfast.

"Are we truly to go?" she asked when they were collected in the hall, her bonnet tied too loosely and one glove still unbuttoned. "I mean, if it is not troublesome. It is not troublesome, is it? Only Miss Darcy said Robinson said—"

“I did say it,” Georgiana said. “And I thought—I hoped—you might call me Georgiana. Robinson told my brother they were old enough to be taken up, if one were careful.”

"Then we shall be careful," said Kitty with great solemnity. She looked down at her gloves as if care might be found among the buttons. "Lizzy, am I straight?"

"Perfect," said Elizabeth.

Kitty made a face at her, then remembered where she was and tried to turn the expression into composure. Georgiana saw the attempt and looked away, but the corner of her mouth betrayed her.

That small betrayal did more for Kitty than any praise could have done. Her shoulders settled. 

They went out by the side door, where a groom had been sent to show the way. The air came warm and clean across the yard, carrying the smell of cut grass and something sharper beneath it — horse, leather, the sun already laying its hand upon the stone.

The west stable was stone-built and orderly, handsome without the least ambition to impress.  Its broad doors stood open on a cool passage, a dark line of stalls, brass hooks, rubbed leather, fresh straw, and brooms set ready for the next use. Swallows cut low under the eaves. Somewhere a horse struck one hoof against wood, and the sound carried through the open door to fade among the walls of the yard.

Kitty stopped as they crossed the gravel. "It is so much larger than our stable at Longbourn."

Georgiana considered this. "I suppose it must be."

"Oh! I did not mean — only everything here is larger than Longbourn. Except Mama's voice. Nothing is larger than that."

Kitty's mouth closed with a little click, as if she had tried to catch the words after they had escaped. Georgiana's composure slipped — not quite lost, not quite held.

Elizabeth, walking a little behind them, looked down at the gravel and trusted the morning air to arrange her countenance.

"My aunt's voice," Georgiana said, after a moment's consideration, "is not loud, but it does travel."

Kitty turned to her. "Lady Catherine?"

Georgiana nodded.

Kitty considered this with great seriousness. “Lizzy once said Lady Catherine could make a compliment sound like an order and an order sound like a sermon.”

“Did she?” Georgiana asked. She glanced back at Elizabeth.

Excellent. “Kitty,” she said warningly.

“But you did say it.”

“I did not expect to be quoted in Derbyshire.”

Georgiana’s answer came after a moment, small but certain. “It is an apt description.”

Kitty turned back long enough to give Elizabeth one triumphant lift of the brows.

She had spent years correcting Kitty: lowering a voice, checking a step, smoothing a folly before it could catch someone else's notice. She had called this sisterly duty and had not always been wrong. 

But correction, even when necessary, had a way of filling a room before the corrected person could enter it. Here, with Georgiana beside her, Kitty was not being corrected. She was being received, imperfectly and with interest.

Elizabeth's hand closed at her side. She did not know why that should sting. She only knew it did — a quick, unreasonable tightness, gone before she could name it, as if she had been shown that something she had carried for years could have been lighter all along.

Just inside the entrance to the west stable they found Mr. Darcy.

He stood inside the open doorway of the first loose box, one hand resting on the top rail, his head turned toward Robinson, who was speaking in the low, practical tones of a man whose whole life had been spent among creatures that did not take kindly to noise. Behind them, the mare stood in the deeper straw, her dark head lowered toward something hidden by the rail, Mr. Darcy’s shadow, and the angle of her own body. One ear turned back and forward; the straw beneath her shifted once, softly.

Mr. Darcy had not heard them at once. Or, having heard, he had not turned before Robinson finished. His sleeves were rolled to the forearm; his coat hung from one of the brass hooks near the door; and there was straw caught in the seam of one boot. His hat was in his hand. He looked less like the formal master of a great house than like a man to whom every living thing in it might, by one route or another, become a responsibility.

The thought came too near admiration. Elizabeth made herself attend to what the mare had been guarding.

It was a ridiculous creature. Its legs appeared to have been designed for some taller animal and delivered by mistake; the knees all knob and angle, the narrow lengths below them too delicate for the undertaking, the hooves no larger than teacups and set down with great uncertainty in the straw. Its coat still had the soft, brushed-up look of something not yet finished, darker along the spine and pale where the morning light found the fine hair at its neck. It smelled faintly of warm milk and clean straw, with the sharper animal scent of the mare behind it.

The foal shifted one foot, then another, and seemed surprised to discover both belonged to him. A small breath came through its nose — not quite a snort, not quite a sigh — and the mare turned one ear back at once. Elizabeth had the absurd thought that if she laid a hand against its neck, she would feel the whole creature trembling with the effort of being alive.

It regarded the world from beneath a narrow blaze with all the suspicion of a young gentleman introduced too early to company.

Georgiana stopped only a foot from the door. Her hands clasped together.

Mr. Darcy turned then. At the sight of his sister, his face changed — a loosening around the eyes, a warmth that did not reach his mouth.

"Georgiana," he said. "You are come at the right time. Robinson tells me our new gentleman has stood only twice before and objected to both occasions."

"He does not look like a gentleman," Kitty said as she bumped up against Georgiana’s shoulder to see better. She put one gloved hand over her mouth. "I beg your pardon."

Robinson’s weathered face scarcely moved, but the lines beside his eyes deepened. Mr. Darcy looked back at the foal.

"True," he said gravely. "At present he looks like an arrangement of legs with an opinion."

Kitty’s gloved hand remained over her mouth, but the giggle escaped around it. Georgiana looked at her first in happy astonishment, as if she had not known such a thing could be said aloud; then amusement caught her too. She turned at once to her brother, not quite asking permission and not quite able to help herself.

"You may come nearer, if you like. Not too near the mare. She is patient, but patience is not consent."

The stable smelled of straw and warm animal and the faint medicinal sharpness of some wash Robinson had used. Dust moved in the light from the high window. The mare turned her head and examined them with one dark liquid eye, then dismissed them. The foal, in an attempt to place itself behind its mother, discovered that its hind legs had gone in two directions at once and needed a moment to find their way beneath him.

"Oh," Kitty whispered, all caution forgotten. "He is splendidly foolish."

Georgiana regarded the foal as if foolishness had been unexpectedly admitted among the virtues.

"He was born before dawn?" she asked.

"A little after three," Mr. Darcy said. "Robinson had the better part of the labor. I had the honor of being informed after all useful work was done."

"Sir did come down," Robinson said.

"To stand in the way with a lantern."

"To hold it steady," said Robinson, which, from his mouth, appeared to be a significant compliment.

Elizabeth pressed her thumbnail into the pad of her finger. A man might be praised at an assembly, admired in a drawing room, commended for fortune, consequence, manners, or person; but there was a different kind of knowledge in hearing a servant defend his usefulness where flattery would be absurd. 

“Many gentlemen would have held it less steadily,” Elizabeth said.

He turned his head toward her. "You set the standard low, Miss Bennet."

"I have four sisters, sir. I have seen lanterns, teacups, tempers, and entire schemes dropped under less provocation."

Kitty, who had bent closer to see the foal's narrow knees, said, "I never dropped a lantern."

"No," said Elizabeth. "You once dropped a candlestick."

"It was not lit. Or not by the time it reached the floor."

"For which we are all grateful."

Georgiana laughed again — quieter this time, but real. The mare shifted, her weight rocking the straw beneath her. Robinson lifted one hand, not sharply, only enough to remind them that the west stable was not a theater. Kitty slipped back at once.

"I am sorry," she said to the mare, not to Robinson.

Robinson's mouth twitched. "No harm done, miss."

Mr. Darcy’s attention returned to Kitty. An adjustment crossed his face — not the whole of his judgment revised, that would have been too easy, but one line of it moved, the way a pen is lifted and set down in a different place.

“The kittens are farther along the stable,” he said. “Robinson’s boy can show you. They are less easily offended than the mare, though I am told one has already formed a high opinion of his consequence.”

“That must be the large one,” Georgiana said, forgetting to be shy in her certainty. “Robinson said there was one larger than the rest.”

Kitty turned to her with full attention. "How much larger?"

"I do not know, exactly. Larger in manner, at least."

"Then we must see him before he becomes unbearable."

This time Georgiana's smile did not ask permission.

Mr. Darcy remained by the foal as they moved away. Elizabeth was conscious of leaving him — of the space opening between them, of the shift from the warm milky scent to cooler musty air in the passage — and then, after they passed into the box the boy led them to, she was not conscious of him at all.

The kittens had been given a wooden box lined with old sacking in a far corner, though their respect for its boundaries was already in question. Their mother, a striped barn cat of narrow face and austere principles, lay curled along one side and opened one yellow eye upon the visitors. Seeing no immediate incompetence, she closed it again. Five kittens lay in a heap against her belly, the whole arrangement at first glance indistinguishable from a small, breathing muff.

Then the heap moved.

Kitty was lost.

She went down upon her knees in the straw with no thought for her gown. Her face lit — a flush of pleasure so unguarded that Elizabeth had to look away from it, down at the straw, where chaff clung to the hem of her own skirt.

Georgiana lowered herself more carefully beside her, arranging her skirts in the manner of a young lady who had never been encouraged to sit on stable straw and had decided that the attempt required strategy. Kitty waited until she was settled before reaching toward the box.

"May we touch them?"

The groom, a boy of perhaps twelve who had been watching them with interest, said, "Robinson says gentle, miss. And mind the queen. She don't like surprises."

"The queen?" Georgiana repeated.

"Stable cat, miss."

Kitty considered the mother cat. "She does look as if she had a court."

The boy grinned and retreated, his duty discharged.

Kitty extended one finger. The mother cat opened her eye again. Kitty stopped. The two regarded one another with the mutual seriousness of sovereign and petitioner.

"You see," Kitty said to Georgiana in a low voice, "one must introduce oneself first. Cats are not like dogs. Dogs have already forgiven you for whatever you are about to do."

Georgiana bent nearer. "How do you introduce yourself to a cat?"

"Slowly. And without hope."

Elizabeth, standing behind them, felt her mouth pull. It was strange to see Kitty certain of anything. Stranger still to see Georgiana receiving instruction from her with such gravity.

The queen permitted Kitty's finger to approach, sniffed it thoroughly, then endured one touch upon the head with the expression of a sovereign tolerating a foreign ambassador. Georgiana, watching, copied the movement. The cat allowed it.

"Oh," Georgiana said. The sound was soft, private. Her fingertip rested on the cat's brow.

Kitty looked pleased with her success. "There. Now she knows you."

"Does she approve?"

"Not yet. But she has not dismissed us."

This distinction appeared to satisfy Georgiana.

The kittens, less invested in ceremony, began to wake. One gray head emerged; one black paw stretched and vanished. A pale creature rolled backward into its sibling and remained there, affronted. Then the largest kitten, a smoky gray with white paws and a chest far too grand for his size, climbed directly over two of the others and placed himself at the front of the box.

Kitty drew in a breath. "Oh, there he is."

Georgiana leaned closer. "That is the one."

"The tyrant?"

"I had not called him that."

"No, but he has."

The kitten opened his mouth. No sound came out. He tried again and produced a squeak that did not at all support his pretensions.

Kitty pressed her lips together. "Poor thing. He believes himself a lion and has been betrayed by his voice."

Georgiana gasped. "He does not know."

"Of course he knows. That is why he is angry."

The kitten, proving it, shoved one paw into the face of the smallest tabby and attempted to climb the side of the box. His back feet found no purchase. He slid down backward into the heap. The pale kitten, now beneath him, objected with a more convincing squeak.

Kitty looked at Georgiana with solemn concern. "We must intervene. He is trampling his subjects."

Georgiana's mouth trembled. Her fingers curled in her lap, and she bent nearer the box as if the kitten required all her attention.

Kitty turned back to the box and lowered her voice into the grave tone she had used, Elizabeth remembered, when judging quarrels between the Gardiner children.

“Miss Darcy—Georgiana. I believe this is a matter for law.”

"Law?"

"Certainly. We cannot permit one kitten to climb upon all the rest merely because he has wider paws."

Georgiana's eyes went wide with attention. "What law applies?"

Kitty considered. "The law against being unbearable."

"Is there such a law?"

"There ought to be.” She looked back. “Lizzy, is there?"

Elizabeth folded her hands behind her. "If there were, half the country would be transported."

Kitty nodded. "Then we must begin modestly. With kittens."

Georgiana bent over the box. The tyrant had recovered and was now attempting to seize Kitty's glove. Kitty let him catch one finger and shook it with great delicacy, greeting a very small and badly mannered gentleman.

"Sir," she said, "your conduct has been observed."

The kitten bit her glove.

"Ah. No remorse."

Georgiana squeaked.

"He must be named," Kitty said. "One cannot reform a creature without naming him. What is the proudest name you know?"

Georgiana looked up, startled by the question. Elizabeth kept her eyes on the kittens and her thoughts to herself.

Kitty, who did not wait long for answers, said, "At Longbourn we would call him General, because Lydia thinks every officer must be obeyed and Mary thinks every general ought to be studied. But he is too small for General."

"Colonel?" Georgiana offered.

Elizabeth's gaze did not move. The word had brushed near too many histories, but not the dangerous one. Colonel Fitzwilliam might have laughed at such promotion.

The kitten planted one white paw on the edge of the box, swayed with great consequence, and then sat down hard upon his brother. "No. A colonel would have better balance."

"Captain?"

At last, the kitten succeeded in climbing over the side of the box, landed shoulder-first in the straw, shook its head against the indignity of the ground, and marched away from his brothers and sisters.

Kitty caught him with both hands. "Captain is too generous. He has deserted his men."

Georgiana peered at the kitten, which made a blind, indignant pass at her nose.

"Then you must take charge of him," Kitty said, and settled him carefully in Georgiana’s lap. "He respects you more."

"I do not think he respects anyone."

"That is why he must practice."

Georgiana curved herself around him. The tyrant, discovering himself enthroned, ceased fighting and began to purr with an authority far beyond his size.

"Oh," Georgiana said, in quite another voice.

The pleasure in it was so naked that Elizabeth turned away.

She had not meant to look for Mr. Darcy.

Nevertheless, she looked.

He had left Robinson and the foal and was walking toward them, his coat retrieved and slung over one arm, his pace unhurried. The light from the yard fell behind him, touching the rolled linen at his forearms and the dark hair loosened slightly by the stable air; and Elizabeth, who had been admiring his conduct, was startled to find herself admiring the man.

Elizabeth stepped back from the girls before he reached her. Her pulse was doing something unnecessary.

Mr. Darcy stopped beside her — not quite so near as he might have done, and not so far as prudence required.

For some seconds neither spoke. Kitty was explaining to Georgiana that the kitten must be held against one's bodice since he considered himself a person of rank, because persons of rank always expected support. Georgiana listened with an attention no music-master had ever commanded.

As Georgiana bent over the kitten, Mr. Darcy’s face altered—not subtly this time, and not quickly hidden. The stern lines of his mouth softened; the care about his eyes eased. For a moment, Elizabeth saw, without disguise, how dearly his sister was loved.

"Your sister has a talent for command," he said.

Kitty was rescuing the pale kitten from being sat upon. "Only where kittens and children are concerned. We do not yet know whether it extends to persons of reason."

"That may be an advantage. Persons of reason are often less easily improved."

She turned back to him and found his attention not on her, but on Kitty. No mockery touched the words. If anything, they were spoken with the gravity of a man who had spent too many years attempting to improve persons of reason and had found kittens a more promising field.

"She likes to be useful," Elizabeth said.

It was not praise, exactly, and for that reason it cost her more.

"That she is."

No compliment. No flourish. He gave the words exactly as much weight as they could bear, and no more.

She had defended Kitty often enough; she had not known how much she had needed another person to see what she defended. The admission — even silent, even only to herself — left her exposed in a way she did not care for.

The tyrant kitten, dissatisfied with rank when it required stillness, attempted to climb Georgiana's sleeve. Kitty intervened.

"You cannot be a captain," she told him. "You have no discipline."

Georgiana's head bent over the kitten. Her shoulders shook once.

Elizabeth felt it land in him — a stillness that deepened rather than broke.

"Mrs. Gardiner spoke yesterday of the Lambton fair."

The change of subject was so abrupt that Elizabeth might have missed its meaning had she not been standing close enough to catch the care beneath it.

"She remembers it fondly," Elizabeth said.

"I believe it is held later this week.” His eyes remained on Georgiana and Kitty. "It is not large. There is some livestock, a few stalls, more ribbons than any county can require, and enough noise to make one appreciate the road home."

Elizabeth smiled before she could stop herself. "An irresistible description."

"I did not intend to recommend it too highly."

"No. That would be rash."

He took the rebuke, if it was one, with a slight inclination of the head. "It may be too much. Georgiana has not cared for it before,” he said. “But I am not certain that before is a useful guide.”

Elizabeth did not answer at once.

There had been a time, not many days ago, when such a sentence would have vexed her. Now she heard in it not refusal, but the effort to reconsider an old certainty.

“Then perhaps she may wish to be asked,” she said.

He turned to her then, and whatever he had meant to say seemed, for a moment, to have deserted him.

Not long. Long enough. She felt the warmth of it on her face like sun through a window, and looked away.

"I shall ask her," he said.

He had permitted the kittens. He would ask about the fair. He was learning, not to become careless, but to let joy make a claim. Elizabeth, standing beside him with her hands clasped too tightly behind her back, understood that the proof she had wanted was here, and that it asked something of her she was not yet ready to name.

A squeak of protest came from the straw. The tyrant had escaped Georgiana's lap, conquered Kitty's knees, and placed his front paws upon the ribbon that hung from her bonnet.

"Sir," Kitty cried, "that is not yours."

The kitten, unmoved by property law, pulled.

Georgiana reached to help. The ribbon gave way in Kitty's hand; the kitten tumbled backward into the straw with the air of a commander betrayed by his own cavalry. Kitty made a sound of outrage so perfectly proportioned to the size of the offense that Georgiana lost all command of herself.

She laughed.

Not prettily. Not properly. Happily.
