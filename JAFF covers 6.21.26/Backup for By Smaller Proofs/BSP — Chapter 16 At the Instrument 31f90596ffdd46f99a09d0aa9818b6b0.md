# BSP — Chapter 16: At the Instrument

An invitation to dinner followed, written with that exact propriety which makes a favor look like the natural consequence of good manners. The Gardiners had been delighted beyond all prudence; they had meant to show Elizabeth Derbyshire’s walks, prospects, and humbler beauties. But they had never once imagined they might carry her, by invitation, into one of the finest houses in the county. 

Elizabeth had been pleased too—pleased, and a little quieted by the ease with which everything had been settled, as if the day’s unexpected meeting had not altered the world so much as she had feared.

Dinner itself had been, in every sense, divine. Abundant without parade, attentive without officiousness, and conducted with such general good behavior that even Miss Bingley’s civilities could be received without owing her any gratitude. Even the Hursts, whom Elizabeth had not yet seen since Netherfield and whom her aunt and uncle had never met at all, contrived a worn civility—Mrs. Hurst pale and languid, Mr. Hurst heavy-eyed and dutifully silent. Both were so evidently reduced by recent illness that the most exquisite course of dinner seemed to find them without any stomach to honor it. 

If there was constraint, it wore its Sunday face; if there was observation, it kept behind the comfort of candles and well-timed questions. They rose from table with the agreeable fatigue of people who had eaten well and been determined to be pleased with one another.

Afterward, they were shown into the room with the pianoforte.

Elizabeth knew it at once, though evening had altered it. On Saturday, Mrs. Reynolds had called it the music room, and Elizabeth had crossed its threshold as a tourist: free to admire the view of the lawn, the little writing-desk near the window, the fine instrument with Mozart open upon its stand, and then to pass on before any object could ask anything of her. 

Tonight the same room had been made ready for people expected to sit, observe, and be observed. The curtains were drawn back from the tall windows, but the glass had gone black except where candles made duplicate flames upon it. The lawn beyond was no longer a prospect. It was a depth.

The pianoforte stood where she remembered it, angled a little toward the room as well as the windows, its mahogany case catching the candlelight in long polished gleams. The music stand held a tidy stack of sheets rather than the single open page she had seen before. Someone had placed two small candles near it, each with a bright brass shield to throw the light downward upon the keys. Their light made the ivory look whiter than it had by day and made the black keys shine like little bars of sealing wax. The scent of fading rose leaves mingled with the clean wool of the thick carpet underfoot.

The room was grand, but not with the dining-room’s public magnificence. Its elegance was more artful: pale walls, white lower paneling, curtains figured with leaves, and chairs arranged so that music might pretend to be conversation while everyone had an excellent view of the performer. A harp stood beyond the pianoforte, gilded, elegant, and neglected for the evening, its strings catching the light so finely that they seemed less fitted for sound than for display. Near the window, the writing-desk was bare, its inkwell capped. The evening was for the people in the room, not those far away.

Elizabeth's glove brushed the back of the carved elbow-chair Georgiana had indicated for her. The wood was smooth from use rather than new polish; its worn edge had the look of something touched often and without ceremony. The silk of her gown rustled faintly against the cushion. Mr. and Mrs. Gardiner sat to her left, closer to the fire in its pristine white frame, but with their chairs turned enough toward the instrument to make refusal of music impossible.

The lush carpet captured the sound of footsteps almost completely, so that people seemed to arrive in fragments: Mr. Hurst's shoe at the hearth as he settled on the sofa at an angle to the fire and the pianoforte, in visible gratitude for both; Mrs. Hurst’s soft sigh as she joined him, graceful and pale; Miss Bingley's fan opening once with a neat dry snap; Mr. Bingley's low laugh before he had finished crossing the room; the small chime of Mrs. Gardiner's bracelets as she settled her hands in her lap.

Miss Bingley chose one of the remaining chairs with an air of perfect accident, though it left her admirably placed to address either Miss Darcy or her brother. She seemed to have recovered completely. There remained only a fineness about her mouth and a brightness in her eyes that made any lingering illness look less like weakness than like an advantage she had no intention of wasting.

Mr. Bingley's good humor had survived the table, and was harder to resent than indifference would have been. "And Miss Bennet is well?" he had asked at dinner, coloring as if he had named one lady too particularly in the presence of seven witnesses and a roast. Elizabeth had answered as Jane would have answered: "Very well, I thank you." The answer had been both true and insufficient. At the far end of the table Mr. Darcy had looked down at his glass. 

Elizabeth was not disposed to pity him.

Mr. Bingley chose the last cushion on the sofa with the Hursts, half-turned toward the instrument and wholly ready to be pleased.

Mr. Darcy stood by the mantel, one shoulder turned a little from the fire. The light caught the strong line of his profile and left the rest of his face in reserve; he looked neither easy nor inattentive, but as if nothing in the room were likely to escape him.

Miss Darcy started to take the chair between Elizabeth and Miss Bingley, but was interrupted.

"Georgiana," said Miss Bingley, "you *must* play. Our friends have not yet heard you properly."

Properly.

Elizabeth had not known a word could curtsy and command in the same syllable.

Miss Darcy smoothed one hand over her gown. "If anyone wishes it."

"My dear girl, everyone wishes it." Miss Bingley looked around the room with the confidence of a person collecting consent from those who had not offered it. Mrs. Hurst inclined her head. Mr. Bingley smiled encouragement. Mr. Hurst inspected the fire as if music might be endured at that distance. "You cannot imagine that we should allow such an instrument to remain silent."

Miss Darcy's color rose.

Mr. Darcy moved one hand along the mantel, and then stilled it.

"I should be very glad to hear anything you wish to play, Miss Darcy," said Mrs. Gardiner, calm, kind. "But only if she is inclined."

Miss Darcy’s look toward Elizabeth’s aunt was both grateful and not relieved enough.

"I shall play," she said.

Elizabeth disliked Miss Bingley a little more for making the words necessary.

Miss Darcy went to the pianoforte, pale gown, pale hands, and all Pemberley's expectation gathered between her and the keys. Earlier, she had received them in the hall with courage enough to cross it first; she had answered Mrs. Gardiner at dinner on the subject of London masters and Derbyshire practice; she had even smiled when Mr. Gardiner declared that the masters had the worse bargain. Elizabeth had treasured that smile absurdly. 

Here, with the candles set to display her hands and every chair turned toward the instrument, that smile looked in danger.

At the music stand, Miss Darcy made one small halt. Most people would not have seen it. Elizabeth saw it because the girl's hand, which had reached toward a sheet of music, came back and touched the edge of the pianoforte instead, as if the wood were safer than the choice.

Mr. Darcy saw it. His hand, which had been resting on the mantel, closed once and opened again.

"Miss Bennet," he said, with a politeness that made the request appear general rather than urgent, "as you are nearest, would you be so good as to assist my sister with the pages, if she wishes it?"

Miss Darcy looked quickly from her brother to Elizabeth. "If Miss Bennet does not mind."

Elizabeth rose at once. "Not in the least."

Standing beside Miss Darcy, she saw the instrument differently. The candles, so pretty from the chairs, made a little stage of the keys; the music was no longer ornament but demand; every face in the semicircle had become a witness.

The first piece was something of Handel’s, grave and graceful. Miss Darcy played with great delicacy, with flashes of quiet brilliance. There was a sureness in her touch when she forgot the room, and a slight constraint whenever she remembered it. Elizabeth listened with more attention than judgment. She had often been ashamed of Mary's performances because Mary performed as if improvement were a duty owed to the neighborhood. Miss Darcy played as if accomplishment had been placed in her hands long ago, and pleasure somehow omitted from the lesson.

When the piece ended, there was the usual praise. Mr. Bingley's was immediate, warm and imprecise. Mr. Gardiner's was sensible. Mrs. Gardiner's was kindly. Mr. Hurst's was inaudible and perhaps digestive. Miss Bingley's was the most elegant and the least useful.

"Charming, Georgiana. Quite charming. Though you know I always think that piece scarcely does you justice. You have such command when you choose to exert it."

Miss Darcy's fingers closed upon one another in her lap.

Elizabeth, standing beside her, felt her own hand tighten on the edge of the music stand.

Elizabeth pictured Kitty at the old square pianoforte at Longbourn, striking a wrong note and laughing before Mary could instruct her. Kitty had no command whatever. Kitty had noise, complaint, ribbons, loyalty when not led astray, and a sort of playfulness that made her parents smile and forget to scold her. And, lately, courage.

"You promised me the duet," Miss Bingley said.

Miss Darcy's fingers tightened again. She lifted her eyes. "Did I?"

"Did you not? Or if not a promise, something very like it. Louisa, you remember the duet we played in town?"

Mrs. Hurst, whose loyalty was weakened by headache but not extinguished, said, "Very pretty."

"Very pretty, and very much admired," said Miss Bingley. "Georgiana has the principal part perfectly, if only she will not be so timid. I am certain Miss Bennet will forgive us a little display among friends."

Among friends.

Elizabeth felt the phrase arrive and arrange the room around itself. Friends did not compel. Friends did not make refusal costly. Friends, she thought, did not require a girl to prove she deserved their admiration before she might have their ease.

Miss Darcy's hand moved among the pages with more haste than certainty. "I am not sure I recollect it well enough." The sheet of music she sought did not appear.

"Nonsense. You recollect everything once you are seated." Miss Bingley rose. "You may play the second part, if you prefer. It will be nothing—only a little thing."

Only a little thing, said by someone who had already made it too large.

Mr. Bingley shifted in his chair. "Perhaps Georgiana has played enough for one evening."

Miss Bingley smiled at him. "Charles, you are always for sparing everyone every exertion. It is very amiable, but it would leave us with no music, no dancing, no conversation, and no improvement of any kind."

"I am always in favor of conversation," said Mr. Bingley.

Mrs. Hurst laughed faintly, then checked herself with a soft cough into her handkerchief. "Caroline is right. Miss Darcy plays so well that it would be a pity to hide it."

A pity to hide it.

Miss Darcy's gaze dropped to her lap.

Elizabeth saw it then, and anger came too quickly for politeness. Her teeth set; before she could prevent it, her eyes had gone from face to face around the room. The girl was not being asked to play. She was being asked to submit to being admired. There was a difference, and every person in the room who chose not to see it was guilty of some small cruelty. When Elizabeth looked toward the mantel, she found Mr. Darcy very still, his gaze fixed upon Miss Bingley with an expression that wanted only a little less civility to be a glare.

Miss Bingley had reached the piano bench. Her hand rested on the back of Miss Darcy's shoulder with an intimacy that belonged either to sisters or to conquest.

"Shall we begin at the allegro? It shows your hand to advantage."

Miss Darcy said nothing.

A second passed. Another.

Then Mr. Darcy spoke.

"No."

It was not loud. It did not need to be.

Miss Bingley's hand left the shoulder.

The room, which had seemed to lean toward the instrument, grew still.

Mr. Darcy took a step away from the mantel. His face had altered very little, but Elizabeth knew, with a certainty she could not have defended to anyone else, that he was angry.

"My sister has already obliged us very handsomely," he said. "If she wishes to play again, she will say so."

The correction was made before them all, and Elizabeth’s breath caught. The words were not severe; but they restored Miss Darcy to the place from which Miss Bingley had removed her: in possession of herself.

Miss Bingley recovered first. "Of course," she said lightly. "I would not press Georgiana for the world. You know my only fault is wishing to hear her too often."

Mr. Darcy bowed, and allowed the excuse to fail unaided.

That was new.

Elizabeth looked down at her hands to hide what might have been too plainly visible in her face. Gratitude was unsafe. Admiration was unsafe. The wish to laugh at Miss Bingley's defeat was, perhaps, the least dangerous of the three.

Miss Darcy remained at the instrument. Her hands touched the keys, too softly to make a sound.

"Thank you," she said.

It was impossible to tell whether the words were for her brother, for the room, or for the permission to stop. Elizabeth suspected Mr. Darcy knew and was pained by it. He returned to the mantel.

Then Miss Darcy drew another piece from the music pile. It looked impossible to Elizabeth even on the page. The pages were worn at the corners, penciled with small reminders that looked like prayers. She set it before her with a steadiness that seemed to be less ease than determination.

She began at once.

The piece was quick and bright, all order at the surface and difficulty beneath. Elizabeth had not sense enough for judgment; but she could hear the places where a hand might falter, and she could see that Miss Darcy did not. When the tricky passage arrived—a turn that seemed to require three hands and no fear—her fingers flew through it as if they had been waiting there all day.

By the fire, Mr. Darcy made a small sound—no word, no interruption, only a low, breathy “yes.”

Miss Darcy did not pause. Her eyes stayed on the page, intent. But a small smile escaped her, quick as the run itself, and then was gone again into concentration.

She finished with a neat, unhurried certainty—as if the last bar were not a release but a choice—and let her hands rest upon the keys a moment longer than necessary, giving the last chord its due.

Then she looked up, and her gaze went first, as it must, to her brother. The pleasure on his face answered her own; for a moment his gravity almost gave way. When Georgiana turned to Elizabeth, Elizabeth forgot every proper observation and could only breathe, very softly, “Well.” It was not clever; it was not even a sentence; but Miss Darcy seemed to understand it. She smiled—almost grinned—before recollecting herself and turning back to the others.

Mr. Gardiner was beaming. "Miss Darcy, may a very ignorant man ask whether that second piece was German? I have always suspected German music of being cleverer than I am."

The corner of Miss Darcy's mouth tilted up.

"Yes, you are right."

"Then I am safe. I have heard of Mozart."

Mr. Bingley brightened. "As have I, though I cannot pretend he has improved me."

This produced a small laugh from Miss Darcy—not much, but enough to set the conversation on safer ground. Mrs. Gardiner asked about the history of the music. Mr. Bingley applied himself to being pleased with everyone. Mr. Hurst closed his eyes and was either meditating upon art or sleeping with unusual refinement.

Miss Bingley retreated to her sister; their conference proceeded behind the fan and beneath the general conversation. Mrs. Hurst glanced toward the mantel and then back at Miss Bingley. She gently shook her head, and then reached a hand toward her temple, closing her eyes.

As Elizabeth seated herself again at Miss Darcy’s left, Mr. Darcy took the seat on his sister’s other side. He offered no apology, claimed no victory, and Miss Darcy, while answering Mrs. Gardiner, seemed easier for his being there.

Elizabeth had seen the man proud. She had seen him mortified. Now every polite gesture felt like a message she could not decipher.

She looked away.

At the hearth, the fire gave a brisk pop. Mr. Hurst, who had appeared to be meditating with his eyes shut, opened one of them with an expression of such grave reproach—as if the coal had addressed him with unbecoming familiarity—that Mr. Bingley was obliged to look very hard at the carpet.

Miss Darcy laughed—only a soft little sound—and then, as if startled by her own ease, pressed her lips together and looked down at her hands. Elizabeth thought, unexpectedly, of Kitty.

Kitty could never have played so beautifully; she would have been too loud, too certain of being wrong, and very likely to laugh first. But she would not have made Miss Darcy ashamed of wanting music to be easy.

The thought brought Longbourn suddenly near, so that Elizabeth almost missed Mrs. Gardiner speaking to her.

"Lizzy?"

"I beg your pardon."

Her aunt's eyes rested on her, warm and too observant. "We were wondering whether you would sing."

Elizabeth looked toward the pianoforte, then toward Miss Darcy, whose hands had quieted in her lap. The choice offered itself plainly enough.

Elizabeth rose.

"Yes," she said, "if Miss Darcy will be so kind as to choose something very easy, and if everyone promises to expect very little."

Mr. Bingley laughed at once. "That is the best promise ever asked of company. I make it with all my heart."

Miss Darcy looked at Elizabeth. Some uncertainty remained, but she was not shrinking anymore. Elizabeth crossed back to the instrument with her. Side by side, they sifted through the music.

"*Very* easy?" Miss Darcy asked.

"Disgracefully easy."

Miss Darcy smiled in earnest.

Elizabeth glanced beyond the music. Mr. Darcy was watching his sister; yet the warmth in his expression seemed, for one dangerous instant, to include the person who had made her smile.

Together, they chose the song.
