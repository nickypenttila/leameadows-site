# BSP — Chapter 28: At Dinner

✅ Complete

---

At dinner, every place was correct.

Darcy had come to depend upon that fact with a confidence so old it had ceased to be confidence at all. The table knew its business. The silver lay at its proper angles; the candles were trimmed; the servants moved without collision or display; each chair had been drawn out to receive the person for whom it was intended. Pemberley, when summoned to order, obeyed.

Miss Elizabeth Bennet did not.

She entered with Mrs. Gardiner and Miss Catherine a moment before Georgiana, and nothing in her conduct could have supplied an accusation. She was dressed for dinner. Her color was a little heightened. She spoke to Mrs. Annesley with composure, smiled when Kitty turned to ask whether she and Georgiana might attempt a duet after dinner, and took her place at table without mistaking chair, glass, or expectation.

There was no impropriety in her.

There was no ease.

Darcy stood until the ladies were seated and then took his own chair. At his left hand, Miss Elizabeth unfolded her napkin with hands that did not tremble. That was worse. If she had trembled, he might have known where to begin with concern. Instead there was only precision: the napkin laid across her lap, the glass touched and released, the glance that traveled past him across the table and found safe lodging with her aunt.

Not with him. She was close enough that he could hear the silk of her sleeve shift against the chair. He would have preferred distance.

This afternoon, he had asked Mrs. Gardiner whether the excursion to Lambton had over-fatigued her niece. He had tried to make the question what any host might ask. He had failed. Mrs. Gardiner's pause before she answered had told him so.

*Whether anything there had made Miss Bennet uneasy* — those had been his words, or near enough to them to condemn him. He had not been able to say less. He could not permit himself to say more.

At Lambton, he had understood immediately what the women at the stall implied. He had seen — or believed he had seen — Miss Elizabeth’s composure arrive a moment after it was needed. He had felt, in one hot instant of public exposure, the whole reckless shape of his own hope placed before her by strangers who had no right to touch it. 

Afterward he had done what propriety demanded. He had withdrawn. He had given her distance. He had placed propriety between them like a shield.

But perhaps such protection, in his hands, had looked like retreat.

"Mr. Darcy," Mrs. Gardiner said, "Georgiana tells me the west stable has acquired a new object of tyranny."

Georgiana colored at once. Kitty looked delighted.

"The stable, I understand," said Darcy, recalled to his duty, "has acquired a kitten of decided opinions. Whether tyranny is yet proved, I leave to those with greater experience of its government."

"Mr. Grayfur is certainly a tyrant," Kitty said. "He struck Mr. Bennet this afternoon."

Darcy looked blankly at her for a moment. Then he remembered.

"The wooden cow from the fair," Kitty added, though she need not have. He had bought the thing himself.

"Ah," said Mrs. Gardiner, with admirable gravity. "Then the assault was political."

Georgiana laughed softly, and Kitty recovered enough to give a full account of the offense: Mr. Grayfur had pushed the cow from a low shelf, not once but twice, and then sat upon the fallen statesman with every appearance of having conquered the county. Mrs. Annesley declared that such conduct could not be encouraged. Georgiana proposed that perhaps Mr. Grayfur mistook the cow for a rival.

Miss Elizabeth smiled.

Darcy saw it because he had been trying not to look at her.

The smile was true, but it vanished almost before it had done its work. She answered Kitty when called upon, added that Mr. Bennet had often endured worse treatment from members of his own household, and let the table laugh. It was precisely the sort of thing she might have said yesterday. Yet yesterday, the remark would have carried her with it. Tonight it seemed to cost her something.

Darcy broke the trout on his plate into pieces he did not taste. She had pleaded a headache this afternoon and gone upstairs. Whatever had passed in the interval, she had not come down to dinner the same.

"Oh, Lizzy," Kitty said, "I forgot — did you have a letter from Jane?"

Miss Elizabeth's hand paused on her glass. "Yes."

"What does she say? Did Jane say whether Mama has forgiven me for being away?"

Miss Elizabeth turned to her sister. "Forgiven you? I believe Mama has nearly arranged your future elegance from Derbyshire."

Kitty brightened. "Then she misses me."

"Exceedingly. She misses you, praises you, laments you, and believes Pemberley will improve you beyond recognition."

"I do not want to be beyond recognition," Kitty said, alarmed. "Only a little improved."

Mrs. Gardiner looked down at her plate. Miss Elizabeth's mouth curved, but the quickness did not stay. She gazed at Kitty with affection, and beyond affection, fatigue.

Darcy's hand tightened once around his fork and released.

"Was there other news from Longbourn?" Georgiana asked. She put the question with care, as if she knew letters from home might contain either happiness or disturbance and wished to be ready for both.

Miss Elizabeth's gaze dropped to her glass. For one second the whole table seemed to wait without knowing that it waited.

"Yes," she said. "Very happy news."

Mrs. Gardiner's expression softened at once. She knew, then. Darcy looked from her to Miss Elizabeth and felt the first clean line of understanding draw itself through him.

Bingley.

"Jane is to be married," Miss Elizabeth said.

Kitty's cry escaped before she could make it ladylike. "Married!"

Georgiana's face opened into surprised delight. Mrs. Annesley smiled. Mrs. Gardiner, who must have already spent her first joy upstairs, received the news again for the sake of the table, warm and composed.

"To Mr. Bingley?" Kitty demanded.

"Yes. He has returned to Netherfield Park."

He had told Bingley.

The thought arrived with the name. He had told him before Bingley left Pemberley — badly perhaps, too late certainly, but plainly. That he had separated him from Miss Bennet on insufficient evidence. That he had been wrong. He had given no instruction to return. He had told the truth and left Bingley to be himself.

Bingley, being at last in possession of the truth, had gone south. And now Jane Bennet was engaged.

Miss Elizabeth had given Jane's happiness its proper dignity, and it had cost her. On the other side of the table, Kitty was half out of her chair in delight.

"Jane engaged! Oh, Lizzy, and I am not there!"

"That is Jane's only complaint," Miss Elizabeth said. "She wishes you were with her."

"And you."

Miss Elizabeth's hand found her glass. She did not drink. "And me."

Darcy could not speak. To congratulate her at once would be proper. To delay would be marked. To say what rose in him — that Miss Bennet deserved every happiness, that Bingley had been a fool, that he himself had been a worse one — would be impossible.

Mrs. Gardiner saved the table. "Your sister writes happily, Lizzy?"

"Very happily. Bewildered, I think, but happy."

"Jane bewildered," Kitty said, "must look almost exactly like Jane composed."

That drew Miss Elizabeth's smile again, and this time it reached her eyes, though not for him. "Very nearly."

Georgiana asked whether Miss Bennet had known Mr. Bingley long. Kitty answered with the authority of a younger sister who had suffered through every history in the county: Mr. Bingley had taken Netherfield last autumn, had given a ball, had danced with Jane more than anyone, had gone away very cruelly — here she stopped, remembering the table — and had now come back sensibly.

"Very sensibly," Mrs. Gardiner said.

Darcy lifted his glass and drank.

He had thought himself prepared for this. He had imagined, in those hours after Bingley's departure, every possible consequence of his confession — except the one in which Miss Elizabeth sat beside him and heard the name of his friend with sorrow under her joy.

He had repaired what he could. But repair, it appeared, did not return a thing to the state before injury. It displayed the break more clearly.

He set down his empty glass.

"But was not Mr. Bingley just here?" Kitty asked.

There it was.

Georgiana glanced toward her brother, only mildly curious. Mrs. Annesley looked to Mrs. Gardiner, as if the geography of gentlemen's movements were not a subject on which she presumed to judge. Mrs. Gardiner became very still.

Miss Elizabeth did not look at Darcy.

That was answer enough.

"Yes," Georgiana said. "He was here not a fortnight since."

"Then he must have gone straight to Netherfield," Kitty said. "How romantic. Unless he stopped somewhere. Did he stop somewhere?"

No one answered. The question had been tossed into the table like a ribbon, bright and harmless. Darcy saw the knot beneath it.

He could say it.

He could say, *I told him.*

Three words. They would account for Bingley's return. They would tell Miss Elizabeth that he had not left Jane's happiness to accident or convenience or the slow correction of time. They would say he had known himself wrong and acted, at last, before being forced.

They would also ask her to see him acting.

The old impulse rose with horrifying ease: to defend, to explain, to set out the facts in order and trust that once arranged they must acquit him. He had done it once in a letter that shook with pride even where it told the truth. He would not do it at his own table, over Jane Bennet's happiness, with Elizabeth Bennet unable to escape him.

"Bingley," he said, and stopped.

Miss Elizabeth's hands went still beside her plate.

He turned to her because he could not help it. He had not meant to meet her eyes. He had not meant to make of the crowded table a place with only two people in it. But there she was, near enough to touch, pale with self-command, eyes bright and guarded, understanding too much and not enough.

"Bingley acted freely," he said.

Silence followed.

It was an absurd sentence. He knew it as soon as it was spoken. Bingley was of age, of fortune, of cheerful temper, and accountable to no one at the table. Of course he had acted freely. Kitty looked puzzled. Georgiana blinked. Mrs. Annesley, mercifully, reached for her glass. Mrs. Gardiner glanced at Miss Elizabeth and then away.

Miss Elizabeth understood.

That was worse.

Her face changed by almost nothing. Darcy would not have seen it six months ago. He saw it now: the slight stilling about her mouth, the way her fingers rested flat against the tablecloth instead of folding, the withdrawal not of attention but of safety. She had heard the confession inside the evasion. She had heard, perhaps, that he wanted her to hear it.

He had claimed without claiming. Even his restraint had betrayed him.

Kitty recovered first. "Well, I am glad he acted freely if it made Jane happy."

"So are we all," said Mrs. Gardiner, with gentle emphasis.

"Indeed," Georgiana said. "I am very glad for Miss Bennet."

"Thank you," Miss Elizabeth answered. She turned to Georgiana with real warmth, and Darcy watched what he had not earned: the softness she could still give his sister. "Jane will be pleased by your kindness."

"I hope she will be very happy," Georgiana said.

"She will," Kitty declared. "Jane is always happy when people are good, and Mr. Bingley is very good when his sisters do not carry him off."

Mrs. Gardiner coughed once into her napkin.

Darcy deserved that.

Miss Elizabeth's color rose. "Kitty."

"What? He is. He was very good before he went away, except for going away."

"It is sometimes possible," Mrs. Gardiner said, "to be good and foolish in the same season."

Kitty considered this. "Then perhaps he has changed seasons."

The table accepted the phrase with more gratitude than it deserved because it let in laughter. Darcy gave what smile he could. Georgiana smiled in earnest. Miss Elizabeth's lips moved, but the effort was too visible to him.

Dinner continued. Conversation turned, by Mrs. Gardiner's careful hand, to roads and the likelihood that Mrs. Bennet would already have informed every acquaintance within walking distance. Kitty supplied three possible speeches their mother might have made to Mrs. Long. Georgiana laughed at the second and looked guilty for laughing at the third. Mrs. Annesley contributed that happy news traveled fastest where it was least required to travel at all.

Darcy performed the part assigned to him. He spoke when needed. He asked Mrs. Gardiner whether she had found the road from Lambton dusty that afternoon. He answered Georgiana when she asked whether the south lawn might be dry enough tomorrow for walking. He did not look at Miss Elizabeth unless politeness demanded it, and politeness, which had once seemed a comforting structure, became a series of traps.

When the ladies withdrew, he rose with the rest. When Miss Elizabeth turned away from him, preceding him to the door, the movement stirred the faint scent of lavender and summer air from her gown. He had no right to notice it. He noticed it and hated himself for wanting the smallest sign that she did not hate his noticing.

None came.

In the drawing room, candles stood reflected in the dark windows. Mrs. Gardiner took her usual chair. Mrs. Annesley settled with her work. Georgiana and Kitty, released from dinner's formality but not from its news, drew together near the pianoforte and whispered with an animation that had Jane's happiness, Longbourn, and wedding clothes all tangled in it. The promised duet would have to wait.

Miss Elizabeth sat beside her aunt.

Not far from him. Not near.

Darcy stood by the mantel for longer than was necessary and learned nothing from the fire. He could not remain there like a man awaiting judgment. He took a chair. Then he did what he had forbidden himself all evening and let himself look at her.

She was tired.

The brightness in her eyes was not vivacity. Her smile, when Kitty exclaimed over some imagined bridal ribbon, arrived a moment late. Once, when Georgiana asked whether Miss Bennet preferred blue or white, Miss Elizabeth answered sensibly and then looked toward the window as if the dark glass had spoken her name.

He had thought patience required silence. He had not understood that silence could resemble abandonment.

He could tell her. Not here; not like this. But soon. A few words only. Not as a claim. Not as a plea. Simply the truth. She need not thank him. She need not forgive him. She need only be released from whatever question had drawn that strained quiet over her face.

He knew, even as he formed the argument, that it was arranged for his own comfort. It assumed the question was one he could answer.

Georgiana rose and went to the window.

"The sky is quite clear," she said, with mild surprise.

Kitty sprang up at once. "Is it? Oh, let me see. At Longbourn one must go almost into the shrubbery to see anything properly, and then Mama says we shall catch cold or be murdered by damp."

"By damp?" Georgiana asked.

"It is one of the less romantic dangers," Miss Elizabeth said.

There. A little of herself. Thin, but real.

Darcy stood before he had decided to move. The room had become too close. The candles, the reflected faces in the glass, the cheerful voices pressing upon what could not be spoken — all of it seemed suddenly unfit for the truth and equally unfit for concealment.

"The evening is mild," he said. "If Mrs. Gardiner approves, we might take a short turn on the lawn. The stars are often clearer away from the windows."

Kitty looked as if he had offered a private ball.

"May we?" she asked Mrs. Gardiner. "Only for a little while? I have not seen Derbyshire stars."

"They are the same stars, Kitty," Miss Elizabeth said.

"Yes, but from a better county."

Georgiana laughed outright.

Mrs. Gardiner looked from Kitty's eagerness to Georgiana's hopeful face, then to Darcy. There was inquiry in the glance, and caution, and something like permission withheld until he understood its terms. He inclined his head, slightly. A group walk. The lawn. The house in sight. Nothing more.

"For a few minutes," Mrs. Gardiner said. "If shawls are fetched."

Kitty clapped her hands once and then remembered herself. "Lizzy, you will come."

It was not a question.

Miss Elizabeth went still.

For a moment Darcy thought she would refuse. He saw the refusal gather: not in discourtesy, but in weariness. She had endured the fair, the letter, the table, his blunder. He had asked for one more act of composure and called it air.

Then her gaze moved to Georgiana, who had turned from the window with all her hope carefully held. It moved to Kitty, who had already placed Jane's happiness among the constellations and required her sister to witness the arrangement. Last, and only because he was standing in the path of it, her gaze came to him.

"Yes," she said.

The word was perfectly civil. Empty.

Darcy bowed because there was nothing else he could safely do.

He had asked for air.

He had not known he was asking for hope.
