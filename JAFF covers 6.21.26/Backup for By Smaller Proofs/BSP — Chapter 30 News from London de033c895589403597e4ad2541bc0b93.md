# BSP — Chapter 30: News from London

✅ Completed

---

The heat had declared itself before breakfast was cleared.

It shimmered upon the lawns, and on Pemberley's stone. Even inside, with the curtains half-drawn, the air was close. Outside, the trees promised shade at a distance and required a walk to earn it.

Elizabeth had come down prepared for Mr. Darcy. She had meant to lie awake and think; sleep, indifferent to her plans, had taken her at once. She had woken with nothing resolved, and had dressed with care, which she told herself was due to the house. 

She had chosen a ribbon with care, which she told herself was due to the heat. She had rehearsed no speech, which was true, because speech would have implied intention; but she had carried downstairs a readiness so complete that it might have passed, in a less honest court, for innocence.

Mr. Darcy was not at breakfast.

Georgiana supplied the explanation. "My brother was called out early to speak with Mr. Hardwick. He manages the south farms, I believe."

"Estate business," said Mrs. Annesley, giving the phrase its proper weight and thereby closing any road to inquiry.

"How early must gentlemen begin estate business?” Kitty asked. “Earlier than breakfast seems unreasonable. Unless the farms are very far away."

"They are not so far," Georgiana said. "But Mr. Hardwick likes the morning."

"Then Mr. Hardwick has a character fit for improvement," Kitty said.

Georgiana laughed, and Kitty, encouraged by success, began at once upon the disadvantages of mornings at Longbourn, where Mama required news before there was any. Mrs. Annesley smiled over her cup. Mrs. Gardiner listened, added two words where two words were useful, and let the girls discover that yesterday's little fracture had mended without ceremony.

Elizabeth buttered a piece of toast she did not want.

Estate business. Of course. Nothing could be more proper. Nothing could be more Mr. Darcy. If he had been proud, she might have been cool. If he had been wounded, she might have been sorrier than she wished. But he had gone to the south farms.

It was so perfectly done that she could have struck him for it, had he been present to be struck.

The thought restored her a little. It was difficult to be entirely overcome while imagining Mr. Darcy brought to justice by a slice of toast.

Across the table, Mrs. Gardiner's gaze landed on her. It did not remain. That was worse than inquiry. Mrs. Gardiner never stared when observation would do.

When breakfast ended, Kitty and Georgiana went to the music room to look for suitable duets, and Mrs. Annesley followed.

Elizabeth was preparing to follow, for no better reason than that motion was preferable to being left behind, when Mrs. Gardiner laid a hand upon her arm.

"Will you walk with me, Lizzy?"

Mrs. Gardiner's voice was mild. The question could have meant nothing more than a wish for air before the day grew hotter, though the day had already done all it could in that direction. But her hand remained for one extra second, and Elizabeth understood that air was not the object.

"With pleasure," she said too brightly. "If pleasure may be had out of doors today."

"A little shade may be found, I think."

"Then we shall pursue it with all the ardor usually reserved for accomplishments and rich husbands."

Mrs. Gardiner only smiled. "Come."

They went out by a side door, where the borders were arranged with that excellent Pemberley taste which made art appear modest and effort appear natural. The house stood behind them in full morning authority. Before them, the garden fell away in ordered beds and clipped edges, then loosened by degrees toward the wider park.

It was intolerably handsome. It did not look like a place from which one ought to run. It looked like stone, water, shade, order, and consequence; and if people were foolish enough to lose their peace among such things, the fault could not be laid upon the architecture.

Mrs. Gardiner opened her parasol. Elizabeth had refused hers, not two minutes before, from some principle she could not now identify. Pride, likely. Pride had managed worse investments.

They walked in silence until the first turn brought them under the partial shade of a lime walk.

"Your uncle's letter came yesterday with Jane's," Mrs. Gardiner said. "I did not speak of it last night. There was enough in one letter to occupy us all."

Elizabeth’s step shortened. "Is my uncle well?"

"Quite well. He writes in excellent spirits, and with his usual indignation against roads, inns, and any horse that has not learned to consult his convenience. There is no alarm."

They passed a bed of late roses. Their scent came up heavy in the heat, less sweet than insistent.

"The matter that called your uncle home has not resolved as quickly as he hoped. I need not leave this hour, nor tomorrow morning, but I must return within a few days."

Elizabeth knew that visits ended. Gracechurch Street had a household that wanted its mistress. Mr. Gardiner had partners, ledgers, warehouses, ships, men who wrote letters and expected answers. Pemberley was not an island. It had only felt like one.

She made herself say, "Of course. My uncle must not be inconvenienced."

"No."

"And you have already been away longer than you first intended."

"A little."

"That is the danger of Derbyshire. It seduces the traveler into delays and then pretends the mountains are to blame."

"Derbyshire has a great deal to answer for."

The reply was kind. It was also exact. Elizabeth heard both and disliked the second for making the first safer.

"I suppose," she said, too quickly, "the real question is Kitty."

Mrs. Gardiner did not answer at once.

"Whether she may remain, I mean. If you must return, and if my father has permitted her visit, and if Georgiana still wishes for her company. I should not like Kitty to be hurried away when she is doing so well. It would grieve her. And Georgiana too, I think."

"I think it would grieve them both."

"And the household is so very proper that impropriety would perish of embarrassment before it reached the staircase."

"That may be among Pemberley's lesser advertised virtues."

Elizabeth tried to laugh and failed to like the sound. "Then Kitty's remaining is possible."

"Likely, I should say. Not settled until Mr. Darcy has been spoken to, and not to be treated as nothing, but likely."

"Mr. Darcy has been all consideration," she said.

It was too stiff. Mrs. Gardiner adjusted her parasol. "He has," she said.

They walked on. The shade broke. The path opened into sun, and the light gave no mercy to Elizabeth’s unshaded eyes. Every stone in the path declared itself. Every border, every turn, every choice of direction stood visible.

"Kitty is the simpler question," Mrs. Gardiner said.

Elizabeth stopped.

Mrs. Gardiner stopped too, but not at once. She took two more steps, then turned back, giving Elizabeth those two steps in which to decide whether she would pretend not to understand.

Elizabeth had no strength for pretense. Not a graceful one.

"Because she is younger."

"In part."

"And because she is useful to Georgiana."

"Yes."

"And because no one at Lambton has troubled themselves to imagine Kitty mistress of Pemberley by Michaelmas."

The words came out before Elizabeth had decided to permit them. They were too sharp. They were also true enough to be vulgar.

Mrs. Gardiner did not reprove her. "That is one reason."

Elizabeth looked away toward the house. From this angle, Pemberley showed a row of high windows and a sweep of pale wall, all sun and composure. 

"I beg your pardon," Elizabeth said. "That was ill said."

"It was plainly said."

"Plainness is not always a virtue."

"No. But it is sometimes useful."

Elizabeth resumed walking because standing still had begun to feel like confession. 

"One must sometimes consider what reasonable people will see," her aunt continued, "especially after unreasonable people have helped point them in the proper direction."

Elizabeth found no answer. Lambton returned to her in pieces: women with ribbons and baskets, smiles too warm to be innocent, a place made for her before she had chosen to stand in it. Last night she had asked a question beneath the stars and punished the answer she had wanted. This morning Mr. Darcy had removed himself, and she had minded it.

"Do you wish to stay?" Mrs. Gardiner asked.

No one could accuse her aunt of cruelty. She spoke the question as she might have offered a chair to a tired guest. Yet Elizabeth felt its full pressure.

"There is Kitty—"

"Lizzy."

Mrs. Gardiner had not raised her voice. Elizabeth shut her mouth. 

She looked down at the gravel. The stones were pale and hot and numerous. A person might count them for the rest of the morning and learn nothing.

"I do not know."

It was not true enough.

Mrs. Gardiner waited.

Elizabeth pressed one gloved hand over the other. The kid was damp at the palm. "I do not know what I know. That is nearer the truth, though not much more useful."

"It may be useful enough to begin with."

"No, it is absurd. I have prided myself all my life on knowing my own mind, and now my own mind behaves like Lydia with a new bonnet — declaring one thing because it is pretty, another because it is forbidden, and a third because someone else has admired it first."

Mrs. Gardiner's mouth moved. She did not smile.

Elizabeth was grateful for that restraint and resented needing it.

"I thought," she said, and stopped.

What had she thought? That Mr. Darcy would remain proud enough to dislike? That Jane's happiness would settle the matter neatly on one side or the other? 

She had arranged him, in every private supposition, so that she might keep the advantage. He had disobliged her by becoming difficult to arrange at all.

"I thought I had more time," she said. "To know whether I am judging him, or only being led by every circumstance that makes him appear—"

She stopped.

"Appear?" Mrs. Gardiner said.

Elizabeth lifted her eyes. "Good."

Mrs. Gardiner tilted her parasol a little lower, bringing its shade over Elizabeth's shoulder. The gesture was small. It undid her more than speech.

"I cannot leave yet," Elizabeth said. "Not yet. I ought to be able to. It would be simpler. It would be safer. But I cannot turn this visit into — into—"

"Into a decision?"

That was worse than any word she had been avoiding, because it was not romantic. It was practical. It had edges.

"Yes."

Mrs. Gardiner put her hand on Elizabeth's arm. "My dear Lizzy," she said.

That was nearly fatal. Had her aunt continued — had she offered consolation, advice, hope, warning, any of the useful things older and wiser women have at command — Elizabeth might have met it. The unfinished tenderness was harder. It permitted tears without demanding them.

Elizabeth did not cry properly. She would have denied it if accused. But her eyes filled, and for half a minute the hot gravel blurred into a pale seam at her feet.

Mrs. Gardiner allowed the half minute. No more.

"Time is not indefinite," she said.

Mrs. Gardiner's face remained composed, but there was a firmness in it which belonged neither to Gracechurch Street nor to Pemberley, but to character. Elizabeth had seen it before in small domestic forms: in the management of tired children, in a quiet refusal to let Mrs. Bennet govern a visit by nerves, in the decision that a carriage would stop before a driver admitted fatigue. It was kindness with a spine.

"You must be fair to yourself," Mrs. Gardiner said. "If you wish to stay because you require time to understand your own mind, say so honestly, at least to yourself. Do not dress it entirely in Kitty's frock."

Elizabeth laughed, wet-eyed, despite herself.

"And you must be fair to him."

The laugh died at once.

"Mr. Darcy has not pressed you. He has, so far as I can judge, taken some pains not to do so. That restraint does him credit. It also cannot be expected to bear every burden forever."

"No."

"If you remain, it will not be the same visit it was yesterday."

"To him?"

"To him. To you. To any person with eyes enough to see what Lambton saw before either of you wished it seen." 

"I do not say this to frighten you with gossip," Mrs. Gardiner continued. "People talk where there is sense and where there is none. We cannot live by their tongues. But neither can we pretend a thing has no shape because we dislike the name others give it."

The music room windows were not visible from where they stood, but she imagined them: Kitty at the instrument, Georgiana beside her, Mrs. Annesley near enough to guide, far enough to let friendship happen. A proper arrangement. A good one.

Elizabeth had wanted to shelter behind it. She saw that now.

"What will you tell Mr. Darcy?" she asked.

"That my husband's business requires my return within a few days, and that Kitty's visit must be settled plainly."

"And of me?"

Mrs. Gardiner's hand left her arm. "That is for you to decide before I must say anything that cannot be unsaid."

The answer was merciful and merciless.

"I hate practical matters," Elizabeth said.

"No, you do not. You hate them when they tell the truth sooner than feeling is ready."

"That is an excellent reason to hate them."

"It is. But not a useful one."

For the first time that morning, Elizabeth's laugh sounded like herself.

They began walking again. The shade had shifted while they stood. The path ahead ran in full sun for several yards before turning beneath another line of trees. It looked short. It looked exposed. It looked unavoidable.

Elizabeth stepped onto the hot gravel.

The heat came up through the soles of her shoes. Behind her stood the house she could not honestly leave. Before her lay a turn she could not see beyond. Beside her walked the aunt who would not choose for her.

She had a few days.

She had less time than she wanted.

She had enough time to make delay impossible.
