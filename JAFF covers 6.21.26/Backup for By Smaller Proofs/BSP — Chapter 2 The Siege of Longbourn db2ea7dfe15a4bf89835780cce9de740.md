# BSP — Chapter 2: The Siege of Longbourn

The shrieking began at half past nine.

Elizabeth was in the morning room with Jane and her embroidery. Or rather, with her embroidery frame balanced on her lap and her needle making no progress whatsoever, because she had not set a stitch since sitting down and had no immediate plans to begin. The morning room faced east, and the April light came in thin and provisional through the tall windows, laying pale squares on the carpet that would shift and shrink as the hours passed. The fire had not yet been built up; the grate held last night's ash and a chill that the sunshine could not quite reach. 

She had chosen the room for its distance from the rest of the household, its view of the garden where nothing of consequence had ever happened, and the fragile hope that she might pass thirty minutes without the contents of a certain letter arranging themselves behind her eyes. She had achieved none of these aims. But the morning room was quiet, and Jane was beside her stitching away, the silk whispering through the linen in small, regular strokes, and for a quarter of an hour, at least, this had been enough.

Then came Lydia.

Not Lydia herself — not yet — but the sound of Lydia, which always preceded her person by some margin, in the same way that thunder precedes lightning for those standing at a sufficient distance. The cry began somewhere upstairs, gathered force along the corridor, and arrived at the first floor landing as a single, sustained note of operatic outrage.

"I will not! I will *not* stay here! Papa cannot — Mama! *Mama*!"

Jane looked up, toward the doorway. Elizabeth looked down, pinning her needle into the fabric. Neither of them moved.

The next ten minutes proceeded with the inevitability of a natural disaster observed from the road. Lydia appeared in the morning room doorway, her face blotched and her fists at her sides and her voice at a volume that caused the window casements to vibrate in sympathy. Kitty followed at her heels, drawn or dragged — it was not clear which — her face carrying the hunted look of a girl who knows the storm is about to turn on her. Their mother came from the other direction, most likely the kitchen, still holding a ring of household keys that jingled with each agitated step.

The facts, as Elizabeth pieced them together over the next quarter of an hour, were these: Mr. Bennet had spent his promised fifteen minutes with Kitty in the library, had emerged long enough to send Hill upstairs with a message for Lydia — *Your father wishes you to know that you will not be going to Brighton, and that no further discussion is required* — and had then locked the door behind him. Lydia, receiving this intelligence from Hill while still in her dressing gown, had dressed with a speed she normally reserved for balls, descended the stairs in a fury, found the library door immovable, and turned her campaign upon the rest of the household.

She produced an argument anyway.

It was, in its way, magnificent. Lydia mounted her case with the passionate incoherence of a barrister who has not read the brief but feels strongly that justice is on her side. Brighton was *promised*. Harriet was *expecting* her. The regiment would think her a *nobody*. She would be the only girl in Hertfordshire — in *England* — who had ever been denied a trip to the seaside by a father who cared nothing for her happiness and a sister — here she wheeled on Kitty, who had been trying to make herself invisible behind the curtain — a *jealous, spiteful, stupid* sister who had ruined everything because she could not bear to see anyone else admired.

Kitty, who had been brave once that morning and had used up the whole of her supply, burst into tears.

"Oh, do not cry," Lydia said, with the contempt of a person who had been crying without interruption for twenty minutes and considered it her exclusive privilege. "You have got what you wanted. I hope you are *satisfied*."

Kitty was not satisfied. Kitty was red-eyed and shaking and pressed against the curtain as though she wished the fabric would swallow her. Jane rose and went to her, and Elizabeth watched and did not move, her mind reeling.

He had called them wanting in propriety. He had written it in his own hand and she had read the words in the lane at Hunsford and she had been so angry — so blisteringly, righteously angry — that she had walked for an hour without seeing the hedgerows. He had called her family improper, unguarded, lacking in every social grace that could recommend them to the world, and she had thought him proud and cruel and blind.

And here was Lydia, who was now informing the morning room at large that she would speak to her father herself, that no locked door in Christendom could prevent her, that she would *make* him listen.

And here was Mrs. Bennet, who had arrived in the doorway already in a state of high agitation and a lace cap that had come unpinned on one side, and who was already promising Lydia that she would go to Mr. Bennet herself, that she would *reason* with him, that a mother's feelings must carry some weight even in a household governed by a man who had no heart.

He had not been blind. He had been, if anything, mercilessly precise.

Mrs. Bennet's embassy to the library door was conducted with all the tactical subtlety of a cavalry charge. Elizabeth heard it from the morning room — the knock, the entreaty, the invocation of nerves, the rising complaint about daughters who were denied their natural rights, the observation that Mrs. Long's daughters had been to Ramsgate *twice*, and the final, devastating silence from the other side of the door that indicated Mr. Bennet was in no danger of being moved.

Lydia had claimed the fainting couch, displacing Kitty to its farthest end without troubling to disguise the shove, and was producing groans of despair at a volume suggesting she wished them to carry through the library door.

Mrs. Bennet returned with the air of a diplomat who has been refused at the gates and wishes everyone to understand that the fault lies entirely with the gates.

"He will not listen," she announced. "He never listens. Twenty-three years I have been married to that man and he has not listened to a single word I have said — not one! — and now poor Lydia is to be *punished* for her sister's jealousy, and I am to have no say in the matter, and my nerves — my *nerves*— you cannot imagine what this is doing to my nerves."

Elizabeth could, in fact, imagine with considerable precision.

The morning room had grown close. Too many bodies, too many tears, the windows shut against the April wind, and Mrs. Bennet's lavender water hanging in the air like a reproach. The pale squares of light on the carpet had crept halfway across the floor by the time Elizabeth escaped to the hallway, where the air was cooler and carried only the distant sound of Mary practicing her arpeggios with grim determination.

By noon, Lydia had adopted a new strategy.

"I shall not eat," she declared, positioning herself in the dining room chair with the posture of a martyr preparing for the stake. "I shall not eat a single bite until Papa relents. You may tell him so. You may tell him I shall waste away entirely, and when I am dead he may put *Here lies Lydia Bennet, who was denied Brighton* on the stone, and I hope he will be *sorry*."

"Oh, Lydia!" Mrs. Bennet pressed her handkerchief to her eyes. "Mr. Bennet, do you hear? She will not eat! She will starve herself, and I shall have to bury my own daughter, and all because —"

Mr. Bennet was not present to hear. Mr. Bennet had not emerged from the library since nine o'clock. A tray of cold meat and bread had been sent in at eleven; Hill had reported that the door opened precisely wide enough to admit the tray and closed again before any human face could follow it. This intelligence Mrs. Bennet received as further evidence of her husband's depravity.

The hunger strike held through the first course with admirable commitment. It held through the laying of the cold chicken with visible effort. It did not survive the arrival of Hill's stewed apples — the smell of them warm and brown-sugared, filling the dining room with the particular sweetness of the Longbourn kitchen — which Lydia had loved since childhood and which reached her with the merciless timing of an army flanking a weakened position. The first spoonful was taken in defiant silence. The second was taken with less defiance. By the third, the strike had been abandoned with no formal announcement of its conclusion, and Lydia had moved on to her next campaign: the letter.

She wrote to Harriet Forster at the dining table, in a hand that Elizabeth — passing behind her chair on the way to the sideboard — could not help but observe. Every third word was underlined. Exclamation marks appeared in clusters. The letter appeared to contain, in its first paragraph alone, the words *cruel*, *unjust*, *Papa*, *impossible*, and what looked very much like *prison*.

"Kitty." Lydia did not look up from her composition. "Kitty, bring me the sealing wax."

Kitty, who had been eating her dinner in the careful silence of a person hoping to be overlooked, set down her fork. "It is in the writing desk. In the —"

"I *know* where it is. I asked you to bring it. Since you have taken everything else from me, you may at least make yourself useful."

Kitty's eyes filled. She pushed back her chair and left the room, and Elizabeth heard her footsteps on the stairs — quick, uneven, with the speed of someone retreating before the tears could arrive in company.

"Lydia," Elizabeth said.

"What?"

Elizabeth opened her mouth. What she wanted to say was this: *You are behaving exactly as he described. Every word he wrote about this family is being staged for my particular edification, and I cannot bear it, and I cannot say why, and the man whose judgment I despised has turned out to be a better observer of my own household than I have ever been.*

What she said was: "The sealing wax is in the left drawer."

Mary, who had been waiting with her customary patience for a silence large enough to accommodate her, found one.

"The observation of the individual is a lesser duty than the governance of the family," she said. "When parents are unable or unwilling to check the follies of a child —"

"Nobody asked you, Mary," Lydia said.

Mary took a measured sip of her water and returned to her plate with the composure of a woman accustomed to preaching into the wind and finding the acoustics perfectly adequate.

Elizabeth looked at her father's empty chair. The dining room was warm and airless, the windows fogged at their lower edges, the remains of dinner growing cold on plates no one had troubled to clear. Afternoon light pressed flat and white against the glass. He had written *occasionally even by your father*. She thought of the library door, shut against the household's noise, and the decanter on the second shelf, and Mr. Bennet behind his wall of books and silence, governing nothing, correcting nothing, retreating from the disorder he had permitted to flourish for sixteen years. He had called it a want of propriety. She might have called it something less polite, had she been writing the letter. But she had not written the letter. He had. And he had not been wrong about a single word.

---

By evening, the house had the exhausted quiet of a field after the armies have withdrawn.

Lydia had retreated upstairs to compose, Elizabeth suspected, a second letter to Harriet and possibly a third to the other officers' wives, detailing her persecution with the industry of a war correspondent. Mrs. Bennet had retired to her room with her salts and a grievance that showed no signs of diminishing before morning. Mary was practicing scales on the pianoforte in the back parlor — the same four bars, repeated with a mechanical precision that suggested penance rather than pleasure. Kitty was somewhere. Elizabeth was ashamed not to know where, and doubly ashamed that this was the first time all day she had given Kitty a thought uncomplicated by her own distress.

The drawing room was empty. The fire had burned low to a red line along the last log, giving off a dry, papery heat that did not reach the corners of the room. The April light was fading in the windows — long amber slants that caught the dust still suspended in the air from the day's commotion — and the room had the particular stillness that comes when too many people have been shouting in it and have finally stopped. It smelled of cold tea and spent candle wax and, faintly, of Mrs. Bennet's lavender water, which had permeated the furnishings like a ghost that refused to leave.

Jane was there, a ghost.

She sat by the window with her hands folded in her lap and her face turned toward the garden, and when Elizabeth came in she looked up with an expression that was not quite a smile but was, Elizabeth thought, the nearest Jane could manage these days.

"Shall we walk?" Jane said.

It was not a question of exercise. Elizabeth knew it and Jane knew it, and neither of them needed to say so. It was the same offer Jane had been making since they were girls, the quiet provision of company without the demand for explanation, the open door that could be walked through or left alone as Elizabeth chose.

"Yes," Elizabeth said. "Please."

They took the lane behind the garden, the one that led past the meadow and toward the copse where they had played as children and confided as young women and where the hedgerows, at least, had the decency not to offer their opinions.

The first breath of outside air hit Elizabeth like cold water. After an evening sealed indoors with Lydia's grief and Mrs. Bennet's lavender and the close, overheated rooms, the evening was a shock of green and wet earth and the mineral coolness of air that had been moving freely across open fields. A blackbird was singing from the top of the hawthorn by the gate — a liquid, unhurried sound that knew nothing of Brighton or Wickham or locked library doors. The gravel crunched under their boots. The meadow grass, still damp from the morning, darkened the hems of their skirts. The light was the thin gold of a spring evening that has not yet decided whether it is finished, and it lay across the lane in long bars that stretched and shifted as the sun dropped toward the treeline.

Jane walked beside her without speaking, and for the first hundred yards Elizabeth let the silence and the open air do their work.

Was it a punishment, to see your family through a stranger's eyes and find the portrait accurate? Or was it something else — unwanted, unwelcome, delivered without tenderness — but honest? And did the man who had forced that clarity upon her know what he had done, or had it been merely the excess of his wounded pride, spilling truths he had no right to offer and no expectation she would keep?

She did not know. She was not certain he had meant it as anything at all.

"Lizzy," Jane said, after they had passed the meadow gate and the house had fallen out of sight behind them. "You have been very quiet today."

"It has been a loud day. I thought someone ought to balance the account."

Jane's mouth curved. Not quite a laugh — Jane did not laugh at her family's distress, it was not in her composition — but an acknowledgment that the observation was fair.

"You are troubled," Jane said. "And not only by Lydia."

Elizabeth looked at her sister. Dear, steady Jane, who had carried her own heartbreak back from London like a parcel she refused to unwrap in public, who smiled her careful smile over uneaten ham, who asked *shall we walk* instead of *what is wrong* because she understood that some questions are better put by offering your company than your curiosity.

"I am thinking," Elizabeth said carefully, "about how well one can know the people one has lived with all one's life, and how badly one can judge the people one has only just met."

Jane considered this. "That sounds like the beginning of a very long walk."

"It may be."

Jane took her arm. "Then we had best keep going."
