# BSP — Chapter 8: The South Drive

---

The morning at the Rose Inn was already too bright by nine. Sun on the yard, sun on the gray stone of the church across the way, sun on the polished brass of the harness as the chaise was brought round to the door. The cloud of yesterday had gone off in the night and taken the easy gray with it. There was no breeze, even in the yard. The sky had settled into a hard pale blue and the leaves did not move.

Mr. Gardiner came down the inn stairs cheerful — ruddy, square-built, the kind of man whose coat sat well across the shoulders because he walked his own warehouse twice a day. Mrs. Gardiner came down hatted and gloved and very nearly girlish, small and neat and brown-haired under her bonnet, her bright sensible face lit up an extra degree by the morning and the place, having slept the sleep of a woman returned to ground she knew by heart. Elizabeth awaited them in the front passage, just out of the sun, watching the chaise turn under the arch. Asked at breakfast, she had told her aunt she had slept perfectly well. She had not slept well at all, which she had told no one.

Best bonnet tied, neatest gloves on. Holding one thought in the palm of her glove like a small coin: *He will not be there*.

The chaise rolled out under the inn arch and turned into the Lambton street, springs creaking, the smell of warmed leather already gathering inside it. The landlord had said five miles by the post-road, less if they turned in at the lodge. They would do it in an hour, the landlord had said, if the horses were not idle. As they passed slowly through town, keeping the dust they kicked up to a minimum, Mrs. Gardiner gave another potted tour. There, the corner where the old market cross had stood until the year before her wedding. Here, the smithy where she had once held a mare's head while the smith argued with her father about the price of shoeing. Yonder the parsonage paddock she had once been chased out of for a debt of crab-apples. Mr. Gardiner laughed. Elizabeth smiled where she was meant to smile.

The town sank behind them. The road bent north and east, climbing past hedgerows where the bees worked the meadowsweet without hurry. A pheasant stood in the middle of the road and held its ground until the very last moment, then took itself off into the bracken in a fluster of indignation.

Elizabeth turned her face to the open window.

The road, she now understood without being told, was the same road. It ran north and east the same way the wall had run north and east, yesterday, on the other side. The wall was somewhere off to her right, behind the hedges and the shoulder of the rise; it had been there all morning, and would be all morning, and she was now driving alongside the inside of what she had driven alongside the outside of. She had not, when she had agreed to this expedition at her aunt's table last night, thought through this fact in this particular form. She had thought through nothing in any particular form. *He will not be there.* That had been form enough. Her gloved hand was at her throat without permission; she put it down.

The turnoff to the lodge came up on the left without warning — a low gray building with a single chimney, and an old woman in the door who curtsied with calm efficiency. They must have passed it yesterday without noticing it, between the unbroken miles of the wall and her own preoccupation with not looking at it. The gates were already open. The chaise turned in.

The drive ran north through woods. Tall woods, kept clear underneath, the trunks of beeches at neat intervals and the bracken at their feet clearly thinned by deer and not by gardeners. The light through the leaves came down in the sort of dappled coins that one saw in pictures and rarely in life. 

Elizabeth had not, in any of her private picturings of Pemberley, allowed it deer or beeches. She had allowed it severe firs, planted in lines, and gravel raked to a finished level. She corrected herself, privately, in the dappled light, and was annoyed to have to. Each correction was a small admission — and she had been making a great many small admissions, lately, about its owner.

The wood went on. They climbed; they descended; they climbed again. At one point the drive bore right around a great oak that had been there, plainly, since the Restoration; at another it crossed a small bridge over a stream so clear one could count the stones in its bed at the speed of a trotting horse. Elizabeth caught herself counting, and stopped.

Then the woods opened.

Elizabeth gasped.

The prospect was one she had only read about in books. It was a trick of the Capability Brown manner of landscape, where the trees stood back at a precise  angle and gave the visitor the house all at once, framed, a living portrait. She had read about the device in books, and disliked it on the page. In a wood, it did not feel like a device. It felt like a welcome.

Pemberley stood well on rising ground a quarter-mile below them, above the river and backed by a ridge of high woody hills. A handsome stone house, large but not enormous, longer than it was tall, with the look of a building that had been added to over a hundred and fifty years by men who had each had the courtesy to consult the men before them. A south front of pale gold stone in the morning sun. Tall windows. A flat broad terrace before it, and beyond the terrace a slope of grass down to the water, and beyond the water the wooded rise on the far side of the valley, hot and dark and full of summer.

Mr. Gardiner said, "Well." 

Mrs. Gardiner turned to Elizabeth, smiling. “Oh, Lizzy. Just so. Just exactly so.” 

Elizabeth was silent. She had been carrying, since April — perhaps since longer than April, perhaps since the very first uttering of the name *Pemberley* by Mr. Bingley's sisters in a Hertfordshire drawing-room — a picture of this place that was a fortress. Stone piled in such a way as to crow about itself. Avenues laid out to lecture one on perspective. A house whose owner could stand on its terrace and look down at the country and find the country wanting.

The house on its rise was nothing like that. It was settled exactly where the land suggested it. Made of the stone of the hill, and built to look at the river and not at the visitor. There was nothing in it of crowing. There was, on consideration, very little in it of pride, in the sense in which she had used that word about its owner for nine months together.

She looked at it a long minute.

*He had never spoken of this place to her* — not once, not in any of those drawing-rooms last winter.

The chaise began the descent.

When it drew up before the door, on the gravel sweep before the south front,  a man in plain dark livery handed them down with the practiced civility of a servant who had handed down a great many strangers in his time without making any of them feel less like strangers. The door opened of itself, or seemed to, and a woman came out of it.

The housekeeper, Mrs. Reynolds, was a respectable-looking elderly person — much less fine, and more civil, than Elizabeth had had any notion of finding her. The first phrase she put out had Lambton in it without putting the word *Lambton* directly: she had heard, she said, that there was a Lambton lady in the party, and she was very glad they had thought to come up to the house, and the family being away she would be happy to show them whatever they cared to see.

Mrs. Gardiner, smiling, said the name she had had as a girl. Mrs. Reynolds smiled in return — a smile of placement, the kind a woman gives when she has just understood whose family she is speaking to — and took them in.

The hall was quiet and cool. After the heat of the gravel sweep, it was nearly a separate climate. Stone underfoot, plaster overhead, a staircase that turned upon itself in an easy decided manner. The stair had been carrying its family up and down for a hundred and forty years, according to Mrs. Reynolds, winking.

There were no flowers. Elizabeth had pictured flowers — banks of them, suffocating — and there were none. The family was not at home; one did not stage flowers for one's own absence. A great clock in the corner kept the time of the house with the sober regularity of a man who has business to attend to and does not mean to be late for it.

Mrs. Reynolds led them through.

The principal rooms were arrayed in good order: a saloon with windows giving onto the terrace and the river, a dining-room a little darker than the saloon with a long table of dark mahogany unrelieved by any cloth, a library at the corner of the house with two walls of books and a wide low window-seat under the third window — its cushion plain dark wool, the curtains above it heavy linen the color of weak tea. 

Elizabeth, who had been learning all her life that the manner in which a room was lived in could be read from a glance, looked into each, and read each, and found in none of them what she had half-wanted to find — some piece of the man she had known last October, some confirming pomp that would settle her judgment back into its old shape.

The books in the library had been read. She could tell at twenty paces. They stood at the angles of books that had been pulled out and put back, and at the small unevennesses of bindings that had taken their owner's hand many times. The window-seat had a cushion that had been sat on. There was a small table by the window-seat with a single book on it, face down to keep its place, as if the reader had been called away an hour ago and meant to return. (She glanced at the spine, briefly: something on the rotation of crops.) The room clearly was not got up for visitors. 

The saloon was the same. The chairs had been arranged for use, not for show. A workbasket sat beside one of them, with a piece of half-finished embroidery laid across it, the needle still in the cloth — Miss Georgiana Darcy's, the housekeeper said, when Elizabeth's eye lit on it. Miss Darcy was very fond of her work; would the lady care to see the morning room next, and the gallery beyond?

The music room had a view of the spread of the lawn — smooth and unhurried, the way a thing looks when it has been cared for for generations. The music room had a pianoforte, a fine one, with a piece of music open on its stand. Mozart, it looked like. The music room held a small writing-desk near the window, its inkwell capped against dust, and a sheet of paper laid blank on the blotter as though waiting for the lady of the house to come back to her morning's correspondence. There was no lady of the house at the moment, but the house went on as if there were.

Mrs. Reynolds, walking ahead, kept up a steady housekeeperly commentary that was neither boastful nor humble. *This room had been re-papered four years since. The chimneypiece in the dining-room was the third Mr. Darcy's putting in. The library had had its present arrangement from the late master, but the present master had added, oh, a great many books, particularly upon the management of estates, and upon the new methods in agriculture, of which he was a great reader.* "He has added a great many books, ma'am, in the last year or two — he is one of those gentlemen who reads about his fields as well as walks them. "

Mrs. Reynolds liked the master of the house. She had liked him as a boy and liked him still as a man, with no interval of having to be persuaded back to it. Her manner was not one that liked a young master from a distance, on the understanding that one ought to. It was the manner in which one liked the weather of one's own house: as a fact, settled, and on the whole a fortunate one.

Mr. Gardiner cleared his throat. "Forgive a London man's curiosity, Mrs. Reynolds — does the master manage the home grounds himself, or has he a steward to it?"

"Mr. Greaves manages the home farm, sir, but the master is in the office two mornings a week and walks the land more often than that. He was raised to know his own soil, if you will pardon the expression."

"I will pardon it. It is the right expression."

Mrs. Gardiner, behind, said quietly to Elizabeth, "She was here in my mother's time, you know. She was a young woman then." Elizabeth nodded. She did not trust her voice for an answer.

They went up the staircase. Mrs. Reynolds, on the landing, paused before a long room that opened off it.

"The picture-gallery," she said. "If the lady cares to step in."

The lady cared to.

The gallery ran the length of the south front. The morning sun came in along its full length in long warm bars. Portraits down both walls. Darcys in a wig and a starched ruff, Darcys in periwigs, Darcys in regimentals, Darcys in the plain dark coats of country gentlemen who declined to be painted in any costume but their own. 

Elizabeth walked the length of it slowly, behind Mrs. Reynolds, with her uncle and aunt at her shoulder, and looked into face after face that bore, in this jaw or that brow, the family thing — the *Darcy* thing — she had thought, until April, was vanity, and was beginning to think might only be the small accident of bone.

Near the end of the gallery, on the right hand: a tall portrait of a gentleman in a dark coat, perhaps thirty years old, perhaps a little less. The painter had caught him standing easily by a desk, one hand on a book, the other at his side, and had caught also the faint expression at the corner of his mouth that one saw when one had just amused someone without meaning to, and which he had not yet decided whether to permit. 

Elizabeth had not seen that expression on his face since Hertfordshire. Last autumn. She tried to put a particular drawing-room to it, and could not — only that he had looked at her, once or twice, like that, and she had not known what to do with it, and so had decided he was sneering.

"That is the master," said Mrs. Reynolds, beside her, with the proprietary calm of a woman who has watched a boy turn into a man and finds the result entirely satisfactory.

"Yes," said Elizabeth.

She did not move on. She stood in front of it longer than she had meant to, the gallery quiet around her, and looked at the easy hand on the book and the half-finished expression at the mouth, and felt — what? Not quite anything she could name.

Mrs. Reynolds had moved on; her aunt also; their voices, several paces down the gallery, had taken up another portrait. Elizabeth heard, without quite hearing, the housekeeper say, "She was very low last summer, you know — we were sorry for it — but she is more herself now."

Georgiana.

Last summer. Ramsgate. A girl of only fifteen, very nearly carried off by a man Elizabeth had stood in a Hertfordshire ballroom and found witty. The cold flush came up from her stomach as it had in April. She had walked into that wrongness with both eyes wide open. She had liked the man. She had defended him in her own head. She had heard his account of the Darcys and given it room, given it weight; and the girl on the wall in front of her was the girl who had been the price.

She turned, with some difficulty, from Mr. Darcy's portrait, and followed the others.

The miniature of Miss Darcy at twelve was at the gallery's far end. A small oval, gold-framed: a fair girl with her brother's eyes and none of his guarded mouth. All sweetness, was Elizabeth's first thought; all softness; the kind of face that had not yet been required to put a wall up against anything. She felt, looking at it, a small protective tenderness she had not authorized.

"She has grown so tall, ma'am, you would not know her," Mrs. Reynolds was saying. She paused, and added, in the same warm key, "She comes down with the master on Monday — and a good thing, too. The house has stood too quiet this summer."

"On Monday?" Mrs. Gardiner said. "Why, that is the day after tomorrow. Shall you have a houseful?"

"Aye, with the master and the young lady, ma'am, and four others, as well. Miss Darcy is bringing her friends out of London with her. We shall be lively again, ma'am, and very glad of it. They set out from town today, and so we shall have them all by Monday afternoon." Mrs. Reynolds paused, considering, and added in the same domestic key, "He has been longer in town this year than is his custom. We do not often have him so long away from us."

Elizabeth, behind her, did the date arithmetic. February. February to July. Five months. The same arithmetic, she thought without permission, that she had heard in another voice eleven weeks ago in a parlor at Hunsford, when a man had said *I have struggled in vain* and meant *I have been struggling for months.* The five months were the same five months. She had not, before this minute, set the two against each other.

“Is Mr. Darcy much attached to Pemberley, then?” Mrs Gardiner asked, smiling to soften her words. 

“Oh, no! Well, perhaps.” Mrs. Reynolds smiled at the miniature of Georgiana. “He certainly is a doting brother.”

Elizabeth turned her face to the nearest window. The sun on the lawn outside was so bright that for a half-second she could see nothing at all.

Could she have been so wrong? 

After they had seen the rest of the public parts of the house, Mrs. Reynolds walked them back down the staircase to the south door. There she stopped and recommended very prettily that the lady and gentleman, before they left, should walk in the grounds a little. She waved toward the river, toward the bench above the falls, where the prospect of the house was particularly fine; the gardeners were not at work this morning in that quarter and they would have the grounds to themselves. 

Elizabeth listened with a half-attention. Her other half was still in the gallery, in front of a portrait, with a small fond housekeeper's voice in it she had not anticipated and could not quite put down.

“Such a kind offer,” Mrs. Gardiner said. “We would be delighted.”

Elizabeth, who would have taken any open door at this moment — air, sea, anything that was not another room of his — gave thanks to the housekeeper that she hoped were the right thanks, and stepped out into the sun.

At the first step out, the heat hit. After the cool of the rooms it was as if they had stepped into a warm bath. A perfect July day, with heat enough to make the shade an event and sky enough to make the green shine. Mr. Gardiner offered Mrs. Gardiner his arm, and they took the path that led west across the front of the house. They passed the terrace, broad and pale and warm underfoot, and then down by an easy slope to the gravel walk that ran above the river.

Elizabeth followed a step or two behind. She had not been able to say, in any room of that house, what she had felt. She did not, in the open air of the lawn, know exactly what she felt now. The sun on the back of her neck was a warm scarf. Her aunt's voice, ahead, was placid; her uncle's, answering, was pleased. The river below the walk ran over its stones with the soft continuous burbling that sang that it was happy to carry on forever.

Where the path bent south at a clump of beeches, the trees fell back, and they had a new view of the valley: their own bank sloping down to the river, the tributary's small stone bridge just below them, and on the far side, half a mile off through the trunks, the line of the south drive. Mr. Gardiner, ahead, stopped at the bend to admire. Mrs. Gardiner stopped beside him.

Elizabeth let her aunt and uncle stop at the bend, and went on alone the few yards to the rail of the bridge over the tributary, and looked across.

Through the trunks of the beeches on the far side of the river, on the south drive, a horseman was riding.

He was a long way off—a quarter-mile, perhaps; perhaps a little more. Through the gaps between the trees she could see only that he was on a dark horse, that he wore a dark coat, that he sat his horse in the manner of a man who had been on horses since he was four. He was not hurrying. He was crossing the ground at the long swinging walk of a horse being let cool at the end of a piece of road.

Something at the back of her ribs jumped without her permission.

She saw him for, perhaps, three seconds. The trees closed.

She looked again. The trees did not open. There was only the brown and white of the trunks and the green of the leaves, and the line of the drive going off northward through them, and no horseman on it.

Her aunt and uncle had not turned. They were looking the other way, at the bridge over the tributary.

She forced herself to draw a breath, slowly.

It must have been, she informed herself in the privacy of her head, a tenant. A tenant of Mr. Darcy's, riding home on his own business. Or a steward. Or a gardener's son on a borrowed horse. Or a stranger to the place altogether. A traveler taking the south road, who had no notion he was passing through anybody's pale. 

It surely had not been the master of the house, who was setting out from London this very morning, who was leaving with a party of five, who would be brought to Pemberley on Monday afternoon in a procession of carriages, who could not, by any arithmetic in the world, be on his own south drive at twenty minutes past eleven on a hot Saturday in late July.

She had come a hundred and fifty miles north on her aunt's account, not on his. She had not come an hundred and fifty miles to put him into trees that did not contain him.

The crossness was sharper than the moment warranted. She felt that, also, and tried to set it aside.

"Lizzy!" said Mrs. Gardiner, brightly, at the end of the bridge. "Come and see. There is a fish in here as long as your arm."

Elizabeth came, and admired the fish, and contributed an observation about its evident contentment in its situation, and walked on with her aunt and uncle along the gravel walk, and did not look back across the river. The image did not leave her. 

They walked another half-hour in the grounds — a small circuit east of the house, by the shrubbery and the kitchen garden, and back round to the south door — and saw nothing on the south drive but, twice, a deer, and once a kingfisher down at the water. 

Elizabeth looked for another sign of the rider, despite herself; and twice she told herself, with displeasure, to stop looking. She was inventing him out of trees. It must be the heat.

By the time they came round the front of the house, the sun stood high, and the chaise had been brought up, and the same quiet man was holding the door. No one else was around. The beautiful grounds, the beautiful house, was empty.

Mr. Gardiner handed Mrs. Gardiner up. Mrs. Gardiner, in the act of being handed, observed that she had had a perfectly delightful morning and that it had been a very great kindness in the housekeeper, and that they ought, perhaps, to leave a little something for the woman by way of —

The hooves came up the gravel from the south — single, unhurried, a horse and one rider, no carriage behind.

*He will not be there.* She had carried the sentence up to the inn and down from it; she had carried it up the drive and around the gravel sweep; she was carrying it, still, standing, about to set her foot on the carriage step.

Elizabeth’s gloved hand was reaching for the carriage door. She turned her head.

The horseman, tall, dark, on a tall bay, dust on its flanks, sweat at its shoulder. The bay was breathing hard. 

Mr. Darcy.

He saw her at the same moment. 

She saw the surprise reach him. There was a small, distinct alteration in the angle of his head, and his hand on the rein checked, and the horse — taking the change of weight — fell from the walk to a halt, four yards from where she stood. His other hand, gloveless, came up to lift his hat.

The bay blew out. The sound, in the high still noon, was very loud.

Elizabeth had not moved.

He said, "Miss Bennet."

She heard her own voice from somewhere outside her body say, "Mr. Darcy."

Mrs Gardiner made a small startled sound from the carriage. Elizabeth did not look at her.
