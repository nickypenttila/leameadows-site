# BSP — Chapter 22: Letters North and South

The daily post came late to Pemberley. Elizabeth had already learned this from the servants — who mentioned it as one mentions weather, without apology — and from the quality of the waiting, which was different here than at Longbourn. At home, the post was a skirmish: Mrs. Bennet's nerves engaged at the sound of the boy's step, Lydia snatching at anything addressed in a hand she recognized, Mary not looking up from her book but listening with both ears. At Pemberley, letters arrived on a tray. They arrived clean. No one scrambled.

It was, Elizabeth thought, the sort of household in which one might write important letters without anyone making a scene about it.

She was about to discover whether this was true.

The summons came around mid-morning, delivered by a servant whose composure could not quite disguise that he was carrying something unusual: an invitation from Mr. Darcy for Mrs. Gardiner and Miss Bennet to join him at their convenience in his office. 

Not the morning room. Not the drawing room.

Mrs. Gardiner received this intelligence with the calm of a woman who had been entirely at her ease at Pemberley and was not unwilling to be diverted. "His office," she repeated, setting down her teacup. "That sounds rather more deliberate than a breakfast-table remark." She regarded her niece. "Do you know what this is about, Lizzy?"

"I may have some idea."

"Then I shall rely on your countenance to tell me when to be alarmed."

They followed the servant through a corridor Elizabeth had not yet entered — narrower than the gallery, paneled in darker oak, with the practical quiet of a house turned toward its own business. The door stood open. Mr. Darcy sat behind his desk.

Elizabeth could scarcely have supposed she should ever see this room. This was the part of Pemberley that faces the estate, not its visitors. She had pictured something — one could not help it — a library in miniature, perhaps, or a gentleman's retreat arranged for admiration. What she found was much plainer than that and harder to dismiss. 

The desk was large but not ornamental. Papers lay in ordered stacks. The inkstand had been used recently; a quill rested beside it with the nib still dark. Bookshelves lined two walls, but these were working shelves — ledgers mixed with volumes, a folio of maps, estate plans rolled and tied with cord. Through the window, the prospect gave onto the nearer grounds: not the grand south view that visitors admired, but the working landscape of stable-yard, kitchen garden wall, and the lane toward the home farm.

This was not display. It was responsibility, reduced to paper and ink. Here tenants became names, repairs became figures, and good intentions either acquired dates and signatures or were nothing at all.

Georgiana was already seated in one of three comfortably worn wooden chairs placed before the desk. She wore a morning dress of white muslin and a look of determined composure that was betrayed, entirely, by her hands. They were pressed together in her lap with such fierce stillness that the effort showed in her wrists.

"Mrs. Gardiner," Mr. Darcy said, rising. "Miss Bennet. Thank you for coming."

He was formal. Of course he was formal. Elizabeth might have expected nothing else and yet found herself surprised by the degree of it. He had arranged chairs. He had cleared space on the desk.

Mrs. Gardiner took the offered chair in the center with the ease of a woman who could sit in any room in England and look as if she belonged there. Elizabeth sat in the other and found that it placed her at an angle to the desk — near enough to see Mr. Darcy's face but turned slightly toward Georgiana, as if the arrangement had been designed to prevent anyone from looking at anyone else too directly.

"I wished to consult you," Mr. Darcy said, "on a matter concerning my sister."

Elizabeth kept her eyes on Georgiana. It was easier than looking at anyone else.

"It has been suggested," Mr. Darcy began — and looked at Elizabeth.

The glance lasted no longer than a breath. He corrected it at once, returning his gaze to Mrs. Gardiner with the air of a man who had taken a wrong turning and wished everyone to understand that the road was entirely to blame. But the damage was done. Mrs. Gardiner's expression did not change, which was itself a change.

"It has been observed," he continued, more carefully, "that Georgiana would benefit from the society of a young woman nearer her own age. Miss Bingley's recent visit has made plain that accomplished company is not always — that is to say, the qualities most valued in a drawing room are not invariably those which best serve a young woman's spirits."

He stopped. The sentence had cost him something. Elizabeth could hear the seams.

"My sister has expressed a wish to invite Miss Catherine Bennet to Pemberley."

Georgiana's hands tightened in her lap.

Mrs. Gardiner took a breath in, and did not look at Elizabeth. "Miss Catherine," she said, in the considering tone of a woman who wished to be certain she had heard correctly before allowing herself an opinion.

"For a visit of some weeks," Mr. Darcy said. "Under proper supervision, of course. With your approval and Mr. Bennet's consent."

"I see." Mrs. Gardiner was quiet for a moment. Her gaze moved to Georgiana, whose color had risen, and then — with the unerring instinct of a woman who had raised four children and observed two nieces at close quarters for twenty years — to Elizabeth.

"Lizzy," she said. "Is this what you want for your sister?"

The question was simple. And ruthless.

*For your sister.* Not: for yourself. Mrs. Gardiner had not asked the other question — not here, not in front of everyone. But Elizabeth heard it anyway, like a note struck and then damped.

"Yes," Elizabeth said. "I do."

"Catherine is lively," Mrs. Gardiner observed. "And not always discreet."

Elizabeth drew a breath. "She is warm. She notices when people are uncomfortable and tries to mend it — not always gracefully, but sincerely. This past spring was not easy at Longbourn, and Kitty bore her share of it without complaint and without making herself the subject. This month, she has taken on the Gardiner children when Jane could not manage everything alone, and she did it without being asked."

She paused. "She has a steadiness, and a goodness of heart. She will make Georgiana laugh. She will not make her feel watched. Or alone. She has earned my trust, and I think she will earn Georgiana's.”

To her aunt, she added, "She is not Lydia." 

"No," Mrs. Gardiner said quietly. "She is not." 

Elizabeth's throat was tight. She had not known, until she heard herself say them, how much she meant every word.

Mrs. Gardiner regarded her for a moment. Then she turned back to Mr. Darcy.

"If Catherine is to come, she must be properly escorted."

"I shall write to Mr. Bennet directly," Mr. Darcy said. "And offer my carriage for the journey."

He did not look at Elizabeth when he said it. He did not make the offer appear a gallantry. That restraint did more to disturb her than gallantry would have done.

"Very good. A defined visit — three weeks, perhaps, to begin. If it does not answer, the arrangement can be ended with no injury to anyone's pride. If it does answer —" Mrs. Gardiner smiled. "We shall all be pleasantly surprised."

"I shall not be surprised," Georgiana said, very softly.

Every head turned to her.

"That is —" She faltered. "I only meant from what Miss Elizabeth has told me of Kitty already, and it all sounded — it sounded exactly right."

Mr. Darcy's expression changed. Not much. Not enough for anyone who did not already know the precise geography of his reserve. Elizabeth felt it at once — a small, treacherous softening — and Mrs. Gardiner, with the accuracy of a beloved aunt, felt *that*.

Elizabeth's hands, which had every right to betray her, remained perfectly still. That surprised her more than anything else in the room.

---

The letters were planned before anyone left the room. Mr. Darcy would write formally to Mr. Bennet. Georgiana would write a note to Kitty, to be enclosed in Elizabeth's letter to her father. Elizabeth would write to her father — separately, in her own hand — and a note to Jane.

Mrs. Gardiner would write to Mrs. Bennet.

"Someone must," her aunt said, with the resigned valor of a woman who has prevented many impending domestic disasters. "If your mother were to hear this news from anyone but her own sister, I shall never hear the end of it. Neither, I fear, will Derbyshire."

Elizabeth returned to her room to write.

The letter to her father was simple enough in principle and agonizing in practice. She was writing to Mr. Bennet in favor of Mr. Darcy's household. She was recommending that her father send one of his younger daughters into the care of a man whom, at the Netherfield ball, she had declared—very much to her own satisfaction at the time—the last gentleman in England with whom she could ever be prevailed upon to dance.

She dipped her pen. She tapped the ink out, and set the pen down. She picked it up, and dipped it again.

*My Dear Father—*

*You will, I hope, forgive the liberty I take in writing on a subject which has lately afforded you so much amusement at my expense; but as the comfort of one of your daughters is concerned, I shall venture to be serious for once.*

*Mr. Darcy has proposed to invite Catherine to Pemberley for a few weeks, as a companion to his sister, Miss Georgiana Darcy. Mrs. Gardiner approves the plan, provided Catherine comes properly attended; and Mr. Darcy offers his carriage for the journey.*

*If you see no objection, you will oblige me very much by allowing her to accept. I have found Miss Darcy gentle and anxious to do right; and I believe Kitty’s good humour would be of real service to her.*

*I am, &c.* 

The letter to Jane was even more difficult. Elizabeth managed to write three sides—three pages of a single sheet. She described Georgiana's music, the beauty of the Derbyshire hills, the charm of Mrs. Gardiner's renewed acquaintance with Lambton, the excellence of the coffee. She mentioned Kitty's prospective visit as a practical arrangement for Miss Darcy's companionship. She said nothing whatsoever about Mr. Darcy.

She read it back. Georgiana appeared in nearly every paragraph. Mr. Darcy appeared nowhere.

That would have to do.

On the fifth morning after the packet left Pemberley, an express arrived. 

Elizabeth knew it for an express before she broke the seal. The handwriting was Jane's, but the exorbitant postage had all the look of her mother’s economy.

*My dearest Lizzy,*

*I hardly know where to begin —*

Not a promising opening, Elizabeth reflected, from a sister whose composure could survive nearly anything.

The letter was long. It was also, in places, very nearly funny, though Jane had plainly not intended it to be. Elizabeth read it at the window of the morning room, with the Derbyshire sun warming the paper, while Georgiana practiced scales and Mrs. Gardiner sat calmly mending a stocking as if the entire county of Hertfordshire had not apparently lost its mind.

Jane reported the following:

Mrs. Bennet, upon receiving her portion of the packet, had required salts, a glass of wine, and the immediate attendance of Hill; and in the agitation of the moment had insisted — before the whole household — that she could not possibly allow any daughter to be sent so far without a mother at hand. She had then gone on, with equal warmth, to wonder why Catherine was to be chosen, when Jane was at home.

Lydia had wailed through breakfast, dinner, and supper for two consecutive days; and the Gardiner children, who are not yet formed to such exertions of patience, suffered accordingly. The weather, happily, was fair, and those who could contrive a walk or an errand were much abroad; but indoors there was little respite from Lydia’s accounts of her hardships, and of the great house and great situation for which she had been destined, and which—by some perverse arrangement of things—had fallen to the one sister who would not know what to do with it.

Kitty did not know what to think and had said so, repeatedly, to anyone who would listen and several people who would not.

At least one mystery was quickly solved. Mr. Gardiner had stopped at Longbourn for an evening on his way to London and had given a partial account of the Pemberley arrangements, which had satisfied no one and alarmed everyone differently.

The Gardiner children, hearing that their cousin Lizzie might remain in Derbyshire even longer, had declared they would not be left alone with Lydia and Mary when Jane went out in the evenings, as Mary read to them from Fordyce and Lydia made them act out scenes from novels she would not name.

*Aunt Gardiner writes to Mama that you are "very well situated,"* Jane continued, *which I am sure she meant kindly; but the phrase has not quieted me as much as she perhaps intended.*

Elizabeth set the letter down a moment. What had Jane taken from that phrase?

What had their mother?

*I do not wish to distress you, Lizzy, but your letter has made me anxious. Are you quite comfortable at Pemberley? You write pages about Miss Darcy and the land and the pianoforte, and say nothing of Mr. Darcy himself. I cannot forget what you told me of his behavior in Kent, nor can I be easy if you are sacrificing your own peace to help a young girl you have known barely a fortnight.*

*If you are easy, write only that, and I shall believe you. But if you are not, do not make Miss Darcy's comfort of more consequence than your own. Kitty can manage without Pemberley; Miss Darcy has those who love her. We cannot spare you to a duty that is not yours.*

Elizabeth folded the letter. She pressed her hands flat upon the table and sat very still.

Jane had read the silence louder than the words. 

*Are you quite comfortable at Pemberley?*

No. Her room was comfortable enough; her thoughts were not.

Worse, she had begun to defend Pemberley without being asked. She had begun to write of its people as if her opinion might matter to them. And even if she had not mentioned him in her letter, the master of the house was always present.

She picked up the letter and read it again.

---

Two days later, Mrs. Gardiner and Mrs. Annesley had taken Georgiana into Lambton for the afternoon — ribbons, or sheet music, or some errand that required three women and a carriage — and Elizabeth was alone in the morning room when the regular post brought two letters from Longbourn.

The first was addressed to Mr. Darcy in a hand Elizabeth recognized as her father's. It was sealed with the Bennet arms, which Mr. Bennet used so rarely that finding the seal must have required Hill, a candle, and twenty minutes of searching through the library desk.

The second was addressed to her.

There was nothing for Mrs. Gardiner. That was either an oversight or a declaration.

*My dear Lizzy,*

*I have received Mr. Darcy's very civil letter, and your own — with Miss Darcy's kind note to your sister enclosed. I found your letter interesting for what it contained and more interesting for what it did not.*

*I shall bring Catherine to Pemberley myself. She has agreed, after the requisite tears, that she can be ready by Thursday. I shall deliver her into Mr. Darcy's care in good order and retrieve her in three weeks if she has not been sent home sooner.*

*I confess some curiosity to see the place that has wrought such a change in my Lizzy's opinions. I recall a time, not very distant, when a certain gentleman's name could not be mentioned in your hearing without provoking a very decided opinion — but I shall not finish that thought, as I perceive you would rather I did not.*

*Your affectionate father*

Elizabeth read it twice. She read the last line a third time. Then she set it down on the table beside Jane's earlier letter and regarded both as a woman might regard two witnesses who had given testimony she could not refute.

Her father was coming to Pemberley.

Her father, who missed nothing and said everything, was coming to the house where she had been living for a week, at the invitation of a man she had once despised, in defense of a sister she had once been ashamed of. And he would remember every opinion she had ever been pleased to pronounce.

And Mr. Darcy, who had once named her father among the family's failings, would be watching.

The man himself appeared at the morning-room door. He held his own letter from Mr. Bennet — opened, read — and something else: a folded sheet, smaller, written in a round uncertain hand.

"Mr. Bennet has given his consent," he said. His voice was formal. His expression was something else entirely. Elizabeth dared not look long enough to determine what.

"He tells me he is coming himself,” she said to his boots.

Mr. Darcy received this in silence. His eyes lowered briefly to the letter in his hand, and when he looked up again there was, about his mouth, the faintest movement.

“It might have been Mrs. Bennet,” he said, after a moment.

Elizabeth’s gaze jumped to his face. Was he laughing? 

“I have ordered the carriage sent to Longbourn,” he continued. “The coachmen will be instructed to make all reasonable haste.”

"My father does not deliberate when he has decided."

"So I gather." He looked down at the smaller letter in his hand. "Miss Catherine has written to Georgiana."

He held it out.

*Dear Miss Darcy,*

*I am very much obliged to you for your kind letter and for the honor of your invitation. I have never been to Derbyshire but Elizabeth says it is very beautiful and that you are fond of music, which I am too, though not so accomplished as I should like. I hope I shall not be too noisy for Pemberley. Elizabeth says it is a very fine house and I shall try to deserve it.*

*Your obedient servant,*

*Catherine Bennet*

*P.S. — Elizabeth also says you have kittens. I should very much like to see them if it is not too much trouble.*

Elizabeth looked up. Mr. Darcy was watching her face.

"She will do very well," he said.

He did not smile. He did not make a compliment of it. The concession was plain, and therefore worth more.

She handed the letter back. Their fingers did not touch.

"Georgiana will be delighted," she said — and heard, too late, how certain she sounded. As if she had any right to be certain of Miss Darcy’s happiness. As if Pemberley’s pleasures had become matters in which she might speak with confidence.

"Yes," he said. "I think she will."

Jane's words rang in her ears.

*Are you quite comfortable at Pemberley?*

No.

Comfort had very little to do with it.
