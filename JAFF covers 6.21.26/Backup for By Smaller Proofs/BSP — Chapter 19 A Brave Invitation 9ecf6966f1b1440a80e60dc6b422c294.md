# BSP — Chapter 19: A Brave Invitation

In the small dining room, no one spoke for a full second. Then everyone did.

"Mr. Darcy?" said Mr. Gardiner.

"Below?" said Mrs. Gardiner.

"Yes, ma'am," said the landlord, satisfied with the effect. "Miss Darcy is with him."

Elizabeth looked down at the broken pen in her hand as if it had acted without her permission. Ink had not spilled, by some mercy. The point lay upon the tray, small and black and accusing.

Her pulse had collected absurdly in her thumb. A minute earlier, Mr. Darcy had been a thought she must not touch; now he was below stairs, asking to be admitted.

"I beg your pardon," she said to no one in particular.

Mrs. Gardiner rose. "Of course, we are at home, Mr. Pratt. Pray show them into the private parlor. We shall attend them directly."

"Yes, ma'am."

The landlord withdrew.

For long moments, the room became a campaign. Mrs. Gardiner looked toward the glass above the mantel; Elizabeth looked toward the door; Mr. Gardiner looked at both ladies and had the wisdom not to look amused until his back was half turned.

"My cap," said Mrs. Gardiner.

"It is perfectly well.”

"It is perfectly well for luncheon with my husband and niece. It is not perfectly well for Miss Darcy."

"Your cap is better bred than most people we know," said Mr. Gardiner.

"Edward."

Elizabeth crossed to her aunt, untied one ribbon, retied it, and was far too conscious of her own fingers. "There."

"And you, Lizzy?"

"I am very well."

"You have ink on your thumb."

Mr. Gardiner offered his handkerchief with the solemnity of a man presenting a diplomatic instrument. "For the honor of Hertfordshire."

She took it. "I expect Derbyshire has seen worse."

"Not in a private parlor, I hope."

Mrs. Gardiner's eyes met Elizabeth's in the glass. The look lasted only a moment but spoke volumes: compose yourself; do not be ashamed; I see you.

Elizabeth returned the handkerchief and smoothed the front of her gown: the muslin Miss Darcy had already seen at tea, but the short blue jacket changed the silhouette to make it different enough. She hoped.

She smoothed the jacket once more, though there was nothing wrong with it.

"Shall we?" said Mrs. Gardiner.

They went down.

---

As they entered the private parlor, Miss Darcy rose from the sofa near the window.

It was not a large room, but the inn had done the best it could. A saffron carpet covered most of the floor; two chairs sat by the empty grate, two sofas loitered near the window. A round table still bore the landlord's idea of elegance: a bowl of late roses, one dropping petals into the water, and the open casement gave onto the inn yard softened by vine leaves. Voices came and went beyond it.

Mr. Darcy stood near the window. At Elizabeth's entrance, he turned too quickly to have been looking at anything outside with real attention.

Then his attention found her, with an intensity no bow could quite disguise.

He inquired after their health; he expressed regret for having called at an inconvenient hour, though a letter refusing Miss Darcy's invitation had almost been begun upstairs. Yet in the instant before civility restored his face, Elizabeth had seen something unguarded: expectation, checked almost at once; relief, perhaps; and something more singular than mere civility allowed.

The sight went through her before she could summon any answer to it. She felt it first as heat in her face, then as a wish to look away, then as an equally strong wish not to.

Instead, she smiled at Miss Darcy.

That was safer. It was also not safe at all.

Miss Darcy's pleasure at seeing them had escaped before she could make it formal. Her color had risen; her hands were folded together; her curtsey, though correct, had a gladness in it no master could have taught.

"We feared we should miss you," she said.

Elizabeth distrusted how glad she was to be the object of it. "You are very kind,” she said. “We were just speaking of writing to you."

"And so we are fortunate," said Mrs. Gardiner, taking Miss Darcy's offered hands with warm ease. "A letter would have deprived us of the pleasure of thanking you in person for last evening."

Miss Darcy looked pleased and uncertain what to do with so much direct gratitude. "It gave me great pleasure."

Mr. Darcy's gaze moved to his sister. His expression altered in a manner too small to name and too plain to miss. Elizabeth saw pride there, and care too: the care with which he kept pride from becoming a demand.

Mrs. Gardiner took the spot on the sofa nearest Miss Darcy, as if by accident and with too much skill for accident. Mr. Gardiner chose the chair from which he could see everyone and obstruct no one. Elizabeth was left the end of the sofa, which placed her not opposite Mr. Darcy precisely, but near enough that any glance from him had a shorter journey than she liked.

For a few minutes there were inquiries and answers. Mrs. Hurst's health. Mr. Bingley's departure. The road to London. The beauty of the morning. Mr. Gardiner's letters. Here Miss Darcy's attention sharpened, and Mr. Darcy's hand, resting upon the windowsill, stilled.

"Is it true," Miss Darcy began, "that you might be obliged to leave Lambton sooner than you expected?"

Mrs. Gardiner glanced once at Elizabeth. "My husband's partner has been taken ill. The business requires Mr. Gardiner's presence sooner than any of us wished."

Miss Darcy's face softened. "I am sorry to hear it."

"So am I," said Mr. Gardiner, "though less on my own account than on my wife's. She has been promised several Derbyshire prospects and is in danger of receiving only letters from warehouses in exchange."

Miss Darcy smiled. "That *is* a poor exchange."

"A scandalous one," agreed Mrs. Gardiner.

"And the children?" Miss Darcy asked. "They are well?"

"Quite well. We have had another letter from Miss Bennet this morning. They are well settled at Longbourn, and eagerly awaiting a litter of kittens before the week is out."

"Delightful," said Miss Darcy.

A brief look of amusement passed over Mr. Darcy’s face.

“Brother?”

"Pemberley may soon be in the same condition," he said. "Or so my groom has warned me."

"Then Derbyshire and Hertfordshire are in perfect accord," said Mr. Gardiner.

There the conversation might have returned to safe ground. Elizabeth could feel everyone moving toward it: weather, roads, the old church, Mrs. Gardiner's childhood walks. Then Miss Darcy looked down at her hands.

"It seems a pity," she said, "to lose so much of Derbyshire when it was so nearly promised to you."

The sentence was not an invitation. It was a sigh given shape. Elizabeth saw Miss Darcy hear herself and falter. "I mean—only—that Mrs. Gardiner spoke of so many places she remembered. And Miss Bennet has seen very little yet. And if Mr. Gardiner must go—" She stopped.

Mr. Darcy gave her his whole attention, steady as a hand offered but not forced upon her.

Miss Darcy glanced at him and drew a breath. "Oh, I am saying it badly." But she did not retreat. 

"If there were any way for you to remain near Lambton," Miss Darcy said, "I should be very happy."

Mrs. Gardiner leaned toward her by a fraction. "I understand you perfectly," Mrs. Gardiner said, gently enough to make the words kind rather than final.

Elizabeth did not know whether to be grateful or afraid.

For a moment no one supplied the answer Miss Darcy had not quite dared to ask.

Then Mr. Darcy spoke. "Why not?"

The words were quiet. Miss Darcy looked up, not rescued from her own wish, but steadied in it.

"Why should Mrs. Gardiner and Miss Bennet lose the rest of their tour if Mr. Gardiner is obliged to return?” he said. “If they would accept Pemberley as a temporary situation, the arrangement might answer every purpose."

Elizabeth's pulse, which had behaved with tolerable civility through the visit so far, forgot itself. Her hands had closed upon each other before she knew they had moved.

Mr. Darcy continued, and now his voice had altered. It was still formal, but a thread had entered it that made the courtesy feel less general than it sounded. "Pemberley can accommodate such an arrangement without the least inconvenience. My sister would be grateful for your company. Mrs. Gardiner would be within reach of Lambton, and of all the places she wished to revisit. Mr. Gardiner could continue to London with less concern." He paused. "The invitation would, of course, be to Mrs. Gardiner as much as to Miss Bennet—and only for such days as remained convenient within your original plan."

So proper. So complete. So impossible to object to without seeming ungracious, fearful, or both.

Relief stole into Elizabeth before judgment could stop it, and was therefore more alarming than any objection.

Its danger lay in its fairness. With this offer, he had left every person in the room a respectable reason to consent and himself no visible claim upon their gratitude. Mrs. Gardiner's comfort, Mr. Gardiner's peace of mind, Miss Darcy's company, the unfinished Derbyshire tour — all had been considered, arranged, and offered back to them as if his own wishes had no share in it.

And yet she could not believe his wishes had no share in it. She was in danger of thinking well of him. 

Elizabeth gripped her hands, folded very correctly in her lap. They looked composed. They did not make her so.

Miss Darcy added, before her courage could desert her, "I should like it very much. If you wished it. I mean, if it would not be inconvenient."

She stopped, pressed her hands together, and sighed. "Pemberley is so quiet now."

The last sentence did it. Not the logic of carriages and inns and two remaining weeks. Not Mr. Darcy's careful propriety.

Miss Darcy's eyes met Elizabeth's for only a moment, then turned to Mrs. Gardiner. It was not childish pleading. It was a young woman's very careful claim upon pleasure.

Mrs. Gardiner heard it too.

Mr. Gardiner, who had been watching the exchange with the calm seriousness he brought to matters of importance, looked to his wife. "It would certainly relieve me of a concern."

"Yes," said Mrs. Gardiner.

"And it would not lengthen your absence beyond what we had already intended."

"No."

"And Jane's letter is reassuring."

"Yes."

Mrs. Gardiner turned to Mr. Darcy. "You are very good, sir. But we could not impose upon Miss Darcy's kindness."

"You would not impose," said Miss Darcy, more quickly than before. She seemed startled by her forwardness, but did not take the words back. "I should be very glad."

Mr. Darcy looked at Elizabeth then.

Only for an instant. Long enough for Elizabeth to feel that the answer mattered to him in a way no arrangement of rooms and carriages could explain. Then he looked away, as if he had remembered himself.

"It would be a great kindness to my sister," he said to the room.

His voice had changed again on the last two words. My sister. Not a display. Not a tactic.

Elizabeth knew she might still object. She could say that London was safest, that Mrs. Gardiner's duty lay with her children, that a young woman did not remove from an inn to a gentleman's house because the gentleman's sister was lonely and the gentleman himself had learned how to ask without asking. Every sentence would be defensible. Every sentence would be cowardice dressed as prudence.

She said none of them.

Mrs. Gardiner's hand moved in her lap, once, as if closing gently upon a decision.

Elizabeth understood, before her aunt spoke, that she would accept.

She ought to have felt alarm. She did feel alarm. It beat high in her throat and made the room too warm. But pleasure was there too: the sudden image of Pemberley in morning light. Miss Darcy beside her at the piano. Mr. Darcy somewhere in the same house, and herself with every proper reason to remain. The two feelings had come together, and she could not separate them.

"We should be honored," Mrs. Gardiner said, "if Miss Darcy is certain she would not repent such kindness."

Miss Darcy looked as though repentance had never been further from her mind. "I am certain."

Mr. Gardiner smiled. "Well, then. I shall go to London with a lighter conscience than Baxter deserves."

The remark restored the room to motion. Miss Darcy smiled outright, freely, as if she had forgotten to be careful. Mrs. Gardiner began at once upon the practical questions: luggage, rooms, carriages, timing. Mr. Darcy answered with calm precision: Mr. Gardiner's departure, the carriage, the trunks, no haste, no obligation, only what suited Mrs. Gardiner best. Within minutes, what had been impossible before noon had acquired dates, rooms, carriage-times, and a measure of anticipation.

Nothing was to exceed the time they had already meant to spend in Derbyshire. That point was settled plainly, and somehow made the whole thing more difficult to distrust.

Only then did Elizabeth realize how still she had been. She let out her breath carefully, as if too sudden an exhale might reveal too much.

She had wished to stay.

He had made it possible to stay.

It was all perfectly proper.

That was the trouble.
