# BSP — Chapter 12: The Horse Chestnut

Tuesday came in with a low white heat over Lambton, the kind that made the hills seem nearer and the stones of the main street look older than they were.

Elizabeth had spent Sunday afternoon in the expectation of Tuesday. She had spent Monday in a still worse condition: expecting it with the assistance of leisure. There had been walks with her aunt, a drive with her uncle, a dutiful admiration of two prospects, three lanes, and one bridge, and through every object deserving attention — every tree, gate, signboard, and remembered house of Mrs. Gardiner's girlhood — Tuesday had stood behind the view like a person waiting to be named.

By Tuesday morning, she had grown so accustomed to dread that its arrival seemed almost an impertinence. It should, after two days' residence in her mind, have had the civility to be less fresh.

It had not.

She drew on her gloves, discovered a wrinkle across the left palm, drew one off again, and began a second campaign against the fingers.

"Lizzy," said Mrs. Gardiner, from her place by the sitting-room window, "if you mean to exhaust yourself before Mr. Darcy has paid his call, I must object. It would be awkward to explain."

"I have no such intention. I am only discovering that Derbyshire air is destructive to kid."

"Derbyshire air has many qualities. I had not known that one."

Elizabeth smiled, because smiling was expected, and because her aunt's tenderness had put on humor for her sake. The glove came right at last. She let her hands fall.

Mrs. Gardiner had already determined, with the quiet authority of a woman who understood her niece, that if Miss Darcy liked it they should walk into the town after the call. Elizabeth, she said, would do better in the open air, even in the heat, than in any stiff little sitting-room visit. 

Mr. Gardiner, having devoted Monday morning to the Pemberley water with rods, tackle, and all the moral seriousness proper to a gentleman authorized to fish by another gentleman of ten thousand a year, had returned with one modest trout and three excellent observations on the character of rivers.

"The trout was small," Mrs. Gardiner had said at breakfast, "but your uncle's account of it was very nourishing."

"I have always maintained," Mr. Gardiner replied, "that a fish gains dignity by being difficult to catch."

"And loses it again by being eaten," said Elizabeth.

So they had laughed, and the morning had gone on; and all the while Elizabeth had listened for she knew not what — wheels, footsteps, a voice below, any alteration in the common sounds of the inn.

A little after twelve, the alteration came: a door below, a step upon the stair, Mrs. Staveley’s voice a bit wobblier than usual.

Mrs. Gardiner folded her work, set it aside, and said only, "There." Mr. Gardiner, who had been reading yesterday's newspaper with every appearance of attending to it, set the page on the side table.

Elizabeth stood.

Her gloves were on and straight. The sentence she had been arranging since Sunday — *I am very happy to make your acquaintance, Miss Darcy* — had been practiced so often that it had lost all resemblance to natural speech and now sat in her mind like a visiting-card left by someone she did not know.

A moment later, Mrs. Staveley appeared at the sitting-room door: a neat, capable woman with a cap as white as her apron. Behind her stood Mr. Darcy and a shadow that must be his sister. Elizabeth had time to think of Ramsgate. She had time to rebuke herself for thinking of Ramsgate. She had time to remember that Georgiana Darcy had not chosen to be the subject of anyone's pity.

Mr. Darcy entered first.

This was, Elizabeth thought, a kindness to his sister and a trial to everyone else. He looked as he had not looked on the gravel: no dust on his boots, no road-weariness in his face, nothing disordered but the brief arrest of his eyes when they found hers. Elizabeth felt the room become too small for the breath she had taken. He bowed to Mr. Gardiner first, then to Mrs. Gardiner, then to Elizabeth, with all proper composure; yet the look that met Elizabeth's for the length of a breath was not quite the look of a man paying an ordinary call. It held too much to be ordinary, and too little that could be spoken of.

"Mr. Gardiner. Mrs. Gardiner. Miss Bennet."

Then he turned, and the young lady behind him stepped forward.

Miss Darcy was a willow. Taller than Elizabeth had expected, and formed on a scale that promised, in another year or two, a very handsome woman. At sixteen, she was in that uncertain country between girl and woman where a composed manner may make youth more visible rather than less. Her clear complexion, gentle reserve, and air of being carefully prepared for every possible civility reminded Elizabeth, painfully, of Jane.

Elizabeth liked her at once. Which was inconvenient.

"Mr. Gardiner. Mrs. Gardiner," said Mr. Darcy, and the alteration in his voice was so slight that perhaps no one but Elizabeth, who had nothing to do but notice him, would have heard it. "May I present my sister, Miss Darcy. Georgiana — Mr. and Mrs. Gardiner, of Gracechurch Street; Miss Bennet."

Miss Darcy curtsied. Her eyes glanced from the Gardiners to Elizabeth and away again, then returned with a small, brave effort.

"I am very happy to make your acquaintance," she said.

So. Elizabeth's sentence had escaped and found another mouth.

Mr. Gardiner received the introduction with his usual pleasant ease; Mrs. Gardiner, with all the warmth that could be offered without overwhelming a shy girl. "We are very pleased you have been so good as to come to us," she said. "I hope your journey yesterday did not fatigue you excessively."

"Not me," said Miss Darcy. Then, as if fearing the answer had been too abrupt, she added, "That is — I was not ill from it. Some of our party were less fortunate."

Mr. Darcy's eyes went to his sister, not in correction, but encouragement. Miss Darcy saw it and breathed in a little more deeply.

"We stopped on Sunday at a posting inn where the supper did not agree with everyone. Mr. and Mrs. Hurst and Miss Bingley have all suffered for it, and the heat of yesterday's road did not improve matters. My companion and I were spared. Mr. Bingley ate everything and is perfectly well, which seems unfair to everyone else."

The last words came out almost under her breath, but there was a hint of mischief in them — a small brightness, quickly offered and almost as quickly recalled.

Mr. Bingley. Elizabeth blinked slowly against the tide of affront toward the name. Jane's sorrow had a face again; and Mr. Darcy stood beside it in her mind, grave, composed, and not to be acquitted. 

Mr. Darcy looked down at Georgiana, the only person in the room who could. "Bingley's constitution has never been governed by ordinary principles."

Miss Darcy's mouth lifted before she could prevent it.

The change was astonishing. Not large; not enough to be called animation. The formal young lady had become, for a second, a sister. Elizabeth saw it and, more dangerously, saw him seeing it. His whole attention had sharpened toward Georgiana, softened around her, and then withdrawn just enough to let her stand by herself.

Not politeness. Not propriety. Feeling, under command.

Elizabeth had not known command could look so much like tenderness.

"Mr. Bingley is at Pemberley, then?" Mrs. Gardiner asked, with no more emphasis than the question required.

"Yes," said Mr. Darcy. "He desired me to offer his compliments."

Elizabeth's fingers curled inside her glove.

Mr. Bingley, within five miles. Mr. Bingley sending compliments, amiable and unconscious, perhaps, while Jane sat at Longbourn with all her sweetness and all her wounds folded neatly out of sight. 

The room seemed, for one instant, to contain not four people but all the winter: Netherfield deserted, London unanswered, Jane's quiet face turned toward the window, Mr. Darcy's letter in Elizabeth's hand declaring, with the severity of a man convinced of his own justice, that he had detached his friend from her sister.

That man was speaking to Mr. Gardiner about her uncle’s morning on the Pemberley water. His manner was easy; his answer exactly judged; his face, if he felt the old accusation pass through the room, gave no sign of it.

No. That was unjust. It was not an accusation passing through the room. It was only passing through Elizabeth.

And had he been unjust? Yes. She would hold to that. Jane's happiness had been treated as a point in another gentleman's calculation; her reserve had been misread, her modesty punished. Nothing in Derbyshire could alter that.

But then Miss Darcy, having been asked by Mrs. Gardiner whether she had often been in Lambton, turned instinctively toward her brother as if the answer belonged to both of them.

"When we were children," Mr. Darcy said, "Georgiana believed Lambton existed chiefly to supply gingerbread and ribbons."

"I was very young," said Miss Darcy, with dignity.

"You were eleven."

"Eleven is young."

"Not when one is demanding blue ribbon with the firmness of a woman of business."

Miss Darcy's blush came with a smile in it. Mrs. Gardiner laughed softly, and Elizabeth, against every principle of self-preservation, nearly laughed too.

He cared for his sister with an attention that had nothing languid in it. Every glance, every small turn of speech, made a place for her to stand. If he loved Mr. Bingley with half so watchful a tenderness — if his friendship had something of this same anxious guardianship in it — then his interference became, not better, but more understandable. A proud man, accustomed to seeing danger before the person he loved saw it; a decisive man, too certain of his own reading; a protective man who mistook silence for indifference because silence, to him, concealed so much.

Not forgiven. Not even softened.

But seen, perhaps, from another side.

Mrs. Gardiner, with the tact that made conversation move as if everyone present had helped arrange it, proposed that if Miss Darcy was not fatigued, they might walk a little. Lambton had changed in some respects and not at all in others; she would like to know which parts Miss Darcy recognized and which parts belonged only to her own memory. Mr. Gardiner declared himself entirely at the disposal of any party that promised shade.

Miss Darcy looked at her brother.

Elizabeth saw the question before he answered it. Not permission exactly. Something older and gentler than permission: *Will this do? Am I right to wish it?*

"If Mrs. Gardiner is not afraid of the heat," he said, "I think the walk would be pleasant."

"I was a Lambton girl," said Mrs. Gardiner. "We were not made of such delicate materials."

Miss Darcy smiled, really smiled, and the matter was settled.

Outside, Lambton received them with a warm curiosity. A woman at the baker's door looked up and curtsied slightly. Mr. Darcy touched his hat. A boy with a hoop drew back against the wall, not in fear but in instant, practiced respect, and stared with open admiration at Miss Darcy's pelisse until his mother, from within the shop, said his name in a tone that restored him to motion. An older man on the opposite side of the street nodded to Mr. Darcy and received a nod in return.

Back in Hertfordshire, Mr. Darcy had been remarked upon because he was rich, proud, handsome, disagreeable, and not given to dancing. In Lambton he was remarked upon hardly at all, and yet. Faces altered as he passed — not startled, not frowning, not greedy for notice. A twitch of a smile here. A respectful lift of the hand there. A shopkeeper's wife inclining her head with something very like pride. They made way for him as one makes way for a person known to belong to the place, not as one makes way for a stranger of consequence.

Miss Darcy walked at first beside Mrs. Gardiner, with Mr. Darcy on her other side. Mr. Gardiner fell in beside Mr. Darcy with some remark on the river, and Elizabeth was left with the agreeable advantage of observing them under cover of lingering to admire the shop windows. The arrangement did not last. Mrs. Gardiner paused twice — once to point out a changed sign, once to identify the house where a former neighbor had lived — and by the second pause, Miss Darcy had turned enough that Elizabeth was included in the conversation almost without anyone deciding it.

"Mrs. Gardiner says there was once a milliner here," Miss Darcy said, indicating a narrow shop now given over to books, prints, and stationery. "But I cannot remember it."

"You are very fortunate," said Elizabeth. "A milliner remembered from childhood can haunt a family for years."

Miss Darcy looked alarmed for half a second, then saw Elizabeth's smile and answered it shyly. 

"Although my aunt may not agree. She has spoken very feelingly of Lambton ribbons."

"They were very fine ribbons," Mrs. Gardiner said. "And very serious matters depended on them."

"Such as?" asked Miss Darcy.

"Whether a girl of twelve should be admired by everyone in church, naturally." Elizabeth included Georgiana in her smile.

Miss Darcy was surprised into a small laugh.

Mr. Darcy looked at Elizabeth then. Only a glance. The others were still smiling over Mrs. Gardiner's ribbons. His attention crossed the little space between them with no word attached. 

Elizabeth looked away first.

They turned from the busier part of the street into a lane that sloped gently toward the churchyard’s far wall. The air changed there. Less stableyard, more grass warmed by sun; less conversation from open doors, more bees in a border of lavender behind a low stone wall. At the end of the lane stood the horse chestnut.

A magnificent tree, its limbs stretched wide, its leaves glossy. Its shadow lay over the path in irregular pieces. The trunk had divided low, as if it had been made expressly to tempt children and then punish them for accepting.

"There," said Mrs. Gardiner, stopping with delight so unguarded that Elizabeth turned to look at her. "Oh, it is so much larger."

Miss Darcy looked from the tree to Mrs. Gardiner. "You know it?"

"Know it? My brother and I nearly lived in it for two summers. Or we thought we did. I daresay we never climbed higher than this." She held her hand scarcely above her shoulder. "At the time, however, we considered ourselves intrepid."

Miss Darcy's eyes brightened. "You climbed it?"

"Repeatedly. And competitively. My brother maintained, without evidence, that he could reach a certain narrow branch before I could. I maintained, with equal justice, that he cheated by beginning before the count was finished."

"Who won?" asked Miss Darcy.

"I did, of course," said Mrs. Gardiner. "It is one of the privileges of memory that it may arrange these matters correctly."

Miss Darcy laughed again, and this time the sound was clear enough that Mr. Darcy's face changed.  His mouth softened. His eyes smiled. The expression was so swift and so unguarded that Elizabeth felt, absurdly, as if she had intruded.

"You did not fall?" Miss Darcy asked.

"Oh, no. The tree was smaller then, and we were sensible children."

Mr. Darcy made a sound that might, in another man, have been called amusement.

Mrs. Gardiner turned to him. "You doubt the general sensibility of Lambton children, Mr. Darcy?"

"I would not presume to question local evidence."

"But you possess contrary evidence?"

"I have one instance."

Miss Darcy looked up at him. "Is this the tree?"

He hesitated.

It was slight. A pause no longer than the shade of a leaf crossing his coat. Yet Elizabeth saw that the story had not been intended for company, or not for this company. Georgiana had asked without calculation. Mrs. Gardiner's memory had opened a door, and Miss Darcy, delighted to find someone else with a childhood in Lambton, had stepped through it.

Mr. Darcy could have closed it. A gentleman with his command of manner could have turned the subject with perfect ease.

He did not.

"This tree," he said. "Unless Lambton has another with the same inclination to punish presumption. Mrs. Reynolds has never forgiven it."

"Mrs. Reynolds has never forgiven *you*," said Miss Darcy, and then blushed at her own boldness.

"Also true."

Mrs. Gardiner's eyes warmed. "Mrs. Reynolds was there?"

"Mrs. Reynolds had brought us into Lambton that morning on some household errand, and arrived after the fact," he said. "Which, from her point of view, was worse. I had been told not to climb it."

"How old were you?" Elizabeth heard herself ask.

His eyes came to hers. "Seven."

"That’s very young," said Miss Darcy, with sisterly loyalty.

"I was old enough to understand the word *no*."

"And young enough," said Mrs. Gardiner, "to consider it an invitation to further thought."

Mr. Darcy inclined his head. "Precisely."

The story, once begun, came with more ease than Elizabeth expected. Not with flourish; Mr. Darcy did not magically become a man of anecdotes. But he told it plainly: the summer morning, the forbidden branch, the conviction — apparently well-founded until the final moment — that the prohibition had underestimated both his abilities and the tree's hospitality. 

Miss Darcy listened with an expression of happy concentration. She must have heard the story before; Elizabeth could tell by the way she waited for certain words. But she loved hearing it from him. Loved, too, perhaps, that he would tell it here, before people not of the family.

"And did you cry?" Mrs. Gardiner asked.

Miss Darcy looked scandalized.

Mr. Darcy looked at Mrs. Gardiner with gravity. "No."

"Ah," said Mrs. Gardiner.

"I roared."

Miss Darcy's hand flew to her mouth. Elizabeth laughed before she could stop herself.

Mrs. Gardiner recovered first. "Then I am relieved. A silent injury is always alarming. A roaring one gives the household useful information."

"That was Mrs. Reynolds's view," said Mr. Darcy. "She informed my father that my lungs were in excellent order, whatever might be said of my judgment."

"And what was said of your judgment?" Elizabeth asked.

His look came to her again, steadier this time. "Very little that was flattering."

It should have been nothing. A commonplace answer. Yet the words landed with more meaning than he had, perhaps, meant to put into them.

Elizabeth turned toward the chestnut leaves.

Miss Darcy, fortunately, had not followed that line of thought. She was looking from Mrs. Gardiner to the tree with renewed interest. "And you truly climbed this one?” she said. "I should like to have tried such a thing.”

There was a small hunger in the sentence, quickly hidden. Not for the climb itself, perhaps, but for the picture of it: a girl racing her brother without masters, without a companion, without the whole great apparatus of improvement and caution that Elizabeth suspected had governed much of Georgiana Darcy's life.

Mrs. Gardiner heard it too. "Then you must imagine me very fleet and my brother very slow. That is how I prefer the story."

Miss Darcy smiled, and the smile stayed.

As they strolled, Miss Darcy asked Mrs. Gardiner whether the churchyard had changed, and whether the lane had always bent so sharply by the wall, and whether Lambton winters had been very severe. She asked Elizabeth if Hertfordshire had many such trees, then looked embarrassed by her own eagerness until Elizabeth said that Hertfordshire possessed trees in abundance, though very few with such a history of moral instruction.

"The fault was not the tree's," said Mr. Darcy.

"No?" said Elizabeth.

"No. The tree performed its office. I misjudged the branch."

There was a pause where there should not have been one. Mrs. Gardiner was speaking to Miss Darcy just ahead of them; Mr. Gardiner attending them genially. The lane was public; a cart had stopped at the turn to let them pass. And yet the sentence seemed to hold between them.

*I misjudged the branch.*

Elizabeth could not answer it.

By the time they turned back toward the inn, the sun had moved just enough to narrow the shadow at their feet. Elizabeth was warm, and more weary than the walk could account for. Miss Darcy's reserve had not vanished; it had only opened, like a door left ajar in a house still mostly closed. Yet through that opening Elizabeth had seen enough to make retreat impossible.

At the inn door, the formal world gathered itself again. Mr. Darcy called for the carriage, but it was already being brought forward.

Mrs. Gardiner thanked Miss Darcy for the pleasure of the call. Mr. Gardiner added his own thanks with a compliment to Lambton shade that made Miss Darcy smile. She began the proper answer, then stopped.

"If you are not otherwise engaged," she said, turning a little toward Mrs. Gardiner and a little, unmistakably, toward Elizabeth, "we should be very happy — I mean, I should be very happy — if you would all dine with us at Pemberley."

Mr. Darcy's head turned.

Not sharply. Nothing in Mr. Darcy's public manner had ever been so ungoverned as sharpness. But the movement was immediate.

"Georgiana," he said, gently.

She looked at him, and the brightness in her face faltered.

Elizabeth saw the falter and resented him for causing it.

He stepped half a pace nearer his sister, enough to include her, not enough to silence her. "I believe we had better not ask Mr. and Mrs. Gardiner to commit themselves to dinner while the house remains in such disorder. Miss Bingley and Mrs. Hurst are not yet recovered, and Hurst is making the best of his confinement with more philosophy than appetite. Tomorrow, perhaps, if Mr. and Mrs. Gardiner and Miss Bennet are so good as to allow it — tea would be less fatiguing to everyone."

Tea. Not dinner. Not a grand invitation. Not a retreat either.

Miss Darcy's face cleared at once. "Yes. Tea. If you would come. I should like that very much."

There was the girl again, not quite hidden by the heiress.

"We should be very pleased,” Mrs. Gardiner said, her smile warm.

"Very pleased," said Mr. Gardiner. "I shall come prepared to praise the trout again, if required."

Elizabeth said what she must say. "Thank you. I should be happy to come."

Mr. Darcy bowed. 

He had hardly spoken to her all morning. Not directly. Not in any way that could be placed before an aunt and uncle and called particular. Yet as he raised his head, his eyes found hers.

This time she did not look away first.
