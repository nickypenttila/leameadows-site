# BSP — Chapter 11: The Note

By eight o'clock, the Rose had already begun its day: boots on the stair, voices in the yard, the smell of toast and wet stone rising together from below. Elizabeth came down to breakfast with the air of a young woman who had slept perfectly well, and the complexion of one who had not slept at all.

She had made, before leaving her chamber, the usual arrangements with her face. A little cold water. A little pinning and repinning of hair. A little straightening of the lace at her cuffs. In the small glass above the washstand, she’d found nothing more damning than paleness, and paleness could be attributed to heat, to travel, to a strange bed, to the Derbyshire air, to any number of innocent causes that had not stood in front of her yesterday on a gravel sweep and said her name.

She had not slept.

Or rather, she had slept in fragments, each one finding its way back to Pemberley. Mr. Darcy on the south drive; Mr. Darcy bowing to her uncle; Mr. Darcy asking after her family; Mr. Darcy's hand, bare and warm under her glove; Mr. Darcy's portrait looking as though it had once known how to be amused; Mrs. Reynolds saying, with all the comfortable certainty of a woman describing the weather, that he was the best landlord and the best master in the world.

The words had behaved badly in the dark. They had not stayed where she put them.

It was one thing to discover that Mr. Wickham had been false. That discovery, though painful, had arranged itself at last into a shape she could understand. She had trusted charm and been deceived by it. It was humiliating, but not incomprehensible. Charm existed to deceive. A handsome countenance and easy manners were traps set in the open field of society, and she had walked into one with both eyes bright and all her judgment asleep.

But Mr. Darcy had not been charming. Mr. Darcy had been everything that should have made judgment easy. Proud. Silent. Disagreeable. Severe in manner, superior in address, almost industrious in making himself disliked. If she had been wrong about *him*, what then had she been using for judgment at all?

She had told Jane, with all the confidence of affection and wit, that he was the last man in the world from whom civility was to be expected. She had told her aunt enough to make yesterday morning's bewilderment possible. She had allowed her father to laugh at him, her mother to abuse him, Lydia to mimic his gravity, Wickham to wound him under her very eyes and be rewarded for the performance with her sympathy. She had carried an account of his character from room to room, from Meryton to Longbourn to Gracechurch Street, and set it down before the people she loved as if she had been bringing them truth.

What if she had given them, not truth, but only her own mortification?

This question had come to her at some hour when the street below the Rose was quiet, when even the ostler's yard had given over its last mutterings. It had come and sat by her pillow like an unwelcome caller.

But by breakfast, Elizabeth was prepared to be composed at any cost.

The public room of the Rose was already busy. Sun entered through two windows that looked onto the yard and laid bright squares upon the flagged floor. A pair of farmers sat near the door over ale and bread, their hats beside them and their voices low. At another table, a commercial traveler was making impressive work of ham and eggs while reading a newspaper two days old. The smell of tea, toast, warm beer, and the faint clean damp of a recently scrubbed floor mingled with the stable-yard scents beyond the open door.

Mr. Staveley had put the Gardiners at a table somewhat apart from the rest, in the angle beneath the smaller window, where the wooden settle made a little enclosure of its own. It was a kindness, or a distinction, or only good innkeeping. Elizabeth was in no state to decide which.

Mrs. Gardiner was already there when Elizabeth came in, neat in her morning dress, her cap arranged with the pretty precision that made everything she wore seem more fashionable than it was. She had a letter open beside her plate, not newly arrived but one of yesterday's, and was smiling over it with the private warmth of a woman who had been returned, by geography, to some younger version of herself. Mr. Gardiner stood at the window, hands behind his back, looking into the yard with the contentment of a man for whom stables, weather, and breakfast were three respectable forms of evidence that the world was good.

"Ah, Lizzy," he said, turning. "You are just in time to prevent your aunt from ordering me another dish merely because Derbyshire air is supposed to require it. I have already eaten enough to satisfy three counties."

"Not three," said Mrs. Gardiner. "Only two, and one of them a small one. Sit down, Lizzy. You look a little tired."

"Do I?" Elizabeth took her place. "Then Derbyshire has used me ill already. I thought it promised better manners."

It was a perfectly acceptable answer. It even sounded, to her own ear, a little like herself. Her aunt's eyes rested on her for half a second longer than comfort allowed, and then moved away.

"Derbyshire manners," said Mr. Gardiner, resuming his seat, "are excellent, if yesterday may be admitted in evidence. Good roads, good prospects, good housekeepers, good cold pigeon pie —"

"The pigeon pie was Mrs. Pratt's daughter's," said Mrs. Gardiner.

"Then Mrs. Pratt's daughter shall stand for the county. I have no objection."

Elizabeth reached for the tea-pot. Her hand was steady. This seemed a considerable achievement and deserved, she thought, more praise than it was likely to receive.

She had just poured when Mr. Staveley appeared at their table with an expression of importance moderated by professional discretion. A broad, civil man with gray at his temples and a manner that could make a guest feel remembered without making that remembrance oppressive. Two days ago, he had welcomed Mrs. Gardiner home under her maiden name; today, he carried a folded note upon a small tray as though the note itself had a rank. Behind him, one of the inn boys bore a covered basket in both arms, its white cloth lifted at one corner by the green of leaves beneath.

"Begging your pardon, Mr. Gardiner, sir," he said, with a slight bow. "A letter. For you. And a basket for the ladies, with Mr. Darcy's compliments."

Elizabeth set the spoon beside her cup with more care than the spoon deserved.

Mr. Gardiner looked up with surprise, then amusement. "For me?"

Mr. Staveley leaned in and lowered his voice by the smallest possible degree. "The boy came from Pemberley with them. You must have made an impression up at the house yesterday, sir."

He placed the basket on the sideboard, where even under its cloth it declared itself too handsome for an ordinary errand, and withdrew with a bow.

There was nothing improper in the words. Indeed, there was scarcely anything in them at all. Yet Elizabeth felt the farmers near the door, the traveler with his newspaper, the yard beyond the window, the whole of Lambton arranging itself around the fact that a note and a basket had come from Pemberley.

When the innkeeper had gone, her uncle broke the seal. Her aunt's brows lifted, not much. Elizabeth laid her spoon against the saucer and did not lift the cup.

"Well," said Mr. Gardiner, after the first line, and his voice had gone pleasantly blank in the way it did when he was deciding how much of his thoughts to show. "This is very civil."

"From Mr. Darcy?" said Mrs. Gardiner.

"Indeed." He read on, and then, after a moment, read aloud: "*Mr. Darcy presents his compliments to Mr. Gardiner, and begs that Mr. Gardiner will command the Pemberley water during his stay at Lambton, if the sport should be agreeable to him. Tackle can be sent down at any hour Mr. Gardiner names.*"

"There," said Mrs. Gardiner, smiling. "Your Derbyshire trout have found you out."

"Or Mr. Darcy has found out my weakness," said Mr. Gardiner. "Which is most alarming, as I have no recollection of confessing it."

"You spoke of the river from the gallery," Mrs. Gardiner reminded him. "And of fish at least twice on the walk."

"Did I? Then I am betrayed by my own conversation. It is a common fate."

Elizabeth kept her eyes on the toast-rack. Mr. Darcy had remembered. That was all. A gentleman had observed another gentleman's interest and responded with a gentleman's courtesy. There was no occasion for agitation in that. There was less than no occasion. If anything, agitation would be vanity.

And yet the courtesy had been so exactly placed. Not to her. Not even first to her aunt. To her uncle, whose trade Mr. Darcy had yesterday acknowledged without a blink, whose company he had not been obliged to cultivate, whose pleasure he had nevertheless remembered before breakfast.

It was mortifying. It was suspicious. It was — she would not call it kind. But civil.

Mr. Gardiner read silently for a few more lines. The amusement in his face altered, not leaving him, but deepening into something more thoughtful.

"There is more?" asked Mrs. Gardiner.

He glanced once at Elizabeth, which was unnecessary and therefore cruel. "*Mr. Darcy also begs that Mrs. Gardiner and Miss Bennet will accept a small basket from the gardens, with his compliments, and hopes Mrs. Gardiner may find something in Derbyshire still equal to her remembrance of it.*"

Her aunt's expression changed at once. The teasing brightness softened. "So prettily said."

"Just," said Mr. Gardiner.

Elizabeth could not disagree. She wished, for the space of one sharp second, that it had been clumsy. A clumsy compliment might have been dismissed as effort. A grand one might have been resented. This was neither. It had the advantage of being addressed to her aunt's history rather than her own feelings, and therefore gave Elizabeth no proper object upon which to exercise displeasure.

"He must have been told," Mrs. Gardiner said, "that I was once a Lambton girl. Mrs. Reynolds perhaps."

"Or Staveley," said Mr. Gardiner. "Staveley has told everyone else."

Mrs. Gardiner laughed.

"We must first determine whether I am a fisherman," said Mr. Gardiner, glancing again at the page.

"I can answer that," said Mrs. Gardiner.

"My dear, you can answer many things, but allow a man one illusion before breakfast."

Elizabeth took up her cup at last and discovered she had forgotten the sugar. She drank it anyway and found it bitter. She ought to say something. A light remark would be expected. Yesterday, in the chaise, she had defended Mr. Darcy to her aunt in phrases that had surprised even herself. Today, when his civility lay on the table between the cups and knives, she could not produce a sentence fit for use.

Her aunt noticed. Of course she noticed. Mrs. Gardiner had not been placed on earth to overlook the discomfort of people she loved.

"Lizzy," she said lightly, "you are very grave over Mr. Darcy's peaches."

"Over his possible peaches, then. One should never be grave over fruit in prospect."

Her uncle chuckled. Elizabeth managed a smile, but it came too late and did not convince anyone who mattered.

"It is only," she said, "a very handsome attention."

"Yes," said her uncle. "And handsomely offered. A very well-judged note."

Well-judged. The word touched precisely where she was tender.

"In the best sense," said Mr. Gardiner. "He gives us room to accept without embarrassment and to decline without offense. That is not so common a talent as it ought to be."

True. Elizabeth knew enough of gentlemen's addresses — formal and otherwise — to know the difference between civility that made space and civility that occupied it. Mr. Collins had never entered a room without filling it to suffocation with his own humility. Mr. Darcy, in three paragraphs, had contrived to make them obliged and free at once.

It was intolerable.

"I see yet another sentence," said Mrs. Gardiner, whose eyes had returned to the note.

Mr. Gardiner turned the page slightly. "Ah. Yes."

He did not read it aloud immediately. That was the first warning.

Mrs. Gardiner leaned nearer. "My dear."

"I am considering whether I am equal to the responsibility of delivering it."

"Then it must be very serious."

"Extremely. It concerns a young lady."

Elizabeth set down her cup too quickly. It touched the saucer with a small, betraying sound.

Her uncle's mouth twitched; her aunt's did not, though her eyes had gone bright.

"*If Mr. and Mrs. Gardiner plan to remain in Lambton until Tuesday,*" Mr. Gardiner read, with admirable gravity, "*Mr. Darcy would be honored to wait upon them at Lambton with his sister, if they would permit the introduction.*"

There was a pause.

Not a long one. Not long enough for any person in the room to have observed it, had any person in the room been attending. At their table, however, it settled like an additional place laid between them.

"His sister," said Mrs. Gardiner.

"So it says."

"Miss Darcy comes on Monday, I think Mrs. Reynolds told us." Mrs. Gardiner's voice remained perfectly mild. "Did she not, Lizzy?"

"Yes." Elizabeth was proud she could get out the word. It was short, but it had stood upright.

"And Mr. Darcy would be honored to bring her to *us*," said Mr. Gardiner.

"To you and my aunt," Elizabeth said, too quickly.

"My dear Lizzy," said her uncle, "your aunt and I are very agreeable people, but I confess I did not know we had become so irresistible to Derbyshire gentry merely by invading their home.”

Her aunt looked down at her plate. It was not enough to hide the smile.

"Perhaps," Elizabeth said, "he is naturally attentive to visitors."

"Perhaps," said Mrs. Gardiner.

Elizabeth touched the handle of her cup. The china clicked once against the saucer. She withdrew her hand.

"Mrs. Reynolds spoke very warmly of Miss Darcy," Mrs. Gardiner continued. "I should like to meet her. A young lady so highly praised by her staff is likely to be worth knowing."

In her mind, Elizabeth saw, not the miniature at twelve, but the story beyond it. The girl of fifteen at Ramsgate, very nearly ruined. The brother writing in bitterness and pride because he had no other way to make the truth known. She had spent half the night with that knowledge moving restlessly inside her, seeking a place to settle. It had found none.

"Yes," she said. "I believe she is."

Her aunt looked at her then. Carefully. Kindly.

Elizabeth found the pattern of the tablecloth suddenly worthy of study.

Her uncle folded the note along its original crease. "So. Do we change our plans and linger a few days longer?"

"We had not fixed our departure," said Mrs. Gardiner.

"We had fixed it in that very firm manner in which travelers fix things, by saying several contradictory things and meaning whichever should prove convenient." He tapped the folded note against the table. "I had some thought of going on tomorrow, if the heat held. Birmingham will not move farther off by our waiting, but neither will it come nearer."

"My dear," said Mrs. Gardiner, "you cannot be offered Pemberley water and refuse it. It would be uncivil to the trout."

He chuckled. "I am glad you put the matter on its proper moral footing."

"And I should like to see the more of the countryside, and down the river, if we may," her aunt added. "Lizzy and I may walk there while you discover whether Derbyshire fish are more obliging than Hertfordshire ones."

"Hertfordshire fish have never had a fair opportunity to disappoint me."

"Then you will write to Mr. Darcy?"

"I shall write to Mr. Darcy," said Mr. Gardiner, "and accept with gratitude. I shall name tomorrow morning for the fishing, if that suits you both. We shall remain until Tuesday at the least."

There it was.

Elizabeth had known it was coming. The whole note had been moving toward it with the quiet inevitability of a carriage approaching the inn door. Still, when her uncle said it, she felt the words close around her.

Remain until Tuesday.

On Tuesday, she would meet Miss Darcy. On Tuesday she would stand before the girl whose history she had carried since April like a sealed letter inside her own chest. Mr. Darcy would come again, not by accident, not on horseback out of trees, but by intention, to this very inn, with his sister beside him.

She could not object. To object would be to explain. To explain would be impossible.

"Lizzy?" said Mrs. Gardiner. "You do not dislike the plan?"

"No," said Elizabeth. Then, because no one believed her, she added, "No, indeed. I should be very glad to meet Miss Darcy."

This was true, which made it no easier to say.

Her uncle's expression softened. He was too kind a man to press when a lesser uncle might have enjoyed himself. "Then it is settled. Mr. Staveley!"

Mr. Staveley appeared with the promptness of a man who had been waiting to be summoned and the dignity of a man who would deny it.

"Sir?"

"If the boy from Pemberley is still below, tell him he shall have an answer directly."

"Very good, sir."

"And Mr. Staveley," Mrs. Gardiner said, glancing toward the sideboard, "may we see what Mr. Darcy has sent before it goes below?"

"Gladly, ma'am."

He crossed to the sideboard and drew back the cloth with a ceremony that would not have disgraced a larger occasion. There were early peaches, currants, cucumbers as fine as any Elizabeth had seen that summer, a little lettuce, herbs, and a small nosegay tucked in at one side.

Mr. Staveley permitted himself a smile. "A very handsome basket, ma'am. Not that one is surprised, from Pemberley. They do things properly up there."

Elizabeth felt the sentence enter the long account being drawn up against her, line by line, witness by witness. Mrs. Reynolds. The portrait. Her uncle's approval. Her aunt's softened face over the mention of Derbyshire remembered. Mr. Staveley, with his napkin over one arm, stating that Pemberley did things properly as though all Lambton had known it for thirty years and only Elizabeth Bennet had required instruction.

"We cannot possibly eat this entire basket by ourselves," said Mrs. Gardiner.

"No," said Mr. Gardiner. "That would be gluttony, and I try to be a moral man, especially on Sundays."

"Mr. Staveley," Mrs. Gardiner said, "would your kitchen be able to make use of some of it for our luncheon? You may keep the rest."

"Gladly, ma'am. Mrs. Staveley will make you a luncheon from it that would do credit to the county."

"Then pray give Mrs. Staveley our compliments and our thanks."

"And Mr. Darcy's compliments also," said Mr. Gardiner, dryly.

"Aye, sir," said Mr. Staveley, with another small bow. "Those travel well in Lambton."

As he departed with instructions for the basket, her aunt reached for the toast. "Well, Lizzy. It seems Derbyshire has not done with us yet."

Elizabeth looked toward the yard, where sun flashed on harness brass as a boy crossed with a roan. She thought of the long park wall, of the south drive, of a portrait's half-finished expression, of a gentleman's hand offered exactly as long as courtesy required and perhaps — perhaps — a shade longer. Her fingers closed once inside her glove before she could prevent them.

"No," she said. "It seems not."
