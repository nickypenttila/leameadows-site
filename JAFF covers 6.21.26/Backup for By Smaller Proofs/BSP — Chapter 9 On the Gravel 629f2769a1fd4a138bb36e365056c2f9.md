# BSP — Chapter 9: On the Gravel

✅ Chapter complete

---

He swung down — economical, no flourish, the dismount of a man who had been doing it since he could walk. He took two steps toward the carriage and stopped, and she could see that he had not, in the two seconds he had had since seeing her, decided what to do with himself.

Elizabeth would have been hard pressed afterwards to say exactly what he looked like in that first half-minute. She had time, and looking, and all the leisure of a frozen world. Yet the impressions came one at a time, each insisting on being noticed before the next would arrive.

The line under the brim of his hat, where he had taken sun on his face; he was browner than she remembered. The dust on his boots and the dust dark along the seam of his coat-skirts, the way dust laid itself on a man who had been riding fast on a dry road. The one glove still on his hand dark with it where it had held the rein.

He was thinner. She thought it the first moment she had seen him. The moment after, she distrusted the thought. It was the sort of thing a woman wishes to see, some evidence of having struggled, and she had learned recently the danger of seeing what one wished to see.

His eyes had gone, at the moment of speaking her name, to her face; and had, at the second of finding her face there, gone away again — to the bay's neck, to the gravel between them, briefly to her uncle, who had come up beside the carriage door and was waiting with the polite suspended attention of a man who has not yet been introduced and does not intend to be presumptuous about it.

Her uncle. Her aunt. She had forgotten, for the moment the world had given her, that there was anyone else in it.

"Mr. Darcy," she heard herself say again, and was at least relieved that the words came in the order in which words are supposed to come, "may I — Mr. Darcy, my uncle and aunt — Mr. and Mrs. Gardiner, of Gracechurch Street."

He bowed to her uncle with a gravity that was, she registered with a small interior shock, exactly correct. There was no interval in it; there was no measuring. Whatever it might have been to the Mr. Darcy of last October to be presented to a tradesman and his wife on his own gravel sweep — and she could supply that calculation in a half-instant, having watched its arithmetic done in a Hertfordshire ballroom — was not, evidently, what it was to this Mr. Darcy. He acknowledged the introduction. He asked, in a low voice, if they had come up that morning from Lambton; he hoped they had not found the road too dusty in the heat.

Mr. Gardiner, a man of the city who had lived all his life by the principle of taking each new acquaintance at his own face, returned that they had had a most pleasant drive, and that the morning had been a very great pleasure, and that the housekeeper had shown them the house with admirable patience.

"Mrs. Reynolds is patience itself," Mr. Darcy said. The corner of his mouth lifted — the half-finished expression of the portrait, exactly. "She has had practice."

Mr. Gardiner smiled at it. Mrs. Gardiner, leaning a little out of the carriage with her kind face under her bonnet, smiled at it too. She said something pleasant, perhaps about the prospect from the gallery windows.

Elizabeth said nothing. She could not at that moment have produced her own name without practising it first.

She had been ready, somewhere in the back of her mind, for various Mr. Darcys, against any of whom she had a small private armory of replies and silences.

She had not been ready for this one.

As a small boy ran up to take the reins of his horse, Mr. Darcy was speaking to her uncle gravely about the road from Lambton, in the manner of a man who knew the road from Lambton. There was no condescension in it. No patronage, no slight tilt of the head that suggested the speaker was being agreeable to someone he need not be agreeable to. The man who had written that letter, she thought, would not condescend. Here, he was that man.

Or perhaps not. Perhaps he was so disordered that he could not muster condescension. Or this was ordinary courtesy, exactly correctly performed by a gentleman to a gentleman in his own avenue, and she was decorating it because she wished it to be decorated.

She had been wrong about a man's character once before this year, and at such cost. She felt the cold flush come up under her ribs again — the fear, this time, of being about to be wrong in the opposite direction, and at the same kind of cost.

She could not get her feet under her. She tried, and could not.

Mr. Darcy had asked — she had not heard the asking; she had heard her uncle's reply — how long they meant to stay in the country; and Mr. Gardiner was telling him, pleasantly, that they were to be at Lambton four days more, and then to take the road south by way of Birmingham. Mr. Darcy bowed. He asked, after a small pause, after Mr. Bennet's family.

It was directed nominally to her uncle, but the question was for her. She felt the small turn of his shoulders that gave it to her.

"They were all well," she said, "when I last had a letter."

"I am glad."

A silence held for a half-beat, of the kind that, in the drawing-rooms of last winter, she would have been at small social pains to fill. She did not fill it. She did not, this morning, have any small pleasant pains in reserve.

Mr. Gardiner — God bless her uncle — turned a little and said something quiet and easy to Mrs. Gardiner about the time, and the heat of the day, and stepped over to settle Mrs. Gardiner's parasol against the corner of the carriage seat where it would not be in her way. It was the smallest possible piece of social tact, and it gave Elizabeth, for the space of perhaps ten seconds, the gravel between herself and Mr. Darcy with no one immediately attending.

He took half a step nearer. Not toward her, of course. Toward the door to the carriage. His voice, when he spoke, was lower than it had been to her uncle.

"I had not known," he said, "you were to be in Derbyshire."

He did not look at her when he said it. He looked past the carriage, at the sweep of his lawn.

She heard it twice. She heard it in the surface meaning — *I would have warned my household to expect you, I would have given orders that the family was to be from home, I would have spared you this awkwardness on my own gravel.* And she heard it underneath. *I came home in the belief I would not see you. I came north because I would not see you.*

She did not know which had been meant. She suspected — she knew, with the certainty one has about things one cannot prove — that both had been meant. He had not, in the half-second between dismounting and speaking, decided which sentence he was making. He had said the one sentence that did the work of both, and trusted her to hear it.

She was not, this morning, in any condition to be trusted with anything.

What she wanted to say was that she had not known it either until yesterday. That she had agreed to the expedition on the understanding, recited to herself twice in the dark before sleeping that he would be in London until the end of August; that she would not, on any consideration she could presently think of, have set foot in his county had she supposed there was the smallest chance of meeting him on it.

What she said was:

"My uncle had a fancy to see this part of the country. He had not been so far north before."

"It is a fine country."

"Yes."

He looked, for the first time since the bow, at her face. A half-second, perhaps three-quarters. Then his eyes went away again, to the carriage.

"You are at Lambton?"

"At the Rose."

"I — " he began, and stopped. Whatever he had been going to say, he did not say. He bowed, instead, very slightly. "I hope you have not found the morning too warm."

"Not at all."

"I am glad."

Then he turned his head a degree toward Elizabeth. He bowed, and held a hand out.

She took his hand — gloveless still, warm under her own glove — and managed, somehow, to step up into the carriage. Her aunt's hand, quietly, was at her elbow as she sat down. She did not look at her aunt. She did not look at her uncle. She found her seat by the river side, in the small shade of the carriage's hood, and put her gloved hands in her lap, and turned her face, with all the composure she possessed, to the window.

She had not, until this morning, supposed that one could feel mortification of a wholly new kind at twenty years old. She had been wrong about that as she had been wrong about, evidently, a great many things.

The trees of the drive came up around them on either side. Through the small back window of the chaise — looking past her aunt's shoulder, between the brim of her aunt's bonnet and the back of the seat — there was a strip of the sweep visible, narrowing as the chaise drew off.

She did not look at it for the first ten yards. She did not look at it for the first twenty. Then she did.

He had not moved. The hand he had given her at the carriage was at his side again, gloveless still, dust along the side of the thumb.

---

For a quarter of an hour no one spoke.

The lodge gate fell behind. The chaise came out onto the post-road, and the unbroken stone of Pemberley's pale lay between the road and the woods inside it, going north and east the way it had gone yesterday at her right. 

The harness creaked. The hooves clopped on. A fly worked the inside of the glass. Mr. Gardiner, opposite, gazed out the window with the ease of a man who had had a satisfactory morning and was content to let it settle. Mrs. Gardiner, beside her, had her bonnet a degree turned, not quite toward Elizabeth, not quite away. Her hands were folded in her lap in the manner of a woman who is letting a silence do its work.

Elizabeth's gloved hands echoed her aunt’s. Against the printed cotton of her skirt, a trace of dust along the side of her right thumb The spot his bare hand had taken her gloved one to hold her steady on the step was still there. She could see it on the kid. She did not, for the first ten yards, or the first one hundred, brush it off.

His hand had been warm in hers. He had not held her hand a second longer than the courtesy required. He had not held it a second less. The exactness of it — the *care* of the exactness — fluttered through her like a small private weather she had not authorized. She looked at her glove and put the thought down.

"A fine fellow, Lizzy."

Mr. Gardiner said it as a man states a discovered fact he is pleased about. He had been waiting, evidently, for the road to put a half-mile between them and the gates before he would say it; now the half-mile was behind him and the verdict could be released.

"He had not, you know, been quite what I had been led — by my wife, I confess, — to expect. The bow was very civil. The question after your father was prettily put. He had no occasion to be pleasant to me, and he was pleasant to me. That is not nothing, in a man." He considered. "Not nothing, in a great man."

"No, Uncle," said Elizabeth. Somehow, her voice came out steady. Nothing else inside her was.

Mrs. Gardiner turned her bonnet a further degree toward her niece.

"Lizzy — was *that* the Mr. Darcy you met at Meryton?"

Elizabeth turned her face to the open window. The wall, still on her left, kept rolling along.

"Yes?" She sounded unsure even to herself.

"I had been led," said Mrs. Gardiner, in a voice the word *carefully* would not be too heavy for, "to expect — by Jane, you know, this winter — a gentleman of a different quality.”

She left a small pause for Elizabeth to speak into. Elizabeth did not.

“A gentleman who had stood up at a country ball and refused to dance with anyone he had not been introduced to. A gentleman who had said something, within hearing, of the second Miss Bennet, that the second Miss Bennet had not chosen to forget."

Another small pause. Another silence.

"Could Jane truly have been so mistaken?" Mrs. Gardiner sighed. “I always thought her views of people more rosy than they should be.” 

It was perfectly mildly said. There was no edge in it. 

But still, Elizabeth bled a bit inside.

She wanted to say that she had been wrong, last winter, to tell Jane those things.That she had carried, for months, an account of a man's character built on a mere half-hour of seeing him in a country ballroom. She had spent the winter besides taking another man's grievance for the truth. That she had been required, in April, on a path at Rosings, to take her account apart in her own hand and look at it. 

And wince.

What she said was:

"I think — perhaps, Aunt — it was a mistake to take a man's worst evening as his settled character. He had been brought into Hertfordshire against his inclination, by Mr. Bingley. He had not chosen any of the company in that ballroom. This morning, though, even when obviously surprised by us, he chose to be civil to my uncle.” To her. She paused. The wall rolled by. "It is possible to mistake a season's weather for a man's character."

"Just so," said Mr. Gardiner, nodding. "A man on his own ground is seldom the man he is on someone else's." He let the matter rest.

Mrs. Gardiner did not nod. She did not, however, press. Behind her sharp eyes, she considered.

A mile of road. The silence returned. Mrs. Gardiner, after a decent interval, in a voice so light it might have been an afterthought, said:

"And had you ever met him since, Lizzy? Since Hertfordshire?"

Elizabeth had been waiting, trying to hide and knowing she could not, for the question.

"I had, in fact, in the spring." She kept her own voice at the same light weight as her aunt's. "He was at Rosings during my stay with the Collinses. Lady Catherine de Bourgh is his aunt, you know. I was several times at the great house — with Lady Catherine, and Miss de Bourgh, and a few others."

“A few other?” Mrs. Gardiner’s brows rose. “Among the few others, was he, Lizzy?”

Elizabeth told herself not to shrug. Not to look guilty.

The wall ended. The hedgerows took up. The road bent gently east.

The small angle of his head as he had said *I had not known you were to be in Derbyshire* came back on her as she watched the hedgerow go by. The half-degree to the right; the way his eyes had not been looking at her when he said it. The way he had trusted her to hear the sentence that could mean *I would have warned my household to expect you* and also could mean *I came north because I would not see you*, and to choose between them. Or not to choose, and to know that both were true. 

The hot crowded feeling that had come up under her ribs at the moment was up there again, in the chaise. Only this time without the armor of his being there in front of her, requiring her to be composed. Her gloved hand was at her throat without permission. She put it down.

A second mile. No one spoke.

Yesterday seemed weeks ago.

"Well," said Mr. Gardiner, easily, into the quiet, "I shall be very glad of my luncheon."

He said it with the cheerful candor of a man who had had a long morning and a great deal of fresh air and considered his appetite a plain civic fact. "It must be coming on toward one. And what a morning it has been — my dear, I am very glad indeed that the country has used you so kindly. Derbyshire is a delightful piece of England, and I shall be very glad to come back to it; though I confess I am the kind of man who likes a place the better for knowing London is at the other end of the road. I am the better for the morning." 

He drew out his watch, considered the face of it, and put it back. "I am also hungry."

"My dear," said Mrs. Gardiner. "I had been thinking of just that. There is — or there was, in my time, and is again, by the landlord's account — a small place at the very end of the inn yard, two steps up the lane that goes by the church. A Mrs. Pratt kept it then; her daughter keeps it now.  And she does, the landlord says. The best cold pigeon pie of three parishes. We might walk over, after we have set ourselves to rights at the inn."

"Excellent," said Mr. Gardiner, "I am perfectly agreeable."

"Lizzy?" said her aunt.

"Yes," Elizabeth said. "Yes — of course."

The chaise turned under the arch of the Rose. The same arch. The same yard. The same boy ran up. Mr. Gardiner stepped down, and turned, and handed his wife down, and turned again to hand his niece.

Elizabeth looked at her hands. The dust on the side of her thumb was still there. 

She gave him her other hand. He took it without noticing, and handed her down.
