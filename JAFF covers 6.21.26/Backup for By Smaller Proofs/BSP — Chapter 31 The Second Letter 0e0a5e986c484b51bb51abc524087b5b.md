# BSP — Chapter 31: The Second Letter

✅ Completed

---

The estate office was blessedly cool. Darcy had ridden back from the south farms later than he intended, with Mr. Hardwick’s figures in his pocket and the dust of three lanes upon his boots. The difficulty at Stony Close had proved, after two hours’ examination, to be neither the tenant’s negligence nor the steward’s oversight, but a drainage fault so plain once found that every man present had looked at the ditch with an expression of personal injury. 

Darcy had known what to do with it. He had stood with Hardwick over the plan, marked the field boundary, named the mason, fixed the order of the work, and left behind him a tenant relieved, a steward instructed, and a problem reduced to dates and labor.

That was the mercy of land. It did not cease to be difficult, but it consented, eventually, to particulars.

The small paneled room was dim after the fields, the shutters drawn against the blazing afternoon heat. On the desk, the silver weight held the day’s letters in place; beside them lay a folded map, two ledgers, a sealed packet from his London man of business, and a note from the housekeeper concerning linen for the west rooms. The pen-knife stood where it always stood, next to the blotter. Someone — Mrs. Reynolds, or a maid acting on her orders — had left a glass of barley water on the side table, already beading in the warmth.

Rents, repairs, servants, walls, horses, weather, names. A whole life reduced to paper, and therefore capable of being touched.

Mrs. Reynolds appeared at the door before he could sit.

"Mrs. Gardiner has asked whether you might spare her a few minutes, sir. She said it would not take long, but she did ask whether you had come in yet."

Darcy laid his gloves on the desk. He was still in his riding coat, his boots dusty, his neckcloth loosened from the heat. The drawing room was out of the question.

"Ask her to come here, if she would not mind the informality."

"Very good, sir."

He drank off the barley water while he waited. It was tepid and faintly sweet and did nothing for the unease that had settled beneath his ribs at the word *asked*. Nothing in Mrs. Gardiner’s manner had ever suggested fuss.

She came in a few minutes later, shown to the door by a housemaid who curtseyed and withdrew. Mrs. Gardiner took in the room — the shuttered dimness, the papers, the master of the house in his riding dust — and did not remark upon any of it.

That, more than the request, told him the matter was not casual.

"Mrs. Gardiner. I beg your pardon for receiving you here. I am not fit for the better rooms."

"Not at all," she said. "I am grateful for your time."

The courtesy was warm. It did not comfort him.

"Will you sit?"

"If you please."

He set a chair for her. She accepted it without ceremony. Darcy sat in his father's chair. Every instinct in him preferred standing — but a host sat when his guest sat.

"I have had a letter from my husband,” Mrs. Gardiner said.

Darcy’s attention sharpened.

"He is well,” she added at once. "There is no alarm on that score. But the business which called him back to town has not resolved as he hoped. I must return within a few days.”

A few days.

The phrase did not alter the office. The map remained half-unfolded. The inkstand stood where it had stood. A fly bumped with dull persistence against the upper pane of the window. Somewhere beyond the stable yard one man called to another, and received an answer. The room did not mark it.

Darcy inclined his head. "I am sorry your stay must end so soon.”

"So am I.”

She said it simply. That made it worse. Had she complained, he could have answered her. Regret gave him nothing to meet.

"I hope Mr. Gardiner has not been inconvenienced by our keeping you so long.”

"My husband,” Mrs. Gardiner said, and there was a warmth in it that did not soften the point, "has survived worse inconveniences than being deprived of my company in Derbyshire.”

Darcy almost smiled. Almost.

"But I must go,” she continued. "And before I do, Kitty’s visit must be settled plainly.”

Plainly.

"Of course,” he said. "Miss Catherine is very welcome to remain, if Mr. Bennet approves and if Mrs. Annesley is willing to continue in her charge. My sister would be happy in it.”

"I believe she would.”

"I shall write to Mr. Bennet at once.”

"Thank you. That will be necessary.”

Necessary. Not sufficient.

Darcy looked down at the map before him. Stony Close, East Meadow, the boundary coppice, the low field where the water gathered. Lines, names, answers. He had spent the morning with men who said what they meant because ditch-water permitted no delicacy.

Mrs. Gardiner was quiet for a moment. "Mr. Darcy, I am sensible of your kindness to my nieces."

He did not move. Praise, from Mrs. Gardiner, was never ornament. It usually preceded a truth.

"I hope,” he said carefully, "that kindness has not made anything difficult.”

"That is what I wish to prevent.”

The fly struck the pane again.

"Elizabeth,” Mrs. Gardiner said, "is under your roof. She is grateful for your sister’s friendship, for your hospitality, and for every proper consideration she has received here.”

Every proper consideration. He heard the mercy in the adjective. He also heard its warning. Something in his chest shifted, as if a weight had been set down on the wrong side of a balance.

"I would not,” he said, "have her feel pressed by any obligation to Pemberley.”

"No. I believe that.”

Her belief should have relieved him. Instead it placed responsibility in his hands more exactly than suspicion could have done.

"Yet gratitude,” Mrs. Gardiner said, "may become a pressure even where no one intends it. So may kindness.” She paused. "So may silence.”

Darcy's hand closed once upon the arm of his chair.

"I do not speak for Elizabeth,” she said. "She must speak for herself, or not speak, as she chooses. But my return changes the shape of matters. I thought it right that you should know it before dinner, rather than after.”

He inclined his head. Alarm touched him, swift and plain, before composure closed over it.

Mrs. Gardiner rose. He rose with her.

"She is not to be hurried,” Darcy said.

"No.”

"Nor left uncertain.”

Mrs. Gardiner’s expression altered by the smallest degree. Not surprise, but, perhaps, the recognition that he had reached the same boundary by his own road.

"No,” she said. "Nor that.”

"Thank you,” he said.

The words were inadequate. They were also all he had.

He bowed. She returned it, with all proper composure, and left him in the estate office among his papers.

---

Dinner did not fail. That was among the cruelties of a well-ordered house.

The soup was clear, the fish excellent, the removes faultless. Servants entered and withdrew; glasses were filled. Mrs. Annesley asked Georgiana some quiet question about a new duet; Kitty supplied the answer before Georgiana could, and Georgiana let her, smiling, because that was how they were now. Mrs. Gardiner spoke of the heat with perfect ease. At his left hand, Elizabeth tasted her soup, smiled at her sister, and did not look at him. The candlelight caught her cheek, leaving her eyes uncertain.

Darcy answered when he was spoken to.

He did not look at Elizabeth too long. He did not avoid looking at her so completely that avoidance became its own confession. He passed a dish to Mrs. Gardiner, asked Mrs. Annesley whether the music room had been too warm that afternoon, and responded to Kitty’s opinion of Mr. Hardwick’s morning habits with enough gravity to make Georgiana shake her head and smile.

Every duty was discharged. Every form observed.

Nothing in the room looked altered. The chairs stood where they had always stood. The silver carried the light as it had done last week, last month, all his life. The portraits above the sideboard observed the family and its guests with ancestral indifference. If a man had believed that certain sentences could change the air of a house, dinner proved him wrong. The forms continued. The candles burned. Miss Elizabeth Bennet lifted her glass, set it down again, and did not know that within a few days she might be gone.

Or perhaps she did know.

That was worse.

---

After dinner he went first to the billiard room, because Bingley had once told him that no man ever improved a difficulty by walking directly toward it. Bingley had meant cards. Or billiards. His philosophy was not always transferable, but his warmth had a way of surviving misapplication.

Darcy stood three minutes beside the table and did not take up a cue.

Then he went to the library.

The library knew too much of him. The estate office held business; the library held continuance. His father’s books. Georgiana’s shawl left on a chair. The globe he had turned as a boy until the seas went round too quickly and his tutor told him no empire had ever been governed by dizziness. The desk at which he had written to Colonel Fitzwilliam, to his steward, to Bingley, to men who wanted answers and received them.

He lit two more candles and sat.

There were several honorable courses in theory. Theory was generous that way.

He might speak to her. He might ask Mrs. Gardiner's permission, request a few minutes in the morning room, say only what must be said. The thought came first because desire was not deadened by discipline; it only learned to enter by respectable doors.

No.

The word was so immediate that he set down the pen he had not realized he had taken up.

She was in his house. Her aunt must soon leave. Every wall of Pemberley would stand behind him if he spoke. Every courtesy he had offered might seem to gather at his back and form an argument.

He would not make his house an accomplice.

Then he might say nothing. That had been honor, had it not? To leave her unpressed, to let his conduct speak only so far as she wished to hear it, to take pains that she should never have to wonder whether Jane's happiness had been offered as a debt.

But Mrs. Gardiner had been right. Silence could become pressure too. It could say he had retreated. It could leave Elizabeth to depart without knowing where his feelings lay.

Darcy drew a sheet of paper toward him.

At Hunsford, he had spoken because he believed himself entitled to an answer. Since then, he had held his tongue because he feared every word might become entitlement again. Neither extreme was honesty. One had demanded too much of her; the other asked too much of silence.

The pen waited in his hand.

He wrote her name.

*Miss Elizabeth Bennet,*

It looked too formal, and not formal enough. Formal enough for the world. Not enough for the distance he owed her. Too much distance for the truth.

He took another sheet.

*Madam,*

No.

He had called her madam in anger and defense, though the anger had been wounded pride and the defense had been, in part, true. The word still carried the smell of Rosings: damp paths, early light, the stiff violence of self-command, his own close hand filling every space because he had been unable to say less.

He set the sheet aside.

*Miss Bennet,*

Better. Safer. Painful.

*I would not have you think—*

Too defensive. It began with himself.

*You may perhaps have wondered—*

Cowardly. It asked her to admit curiosity before he had earned the right to notice it.

He drew another sheet.

*Miss Bennet,*

He stopped.

The first letter had defended. It had explained, argued, arranged the facts in order and demanded justice from a woman he had already injured. It had been true. That was not enough to save it from pride.

*I cannot allow my silence to be mistaken for indifference.*

Too blunt? Too much? Too little?

He left it.

The next sentence would not come. He rose, walked to the shelves, and returned without knowing which books he had passed. He sat again.

*My feelings are unchanged.*

He did not write them at once. He let them stand in his mind until they lost the first violence of hope and became merely true.

If he wrote only that, it would be a demand. If he did not write it, the letter had no purpose. He had learned, too late but not too late for this, that truth was not made honorable by being incomplete.

*My feelings are unchanged, except in this: that I know better than I once did how little right they give me to disturb your peace.*

The sentence remained on the page, black, exact, intolerable.

He sat back.

It was not all. It was perhaps too much. It was certainly not all he wanted to say.

He wanted to say that he loved her still, and differently, and more truly for the months in which hope had been denied the easy work of imagination and forced him instead to observe, to wait, to suffer correction. 

He wanted to say that the woman who had refused him had been more present to him than any woman who had ever smiled. 

He wanted to say that every proof he had tried to give had taught him that proof itself was not the point. She was free, and because she was free, anything she gave would be grace.

He wrote none of that. What he wanted had no place in a letter she might open alone, in a house where his name was on the walls.

*I ask for no answer.*

Then stopped, because even that might be false. He wanted an answer as a starving man wanted bread. Want was not the measure of what might be asked.

He amended the line:

*I ask for no answer now, nor for any kindness you do not freely wish to give.*

There was no eloquence in it. Good.

Eloquence had served him badly.

The clock in the hall struck eleven. He had not heard the quarter hours.

He wrote slowly then, not the whole of what he felt, but the shape around it. That he had not wished to press her while she remained his guest. That her sister’s happiness was not his to offer as evidence. That if he had erred in silence, it was not from indifference. That he would accept, without resentment and without further intrusion, whatever her silence or speech might tell him.

He wrote no blessing.

He paused over that. The first letter had ended with *God bless you*. It had been sincere; it had also been final, a dismissal disguised as prayer. He would not borrow it now.

He signed his name because a letter required a name, and because there was no humility in pretending she did not know the hand.

Fitzwilliam Darcy.

Then he sanded the page, folded it, and sealed it before courage could degrade into revision.

He had written what was true.

He had not written what he loved.
