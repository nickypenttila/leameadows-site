# BSP — Chapter 3: An Errand to Meryton

✅ Chapter complete

---

The walk to Meryton was Jane's idea, and like most of Jane's ideas it arrived so wrapped in quiet reasonableness that Elizabeth had agreed before thinking to examine why.

"I ought to post my letter to Aunt Gardiner," Jane said, standing in the hallway with her bonnet already in hand and her pelisse buttoned, as though the decision had been made some time ago and she was merely informing Elizabeth of its existence. "And the morning is fine. Will you come?"

The morning was, in fact, fine — aggressively, unhelpfully fine, the kind of April morning that insists on warmth and birdsong and a sky of flawless blue when one would rather it had the decency to rain. Elizabeth had spent the past three days inside Longbourn's walls, and Longbourn's walls had begun to feel like the interior of a clock: the same mechanisms turning at the same intervals, producing the same sounds. Lydia wept. Mrs. Bennet lamented. The library door stayed shut. The household revolved around the absence of Brighton with a grim constancy that would have been admirable had it been directed toward any useful purpose.

"Yes," Elizabeth said. "Gladly."

Kitty came too — not gladly, but with the restless need of a girl who had been trapped indoors with a sister who blamed her for everything and a mother who had not yet forgiven the messenger. She said little on the lane, walking a half-step behind Elizabeth and Jane with her shawl pulled close despite the warmth, and Elizabeth thought: the cost of courage, it seemed, did not end with the spending of it.

The road to Meryton ran between hedgerows just coming into leaf — hawthorn and blackthorn, white blossom caught in the branches like scraps of linen snagged on a line. The air smelled of turned earth from the fields beyond, and of something sweeter beneath it, May blossom or early honeysuckle. Elizabeth had enjoyed the fragrance of a Hertfordshire spring since childhood, but today it carried an unwelcome sharpness. A cart passed them going the other direction, the driver touching his hat. Birdsong threaded through the hedgerows — a wren, insistent and close, and further off a cuckoo repeating its two notes with the self-satisfaction of a bird that has never been contradicted.

They heard Meryton before they saw it.

The high street, on an ordinary morning, held its ordinary traffic: the butcher's boy, the postman, a scattering of women and servants on their errands, and perhaps two or three officers strolling between the draper's and the coffeehouse with the unhurried air of men whose chief occupation was being admired. But today the street had the disordered energy of a household packing to move. Wagons stood at angles outside the inn, half loaded with trunks and equipment cases. A pair of soldiers carried a rolled tent between them with the careful coordination of men who had done this before and resented doing it again. Orders were being given somewhere — Elizabeth could hear the bark of a sergeant's voice from behind the assembly rooms — and the whole scene had the dusty, restless air of a place about to lose its reason for notice.

The regiment was leaving. Elizabeth had known this in the abstract — the removal to Brighton had been the fixed point of Lydia's universe for weeks — but seeing the physical evidence of departure on the high street made it real in a way that Lydia's tantrums had not. The officers would go. The red coats would disappear from Meryton's lanes and shop doors and evening parties. All would return to dreary dullness, as Lydia put it. What was leaving would not be coming back — not this regiment, not these officers, not quite this arrangement of a town.

And with them would go Mr. Wickham.

Elizabeth had not allowed herself to think about encountering him again.

That was not quite true. She had thought of little else for three days, in the way that one thinks about an approaching interview with the tooth-drawer: with the steady, low-grade dread of a person who knows the appointment is necessary and wishes profoundly that it were not. She had rehearsed her face. She had rehearsed her voice. She had reminded herself, with the stern internal discipline of a governess addressing a particularly willful charge, that she knew exactly what Mr. Wickham was and that this knowledge, however painful, was also armor.

She had told herself she was ready.

Jane posted her letter at the receiving office with the quiet efficiency of a woman who completes tasks while others are still considering them. Elizabeth waited outside with Kitty, watching the street. A dog slept in a patch of sun outside the saddler's. The smell of fresh bread reached her from the bakery two doors down — warm, yeasty, indifferent to crisis.

Then Kitty's hand closed on her arm.

Elizabeth followed her sister's gaze up the high street and saw them at once. Mrs. Forster, in a walking dress of sprigged muslin and a bonnet trimmed with silk flowers that nodded with each step, coming down from the direction of the draper's with two bandboxes and the animated expression of a woman who has been shopping with great success. Beside her, carrying a third bandbox and a paper-wrapped parcel with the easy grace of a man performing a favor he wished to be noticed, walked Mr. Wickham.

He looked well. He always looked well. That was the machinery of it, Elizabeth saw with a clarity that sat in her chest like a swallowed stone. The uniform sat perfectly. The smile was already in place — not the full smile, not yet, but the preliminary version, the one that warmed his features into an expression of open, uncomplicated friendliness before he had any reason to be friendly. She had once found that smile charming. She had once thought it the natural overflow of an amiable disposition. She watched him shift Mrs. Forster's parcel to his other hand so that he could touch his hat to a passing shopkeeper's wife, and she saw the gesture for what it was: a performance so practiced that the performer no longer distinguished it from sincerity.

*He told his story with such an air of openness.*

The words from the letter arrived without invitation, as they had been arriving for days — at breakfast, in the garden, in the dark of her bedroom ceiling — inserting themselves between Elizabeth and the world like a pane of tinted glass she could not remove.

Denny was with them, a few steps behind, talking to a younger officer Elizabeth did not recognize. He spotted the Bennet sisters first and raised a hand in greeting. The gesture caught Wickham's attention. As Wickham spotted them, his smile completed itself.

"Miss Bennet! Miss Elizabeth! What a welcome sight on a morning of dust and drudgery." He shifted the bandboxes with theatrical resignation. "As you see, I have been pressed into service. Mrs. Forster's shopping will not carry itself, and the colonel has abandoned me to my fate."

Mrs. Forster laughed — a bright, silly laugh that reminded Elizabeth, with a pang she had not anticipated, of Lydia. "Oh, Mr. Wickham is too obliging. I told him I could manage perfectly well, but he would insist."

"I am a man of deep principle," Wickham said. "Chief among them: never allow a lady to carry her own bandboxes when an officer with two free hands is available."

Jane smiled at this with the serene goodwill she extended to all the world. "Good morning, Mr. Wickham. Mrs. Forster — how nice to see you."

Elizabeth watched Jane curtsey to Wickham and felt a sting like vinegar on a paper cut — small, precise, and entirely private. He did not deserve it. But Jane's kindness was indiscriminate — it fell on the worthy and the unworthy with the same gentle constancy, like rain. Elizabeth had always admired this quality, had always considered it the purest expression of her sister's character. 

She did not admire it now. She envied it. Jane could be kind to Wickham because Jane did not know what Elizabeth knew, and ignorance, in this instance, was not merely bliss but a species of freedom that Elizabeth would have traded half her wit to possess.

"But where is Lydia?" Mrs. Forster looked past them down the street, as though Lydia might be trailing behind at her customary delay. "I have not seen her these three days. She has not answered my letter. I was beginning to think she had been carried off by pirates — though I told Colonel Forster, the pirates would bring her back within the hour, for Lydia would exhaust even a pirate's patience."

She laughed at her own joke. Kitty did not laugh. Kitty had gone pale — the color leaving her face as visibly as water draining from a basin. Her hand on Elizabeth's arm tightened.

"Lydia is at home," Jane said, with a smoothness that betrayed no effort. "She has been a little unwell. Nothing alarming — merely a headache that has kept her indoors. She will be sorry to have missed you."

The lie was so clean, so perfectly shaped to the occasion, that Elizabeth felt a complicated admiration. Jane had not planned it. Jane did not plan lies; she simply produced the kindest available version of reality and delivered it with such conviction that it became, for the duration of the conversation, true.

"Oh, poor Lydia!" Mrs. Forster pressed a gloved hand to her collarbone. "She will be devastated to miss the packing — she did so want to see the regiment off. You must tell her I asked after her."

"What a pity," Wickham said. He was looking directly at Elizabeth. Not at Jane, not at Kitty, not at the street. "I had hoped for the pleasure of your company before we depart, Miss Elizabeth. We have had so little opportunity to talk since your return from Kent."

The word *Kent* landed on the conversation like a coin on a counter. Elizabeth heard it ring.

"Kent was uneventful," she said. "Mr. and Mrs. Collins are very well. Lady Catherine is in excellent health and excellent voice. I believe that covers the substance of my visit."

Wickham's smile did not falter. That was the thing about his smile; it had the resilience of a well-engineered structure, built to absorb shocks and redistribute the weight. "And did you encounter anyone else of note at Rosings? I understand Mr. Darcy is often there at Easter. A great favorite of Lady Catherine's, as I am sure you know."

*Georgiana. Ramsgate. Thirty thousand pounds and a girl of fifteen.*

The words pressed against the backs of Elizabeth's teeth. She swallowed them.

"Yes, Mr. Darcy was there," she said. "We exchanged the usual civilities. I found the park very fine."

"The park," Wickham repeated, with the faintest emphasis — an emphasis so slight that only someone listening for it would have caught it. "Yes, Pemberley's grounds are said to be superior, but Rosings has its merits. And Darcy? Was he his usual charming self?"

The irony was laid on like varnish — thin, smooth, inviting her to share it. A month ago she would have laughed. A month ago she would have met his eye and said something clever about Darcy's deficiencies and felt the warm complicity of a shared joke. And she would have been wrong, and she would not have known it.

"Mr. Darcy was perfectly civil," Elizabeth said. "I have no complaint."

Something moved behind Wickham's eyes. A recalculation. Elizabeth watched it happening: the rapid, invisible adjustment of a man who has expected one response and received another and is now determining what the discrepancy means. She had always spoken freely about Mr. Darcy. She had always been willing — eager, even — to hear and echo Wickham's version of Mr. Darcy's character. And now she had called him perfectly civil and offered no complaint, and the flatness of these words, their careful neutrality, was louder than any criticism.

"Well," Wickham said lightly. "I am glad to hear it. Darcy can be — when he chooses — capable of tolerably good manners. Though one wonders what prompts the choice."

Elizabeth said nothing. The silence was a mistake — she knew it even as it formed, knew that her old self would have filled it with something pointed and witty, would have risen to the bait, but she could not produce the words. Every clever remark she might have made about Mr. Darcy now tasted of the lane at Hunsford and the mortifying collapse of her own judgment.

Wickham let the silence sit almost to the point that the others noticed. Then he turned his attention — easily, smoothly, with the practiced pivot of a man accustomed to steering conversations toward more agreeable ground — to Kitty.

"And Miss Kitty, you are very quiet today. I hope you are well?"

Kitty made a sound that might have been affirmation. Her arm was now pressed against Elizabeth's. She had not looked directly at Wickham since his approach, and she did not look at him now. Her gaze was fixed on the paving stones with the desperate concentration of a girl attempting to disappear into the ground by force of will.

"She is quite well," Jane said, with a warmth that covered Kitty's silence like a blanket laid over a shivering child.

"And Miss Lydia not with you? I had half expected to see her." Wickham's tone was light, conversational, and Elizabeth heard beneath it a thread of specific interest that had nothing to do with headaches.

He shifted one of Mrs. Forster's bandboxes higher on his arm and turned to Jane. "Pray remember me to Miss Lydia," he said, with the easy grace of any officer taking leave of a family; and then, as an afterthought too neatly placed to be one: "She was always very kind to me."

Elizabeth heard her own voice before she had decided to use it. "I am sure she thought of you as one of the family."

It was the past tense that was wrong. *Thought*. She had meant *thinks*, and some truer self had intervened to correct her; and Wickham, who had been smiling at Jane, now turned the smile upon Elizabeth and let it rest there a moment longer than smiles ordinarily rested.

"Oh, but you must let me carry her a word, Miss Bennet —" Mrs. Forster broke in brightly, her bandbox hitched against her hip. "I shall write to her directly we are back, and she will think herself forgotten if none of you send anything. A line only."

Jane smiled, and her smile was very kind, and very composed, and very slightly late. "You are good to think of her. But Lydia is resting, Mrs. Forster — best not, I think."

*Best not.* Elizabeth had never heard Jane refuse a kindness with those particular two words. She did not look at her sister. She did not need to.

"Well, you must come this evening at any rate," Mrs. Forster went on, undeflected. "Colonel Forster insists on a farewell party before we leave for Brighton — we shall be a small, merry company. The Harringtons, of course, and Captain Carter, and Miss King — who grows prettier by the fortnight, I declare. Ten thousand pounds will do wonders for a young woman's complexion."

It was the smallest thing. Wickham, who had been smiling still, did not stop smiling. But something behind his eyes fixed, briefly, upon the name, and then released it, and then smiled on.

Elizabeth felt Kitty move at her elbow — a small, instinctive half-step back, as though from a hot thing on a stove — and Kitty did not look at Wickham again after that.

"You are very kind," Jane said of the invitation, with the same serene grace. "I am afraid we cannot. Our father has particular plans for the family this week, and we are not at liberty. But we are grateful for the invitation, and please give our regards to Colonel Forster."

Mrs. Forster accepted with a pout that did not quite conceal her relief at having fewer places to set.

Another soldier called to Wickham from up the street — something about the quartermaster and a missing inventory — and Wickham shifted the bandboxes with a rueful shrug.

"Duty calls, I am afraid. Mrs. Forster, shall I leave these at the inn?" He turned to the sisters with a bow that included all three of them and excluded none and that Elizabeth recognized, with a specificity that made her jaw ache, as the exact bow he had given her on the evening they first met in Meryton. The bow that said *I am the most agreeable man in this room and I have chosen to direct that quality at you.* "Miss Bennet. Miss Elizabeth. Miss Kitty. I hope we shall meet again before we must leave you."

He walked away up the high street with Mrs. Forster's parcels balanced on one arm and Denny falling into step beside him. The dust from a passing wagon rose around his boots and settled on the hem of his coat. He said something to Denny that made Denny laugh.

He looked back.

Just once — a glance over his shoulder as he reached the corner by the saddler's, so brief that Elizabeth might have missed it had she not been watching. It was not the look of a man bidding farewell. It was not the look of a man admiring a woman he had once flattered. It was the look of a man taking inventory: quick, careful, cataloging what he had observed and storing it for future use. His face held the same easy pleasantness it always held. But his eyes, in that half-second before he turned the corner, were the eyes of a man who had noticed a lock where there used to be an open door, and who was already considering what that meant.

Five minutes. It had taken five minutes to prove that carrying a secret and carrying it well were not the same thing.

They walked in silence past the milliner's, past the bakery, past the turn where the Meryton road began; and behind them the regiment's noise went on — a cart-wheel, a shouted order, a laugh — and each sound was a little farther off than the last, and none of them was a sound that would be there next week, or the week after, or ever again in quite that arrangement.

Elizabeth did not look back. She had looked her fill.

At the turn by the hedgerow, Kitty spoke. It was not a sentence; it was the beginning of one.  “*Lizzy, do you think”—*and she did not finish it. She pressed her lips together and shook her head once, as though to unask the question, and walked on.

Ahead of them the road bent toward Longbourn, and the hedgerows closed quietly behind.
