# BSP — Chapter 33: The Drawing Room

✅ Chapter complete

---

Elizabeth knew the light inside Pemberley at mid-morning now. It came through tall windows with the steady goodwill of a house that had been admitting summer for two hundred years and saw no reason to make a spectacle of it. The entrance hall received its portion without comment; the passage beyond received somewhat less; and by the time it reached the morning room, it had become the sort of light in which ordinary things — a writing desk, a chair drawn near the window, an aunt's face turning from a letter — looked no grander and no smaller than themselves.

Elizabeth crossed the threshold with Mr. Darcy beside her and found that the house did not remark upon it.

The house simply received them. The hall smelled of beeswax and warm dust and the distinct mineral stillness of old stone on a summer day, and none of it had altered because she had said yes on a gravel path that morning.

She had expected — not trumpets. She would have objected to trumpets. But some small shift in the furniture of the world, perhaps, to acknowledge that the furniture of her life had been rearranged entirely.

Mr. Darcy — William, he’d said she might call him — paused at the foot of the staircase. His hand was still near her arm — not touching it, not quite, but near enough that the air between them felt occupied.

"I should speak with Mrs. Reynolds," he said.

"Yes."

"And you will want —"

"My aunt. Yes."

They stood for a moment in the kind of silence that is not uncomfortable but is not, strictly speaking, silence, since it contains the sound of two people deciding how to be ordinary again after being extraordinary for half a morning. Then Mr. Darcy bowed — the slightest movement, nearly nothing — and turned away.

He crossed the hall, turned the corner, and disappeared. She pressed a hand against her ribs, breathed once, and went to find Mrs. Gardiner.

She found her in the morning room. The girls must be at the pianoforte in the music room, and conversation required a door between itself and music. She sat near the window with a letter open in her lap and her reading spectacles pushed up onto her forehead, which gave her the appearance of a woman interrupted between two thoughts and not yet committed to either.

She glanced up when Elizabeth entered. Then she took a longer look.

"Lizzy?"

"Aunt."

Mrs. Gardiner removed her spectacles, folded them, and set them on the table beside her letter with a precision that reminded Elizabeth, with a small shock, of her father. People who read a great deal, she supposed, developed the same habits with spectacles.

"Sit down," Mrs. Gardiner said.

The chair was firm and placed at an angle that allowed her to see both her aunt and the window, beyond which the day continued in its unembarrassed sunshine without the slightest intention of being solemn.

She had planned, on the walk from the entrance hall, to say it gracefully. She had composed a sentence involving the words *material change* and *sentiments* and perhaps *understanding*, which was the proper word for the thing that had just happened to her, though it failed entirely to describe it. She had planned to be calm, measured, and above all witty, because wit was the armor she reached for first and last and always.

What she said was, "Mr. Darcy has asked me to marry him, and I have said yes."

The sentence lay in the room like a stone dropped into still water. Not thrown — dropped. It made no splash. It simply sank.

Mrs. Gardiner was quiet for three seconds.

In the first second, her aunt's face did not change.

In the second, something behind it moved — not surprise, not satisfaction, not the knowing warmth of a woman who had seen it coming, but something closer to the expression of a person who has been holding a door steady against a wind and feels the wind stop.

In the third second, Mrs. Gardiner said, "Yes. I rather thought you might."

The clock on the mantel ticked once into the silence.

"You are not astonished."

"I am a great many things at this moment, Lizzy. Astonished is not among them."

"You might at least pretend."

"I might," Mrs. Gardiner agreed. "But you told me the truth just now, without a single clever word. I think you deserve the same.”

The kindness of that sentence — precise, unornamented, and aimed with the quiet accuracy that was her aunt's gift — nearly undid Elizabeth entirely. She looked down at her hands, which were folded in her lap in the attitude of a woman with nothing to confess, and found that they were steady. 

"How long have you known?" Elizabeth asked.

"Known is too strong a word. Hoped, perhaps. Observed." Mrs. Gardiner paused. "Since the evening he stood in the drawing room and watched you with Georgiana, and his face — but that is his story to tell, not mine."

"And you said nothing."

"What would you have me say? 'Lizzy, I believe Mr. Darcy is in love with you, and I believe you are in love with him, and I believe you will discover it approximately three weeks after it has become obvious to everyone else in the house'? That seemed indelicate."

Elizabeth laughed — the first real one all morning — and it surprised them both. "I would have denied it," she said.

"Vigorously."

"With evidence."

"Incorrect evidence, but offered with conviction. Yes." Mrs. Gardiner smiled. It was the smile Elizabeth had watched all her life and never fully understood until now — the one that said *I have been where you are, and I will not diminish it by saying so.* "Are you happy, Lizzy?"

The question was simple. The answer should have been simple.

"I am —" Elizabeth began, and stopped. She tried again. "I think I may be happy. It does not feel quite as I expected."

Mrs. Gardiner nodded once.

"I thought it would feel more like certainty."

"And what does it feel like?"

Elizabeth shifted in the chair. "Like having set down something very heavy that I did not know I was carrying."

Mrs. Gardiner reached across the space between their chairs and laid her hand on Elizabeth's arm. The gesture was brief, but the warmth of it stayed after her aunt's hand withdrew.

"And Mr. Gardiner's letter?" Elizabeth said, because someone must return them to practical ground. "Your departure?"

"I suspect Mr. Gardiner's letter can accommodate a slight delay in its urgency." Mrs. Gardiner's eyes were bright. "I shall have to write him a very different letter now. He will be insufferable. He told me in Lambton that he liked Mr. Darcy's manner, and he has been waiting ever since for an opportunity to be right about something."

Elizabeth opened her mouth, closed it, and shook her head. "I cannot decide whether you are comforting me or teasing me."

"Lizzy, I have been married fifteen years. I assure you it is possible to do both at once."

They sat together for a time after that, not speaking much, not needing to. The morning room held them with the quiet hospitality of a space meant for small parties and private conversation, and the light through the window moved across the floor with the unhurried progress of an afternoon that had nowhere particular to be.

At last Mrs. Gardiner said, "You will want to tell the girls."

"Yes."

"Shall I come with you?"

Elizabeth shook her head. "No. I think I should like to walk back through the house by myself for a moment.”

She rose, straightened her skirt, and paused at the door.

"Aunt."

"Yes?"

"Thank you for asking whether I wished to stay."

Her aunt tilted her head and gave it a little shake. "You were always going to stay, Lizzy. You simply needed someone to give you permission to know it."

---

The girls were in the drawing room.

Kitty sat at one end of the sofa, a piece of sheet music set aside on the cushion beside her, looking as though she had exhausted its possibilities some time ago. Georgiana sat beside her with a book open in her lap, reading with the absorption of a young woman who is not reading at all but is using the book as a screen behind which to think. Mrs. Annesley occupied her customary chair near the window, her embroidery progressing at the steady rate of a woman whose hands had learned to work without consulting her attention.

And Mr. Darcy. William.

He stood near the fireplace — unlit, in summer — one hand resting on the mantelpiece and the other at his side. He had changed his coat; the riding coat was gone, and in its place something darker, neater. Which meant he had been to his rooms, which meant he had stood before a glass and considered how he wished to appear in this room at this hour, and had decided upon a dark blue coat and a composed face and the fireplace.

When she stepped into the room, his eyes snapped to her.

Elizabeth had been watched by him before. She had been watched at Netherfield, where his gaze had felt like judgment. She had been watched at Rosings. She had been watched across dining tables and drawing rooms and garden paths, and she had learned to feel his attention the way one feels weather — a pressure system, constant, not always comfortable, impossible to ignore.

This was different. This was the face of a man who had offered everything he had and been accepted and did not yet know what to do with his hands.

She almost smiled. She crossed the room and sat down in the chair nearest the sofa, and as she sat, Kitty’s head turned.

Kitty's gift — Elizabeth had not always recognized it as a gift — was speed. She read rooms the way other people read print: left to right, top to bottom, the essential meaning extracted before the full sentence had been parsed. She took one look at Elizabeth, one at Mr. Darcy, and her face broke open like a window flung wide.

"Lizzy!"

"Kitty —"

"Lizzy, you are — are you — has he —"

She was already off the sofa, crossing the room. She caught Elizabeth's hands — both of them — and squeezed with a force that was slightly painful and entirely welcome.

"I *knew* it," she said. "I told Georgiana — I said, last week, I said *something has changed*, and she said I was imagining things, but I was not imagining things, I was —"

"Kitty." Elizabeth freed one hand and placed it over Kitty's, which were still gripping and showed no signs of stopping. "Yes."

"Yes?"

"Yes."

Kitty's eyes filled. She tucked herself against Elizabeth's side and held on. Elizabeth wrapped an arm around her.

Georgiana had risen from the sofa. She stood quite still, her hands clasped before her — a girl who had been taught, by circumstance and temperament, that wanting something too visibly was a form of risk.

Elizabeth held out her free hand.

Georgiana took it. Her fingers were cool and trembled once before steadying.

Her eyes found him.

From the fireplace, Mr. Darcy watched. His hand, which had rested so carefully on the mantelpiece, tightened once against the marble and then released. He looked from Georgiana's hand in Elizabeth's to Kitty's flushed face, and something in him seemed to settle.

---

Someone — Mrs. Annesley, probably — had quietly sent for a luncheon tray. Elizabeth ate bread and cold ham standing at the window without tasting either, and realized it was the first food she had taken all day.

Mr. Darcy was at the writing desk in the corner of the drawing room, his hand resting on the inkstand as though he had been arranging necessities in his mind for some time.

"I will write to your father," he said.

"Yes." Elizabeth crossed to the desk and drew out the second chair. The desk’s surface was wide, polished, and orderly in the way that his surfaces tended to be — everything aligned, everything in its place, the blotter centered with a precision that was either admirable or mildly alarming, depending upon one's feelings about blotters. "And I must write to Jane."

The pen was already cut. She drew a sheet of paper from the tray, dipped the nib, and wrote:

*My dearest Jane —*

*I have news that I think will surprise you, though I begin to suspect it will surprise no one but myself.*

The rest came quickly — honestly, without cleverness, because she was done dressing truth in wit when simplicity would serve.

Her father's letter was harder.

She sat with the pen in her hand and the blank page before her and thought about what she would say to a man who had stood in the library at Pemberley and told her to *know why you stay*. 

He had asked with love and expected — what? That she would stay for Kitty's sake. For duty. For the sensible reasons that a sensible daughter offers a sensible father. He would not have expected this.

The truth was not complicated. It was only difficult to say to a man who had spent twenty years hiding behind a newspaper rather than say difficult things himself. *I stayed because I wished to. I stay because I choose to. The reason is not duty, Papa. The reason is that I have found a man I can trust — not because he has never erred, but because he has erred and corrected and did not ask me to pretend the error away.*

She would not write that — not in those words. But she would write something near it, and he would read it in his library with his spectacles on and his newspaper folded and the decanter at his elbow, and he would understand. Not everything, perhaps. But enough.

"There is Lady Catherine to consider as well," Mr. Darcy said, from the other end of the desk. His pen had stopped.

"She will not be pleased," Elizabeth said, which was so enormous an understatement that it briefly achieved comedy.

"No."

"Maybe we should invite another cousin of mine. To soften the blow."

Mr. Darcy glanced at her. "I was not aware that cousins served a medicinal purpose."

"Or of yours," she said.

He was quiet for a moment. Then: "I have not thought enough about what Anne might wish." He looked down at his unfinished letter. The ink had gathered darkly at the end of one word. After a moment, he blotted it.

Kitty's voice drifted in from the sofa, where she was telling Georgiana something about wedding clothes with the happy authority of a girl who had never attended a wedding but held very firm opinions about sleeves.

"Mama will be impossible," Elizabeth said, turning back to her letter.

"I expect Mrs. Bennet will be —"

"Rapturous. Incandescent. Vindicated." Elizabeth heard her mother's voice in her own head with the vivid clarity of a theatrical performance that required no stage. *Three daughters settled! And one of them at Pemberley! Oh, Mr. Bennet, did I not say — did I not always say —* "She will credit herself entirely."

"Naturally."

"She will tell every woman in Meryton."

"I would expect nothing less."

"She will write to Lady Lucas before my father finishes reading your letter.”

Mr. Darcy's pen resumed its progress across the page. "Then I had better write short."

She took in the bent head, the careful hand, the gravity with which he had delivered that small terrible joke, and knew she had chosen well.

---

The dinner bell found them still in the drawing room. The room had altered while no one attended to it: Kitty's ribbon lay across the sofa arm, Georgiana's book had slid facedown on the cushion, and the ink on all the letters had dried.

They went to dress, and then reassembled and moved to the dining room in the loose procession of a household too small for ceremony but too well-bred to abandon it entirely. Mr. Darcy offered Mrs. Gardiner his arm. Georgiana followed, with Kitty beside her, talking — Kitty was always talking now, and the sound of it didn’t grate but belonged, the way birdsong belongs to a garden not because it is beautiful but because it would be wrong without it.

Georgiana paused just inside the door. She had been quiet through the afternoon — not withdrawn, not unhappy, but quiet. Now she looked at Elizabeth, and at the table beyond, and at the chair at the foot where she had sat every evening since she was old enough to sit at dinner.

"Would you sit here?" Georgiana asked. Her voice was soft, but steady. "I should like to see you here."

Elizabeth opened her mouth to protest — it was too soon, it was not proper, she was not yet anything but a guest with a promise — but Georgiana had already moved to a chair along the side with the settled grace of a young woman who had made a decision and did not intend to discuss it. She looked, not diminished, but relieved, as if the place she had given up had not been lost but filled.

At the head of the table, Mr. Darcy's hand stilled on the back of his chair. He looked first at Georgiana — a look so brief Elizabeth might have missed it — and then at Elizabeth. Then he nodded, once.

Elizabeth sat at the foot of Mr. Darcy's table.

The chair was the same as every other chair in the room — mahogany, upholstered, neither especially comfortable nor especially grand. It did not feel like a throne. It felt like a chair. But from this chair the room arranged itself differently. She could see all of them at once: Mr. Darcy at the far end, too composed by half; Mrs. Gardiner to his right, amusement tucked into the corner of her mouth; Mrs. Annesley next to Mrs. Gardiner with her hands folded and her attention everywhere; Georgiana on his left, and beside her Kitty, one dark ribbon nearly touching one pale one, their whispers threaded through the clink of spoons and glass.

Mrs. Annesley praised the soup. Kitty said something about the bread. Georgiana said something to Mrs. Gardiner across the table — a murmured word that made Mrs. Gardiner — Elizabeth's aunt, and soon to be Georgiana's by whatever bonds affection made before law had caught up — smile.

Steam rose from the soup between them and blurred him for one brief, ridiculous instant, and she thought: *I will see this face across this table for the rest of my life.* The thought did not frighten her. It settled somewhere beneath her ribs, warm and strange. The linen lay cool beneath her wrist; the silver, when she touched it, had already taken the warmth of her fingers, as if the house had decided she belonged here before she had. 

The bread broke under Kitty's hand with a soft crackle that sounded, impossibly, like home — not Longbourn, not yet Pemberley, but something being made between the two. A candle near Mrs. Annesley bent its flame once, then steadied, and the smell of beeswax and soup and the faint green breath of the grounds through an open window was so ordinary, so unremarkable, that Elizabeth had to look down at her plate for a moment before she could trust her face.

The dining room at Pemberley was not large, for a great house. The table could seat twenty and held six. Outside, the grounds stretched into evening, and beyond them the road that led south to Longbourn, where a different table waited in a different life.

Elizabeth picked up her spoon.

Halfway through the soup, Mr. Darcy caught Elizabeth's eye across the cloth. His face held the last of the evening light, and for once she did not have to ask what it meant.

Elizabeth lifted her glass.

A moment later, Mr. Darcy lifted his. No toast was spoken. None was required.

The wine was light and sharp on her tongue. Near Georgiana, Kitty began another sentence before she had finished the last; Georgiana's laugh, small and astonished by itself, slipped into the candlelight.

It was enough. It was, she found, exactly enough.
