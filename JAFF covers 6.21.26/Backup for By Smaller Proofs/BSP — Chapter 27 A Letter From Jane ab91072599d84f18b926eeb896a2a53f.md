# BSP — Chapter 27: A Letter From Jane

✅ Chapter complete

---

Pemberley had not altered while they were gone.

That was the unkindness of it.

The same doors opened. The same servants appeared with the same noiseless precision. The same cool hall received them from Lambton as if nothing in the world had been disturbed by a bonnet, a shopkeeper’s smile, half a sentence left unfinished, and Mr. Darcy’s hand beneath hers at the carriage step.

Elizabeth entered with the others and understood, with a clarity almost worse than confusion, that the disturbance was entirely her own.

Mr. Darcy did not follow them in. Having handed them down with all proper attention, he took his horse’s bridle from the groom and said something to Georgiana of Robinson, the west stable, and the new foal. A perfectly natural excuse. Also, Elizabeth discovered, another fact capable of four meanings.

Kitty was first across the threshold, still bright with the afternoon, one hand guarding the reticule in which Mr. Bennet the wooden cow had acquired all the consequence of a family relic. The fair came in with her. It clung to her pelisse in a trace of gingerbread; it had left dust along Mrs. Gardiner’s hem and a faint crease in Georgiana’s new bonnet ribbon. Somewhere outside, his horse’s hooves crossed the stones toward the stable-yard, each strike too distinct in the quiet.

Within doors, every footfall was softened, every voice lowered, every object already restored to its proper place.

Elizabeth had no proper place to restore herself to.

The hall offered no opinion. Its floors shone, its doors closed softly, and every servant had the mercy to notice nothing. Such discretion ought to have been kindness. Instead it left Elizabeth alone with the one witness she could not dismiss: herself.

The witness was exacting. It knew what must be done. Kitty’s delight must be answered. Miss Darcy’s bonnet must be praised. Mrs. Gardiner must not be alarmed. Mr. Darcy’s absence must not be examined, though every hoofbeat outside seemed determined to offer itself as evidence.

Elizabeth did each thing in turn. She smiled at the wooden cow. She admired the bonnet. She answered her aunt with enough sense that no one required the answer twice.

It was not composure.

It was obedience to the shape of composure.

A footman approached with the salver. Mrs. Gardiner glanced at the direction of the uppermost letter and took it with a smile. “From Gracechurch Street. Your uncle has remembered that his wife exists, which is always agreeable.”

The next letter was offered to Elizabeth.

Jane’s hand.

For one brief, blessed second, everything steadied. Even when Mrs. Bennet had dictated half a page and Lydia had seized the pen to add underlinings, Jane’s hand remained composed. It belonged to home; to candor without severity; to affection which did not press to be understood before it had been offered.

Elizabeth closed her fingers round it too quickly.

“From Longbourn?” Mrs. Gardiner asked.

“Yes. From Jane.”

“How fortunate. You will have news to carry us through the evening.”

Elizabeth meant to answer. She could not immediately find the words. Mr. Darcy entered then from the outer door, his hat in one hand, the faint scent of horse and summer air coming in with him before the house swallowed both. He spoke first to Georgiana — something of Robinson’s verdict upon the foal, perhaps, or of whether the bonnet should be sent upstairs — but Elizabeth could feel the care with which he did not look toward her.

It should have relieved her.

It did not.

“I believe,” she said, holding the letter as if it required all her attention, “I have a small headache. The sun, perhaps. Nothing of consequence.”

Mrs. Gardiner’s concern sharpened at once; Kitty looked stricken; Georgiana turned from her brother, her question to him left unfinished.

“Only a very slight one,” Elizabeth said, before their kindness could make a scene of it. “I shall read Jane’s letter and be restored to perfect sense.”

Mr. Darcy had ceased speaking. His face showed concern, quickly checked.

His face showed concern, quickly checked. Not coldness. Not warmth that might be observed. Something held in such strict custody that she could not tell whether it had been meant for her or for the room.

“Miss Bennet,” he said, “I hope you will soon be better.”

“Thank you.”

Two words. They had managed a whole conversation of two words.

She went upstairs before she could decide whether that was better or worse.

---

The bedroom was easier than she could bear.

Afternoon light lay softly across the carpet. A glass of water waited by the washstand; the bell-pull hung within easy reach; the upholstered chair near the window had been placed at just such an angle as might invite rest.

She had fled to be alone and found herself surrounded by consideration.

Jane would fix it. 

She laid her bonnet and gloves on the bed with less care than either deserved, crossed to the washstand, and pressed cool water to her temples. Then she sank into the chair by the window.

Jane’s letter smelled faintly of home.

Elizabeth held it a moment longer. The paper had warmed a little beneath her fingers. As if steadiness might be borrowed from paper, she smoothed her thumb once over Jane’s direction.

She unfolded the sheet.

*My dearest Lizzy,*

*I hardly know how to write what has happened, though I have written the words twice already and still cannot make them look less extraordinary.*

The comfort of Jane’s hand vanished at once.

She read on.

*Mr. Bingley is returned to Netherfield.*

The words struck before meaning could follow.

There were more words. Elizabeth saw them. She did not at first understand them. She read on, too quickly now, searching for the shape of the event before the fact itself could settle.

*He came yesterday, quite unexpectedly. We had heard nothing of his intention, though my mother insists she had felt all morning that something particular would occur, and that her nerves had foretold it with remarkable accuracy. Papa says her nerves are entitled to the credit of every event in the county, since they are never quiet enough to miss one.*

The sentence had her father’s dry turn in it, but it drew no laugh. Elizabeth read past it with her pulse still beating too high.

*He called at Longbourn before dinner. I will not pretend I was not surprised, or that I behaved with all the composure I wished. He looked very much as he used to look, and yet not quite so. There was a steadiness in him which I do not know how to describe. He asked whether I had lately heard from you, and whether you and my aunt remained at Pemberley. He spoke warmly of Derbyshire.*

Warmly of Derbyshire.

Elizabeth lowered the letter. Beyond the window, the rising ground climbed steadily away from the house, offering no road, no answer, no visible proof of any journey begun from Pemberley.

Mr. Bingley had been here. He and Mr. Darcy must have had hours together — at dinner, after dinner, in all the ordinary intervals by which gentlemen contrived to be private without seeming to arrange it. Then Mr. Bingley had gone south. Now he was returned to Netherfield.

Nothing in that proved anything. That was precisely why it would not leave her alone.

*Today he came again. Before dinner, he had spoken to Papa.*

Elizabeth’s hand tightened on the page. There was only one meaning to that, and yet she read on as if Jane might soften it, explain it, make it less enormous.

Jane’s next line was very short.

*Lizzy, I am very happy.*

Elizabeth’s hand went to her mouth. For a moment, there was nothing in her but joy.

Jane was happy.

Jane, who had smiled at Netherfield when she had been feverish and still tried to make everyone comfortable. Jane, who had borne disappointment with such gentleness that others had mistaken it for indifference. Jane, whose good opinion had been used against her by those who did not deserve it. Happy.

Mr. Bingley had returned. Mr. Bingley had spoken. Mr. Bingley had not drifted forever in some London drawing room, warm-hearted and weak, while his sisters arranged his future and his friend’s error stood between him and the woman he loved.

Jane was happy.

A laugh rose then, unsteady and bright, with tears close behind it.

Then joy, having filled the room, began to make space for other things.

She had not been there.

That was the first wound. Not that Jane was happy — never that — but that Jane’s happiness had entered Longbourn without her. Netherfield had opened again. Bingley had crossed the drive. Mr. Bennet had been spoken to. Jane had accepted. And Elizabeth had been miles away, learning of it only after happiness had already taken its seat among them.

She had received it by post. 

She read the rest with an attention sharpened almost to pain.

Jane was restrained. Jane was bewildered. Jane did not write like a woman who had expected to be rewarded. She wrote like one who feared that happiness might vanish if described too boldly.

*I cannot account for the suddenness of his return. He says he has long wished to come back, and that he has been very foolish. You know him well enough to imagine the kindness of his manner; indeed, I think there is more kindness in it than ever, for it seems less anxious to please everybody and more desirous of speaking truly.*

Less anxious to please everybody.

Had Mr. Bingley become more himself, or had someone restored him to himself? Had absence taught him to know his own heart? Had his sisters’ designs exhausted themselves? Had London at last become dull?

Or had Mr. Darcy told him?

The thought was not new. It had been waiting beneath every line. It rose now because Jane, with all her mildness, had written the only sentence capable of summoning it.

*He has been very foolish.*

Elizabeth heard, without wishing to hear, another letter.

*I had often seen him in love before.*

The words came back with their old cold confidence. At Hunsford, Mr. Darcy had been certain of Mr. Bingley’s heart, certain of Jane’s reticence, certain of his own right to divide them. Elizabeth had hated him for it — and then hated herself for having been so certain, too.

If Mr. Bingley had returned because Mr. Darcy had spoken, then Mr. Darcy had corrected in humility what he had once done in certainty.

If Mr. Bingley had returned for some other reason, then Elizabeth had wished Mr. Darcy into virtue without evidence.

There lay the humiliation.

*My mother is in such spirits as you can imagine, though perhaps you cannot, for imagination has limits and Mama’s spirits have none. She has already mentioned lace, settlements, gowns, Mrs. Long’s astonishment, and the probability of my being the happiest creature in the world no fewer than twelve times. Papa has been very kind. He says Mr. Bingley is a sensible young man because he has chosen the best woman in England and foolish because he took so long to discover it.*

Elizabeth smiled through fresh tears.

*Lydia remains out of humor. She says there is no merit in being married if one must be engaged in so quiet a way, and that she would have liked at least a carriage upset or a duel. Mary has observed that those who marry from the purest motives need not desire violent distinction. Mama told her not to be tiresome on such a day.*

Jane wrote that Kitty’s absence had been much felt, that their mother had lamented it in one breath and rejoiced in it in the next, because “dear Kitty must learn elegance at Pemberley, though she is cruelly missing all the wedding talk.” Jane hoped Kitty was well, hoped Miss Darcy would not tire of her, hoped Elizabeth would tell her everything of Derbyshire when they were together again.

Then, near the end:

*I wish you were here. Not because I would recall you from what is proper or pleasant, but because there are some joys one longs first to tell to the person who has known the sorrow. I think you will understand me even when I hardly understand myself.*

Elizabeth’s tears came then in earnest.

She understood Jane’s restraint, her gratitude, her wonder. She understood, too, the kindness of that sentence: Jane wished her there, but did not call her home.

The letter had brought happiness from Longbourn. It had not brought Elizabeth back to it.

Elizabeth folded the letter once along its crease and then opened it again immediately, as if evidence might rearrange itself under inspection.

It did not.

Jane was happy. Mr. Bingley had returned. He had been very foolish. He had spoken to Papa.

And still there was no proof of Mr. Darcy.

She rose too quickly. The room shifted, though whether from the headache she had invented or the one she had produced, she could not tell. She crossed to the washstand, dipped her fingers in the water, and touched them to her temples again. The water was cool. It did not answer any question.

If Mr. Darcy had acted, what then?

Nothing, she told herself. Nothing must follow. Jane’s happiness was Jane’s. Mr. Bingley’s choice was Mr. Bingley’s. Mr. Darcy’s conscience, if conscience had moved him, belonged to him alone. Elizabeth had no right to build a private claim upon another person’s repentance.

But if he had acted and said nothing — if he had repaired the injury without presenting the repair at her feet — then the proof was cleaner than gratitude. It asked nothing. It stood at a distance. It did not say, *See what I have done; love me for it.*

That was unbearable.

She had been wrong before. At Hunsford, she had been magnificently, eloquently wrong. Wrong with arguments, wrong with proofs, wrong with the whole force of injured pride and lively intelligence. She had believed herself discerning because she had been offended. She had believed herself just because she had been angry.

And since then? Since Pemberley? His altered manner — if it had altered. His care for Georgiana. His civility to the Gardiners. His patient attention to Kitty. His handing liberty to his sister in small portions, and looking as if each portion cost him something dear. She had called that proof. She had watched, weighed, considered, corrected herself.

And still she did not know whether she was seeing more clearly, or only wanting more dangerously.

A knock sounded.

“Lizzy?” Mrs. Gardiner’s voice came through the door. “May I come in?”

Elizabeth folded the letter at last. “Yes. Of course.”

Mrs. Gardiner entered with a glass in one hand and concern in her face. She took in Elizabeth’s eyes, the letter, the abandoned gloves, and the chair pushed back at an angle. She set the lemonade on the small table beside it.

“I brought you lemonade,” she said. “It is a very poor remedy for a headache, but an excellent excuse for bringing oneself into a room.”

Elizabeth managed an unsteady laugh. “Then it is better than most remedies.” 

Her aunt set the lemonade on the small table beside Elizabeth. “My dear,” she said, “what has happened?”

Elizabeth’s fingers closed round Jane’s letter until the paper creased faintly beneath her thumb. There could be no keeping such news from her aunt. Nor did she wish to keep it. “Good news. Jane.” She tried to smile as the words deserved. “The best news.”

She held out the letter, then drew it back. Some parts were Jane’s before they were anyone else’s. “Mr. Bingley has returned to Netherfield.”

Her aunt’s hand went to her heart. “Oh, Lizzy.”

“He spoke to my father.”

The delight in her aunt’s face was immediate and unguarded. “He has proposed?”

“Yes.”

“And Jane?”

“Very happy.”

“My dear girl.” Her aunt came to her then, not with exclamation, not with a hundred questions, but with both hands extended. Elizabeth took them and was drawn, for one moment, into an embrace that smelled of lavender, road dust, and gingerbread. “What joy. What perfect joy for her.”

“Yes,” Elizabeth said into her aunt’s shoulder. “It is.” Her breath caught.

Her aunt held her a little longer and then stepped back enough to look at her. “My dear, I cannot lay all this at joy’s door. You were not easy before Jane’s letter.”

Elizabeth gave a watery laugh. “Then I am worse than I thought, for I cannot even receive happiness properly.”

“That is nonsense, and you know it.”

“I wish I knew it.”

Elizabeth turned away. She took up Jane’s letter, folded it, and aligned the edges with unnecessary care. “Mr. Bingley was lately here.”

“Yes.”

“He left Pemberley, and now he is at Netherfield.”

Mrs. Gardiner waited.

Elizabeth could not say, *Mr. Darcy separated them once.*

What she said was, “He and Mr. Darcy were in the same house for days before he went. They must have spoken of many things.” Of Jane, her mind supplied at once. “That is all I know.”

Mrs. Gardiner did not answer at once.

“It is not enough to mean anything,” Elizabeth said quickly. “Mr. Bingley may have returned from his own conviction. He ought to have done. Perhaps he did. Perhaps he needed no one to tell him what he felt. Perhaps men of large fortune go from Derbyshire to Hertfordshire and propose the next day from no cause at all except the excellence of Hertfordshire roads.”

“Come, Lizzy. Sit down.” Her aunt guided her gently into the chair, then drew the dressing-table chair close and sat.

“I do not know anything,” Elizabeth said.

There it was. Flat and ugly and true.

Mrs. Gardiner studied her for a moment. “You know that Jane is happy.”

“Yes.”

“And that Mr. Bingley has chosen her.”

“Yes.”

“And that whatever brought him there, his speaking to your father was his own act.”

Elizabeth nodded. “Yes.”

“Those are not small things.”

“No.”

“But they are not the whole of what troubles you.”

Elizabeth looked down at the letter. Jane’s happiness should have been able to fill the whole room. Instead it had become a lamp turned upon every hidden corner of Elizabeth’s mind.

“Aunt,” she said, “have you ever been afraid that you have corrected one error only by falling into another?”

Mrs. Gardiner did not smile. “Often. It is one of the few amusements of growing older.”

Despite herself, Elizabeth almost smiled; some of the tightness in her chest eased.

“I believed Mr. Darcy proud and ungenerous,” Elizabeth said. “In some things, I was right. In more, I was wrong. Since coming here, I have seen —” She stopped again because the words would not remain general. “I have seen him act otherwise than I expected.”

“Yes,” Mrs. Gardiner said.

“And I have tried to be fair.”

“Yes.”

“And perhaps I have become something else.”

Her aunt’s hand came to rest on the back of the chair.

Elizabeth’s mouth twisted. “Partial.”

The word hung there. It was not confession, but it was nearer to one than Elizabeth had meant to come.

“Partiality is not always an enemy to judgment.”

“It has been to mine before.”

“Ah. With Mr. Wickham.”

Elizabeth flinched. Mrs. Gardiner saw it, but did not retract the name. There was mercy in that, too. Wickham should not be allowed the dignity of being unspeakable.

“I liked Mr. Wickham because he made liking him easy,” Elizabeth said. “I disliked Mr. Darcy because he made disliking him easy. There was sense in neither judgment, only pleasure. One pleasure was being flattered. The other was being offended.”

Mrs. Gardiner was silent a moment. “And what pleasure is there now?”

Elizabeth did not answer.

A sound came from below: a door closing, a man’s step in the passage, then quiet again. Pemberley had a way of making even distant sounds seem deliberate.

Mrs. Gardiner followed the direction of her attention.

“I saw Mr. Darcy as I came up,” she said.

Elizabeth went still.

“He asked after you.”

She could not trust herself to speak.

“He wished to know whether you had recovered from the fatigue of Lambton.” Mrs. Gardiner paused. “Or whether anything there had made you uneasy.” Her brow drew together, as if the wording had puzzled her.

Elizabeth’s face warmed at once.

*Anything there.*

Not the crowd. Not the heat. Not the length of the walk. *Anything*.

The word carried her straight to Lambton: to the bonnet-stall, the woman’s smile, Mr. Darcy’s careful withdrawal afterward. Whether it meant concern, apology, or only civility, she could not tell.

“How very considerate,” she said.

Mrs. Gardiner’s brows rose slightly.

Elizabeth heard herself and wished to snatch the words back. They had come out too polished, too brittle.

She tried again. “I was not made uneasy by Lambton. Not — that is, not by anything of consequence.”

Mrs. Gardiner did not press her. “He did not come himself. That was well done of him, I think.”

Elizabeth’s answer came more softly than she intended. “Yes.”

“He appeared concerned.”

Elizabeth had not prepared herself for that word.

Mrs. Gardiner held her gaze with calm. “I am not matchmaking. Do not prepare a speech. I am observing that he appeared concerned, and that he chose not to force his concern upon you. Those are both facts.”

Facts. Elizabeth had wanted facts. She had asked, silently and often, for proof. She had not considered that proof might be made of restraint; that it might arrive by post, addressed in Jane’s hand, and say nothing of Mr. Darcy at all.

Mrs. Gardiner rose. “You should not sit here until dinner.”

“I do not mean to be ungrateful for Jane’s happiness by wandering about like a tragic heroine.”

“I would not advise tragedy. It requires better lighting and more obedient hair.”

Elizabeth looked at her aunt in astonishment, then laughed.

“There,” said Mrs. Gardiner. “That is improved. Walk, Lizzy. Not far, and not where you will lose yourself. But walk. Thoughts are apt to grow very large in a closed room.”

Elizabeth looked toward the window. The late light lay across the lawn, soft and slanting. Beyond it, the paths dropped toward the wilds, where Georgiana had once shown them a secret and where Elizabeth had first seen something of Pemberley that was not grandeur.

“Perhaps I will.”

---

The air outside was cool beneath the beeches, where moss softened the path’s edge and the first damp breath of the stream rose through the ferny hollows. A rabbit broke from the fern at Elizabeth’s approach and vanished under the trees. The noise and color of the Lambton fair seemed to belong to another day.

Elizabeth walked without choosing the direction. Her feet, being wiser than her mind, took her toward the lower path.

The gravel gave way to firmer earth. Branches drew nearer. The house, when she looked back, stood in partial view between the trees, not softened exactly, but made less declarative by distance. It had once intimidated her even in imagination. Then she had seen it and found it beautiful in a way that did not boast of its own importance. Then she had met its master in its grounds and learned that Pemberley’s restraint answered only one question about him.

The path bent again, and the house disappeared.

She remembered Georgiana walking here, offering names of shrubs and small histories of improvements as if each sentence were a coin she had saved and was nervous to spend. 

Georgiana’s laugh had been rarer then. Since then, he had opened doors.

Not all at once. Never theatrically. He had permitted a fair. He had invited Kitty. He had made Georgiana’s pleasure possible, then withdrawn the instant local hopes placed Elizabeth where no one had asked whether she wished to stand.

Had he withdrawn for her sake, not his own?

The thought stopped her where she stood.

A bird rustled the leaves above her. She looked up and saw nothing but motion after the fact.

Might he have been protecting her? The thought was both reasonable and dangerous. It fit the evidence too well. His immediate correctness, his care not to leave her exposed at the bonnet-stall, his refusal afterward to walk at her side, his inquiry through Mrs. Gardiner instead of coming himself — a pattern could be made of it.

A man may be correct from kindness.

Or from embarrassment.

Or from regret.

Elizabeth drew a breath and found it unsteady. 

She resumed walking.

The path descended a little, and the water grew louder, though still unseen. Pemberley did not announce all its beauties. It let them arrive by turns, as if the visitor’s heart might be trusted to keep pace.

Elizabeth walked faster.

A twig caught at the hem of her gown. She freed it with more force than necessary and then stood with the fabric in her hand, breathing as if she had been the one caught.

What did she want?

Elizabeth had never thought herself timid in the matter of wanting. She had refused Mr. Collins against every practical argument, and Mr. Darcy against every worldly one. She knew how to reject what her heart could not accept.

It was the accepting that frightened her. Nor could she pretend that feeling, because inconvenient, did not exist.

What did she want?

She wanted Jane happy. That was simple.

She wanted Mr. Bingley worthy of Jane’s happiness. That was only just.

She wanted Mr. Darcy to have told him. There. It was said nowhere, but it existed. She wanted Mr. Darcy to have done the difficult, quiet thing. She wanted his reserve at the fair to have been protection rather than retreat. She wanted his inquiry to mean concern, not mere civility. She wanted the evidence to point where her heart had already gone.

Her heart.

Elizabeth gave a short laugh that startled a nearby blackbird from the undergrowth. It scolded her from a safer branch, and she could not blame it.

Very well. Her heart, since euphemism had failed.

It had not declared itself grandly. It had not asked permission. It had done far worse: it had made evidence of his restraint, his kindness, his respect, his patience. It had built, piece by piece, an argument for him; and because Elizabeth understood arguments, she had mistaken construction for caution.

Fairness had become desire without asking leave.

The water appeared through the trees, silvered in the late sun. Elizabeth stopped at the turn. She remembered standing not far from here with Georgiana, listening as she explained the stepping-stones and blushed at having made so much of so small a place.

She smoothed open the letter again.

She read only one line.

*Lizzy, I am very happy.*

“Then let it be enough,” Elizabeth said aloud. Her voice sounded strange in the trees.

Be happy for Jane. Be happy without dragging Mr. Darcy into every corner of it.

But he was already there.

She knew enough of who he was to be afraid. He was proud. He was honorable. He could be severe. He could be wrong with alarming strength. He loved deeply and guarded fiercely. He had learned, or was learning, that protection could become imprisonment and judgment could become injury. He listened. He altered. Not always gracefully, not always easily, but enough that she could no longer pretend not to see it.

The question was whether she could trust herself where he was concerned.

She folded Jane’s letter again, slower this time.

She had come out for certainty and found none. Only the shape of her own wish, clearer than before and far more difficult to dismiss.

She wanted him to be good. She wanted his goodness to have reached Jane. She wanted it enough to distrust herself for wanting it.

Elizabeth stood until the light shifted and the first coolness of evening entered the shade. Dinner was soon. Mr. Darcy would be there, and Mr. Bingley’s name would be spoken. Elizabeth would have to sit at table with one and hear the other without betraying herself.

The return path was shorter, or else her thoughts had spent themselves into a shape that could be carried. The house reappeared by degrees. First the upper windows, bright with reflected sky. Then the stone front. Then the steps, the door, the settled order of a place that could contain fairs, letters, misjudgments, and dinner without altering its expression.

Elizabeth envied it.

By the time she reached her room, the maid had come to lay out a fresh gown. Elizabeth set Jane’s letter on the dressing table beside her gloves and stood alone before the glass.

Her face looked like hers. That was something. Her eyes were a little too bright, but candlelight would forgive much. She smoothed one curl, failed to improve it, and let it be.

She picked it up once more, then hesitated. It was not a letter for dinner tables or eager questions. She set it beneath her handkerchief, where it would be safe and near and not visible to the first person who entered.

Jane was happy.

Mr. Darcy would be at dinner.

Elizabeth rang for her maid.
