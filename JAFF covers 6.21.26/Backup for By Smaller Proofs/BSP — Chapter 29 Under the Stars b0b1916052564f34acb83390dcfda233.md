# BSP — Chapter 29: Under the Stars

✅ Completed

"Yes," Elizabeth said.

It was a small word, and therefore more easily managed than any larger one. It committed her to nothing beyond a few minutes upon the lawn. It did not say she wished for air. It did not say she wished for company. It did not say she wished for anything at all.

Kitty, however, received it as a promise of general happiness. "Oh, excellent! Georgiana, you must show me where the stars are best seen. I am certain Pemberley has a particular place for it."

"I do not know that we have a particular place," Georgiana said, but with such pleasure at being consulted that the uncertainty became almost eagerness. "The south lawn is open."

"Then we shall make it particular by going there.”

Mrs. Gardiner rose with a look that took in Kitty's delight, Georgiana's quiet animation, Mr. Darcy's stillness, and Elizabeth's face. Elizabeth did not know what her aunt saw there; she only knew that Mrs. Gardiner's expression softened, and that softness was almost worse than inquiry.

Elizabeth did not look at Mr. Darcy. She had been not looking at him all evening, and the effort of it had grown no easier with practice.

"Shawls, then," said Mrs. Gardiner. "If we are to admire the heavens, we must not do so at the expense of our throats."

Mrs. Annesley raised a hand, as if to excuse herself from any further exertion. "You must admire them for me. I have had all the exercise my spirits can support today.”

"Your relations have worn you quite out," Kitty said with sympathy, then colored as if she had perhaps spoken too plainly.

Mrs. Annesley’s smile deepened. "My relations have the advantage of being numerous.”

"Then we shall admire the stars for you,” Kitty said, recovering. "Though I am not always perfect with their names.”

"That is because the stars have very poor names,” Elizabeth said, because something was required of her, and wit was easier than truth. "They would be remembered more often if anyone had thought to call them after people one knew.”

The shawls arrived, and the party went out by the terrace doors. The night received them coolly but not coldly. The house behind them showed here and there a square of warm gold; before them the lawn fell away into a gentler darkness, and above it the sky opened, clear and deep, with the stars set high beyond all management.

Elizabeth drew one breath and then another. The sight checked her. There was a greatness in the sky which no drawing room could answer; the stars seemed at once remote and piercingly near, and for a moment all the anxieties of rooms and voices were held at a distance.

But only at a distance. The air was easier than the room had been; it made fewer demands. It did not require her to answer congratulations, or accept kindness, or sit near a man who said very little and meant too much by it.

Kitty stopped almost at once. "Oh."

Georgiana stopped beside her. "Are they different?"

"They are higher," Kitty said. "Or brighter? Or perhaps I am only looking up properly. At Longbourn there are trees and chimneys and Mama saying one must not stand in the damp."

"They are the same stars, Kitty," Elizabeth said.

"Yes, Lizzy, but from a better county."

Georgiana turned her head. "A better county?"

Kitty didn’t take her eyes off the stars. "I did not mean Hertfordshire is a bad county. Only that Derbyshire is a better one for seeing things. Mountains and lawns and all that. Hertfordshire is very good for officers."

"That is not a recommendation," Elizabeth said.

"No," Kitty agreed, with feeling. Then, remembering herself, "At least, not always."

Georgiana looked from Kitty to Elizabeth with the careful expression of one who wished to understand the joke without being obliged to ask for its history. "I do not think counties can alter the stars."

Kitty's chin lifted a very little. "I did not say alter."

"No. Of course not. I only meant—"

"I know very well what I meant," Kitty said, and then looked as if she did not know it half so well as she would have liked.

The silence that followed was tiny, but complete. It might have passed unnoticed at a larger party; here, under the open sky and with every feeling already sharpened, it seemed to Elizabeth as visible as the moon.

Georgiana drew her shawl closer about her, as if she had been the one corrected.

Mr. Darcy moved. It was not much — half a step, perhaps, toward his sister. Then he checked himself and was still. But the half-step had carried him nearer to Elizabeth, and she felt the closeness of it like a change in temperature.

Georgiana seemed to feel his movement without looking at him. She straightened a little, her hands still holding the shawl close. "I beg your pardon. I did not mean to correct you.”

Kitty’s chin dropped. "Oh! You did not. Or if you did, I dare say I required it. Lydia says I am always saying things wrong, though she says so when she is saying things worse."

"Then perhaps," Georgiana said, very softly, "Hertfordshire has the same stars under worse management."

Kitty stared at her.

"I only meant—" Georgiana began, but the words trailed away.

"Yes," Kitty said, in delight. "That is exactly what I meant."

The thing was mended almost before it had torn. A mistake, a sting, a blush, an apology; then laughter standing close behind it, admitted without trial. Kitty moved nearer to Georgiana and began explaining, with more charity than accuracy, the various defects of Longbourn's shrubbery as an observatory. Georgiana listened as if no observatory had ever been more deserving of study.

Elizabeth watched them and felt something within her twist.

It was not envy. Or if it was, it was envy of the simplest sort: that two girls might misunderstand each other, be sorry, and go on. That affection might be so lightly bruised and so easily restored. That no letter need be written, no pride defeated, no history revised.

Mrs. Gardiner had drawn Kitty and Georgiana a few steps ahead, pointing out how the Plough led the eye toward the Pole Star. Their voices carried back softly — instruction, question, laughter — and left behind them a silence that belonged only to Elizabeth and the man beside her.

"They are very happy together," Mr. Darcy said. He had not moved again since the half-step, but the night air carried warmth from his coat — clean linen and something that was only him — and his voice reached her with the intimacy of something said in a smaller place.

"Yes," she said. "They are."

"I am glad of it."

That might have been all. It ought to have been all. The words were harmless, and true, and entirely suitable to the situation. But he said them with such care that Elizabeth heard not only gladness in them, but cost. He had once feared Kitty. He had feared Longbourn, Lydia, noise, influence, disorder — everything that might touch his sister and alter her. Now Kitty stood beneath his stars and made Georgiana laugh.

Elizabeth did not know whether she wished to forgive him for that too.

"Miss Bennet," he said, "is the air too cool? We need not remain if you are fatigued."

There it was again: care made perfectly correct, kindness offered in such a manner that it could not be refused without seeming petulant nor accepted without seeming weak.

Elizabeth said nothing.

Kitty and Georgiana moved a few paces farther, still arguing amiably over whether a star could be said to belong to a county if every county saw it. 

Elizabeth glanced back at the house. The windows still showed their squares of warm gold. The drawing room had been too bright for truth; the lawn, it seemed, the sky might be dark enough for every thought she had forbidden herself to have.

"No," she said. "The air is not too cool."

"Then perhaps the walk was ill-timed."

"It was very kindly meant."

Something changed in his voice at that. Elizabeth heard it, and resented hearing.

"I did not mean to impose upon you," he said.

"No. You are very careful not to impose."

The sentence came out more sharply than she had intended. Or perhaps exactly as sharply as some ungoverned part of her had intended and the rest of her had failed to prevent.

Mr. Darcy accepted it. He did not defend himself. He only stood with that grave attention which had, at dinner, made every silence feel inhabited.

Elizabeth ought to have stopped. She knew it. 

Instead she said, "Did you speak to him?"

Mr. Darcy did not pretend not to understand.

"Yes."

One word. The same word she had given Kitty, and just as little protection against what followed.

Elizabeth's hand tightened in the fringe of her shawl. "Before he returned to Netherfield?"

"Yes."

The lawn seemed to grow very still. From some distance came Kitty's voice — "No, but if Hertfordshire sees it too, then it is a shared star, which is very different" — and Georgiana's soft, uncertain laugh.

"You told him," she said.

"I told him what I should have told him long before."

Her breath caught, and she was angry with it for doing so.

Mr. Darcy looked toward the girls, then back to her. "But that is all I did. Bingley acted for himself."

She knew it was true by the care with which he said it, and by the very refusal of triumph in his voice. He would not take Jane's happiness into his hands. He would not make a claim upon it. He would not even let Elizabeth thank him, if gratitude had been possible.

It was noble. It was insufferable.

"How very convenient," she said.

She had already said too much. She said more. "You were willing enough to tell me what you had done when it condemned you."

The breath he let out was careful, a slow, controlled thing, as if steadying himself.

Elizabeth wished, with a violence that startled her, that he would be angry. If he were angry, she might meet him with anger and know what to do with herself. If he defended himself, she might retort. If he was proud, she might despise him.

He was none of these things.

"I did not mean to conceal it."

"No?" The laugh that escaped her was not pleasant. "Then you have achieved concealment by accident, which must be a relief to a gentleman so seldom careless."

He was quiet for a moment. When he spoke, it was not with pain, though pain was there. It was with understanding, and that was unendurable.

"I deserved that," he said.

"No," Elizabeth said at once, because he had not. Or because he had. Or because his readiness to accept it deprived her of the poor satisfaction of having been unjust. "No, you did not. That is—"

She stopped. She could not apologize without breaking; she could not continue without cruelty.

Mr. Darcy did not help her. Perhaps he could not. Perhaps he had learned, too late and too well, not to seize upon any opening she had not meant to give.

The shadow had made truth possible. It had not made truth kind.

"Lizzy?" Kitty called.

Elizabeth turned too quickly. Her shawl slipped from her shoulders. Mr. Darcy caught it before it touched the grass. He held it out without a word, not toward her shoulders, only toward her hand.

She took it. Their fingers met — bare, brief. Elizabeth pulled the shawl against herself as if she had been burned.

Kitty stood beside Georgiana, both of them looking back. The easy line between them had gone uncertain again. What had they seen?

Mrs. Gardiner came forward.

"I believe," she said, in the calm tone that could restore a room, a table, or an entire family to order, "that the stars will remain where they are until tomorrow. The air is growing cool, and Mrs. Annesley will begin to think we have gone in search of another county."

Kitty accepted the rescue first. "Only if it has better stars."

"That would be difficult to determine tonight," Mrs. Gardiner said. "Come."

They turned back toward the house.

For a few steps, no one spoke. The gravel sounded too clearly underfoot. Kitty and Georgiana walked behind them now. Mrs. Gardiner kept near Elizabeth without touching her. Mr. Darcy followed at a proper distance, and that propriety, too, accused them both.

Elizabeth could feel the heat in her face though the night had cooled. She had asked the question. She had received the answer. She had wanted him to have done something; she had wanted him not to have done it for credit; she had wanted him to tell her without making her grateful; she had wanted, impossibly, not to have to want anything from him at all.

At the terrace, Kitty and Georgiana caught up to them. "Georgiana, I did not mean Derbyshire owns the stars,” Kitty said. "Only that it manages them badly."

In the light from the windowed door, Georgiana's smile returned, small but real. "Perhaps only in the shrubbery."

Kitty laughed, relieved.

Elizabeth could not bear it.

"I am very tired," she said, and the words were addressed to no one in particular because if she looked at her aunt she would be understood, and if she looked at Mr. Darcy she would be undone. "Pray excuse me."

"Lizzy—" Kitty began.

"I shall only go in."

She did not wait for more. She went through the bright door ahead of them all, into the candles and warmth and ordinary safety of Pemberley, and carried the night in with her.
