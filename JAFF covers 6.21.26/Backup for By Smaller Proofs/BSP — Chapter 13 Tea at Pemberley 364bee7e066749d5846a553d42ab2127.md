# BSP — Chapter 13: Tea at Pemberley

---

Wednesday had the polished stillness of a day arranged for display and left too long in the sun.

The road to Pemberley had not altered. That was the trouble. Elizabeth had come to it two days ago with a tourist’s conscience and an aunt’s arm ready at her side, prepared to admire, to be instructed, and to go away. Today she was expected. 

The trees should have had the decency to look different. The stream should have lost some of its assurance. The house, when it rose grand again from its woods and breadth of lawn, should at least have offered some outward proof that Saturday and today were not the same day at all.

It did not.

Stone, water, lawn, gorgeous light: all held their places. Pemberley did not explain itself for her comfort. It left Elizabeth to feel the alteration alone.

Mr. Gardiner, who had spent half the drive maintaining that tea was an admirable institution because it permitted a man to be sociable while admitting he required cake, leaned toward the carriage window as they rounded the final turn.

“Well,” he said, with quiet satisfaction, “I find I admire a house still more when I have fished its water.”

“My dear,” said Mrs. Gardiner, “you admired it Saturday.”

“Saturday, I admired it as architecture. Today I admire it as a patron of trout.”

Elizabeth smiled because her uncle deserved it. His good humor had carried them across several awkward roads before this one. Her hands lay folded in her lap. Her gloves were clean. Her gown was exactly what it ought to be: a morning muslin of no pretension, decent for tea, suitable to a call, and chosen by Mrs. Gardiner with the silent care of a general selecting ground.

Whether it became her had not been discussed. The gown’s duty was not to make her pretty, but to make her correct.

At the door, everything proceeded before discomfort could find a place to stand. A servant appeared as the carriage stopped. Another received Mr. Gardiner’s card. A footman in livery held the grand front door open. The hall was cool, and scented with beeswax, as before, but now late roses, lavender, and clipped green sprigs adorned two narrow side tables and the round oak table in the center. Their scents .

Elizabeth had crossed that hall as a tourist. Mrs. Reynolds had arranged the past around her, and Elizabeth had been permitted to admire from afar. Now she must enter as an active guest, answer greetings, accept tea, and try to discover what sort of countenance a woman ought to wear when visiting the house of a man whose proposal she had refused.

They were shown not into the great drawing-room as she had half feared, but into a smaller drawing-room on the garden side of the house. Smaller by Pemberley measures, that is, which meant that it would have held the Bennets’ best parlor, the Lucas Lodge drawing-room, and perhaps Mr. Collins’s entire sense of consequence without inconvenience. Pale green silk covered the walls above white lower paneling. The curtains, soft cream figured with leaves, were drawn back from high windows. Sun crossed the carpet in broken pieces, gold and green, as the branches moved outside.

Carved chairs polished by generations of hands stood near the windows with a low striped sofa among them, all turned toward the tea table rather than the fire. It was not a low table for books or elbows, but a polished Pembroke table drawn into service, high enough for cups, plates, and Miss Darcy’s careful hands. Roses in a bowl gave off the rich, bruised sweetness of late summer. The tea things were already laid on it: teapot, hot-water urn, cups, sugar, cream, plates, and silver tongs, all arranged as if no hand had ever hesitated over them.

Simple tea, then.

Simple tea at Pemberley, it appeared, required old porcelain, thin bread and butter, preserved apricots in a shell-shaped dish, three kinds of cake, and plates so fine Elizabeth expected them to ring if anyone looked at them too sharply.

Miss Darcy stood beside it.

She was dressed exactly as the occasion required. Hair pinned casually up. A pale gown, beautifully made, with no more ornament than a ribbon at the waist and a narrow edging that rewarded attention without begging for it. She was at home, and therefore more exposed than she had been in Lambton. In the street she had possessed the advantage of motion. Here, in her brother’s house, before a table arranged for hospitality and a company waiting to see how she would use it, she looked very young indeed.

Mr. Darcy stood a little behind her and to one side, letting her lead but there a moment if she needed support. His dark coat, faultless linen, and grave propriety matched the room — exact, costly, restrained, and so well judged that the clothes disappeared into the man.

Miss Caroline Bingley, by contrast, had chosen to be noticed.

She rose from one of the chairs near the tea table with admirable grace, one hand resting on its arm as though the exertion had social consequence. She was pale — truly pale, not fashionably so — and there was a tightness about her mouth that no art could turn into delicacy. Yet she had dressed for contest. Her silk was too rich for the hour, her lace too elaborate for a quiet country tea, the chain at her throat too bright against a complexion not yet recovered from illness. She looked like a woman who had been unwell and had determined to make recovery as expensive as possible.

Miss Darcy came forward first. Her curtsey was exact; her voice, low. Yet when her eyes met Elizabeth’s, a brightness came into them that had not been there when the door opened.

“I am very glad you could come,” she said.

“So am I,” Elizabeth replied, curtseying, and found that it was not only politeness.

Mrs. Gardiner answered with all her easy warmth. Mr. Gardiner bowed, and Miss Darcy’s hands clasped once before her, then released, with the effort of receiving them all properly.

Only then did Miss Bingley advance.

“Miss Elizabeth Bennet,” she said, with a smile that had to travel some distance before reaching civility. “What a very unexpected pleasure.”

There it was: Meryton, Netherfield, Jane’s unanswered hopes, all folded into eight words and a bare curtsey.

Elizabeth returned the civility. “Miss Bingley. I hope your journey has not fatigued you.”

“Quite recovered, I thank you. Or nearly. A little fatigue after the road is nothing. We were all so…  concerned when we heard a Miss Bennet was in the neighborhood.”

A Miss Bennet.

In an instant Elizabeth saw the calculation Miss Bingley must have made. Jane, somehow come into Derbyshire. Jane within reach of Mr. Bingley. Jane’s quiet beauty appearing at Pemberley like a ghost invited to tea. Then the calculation corrected itself. Merely Elizabeth: less alarming, perhaps, but not more welcome. 

There are disappointments, Elizabeth supposed, which even a sickbed cannot make restful.

“Your concern does you credit,” said Elizabeth.

Miss Bingley’s smile thinned.

“Miss Elizabeth,” cried Mr. Bingley, coming forward from somewhere near the windows with all the warmth his sister lacked. His coat was as well cut as Mr. Darcy’s, but worn with none of Mr. Darcy’s gravity; even his cravat seemed to have been tied in better spirits if not perfect style. “This is indeed a pleasure. I had no notion — Darcy told us only yesterday — that you were in Derbyshire. Mrs. Gardiner, Mr. Gardiner, yes? How do you do? I hope you are all well. Your family is well? Miss Bennet is — that is, your sisters are all well?”

He had colored before he finished.

Elizabeth saw it. Miss Bingley saw it. Mr. Darcy, who was bowing to Mrs. Gardiner, paused no longer than a man might pause because another person had spoken too quickly; but Elizabeth saw that too.

Jane had not been invisible. Not to him.

“Very well, thank you,” Elizabeth said, and because she was not cruel, or not cruel in that way, she added, “My eldest sister was quite well when I last heard from Longbourn.”

Mr. Bingley’s color rose higher; his smile came and was checked almost in the same breath. “I am very glad. Very glad indeed.”

“Charles,” said Miss Bingley, “you are keeping Miss Darcy from her table.”

He moved at once, with an apology so ready and good-humored that it might have disarmed anyone whose heart had not been sitting for months beside Jane’s sorrow. Elizabeth looked down at the carpet. Sunlight caught on her shoes.

With only a little shifting of chairs, and only one pause in which no one seemed entirely certain who ought to sit first, they were arranged at last. Mr. Gardiner was given a chair near Mr. Darcy. Mrs. Gardiner placed herself where she could speak to Miss Darcy without seeming to examine her. Elizabeth found herself on the striped sofa nearest the windows, the damask cool through the thin fabric of her gown and ridged beneath her gloved fingertips. Miss Bingley settled nearest the tea table before anyone had asked her to do so.

Miss Darcy looked at the cups. Then at the urn. Then, once, at her brother.

Mr. Darcy did not move. He said, in a tone belonging to the room at large, “Mrs. Reynolds tells me the apricot cakes are excellent today.”

Miss Darcy’s eyes went at once to the dish, with the look of a girl who had been offered the very subject she required. “Yes. Mrs. Annesley thought — I mean, I hope you will try them. They are made with fruit from the south wall.”

“Then we are obliged to Pemberley’s gardens for a second time,” said Mrs. Gardiner. “The basket you sent us yesterday made a luncheon that Mr. Gardiner has spoken of ever since.”

“I spoke of the trout also,” said Mr. Gardiner. “I am a man of balanced gratitude.”

Miss Darcy smiled and reached for the teapot.

“Allow me, dear Georgiana,” said Miss Bingley, leaning forward with languid kindness. “The larger cups are for stronger tea; Mrs. Reynolds always prefers it so when there is company. You are too careful of everyone’s comfort to trouble yourself with every little household habit at once.”

Miss Darcy’s hand paused, two fingers still on the handle of the teapot.

The sentence sounded like assistance and acted like command. It praised Georgiana’s sweetness, corrected her hand, claimed knowledge of Pemberley, and placed Miss Bingley, with one movement of the wrist, between the mistress of the house and her own table.

Mrs. Gardiner did not move beside Elizabeth; she merely became, in some manner impossible to accuse, more attentive.

Mr. Darcy crossed to the table. Not quickly. Not as if anything had happened. He took up the plate of apricot cakes and offered it first to Mrs. Gardiner.

“Mrs. Gardiner, you must tell my sister whether the south wall has done credit to Lambton.”

The plate passed from him to Mrs. Gardiner, and the room altered by an inch. Miss Bingley was no longer advising the hostess. Miss Darcy had her gardens returned to her. Mrs. Gardiner, with an apricot cake in her hand, had been made as necessary to Pemberley’s judgment as any lady in the room.

Elizabeth had never known a plate of cakes could be so well employed.

Mrs. Gardiner accepted one. “I shall consider myself appointed judge.”

Miss Darcy poured. Her hand was steady. The tea fell amber-dark into the cup. Steam rose with the scent of China tea, hot porcelain, sugar, and butter from the cakes. When Elizabeth received her cup, the porcelain was thin through her glove and warm enough to make her adjust her hold at once. Tiny blue flowers circled the saucer, each petal painted with such patience that it seemed unreasonable for any household to possess them in dozens.

Mr. Darcy came to her next with the plate.

Elizabeth chose one of the smallest cakes. Mr. Darcy held the plate perfectly still; she was the one who took too long over the choice. It was crisp at the edge, soft within, and tasted of almond, butter, and the tart sweetness of apricot. Longbourn had cake. Gracechurch Street had excellent cake. Pemberley had cake that appeared to have been born knowing the difference between perfect plenty and excess.

Miss Bingley observed the plate as if it had betrayed her.

“Miss Darcy’s taste is always elegant,” she said. “Even in these smaller matters. I often tell her that when she is mistress here in earnest, she will make Pemberley all that it ought to be.”

Miss Darcy blushed. Not with pleasure. Elizabeth could not like the phrase *in earnest*. A girl might be young, shy, and unsure of her teapot, and still be mistress of her brother’s house already.

“Surely,” said Elizabeth, before she had quite decided to speak, “Pemberley seems already to know what it ought to be.”

Miss Bingley’s gaze flicked to her.

Mr. Darcy’s did as well.

Elizabeth took a sip of tea. It was too hot and very good.

“I mean,” she added, with all possible mildness, “that a house with so much history must guide its inhabitants as much as be guided by them.”

“How prettily said. One might almost think you had been studying great houses, Miss Eliza.”

The old name. The old tone. Netherfield’s drawing-room opened in Elizabeth’s mind. Miss Bingley with her book unread, Miss Bingley walking around the room to secure attention, Miss Bingley asking Mr. Darcy to admire her figure and receiving less than she had arranged for. Elizabeth had once found it absurd. In Pemberley, it was sharper. 

Miss Bingley was measuring territory; not because Elizabeth had conquered any of it, but because Miss Bingley did not know how to enter a room without drawing boundaries.

“I have had excellent instruction,” Elizabeth said.

“Mrs. Reynolds?” said Mr. Bingley, brightly; then, perhaps realizing that he had entered a conversation whose floor was less secure than it looked, he added, “A capital woman, Mrs. Reynolds.”

“She is,” said Mr. Darcy.

Two words. Nothing more. Miss Bingley’s fan shifted once against her wrist; Miss Darcy’s color eased. Elizabeth had not expected to be pleased by Mr. Darcy’s loyalty to his housekeeper. Worse, she could not help being pleased.

Mr. Gardiner, with that practical kindness which made him hard to defeat in company, asked Mr. Bingley whether he had ever fished the Pemberley water. Mr. Bingley turned at once, grateful for any subject with fresh air in it.

“No, never. Darcy has offered it often enough, but I am a disgrace to every sport I attempt. I am too tender-hearted with fish; they perceive my weakness and take advantage of it.”

“Then they cannot complain of your intentions,” said Mr. Gardiner.

“My intentions are always excellent. My execution is generally in need of supervision.”

Mr. Darcy’s mouth almost moved. “So I have observed.”

Mr. Bingley laughed, and the room followed. He had that gift of making agreement easy. Elizabeth remembered Jane’s face when he first asked her to dance. Jane’s unguarded happiness in the carriage after Netherfield. Jane’s brave composure when the letters stopped. The last bit of her cake lost its sweetness.

Mr. Bingley turned to her. “And your family truly remains well? Miss Bennet has not — that is, she did not suffer from the heat this summer? I remember she was once unwell after a ride in bad weather.”

Miss Bingley set down her cup with a sound too soft to be called a correction and too pointed to be an accident.

Elizabeth found herself studying Mr. Bingley more closely than civility required. He was thinner. Not dramatically; no romance-novel wasting, no visible ruin. Only a leanness at the cheek, a little less boyish fullness about the face, as if the months since Netherfield had taken an unexpected toll. His eyes were still kind, and fixed on her with an eagerness too open for perfect civility.

“Jane is very well,” Elizabeth said. “She is not often ill, though she is very often patient.”

The sentence had more edge than she intended. Mr. Bingley did not seem to feel it. Mr. Darcy’s fingers tightened once about his cup; he looked down and did not drink.

Miss Bingley did too. “Miss Bennet was always sweetness itself. So placid. One could never know what she felt, which is a great advantage in society.”

Elizabeth’s last crumb of cake grew dry upon her tongue.

A great advantage. Jane’s reserve, made ornament. Jane’s pain, made proof that none existed.

Before Elizabeth could trust herself to reply, Miss Darcy spoke.

“I think,” she said, her voice small but clear, “that quiet manners do not always mean a quiet heart.”

The room changed.

Not by any movement large enough to be named. Miss Darcy’s eyes dropped to the cups; Miss Bingley stared at her with the astonishment of a person struck by a cushion and uncertain whether to complain.

Mr. Darcy did not smile. He did not praise her. He merely inclined his head towards his sister, once, as if she had said no more and no less than the truth. Miss Darcy’s fingers tightened around the handle of the milk jug; then she set it down without spilling a drop.

“No,” said Mrs. Gardiner, gently. “It is not.”

Elizabeth looked at Miss Darcy, and a warmth rose so sharply beneath her ribs that she had to look away.

“Miss Bennet is very kind,” Mr. Bingley said.

The words were simple enough to be safe, except that they were not safe at all.

“Indeed,” said Miss Bingley, rallying. “All the Bennet ladies are known for lively qualities of one kind or another.”

Not quite an insult. A little veil dropped over the word *lively*, a little pressure placed where Lydia, Kitty, Meryton, and every remembered mortification might be expected to stir.

Mr. Darcy turned to Mr. Gardiner.

“Did you find the lower bend difficult yesterday? Greaves tells me the bank has altered since the spring rain.”

Mr. Gardiner accepted the change of subject with the mildness of a man who had never in his life objected to being useful. “I did, yes. The current turns more sharply than it appears from above. I lost a fly to it, which I mention only because I had been fond of the fly.”

“Tragic,” said Mr. Bingley. “One grows attached to a good fly.”

“You would not,” said Mr. Darcy. “You would attach it to your sleeve and apologize to it.”

Miss Darcy laughed.

Not the polite small laugh from earlier. A real one, startled out of her by the picture. Mrs. Gardiner smiled into her tea. Even Miss Bingley permitted herself a thin laugh, perhaps because refusal would have been the greater discourtesy.

Elizabeth watched as Mr. Darcy make room for everyone.

He did not command the conversation. He did not perform in it. He gave each person, by turns, something safe to hold. He knew when to turn a sharp room toward fishing, when to let a shy girl’s courage pass without display, when to make hospitality look like accident, and when to say almost nothing at all.

Nothing to Elizabeth that anyone could name.

Enough to Elizabeth that she could name nothing else.

At Netherfield, she had thought his attention proof of disdain.

Here, she began to understand that attention might be service as well as judgment.

As the tea went on, everyone except Miss Bingley took another cake. Miss Darcy asked Mrs. Gardiner whether she had walked often by the church when she was young. Mrs. Gardiner answered with a story of a winter so cold the pump in the yard froze and the boys of the town slid down the lane until the curate complained. Mr. Bingley asked whether Hertfordshire lanes were as good for sliding, and Elizabeth said she could not recommend them for dignity. Miss Bingley remarked that country amusements were wholesome when kept in their proper season. Miss Darcy’s mouth curved over her cup.

By degrees, the room lost its first stiffness.

Or rather, Elizabeth lost her fear that stiffness was all there was.

The chair beneath Miss Bingley’s hand creaked whenever she shifted. Silver tongs clicked against sugar. A bee worried at the window before finding its way out through a raised sash. Somewhere beyond the open windows, a gardener’s shears made a clean little rhythm: snip, pause, snip. 

Pemberley, which had seemed in the hall to wait in judgment, began to sound like a house where ordinary afternoons could happen: cups set down, laughter not quite suppressed, a young lady growing braver by inches.

Miss Darcy had poured the cups without mishap. She had offered the second round of cakes. She had answered Mr. Bingley’s cheerful sallies, Mrs. Gardiner’s kindness, and one of Elizabeth’s quieter questions about music. She was still constrained, still watchful; but she had ceased to look as if the table might accuse her.

Miss Bingley saw it too.

“You must play for us next time, Miss Darcy,” she said. “Your visitors should know how accomplished you are. I always say your performance improves whenever you can be persuaded to overcome your modesty.”

Miss Darcy’s back straightened by a quarter-inch, and the pleasant curve of her mouth vanished.

Elizabeth felt Mr. Darcy’s attention move.

“Miss Darcy spoke of music so prettily at Lambton,” said Mrs. Gardiner, “that I was quite persuaded she must play well.”

Miss Darcy looked at her, startled and grateful.

“I do play,” she said. “But not always well before company.”

“Then you are like every musician of sense,” said Elizabeth. “The instrument behaves worse when watched.”

“Does it?” Miss Darcy asked, leaning a little forward despite herself.

“Certainly. At Longbourn our pianoforte has been known to develop opinions merely because a guest entered the room.”

Mr. Bingley laughed. “I should like to hear such an instrument defend itself.”

He had said it with open pleasure. Only after saying it did it seem he remembered what else Longbourn contained. His smile remained, but his eyes had gone searching again, as if one cheerful sentence might yet bring Jane into the room.

Miss Bingley’s fan flicked open.

It was not hot enough for a fan. It was, however, an excellent object with which to be composed.

Mr. Darcy crossed to the window and lowered the sash by an inch. “The sun is coming round.”

It was addressed to no one. It served everyone. The beam that had been striking Miss Darcy’s hands moved off to the floor.

“Thank you,” said Miss Darcy.

He inclined his head and remained by the window.

Seeing him there, Elizabeth understood with uncomfortable clarity why he had seemed so wrong at Meryton. He had been a great house’s master in rooms that made no place for him. A man trained to breadth, duty, privacy, and consequence, compressed into assemblies and card tables and the easy judgments of a neighborhood that wanted him agreeable on command. 

None of that excused him. It did not excuse insult. It did not excuse Jane. But here, with light behind him and the room arranged around obligations he knew how to meet, he fit.

The knowledge would not sit comfortably.

It was also impossible to unknow.

When the visit had stretched to something near three quarters of an hour, Mrs. Gardiner rose. Miss Darcy rose too quickly, checked herself, and smiled at her own haste to do the proper thing. Elizabeth caught the smile.

“We have trespassed on you long enough,” said Mrs. Gardiner.

“No,” said Miss Darcy, and colored. “I mean — not at all. I am very glad you came.”

Miss Bingley’s smile returned at once. “Miss Darcy is always so affectionate in her attachments. It is one of her sweetest qualities.”

Her fan tapped once against her palm: a small sound, neat as a pin set into silk.

Miss Darcy’s shoulders drew in by the smallest degree.

Elizabeth set down her saucer. The porcelain made no sound.

“We are very glad we came too,” she said. “I had not known Pemberley could be so pleasant indoors.”

Mr. Darcy’s eyes came to hers; for a moment he seemed uncertain whether she had offered praise, reproof, or some uneasy mixture of both. Miss Darcy looked delighted. Mrs. Gardiner’s eyes flickered with private amusement.

Miss Bingley blinked. “You surprise me, Miss Eliza. I should have thought you found the house very pleasant on your first visit.”

“I admired it then,” said Elizabeth. “That is not always the same thing.”

No one spoke for half a second.

Then Mr. Bingley, blessed Mr. Bingley, said with feeling, “Very true. I have admired many things I did not particularly wish to sit on.”

Mr. Gardiner laughed. Mrs. Gardiner smiled. Miss Darcy’s laugh escaped before she could consult anyone about it. Even Mr. Darcy’s lips tilted up.

Miss Bingley shut her fan.

At the door, Miss Darcy came forward before anyone could prompt her.

“If you are not engaged tomorrow,” she said, looking first to Mrs. Gardiner, then to Elizabeth, “perhaps you might come again. Not for tea, if that is too much trouble. Only — there is a walk I think Mrs. Gardiner might enjoy, and Miss Bennet said yesterday that Hertfordshire trees have less moral instruction than ours.”

Mr. Darcy waited until Georgiana had finished, then made the invitation proper.

“If it would be agreeable,” he said, “and if the heat is not too great, we would be honored to show you the lower walk tomorrow afternoon. It is shaded nearly all the way.”

The lower walk. The invitation collected meanings as it stood there: shade, memory, Georgiana’s courage, Mr. Darcy’s voice making room for all of them.

Mrs. Gardiner accepted with the air of a woman who had seen a young girl’s courage hold for nearly an hour and would sooner have disappointed a bishop than refuse it. And, Elizabeth knew from the past week, her aunt was not easily daunted by a walk.

Miss Bingley, still seated, looked down at her closed fan, opened it halfway, and shut it again.

Elizabeth curtsied. “I should like that very much.”

It was true.

That was the trouble.

As they descended the steps and entered the carriage that waited in the bright afternoon, Elizabeth looked back once. Miss Darcy stood at the door, pale gown in the shadow, smiling as if the afternoon had given her more than she had dared ask. Mr. Darcy stood a little behind her, exactly where he had stood all afternoon: near enough to steady, far enough to let her be seen.

Miss Bingley was not visible from the door.

Mr. Bingley was. He stood beside Mr. Darcy now, and when Elizabeth turned, he bowed with all his old warmth and a little less of his old ease.

“Pray remember me to your family,” he said.

Then, after one small pause: “To Miss Bennet especially, if you please.”

Jane, then, had not been left wholly behind in Hertfordshire.

Elizabeth inclined her head. “I shall.”

Mr. Darcy’s hand shifted on the doorframe, then stilled.

She entered the carriage with tea and roses still clinging to her gloves, the taste of apricot lingering where sweetness had gone sharp on her tongue. Pemberley was behind her, but it would not return to being only stone and water and excellent prospects.

Tomorrow, she would come back.
