# BSP — Chapter 6: The Park Pale

✅ Chapter complete

The road into Derbyshire took Elizabeth and the Gardiners under a sky that had been making up its mind since morning and had at last settled on something low and soft and grey, without temper in it, without any particular intention of rain. By early afternoon the light through the chaise windows was that pale, even spread which makes all greens the same green and all distances the same distance, and which, Elizabeth thought, was perhaps the kindest weather for traveling — the horses neither hot nor chilled, the postboys neither hurried nor sullen, the countryside laid out as if for inspection without vanity. It was the last week of July, though nothing in this afternoon proposed to say so.

They had been five days on the road — an idle afternoon at Kenilworth among its ruins, a morning at Matlock Bath among its waters, on the principle that one might as well see England as cross it. The post-chaise was the fourth they had been in since leaving Meryton, or the fifth, depending on whether one counted the small exchange at the last stage where one of the horses had gone lame within a half-mile of the post-house and they had been obliged to wait while another was brought. Mr. Gardiner had taken this delay with the philosophic calm of a man who had hired a great many horses in his life and knew that a certain proportion of them were bound to disappoint. He had used the hour to stretch his legs, to speak to the ostler about the road beyond Leek, and to come back with the intelligence that the surface was very tolerable now, since the rain of the week before had laid the dust without softening the gravel, which was the most one could reasonably ask of an English highway.

Mrs. Gardiner had listened to all of this with the patient affection of a wife who had heard her husband discuss road conditions for fifteen years of marriage and still enjoyed the sound of him saying *tolerable.* When they were underway again she had pointed out, from her window, the first true hill of the stage — not a Derbyshire hill yet, she said, but one of its cousins — and had begun to speak of the country of her girlhood.

Elizabeth listened, and watched, and did not think of him.

There was the rhythm of the springs, and the way the body learned by the third day to take the jolt of a poor rut in the knees and the shoulders and not at all in the head. There were the hills ahead, taller by the mile, shouldering against one another in a way she had not seen in Hertfordshire, nor indeed anywhere south. There was the ham and the very indifferent cheese at yet another White Lion, and the change of horses at the next stage, and whether the inn at Lambton would have a fire lit in their parlor.

There had been the lame horse at the last stage — its dropped head, the ostler's hand on its neck, a picture that had held her the better part of an hour. That had been real. That had been the proof.

*Proof,* she told herself, *that I am not thinking of him.*

"That stone in the hills, my dear," said Mr. Gardiner. "That's limestone, or has the look of it. I knew a man in Cheapside who bought Derbyshire stone by the boatload — he would have it as good as Portland for a wall that meant to stand fifty years without staining."

"My father would have gone further," said Mrs. Gardiner. "He held it the handsomest stone in England. But he was a Lambton man, and a Lambton man will not admit there is a superior stone anywhere on principle."

Mr. Gardiner laughed. Elizabeth smiled at the window.

They climbed a long, slow rise, and the hills were closer now, and the light on them was still that flat pale afternoon light that made every sheep on every hillside look as if it had been painted and unreal. A stone wall began, some way before the crest, on the right-hand side of the road. It was a wall of the ordinary sort, squared blocks laid without mortar, nothing handsome, nothing striking, breast-high or a little above — the kind of wall one passed a hundred times in a day on a country road and never looked at twice.

"Now here," said Mrs. Gardiner pleasantly, leaning a little toward the window, "is where the Darcys' pale begins. We are on Pemberley ground for the next — oh, I should think four miles, or the nearest thing to it."

Mr. Gardiner, who had been halfway through a remark about the ostler at Leek, stopped and looked. "Four miles? Along the road?"

"Along the road. And a great deal more besides, inland. It is a very considerable park."

"A pale?" Elizabeth said.

"Only the wall that bounds the park. One calls it a pale — I cannot tell you why, except that my father did, and his father before him. I believe in very old times they were made of oak stakes rather than stone, and the word came from that. A park pale is what one calls the enclosure around the whole of a gentleman's ground, where he keeps his deer and his coverts and what you would call his *property,* though I never heard a Derbyshire man use so blunt a word for it."

"A wall round it all,” Mr. Gardiner said.

His wife nodded. "A wall round it all."

Mr. Gardiner made a small impressed noise, being a man of London who measured his own premises in feet and was prepared to be generous about country estates measured in miles. He leaned slightly to see past his wife to the window, and the wall flowed past, unbroken, squared block upon squared block, no gate, no lodge, no indication that anything lay beyond it but the wood and the same hillside it had been cut from.

Elizabeth did not lean. She did not need to. The window on her own side showed her nothing but the long pale rolling by, an arm's length from the wheels, at the steady easy pace of the post-horses — and so the pale rolled and rolled, and did not end.

The name had passed her without catching — Mrs. Gardiner had said it like *the Vicar's garden* or *the river* — and only now, a full minute late, did it come back on her. Then she saw the wall.

It was, as her aunt had said, only a wall. It had none of the grandeur that the imagination would have supplied. There were no obelisks, no iron crests, no piers of dressed stone with carved escutcheons. It was a Derbyshire field wall, a little higher and a little straighter than a field wall was obliged to be, laid in the patient competent hand of men who had been laying walls in these hills since there had been hills to lay them in. It did not announce itself. It did not need to. What announced it was that it did not stop.

A mile, she supposed, was a great many strides of a horse. A mile was the length of the Longbourn lane taken twice, and the lane had always seemed to her a respectable distance, such that one began a letter at one end of it and had finished the first page before one reached the other. They would pass this wall for four miles. Four such lanes, laid end to end, and all of them only the edge of a thing. Beyond the wall she could see, through the gaps between the trees, what must be the park — open grass, and the shoulder of a further rise — but no roof, no chimney, no glint of water from any lake. The house, wherever it sat, sat too deep in its own ground to be glimpsed from the road. Only the pale told her where she was.

She had not, in all the weeks since Hunsford, actually pictured it. She had known, as one knows a fact without looking at it, that the estate existed, and that it was somewhere, and that it was large. The largeness had been a word. Now the largeness was a wall her knee was very nearly brushing against through the glass.

She turned her face to the window and kept it there, and put her gloved hand up under her chin as though she were considering the hills, which she was not. The wall went on. A little ash sapling had taken root between two of its stones, high up, and someone had not yet got round to pulling it out; she watched the sapling approach and pass. A pheasant scuttered along the wall's foot on the outside and was gone. The wall went on.

She found that she had been, all afternoon, and perhaps all morning, and perhaps all the week, thinking of him. The horses and the ham and the shape of the hills had been a screen. The wall had not the courtesy to be a screen. The wall was very plain, and it was very long, and it belonged to him.

"You are very quiet, Lizzy," said her aunt, after a time, not looking round, still cheerful at her own window. 

"I am admiring the country, Aunt," said Elizabeth — which came out even, which surprised her.

"It is worth the admiring. We shall have better of it tomorrow, if the sky clears."

The pale ended. There was no gate where it ended, and no lodge; it simply turned away from the road and went off up the hillside to the northeast, a pale seam in the green, and then dipped out of sight into a fold of the land. The chaise rolled on between ordinary hedges, the hills resuming their ordinary business of being hills, and the country looked, very suddenly, like any other country one had passed through in an afternoon.

Elizabeth did not know whether she was relieved or bereaved. She had never before felt a loss at the ending of a simple wall.

---

Lambton, when they came into it, was a small handsome gray town of the sort that had been gray and handsome since before any of them were born. Sheltered in its valley, at ease with itself, unpersuaded by the century. Her aunt grew visibly younger by the yard. She pointed out the church, and the lane to the rectory, and the corner where Mr. Someone had kept a shop when she was a girl and was perhaps keeping it still if he had not had the discourtesy to die. When the chaise turned at last into the yard of the inn — the Rose, it was called, a broad low building of the same gray stone as the walls, with stables down one side and an ostler already coming to the horses' heads — she named the landlord — Staveley, as she recalled him — and was proved right within a minute by Mr. Staveley himself, who appeared at the door wiping his hands on a cloth and professed himself honored to welcome Miss Shelby home, though she had not been Miss Shelby these twenty years.

This produced, in the yard, among the trunks being handed down and the postboys waiting for their tipping, a small warmth that did the work of several speeches. The landlord's wife came out and curtseyed to Mrs. Gardiner as to a neighbor rather than a stranger, mentioned a cousin in Liverpool whose health had lately improved, and said that the parlor abovestairs had a fire lit since noon against the damp, and that a letter had come for Miss Bennet from Hertfordshire and was laid out upon the mantel.

Elizabeth sat in the well-warmed parlor on a bench beside  the window below which lay the main street of Lambton,  orderly and quiet and wearing its overcast like an old shawl. Elizabeth skimmed Jane’s lines — Longbourn, the twenty-third — while her aunt and uncle consulted with the landlady about supper. The letter contained nothing remarkable — the weather at Longbourn had been as indifferent as the weather here; their mother was well and had turned sharp against the curate; Mary had undertaken a new course of improving passages. *Kitty* *had the Gardiner children in fits of laughter yesterday by a very creditable impression of Lady Lucas.”* Elizabeth read this last sentence twice, and smiled at it, and folded the letter and put it in her pocket, and was grateful beyond any proportion the letter itself could possibly account for. 

Supper came up at seven. Good plain food: a fowl, a pudding, a little dish of something her aunt identified as a Derbyshire cheese and ate with the attention of a connoisseur. They took the meal in the parlor between the fire and the window, with the light gone soft outside into the particular blue-grey of a northern evening under cloud. Mr. Gardiner was mellow. Mrs. Gardiner was happy. Elizabeth was — something. She did not know what she was. 

"Now, Edward," said her aunt, at the point in the meal where she had finished her fowl and begun to think about tomorrow, "what do you say to our taking Lizzy to see Pemberley in the morning?"

Mr. Gardiner set down his glass. "Can we?"

"They show it very handsomely, I am told. I have not seen it within since I was a girl, and then only the hall and the great staircase, because my father knew the then housekeeper and she was good enough to indulge a curious child. They let visitors in, you understand, when the family is not in residence — which, Mr. Staveley tells me, is the case at present. The family is in town."

"In town." Mr. Gardiner was pleased. "Then we may satisfy our curiosity without disturbing anybody."

"Exactly." Mrs. Gardiner turned her bright sensible face to Elizabeth across the cloth. "Lizzy? It is a beautiful house, and a beautiful park, and you have come all this way. Shall we?"

Elizabeth took a breath, steadier than she had any right to expect, and reached for the line she had been keeping in reserve without knowing she had been keeping it.

"Are there no other great houses in the neighborhood?"

It came out, and it sounded, to her own ear, very much as she had meant it to sound. Careless. Faintly droll. The sort of question one might put to one's aunt when one's aunt had become a little too enthusiastic about country seats.

Her aunt only laughed at her, the frank affectionate laugh of a woman who had not heard any of the things that would have made the question mean anything, and who had, instead, heard a tired niece at the end of five days' traveling trying to be witty.

"Oh, my dear, we shall not concern ourselves with any others. There is none within twenty miles half so worth a morning as Pemberley, and I mean to have my morning."

She said it in the tone of a woman claiming a piece of her childhood back. There was no challenge in it. There was scarcely any persuasion in it. Mrs. Gardiner had not seen the question for what it was — and so the question, having nothing to resist, lost its shape.

Elizabeth had not prepared a second line. She had been too confident of the first.

*The family is not at home.*

"Then I suppose," she said, after a pause just long enough to be worth the noticing and not long enough to be remarked upon, "we had better go."

"That's my Lizzy." Her aunt reached across and pressed her hand, warm and open, and then turned to her husband to talk of hours, and of whether they should take the chaise or walk from the lodge, and Mr. Gardiner was all for walking if the sky would only consent, and the conversation went on above Elizabeth's head as comfortably as any conversation she had sat through in her life.

She sipped her Madeira. She looked, by accident, out at the window, where the street was nearly dark now and the overcast, holding the last of the light, lay low and even over the roofs of Lambton. Over her aunt's childhood church, and the landlord's cousin's shop, and the road that ran, she now knew, past four unbroken miles of quiet, competent wall.

*He will not be there.*

The fire made its small sound. Her aunt laughed at something her uncle had said. The letter from Jane was still in her pocket, and her hand had gone, without her permission, to rest upon it. She must recount Jane’s story of Kitty and the children.

*He will not be there.*

Half a promise. Half a prayer.
