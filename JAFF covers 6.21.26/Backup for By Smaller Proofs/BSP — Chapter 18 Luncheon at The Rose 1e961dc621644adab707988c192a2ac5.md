# BSP — Chapter 18: Luncheon at The Rose

✅ Complete

---

By the time Elizabeth had done with the broth and bread at luncheon, the letters from London had done what no road, prospect, or Derbyshire summer had yet managed: they had turned the Gardiners' concerns toward home.

On the table in the private second dining room at the Rose. Three letters from London lay open on the table, or had done; Mr. Gardiner had folded them back and pushed them aside, but they persisted.

The first was from Mr. Granger's own hand, written in a cramped uneven script that made his fever more convincing than any complaint could have done. The second, from the warehouse clerk, was clearer and therefore worse: a customer impatient, goods waiting, and no one in Gracechurch Street with authority enough to make either behave. A third note added little but urgency.

Mr. Gardiner had read all three that morning, written two answers, abandoned one, and come down with the look of a man who has discovered that business allows a holiday only until it chooses to recall him.

Mr. Gardiner must go to London. The only question was whether Mrs. Gardiner and Elizabeth must go with him.

The landlord had served cold fowl and a dish of trout with anxious ceremony, as if good attendance might soften disappointing news. Jane's letter lay apart from the London ones, marked already by Mrs. Gardiner's careful thumb, as if domestic comfort might be kept separate from commercial alarm by the breadth of a table.

"The children are well," her aunt said, for the third time; reassurance had become a household duty. "Jane says Edward has a new tooth, Harriet has made no fresh attempt upon the poultry-yard, and Marianne has attached herself to your mother with such devotion that Mrs. Bennet considers herself both adored and persecuted."

"That is a tolerable account of Longbourn," said Mr. Gardiner.

"It is a very good one," Elizabeth replied. "If Mama has only one child adoring her and no daughters contradicting her at breakfast, she must be in a state of comparative felicity."

Mrs. Gardiner smiled, but it was the smile of one accepting comfort because comfort was required.

Mr. Gardiner turned one of the London letters over, though he had read it twice. "Granger is not a man to exaggerate illness."

"No," said his wife.

"Nor to write three pages when one would serve."

"Which, as you know," said Mrs. Gardiner, "makes the three pages alarming."

Elizabeth looked at the trout and could not have said whether it was good or ill-dressed. She met Mr. Granger twice before, in Gracechurch Street. A dry gentleman with a pen behind his ear, a cough that signified calculation rather than weakness, and a talent for making any commercial matter sound both urgent and dull. If Mr. Granger had taken to his bed, or near enough to it to write for help, it must be dire.

"I had hoped," Mrs. Gardiner said, "that the matter might be one Mr. Fisher could manage. He has been so many years in your service."

"Fisher manages the ledgers brilliantly. But he cannot manage Baxter. It’s taken us years to learn how to do it, and Baxter doesn’t have the advantage of being one of the owners of the business."

"Who is this Baxter?" Elizabeth asked.

"A man who owns cloth, carts, opinions, and an unfortunate belief that all three should arrive precisely when he wishes them to," said Mr. Gardiner. "He has been patient for six years. It seems he has chosen this week to reform his character."

"And Mr. Granger ill." Mrs. Gardiner shook her head. She folded Jane's letter once, then unfolded it again. "We have not been long from Longbourn."

"No," said Mr. Gardiner. "Three weeks, when we set out, if roads, weather, and children permitted. We have had roads and weather. The children, according to Jane, have behaved better than any reasonable traveler has a right to expect."

Elizabeth set her fork down; it struck the plate with a small, neat sound that felt louder than it was.

Another fortnight.

The words ought to have called to mind roads still to be traveled, villages yet unseen, prospects her aunt had named with a girl's bright memory and a woman's moderation. They should not have made her think first of Pemberley.

She was ashamed of the swiftness.

Pemberley was not a destination. It was not even properly an event. It was a house at which she had been received with more kindness than she had deserved, a family into whose concerns she had been accidentally drawn, and a gentleman whose civility had become, in a very inconvenient manner, less easy to dismiss each time she saw it.

Two weeks meant nothing.

It meant everything.

"We shall return with you, of course," Elizabeth said.

She had spoken briskly; that was something. Briskness had always been a useful servant to her, ready to dress reluctance as good sense and feeling as decision. It did not, however, alter the feeling.

Her aunt looked at her, surprise in the tilt of her brows. “Of course? We might well carry on as well on our own, don’t you think?”

“Oh, I don’t know,” said Mr. Gardiner. “Two ladies, alone—”

"My dear," Mrs. Gardiner replied, with the patience of a woman well acquainted with her husband's notions of danger, "we should be in Lambton, not the Sahara. I have three old friends within a quarter-mile and a landlord who has been apologizing since the post-chaise scratched the gatehouse."

Elizabeth cut a piece of fowl with unnecessary precision. "Uncle. You cannot go to London and leave Aunt and me to prosecute a tour by ourselves."

Mrs. Gardiner's mouth opened, shocked. Elizabeth looked down before she could be betrayed into smiling. Smiling would have admitted relief, or pain, or both. She was not sure which had come first.

"It is not that we should be by ourselves in any alarming sense," Mrs. Gardiner said. "We are at Lambton, where I have old acquaintance enough to make solitude difficult. The inn is respectable. The roads we meant to take are no wilderness."

"Still," said Mr. Gardiner, "I do not like leaving you. But I do not like dragging you back to an overheated city and the danger of Granger's fever."

"And I do not like being dragged back," Mrs. Gardiner said, with the candor of a wife too sensible to pretend for the sake of neatness. "But neither do I like going on without you."

Elizabeth placed her fork beside her plate. "Then there is no difficulty. We return."

There. It was said. A proper sentence, composed of duty and good sense. If something in her rebelled at it, that something was not to be consulted. 

Aunt Gardiner refolded Jane's letter, this time exactly along its crease. "Miss Darcy will be disappointed."

The name touched Elizabeth before she could prepare for it; her hand tightened on the stem of her glass, and for a moment all the noise of the room seemed to draw back.

"Oh," she said.

"She wrote so prettily this morning." Mrs. Gardiner set down her glass. "It was a shame to have to tell her no."

Elizabeth turned her own glass once on the tablecloth, and did not look up. Last night. Miss Darcy at the instrument: pale hands, careful courage, the small smile that had escaped after the quick passage and then been caught back like contraband. Miss Darcy laughing at Mr. Gardiner's ignorance of German music. Miss Darcy looking brighter when Elizabeth promised to expect very little.

"She was very kind," she said at last.

"More than kind," said Mrs. Gardiner.

Mr. Gardiner looked from one to the other. "Only Miss Darcy? I seem to recall Mr. Bingley was quite solicitious. And, of course, our esteemed host.” 

Elizabeth did not dare speak of that gentleman. She reached instead for Jane's letter, though she did not need to read it. She needed only the ordinary feel of paper in her hands, Jane's neat familiar script. Jane's hand, neat and even, lay open where Mrs. Gardiner had left the page. *The children continue in excellent health. Mama is in very good spirits, chiefly owing to a new conjecture concerning Mr. Bingley, founded upon nothing whatever. Kitty sends her duty to our aunt and uncle and asks whether the Derbyshire sheep are truly as steep as mountains.*

Kitty. Sheep as steep as mountains. Elizabeth had to smile. It was a small, sad smile.

"I shall write to Miss Darcy," she said. "If Aunt wrote this morning, perhaps the next should come from me. I can thank her for last night and explain that our plans have been altered."

"Kindly," said Mrs. Gardiner.

Elizabeth looked up, into her aunt’s warm, too-observant gaze.

"Yes," Elizabeth said.

It would be easier by letter. A letter permitted gratitude to be measured, regret to be folded into proper phrases, and everything dangerous to be left unsaid. She could be all that was civil at a distance. Distance had become, in a surprisingly short time, one of the most respectable things in the world.

Mr. Gardiner pushed his plate away by an inch. "If I leave tomorrow, you might remain here one more day and follow at a more tolerable pace. Or we may all begin southward together in the morning. There is no reason for my business to make three people uncomfortable at the same rate."

"There is every reason," said Mrs. Gardiner. "One’s family should be uncomfortable together."

"My dear, that is a principle upon which many families have conducted themselves, but I am not certain it has improved society."

Elizabeth rose before the conversation could become too tender to endure. "I will fetch my writing case."

"No need, Lizzy. You haven’t had your coffee. Stay. The ink here is serviceable."

"The pens are not."

Mr. Gardiner looked toward the writing desk near the window. "A grave charge."

"I shall not risk Miss Darcy's opinion of me upon an inn pen. She plays Mozart. She will notice."

Mrs. Gardiner laughed then, and the sound eased the room for a moment.

Elizabeth crossed to the small table near the window where a tray, a chipped blue vase, and the offending pens had been arranged. Outside, Lambton's street moved through its ordinary early afternoon: cart wheels, one child calling to another, two women pausing beneath the inn sign with baskets on their arms. 

She stood there a moment. Ordinary life. In full possession of itself.

She took up a pen. The nib was blunted; she set it aside and took up another, which was worse.

The door opened.

The landlord appeared with a countenance full of importance imperfectly restrained. "Beg pardon, sir. Mr. Darcy and Miss Darcy are below, and ask whether Mr. and Mrs. Gardiner and Miss Bennet are at home."

Elizabeth's hand closed on the pen.

The nib snapped.
