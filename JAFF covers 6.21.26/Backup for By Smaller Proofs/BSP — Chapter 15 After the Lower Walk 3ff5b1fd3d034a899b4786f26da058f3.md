# BSP — Chapter 15: After the Lower Walk

✅ Chapter complete

---

The lower walk had followed them indoors.

Georgiana entered the morning room with it still upon her: the quickened color from the air, one glove half-drawn from her fingers, the trace of pleasure she had not yet had time to dismiss. She came in at Darcy’s side and looked, for the space of three steps, younger than she did at the instrument and less young than she did when she was afraid.

Then she saw Miss Bingley.

The brightness did not vanish. That would have been less difficult to bear. It reduced itself by degrees: a checked step, a lowered glance, the glove folded once, and then once again, with unnecessary care.

Something must be done.

Bingley was also in the room, standing near the tea table with all the satisfaction of a man who had discovered that exercise, if endured by others, might still end in cake for himself. Mrs. Reynolds, or some servant more punctual than the party, had anticipated them. The tea stood ready but untouched. Cups waited beside the pot. A plate of small seed-cakes stood where Bingley could admire them without appearing to have arranged himself for that purpose.

“You have returned at the very instant,” he said. “Tea was in danger of becoming punctual without you.”

Georgiana smiled, and the smile nearly recovered itself.

Miss Bingley set down her book, her finger in what looked to be the same spot it had occupied when they left. Darcy did not suppose a page had suffered.

“My dear Georgiana,” she said, “how animated you are. Miss Bennet’s spirits are contagious, I see.”

There it was.

A civil sentence. A sisterly tone. No word that could be challenged without making the challenger absurd.

Georgiana’s fingers closed round her gloves.

Darcy had heard Miss Bingley say such things a hundred times. He had heard them in drawing rooms, at Netherfield, in town, and in this house. They had belonged, he supposed, to the ordinary climate of her conversation: scented, decorative, faintly oppressive, and, for a man with a door always at his disposal, easy to escape.

He had not considered what that climate might do to a girl who had nowhere to go.

“Miss Darcy did find the walk agreeable,” he said.

Miss Bingley’s eyes came to him, quick and assessing. “How fortunate.”

Bingley, who had never in his life willingly permitted an edge to remain an edge, seized the safer half of the remark. “And was it? Agreeable, I mean. I regret missing it, though I cannot pretend to regret being present when tea arrived.”

“Yes,” Georgiana said. “Very pleasant.”

Five minutes ago, at the lower bridge, her pleasure had outrun speech. Now she had returned to a phrase any governess in England might approve.

For a moment, Darcy did not move. He wanted to leave the room.

He reconsidered.

Leaving would have been the old habit, though he would not have called it that. He would have gone to his office, closed the door, considered the matter, written a letter perhaps, made some arrangement perhaps, and returned with a decision polished enough to look like certainty. Georgiana would have remained meanwhile with Miss Bingley’s care, Bingley’s kindness, and her own habit of making herself easier for everyone by becoming smaller.

Darcy crossed to the tea table instead.

“Georgiana,” he said, “will you pour?”

Her eyes lifted to his. He had given her the room, and she knew it.

“Yes.”

Miss Bingley’s hand moved as if to assist. She stopped, but not before Darcy saw the movement.

He took a chair where he could see his sister’s hands and Miss Bingley’s face, which was not the same as studying either. Bingley sat near the cakes. Miss Bingley settled opposite Darcy, one finger still keeping her place in the unread book.

Georgiana poured. Her hand was steady. Not as easy as it had been at the stream, not nearly as bright, but steady.

He had been proud of steadiness for years.

He was no longer certain it was enough.

Georgiana glanced toward the empty chairs by the hearth. “Are Mr. and Mrs. Hurst still unwell?”

Miss Bingley’s mouth arranged itself into solicitude and did not quite succeed. “They remain in their chambers. Louisa’s head is not improved, and Mr. Hurst has discovered that solitude, when accompanied by a sofa and no expectation of conversation, is the finest medicine in Derbyshire.”

“I am sorry for it,” Georgiana said.

“As am I,” said Miss Bingley, with the air of a sister whose own comfort had suffered most by their illness.

“Will the Gardiners come again?” Bingley asked, taking his cup with gratitude disproportionate to tea. “A delightful man, Mr. Gardiner.”

Darcy answered because an answer was required. “Miss Darcy has invited them.”

“I am glad of it.” Bingley smiled, an obvious, wide curve. “Very glad. They are excellent company. And Miss Elizabeth — that is, Miss Bennet — she seemed much pleased with the walk?”

Miss Bingley’s spoon touched the side of her cup. One neat sound.

“Miss Bennet has always been easily pleased by country rambles.”

The old irritation might once have been enough to occupy him. Today, he also observed the sentence as it passed him and struck elsewhere.

Georgiana looked down at the cup she was filling.

There. The woman had found the tender place.

Darcy said, “The lower walk is generally admired.”

Miss Bingley smiled. “Generally, yes.”

“Perhaps—” Georgiana began.

She stopped.

Darcy waited.

Her hand rested beside the teapot. She did not look first at him. She looked at the cups, then at the open window, then at him. “If Mrs. Gardiner and Miss Bennet are not engaged another day, might they perhaps come again? Not only to walk. I mean — if dinner would not be too much trouble.”

Dinner.

The word entered the room with several others in attendance: intimacy, precedence, family, expectation. Miss Elizabeth Bennet seated at his table. Georgiana looking down the length of the table and finding someone who might smile back.

Bingley looked delighted. Of course.

Miss Bingley looked at Darcy.

Georgiana’s color had risen. “Or an evening,” she said, less certainly. “Only if it would be proper. I do not know — I only mean that Mrs. Gardiner spoke so kindly of the neighborhood.”

Darcy set his cup down. He had not drunk from it.

Ramsgate had begun with kindness. And ended with a near-elopement.

No. That was cowardice. Ramsgate had not begun with kindness. It had begun with concealment, vanity, calculation, and his own absence.

Still, the old caution rose at once. It loomed with all its proofs: Georgiana’s age, her fortune, the world’s appetite, his own failure, Wickham’s smile, the sea air of a place he had thought safe because he had arranged it.

He looked at his sister’s face.

He saw no folly there. Only the mortifying courage of asking for a pleasure while already braced to have it refused.

“We may certainly ask,” he said, “whether another visit would be agreeable.”

It was not a promise of dinner. It was not a refusal.

Georgiana heard both halves and chose, for once, the kinder one. “Thank you.”

Thank you.

For permission to ask for guests in her own brother’s house.

Darcy ordered his hand not to clench.

Miss Bingley’s smile returned, softer and therefore more dangerous. “How charming it is to see young people so quickly attached. New friends are always the most delightful before one knows them very well.”

Bingley frowned, not in comprehension, but in discomfort at the air becoming less easy. “I have found them delightful afterward as well, in many cases.”

“That is because you are generous, Charles.”

“I should hope so,” said Bingley. “It is a much pleasanter failing than the opposite.”

Darcy almost smiled. His mind had already turned back to the lower walk.

Georgiana returning with color in her face.

Georgiana laughing before she remembered to ask whether laughter was safe.

Miss Elizabeth Bennet sharing her smile. She had not flattered Georgiana. Nor drawn her out by force. She had not praised her accomplishments, instructed her in confidence, or made the girl’s shyness an object of display.

She had treated Georgiana’s silence not as a failure to be corrected, but as a pace to be matched.

And Georgiana had returned larger than she left.

“Darcy?”

He looked up.

Bingley was watching him with open concern. Miss Bingley watched with something more pointed. Georgiana looked at him with the readiness he knew too well: the anxious preparation to believe his silence must have been caused by something she had done.

“I beg your pardon,” he said. “What did you ask?”

Bingley laughed, too kindly to leave embarrassment in its traces. “Only whether the Gardiners remain long in Lambton.”

“A few days more, I believe.”

“You believe?” Miss Bingley said. “How unlike you to be uncertain, Mr. Darcy.”

He had earned that. He had not been listening.

“Uncertainty is not always avoidable.”

“No,” said Miss Bingley. “But one generally tries.”

Georgiana set down the sugar tongs with unnecessary care.

Darcy turned to his sister. “Did Mrs. Gardiner say whether she had known the old rectory lane?”

Georgiana blinked, then brightened a little at the safe subject. “Yes. She remembered the lane after rain. She said they used to try to cross without wetting their shoes.”

“A universal ambition,” Darcy agreed. “Rarely rewarded.”

“She said they never did,” Georgiana added. “Or at least never told their mother when they did.”

Bingley laughed. “A proper distinction.”

The room shifted by an inch. Not enough to redeem it. Enough to prove that it could be moved.

Darcy took up his cup and drank.

Cold.

Fitzwilliam would have done it better.

The thought came with such force that he nearly set the cup down again. Fitzwilliam would have found some careless speech for Georgiana to answer, some joke broad enough for Bingley and harmless enough for Miss Bingley, and he would have made the room turn before Georgiana knew she had been rescued.

But Fitzwilliam, too, would have watched. After Ramsgate, perhaps more than before. He had a soldier’s cheerfulness and a younger son’s ease, but he did not lack caution. Between them, he and Darcy had arranged masters, companions, journeys, letters, allowances, visits, proper companions, proper music, proper safeguards.

They had also prevented.

They had prevented very well.

Georgiana attended carefully while Bingley explained that he had once attempted to cross a ditch at Netherfield and had been betrayed by a bank “of most respectable appearance and no principles whatsoever.”

She smiled. She did not laugh.

Fitzwilliam had always been the more cheerful guardian, which was not the same thing as being the less cautious one. Between them, they had contrived to make Georgiana safe.

They had made her lonely.

The realization did not arrive like a blow. A blow would have been cleaner. It came like a sound heard through a wall: indistinct at first, then impossible not to hear.

Miss Elizabeth Bennet had laughed with Georgiana, and Georgiana had not been diminished by it.

Liveliness had not made her reckless. Wit had not made her impertinent. Attention had not made her vain. Miss Bennet had not made Georgiana less careful in any way that mattered. She had only made carefulness less onerous for half an hour.

Darcy’s thumb moved once along the handle of his cup.

Miss Bingley was speaking, again. “Of course, Georgiana, if you invite new friends to dinner, you must allow me to advise you on the table. A small party may be more difficult than a large one. Every deficiency is more noticed.”

The spout dipped; tea touched the saucer before Georgiana righted it.

Miss Bingley had not forbidden anything. She had not said Mrs. Gardiner was unsuitable. She had not said Miss Bennet was dangerous. She had only placed dinner among deficiencies and made Georgiana a girl who would need advice before she dared hospitality.

Suitable company.

He had sought suitable company for Georgiana. He had chosen it, approved it, measured it by family, manner, accomplishment, and consequence. He had feared vulgarity, fortune-hunters, unchecked spirits, improper intimacies, the contagion of low connections and heedless girls.

He had not feared refinement.

“Georgiana,” he said.

She looked up at once.

“If we are to ask the Gardiners and Miss Bennet another day, you may decide whether it is to be a walk, tea, or dinner. Mrs. Reynolds will assist with the arrangements.”

Miss Bingley’s eyes sharpened.

Georgiana’s lips parted. “I may decide?”

“Yes.”

It was one syllable. It cost him more than it should have done.

Bingley broke into a grin. “Excellent. I vote for dinner, if votes are collected.”

“They are not,” Darcy said.

“Then I withdraw my vote and retain my opinion.”

Georgiana laughed, carefully.

Darcy did not look at Miss Bingley. He did not trust himself to do it without making too much of what she had done, and too little of what he had permitted. He took another sip of cold tea.

Caroline’s fan opened. “How very generous you are, Mr. Darcy.”

“No,” he said.

The word was out before he had considered it. It was too flat. Bingley looked at him. Georgiana looked at him. Miss Bingley’s fan stopped.

Darcy set down his cup. “My sister is mistress here. It is not generosity to remember it.”

Silence followed.

Georgiana’s color deepened, but not unhappily. “Thank you,” she said again, softly.

Gratitude where there ought to have been only right.

He could not bear it.

He bore it. Swallowed it down with the tepid tea.

Elizabeth Bennet had been in the room for less than an hour the day before and not at all today after the walk, yet the morning room had altered around the fact of her. Bingley was brighter because she existed within reach of Jane. Georgiana was braver because she had laughed with her by water. Caroline was sharper because she saw both things and could not govern them.

Darcy was less certain because Elizabeth Bennet had once told him the truth so completely that he still found pieces of it in rooms she had left.

At last the tea table dispersed by the natural degrees of a household that had performed enough civility for one afternoon. Bingley remembered a letter he had not finished. Miss Bingley declared the heat oppressive. Georgiana gathered the cups together until Darcy asked a servant to take them and saw her relinquish the task without apology.

He stood.

“I must write to Fitzwilliam.”

Georgiana looked up, startled. “Is anything wrong?”

“No.” He made himself say it without impatience. “Nothing is wrong.”

It was true.

It was not sufficient.

Bingley, already halfway to the door, turned back. “Give him my regards. Tell him I have become a serious fisherman by association.”

“I will not tell him that.”

“You may tell him I intend to become one. That is nearly as good and much less falsifiable.”

Darcy inclined his head. It might have been amusement. It might have been fatigue. He could no longer tell.

He left them then — not abruptly, not fast enough to betray haste. Down the corridor, past the open door where the afternoon light crossed the floor, toward the office where estate papers waited in correct piles and a letter from his steward lay exactly where he had left it.

The office looked the same as ever. Ledgers squared off. Pen-knife beside the blotter. The sand was dry in its little silver box. Mr. Greaves’s memorandum concerning the north coppice waited beneath a weight, patient and legible and much more reasonable than any human being in this house.

Darcy shut the door.

For half a minute he did not go to the desk. He heard nothing from the hall.

That had been his arrangement too.

Quiet rooms. Proper women. Suitable company. A brother who loved her and loved her so vigilantly that he had made himself, in all practical matters, another locked door.

He drew out the chair and sat.

*Georgiana is well.*

The sentence presented itself at once, neat as a visiting card.

It was true. She was well. She practiced, rode, read, wrote, took the air, sat with Mrs. Annesley, received Miss Bingley, and answered questions in a voice everyone praised for its sweetness. She was safe from Wickham. Safe from scandal. Safe from folly. Safe from almost everything that had teeth.

He took a sheet of paper.

*Georgiana is well.*

The ink darkened, settled, and made the lie visible.

Not a lie. Darcy would not permit himself that. Fitzwilliam, reading it, would understand the plain sense of the words. Georgiana was not ill. No new danger had appeared. No name had been spoken which must send either of them riding all night.

The sentence was true.

It was not enough.

He thought of Elizabeth on the lower walk, though he had not stood near enough to hear every word. A tilt of her head. A glove darkened by moss. The refusal to make Georgiana’s shyness the subject of the morning. The mercy of treating a nervous girl as a companion and not a project.

At Hunsford she had shown him no mercy at all.

No. That was not true. She had given him the only mercy in her power. She had refused him plainly enough to make self-deception impossible. She had named his offenses. She had left him the labor of believing her.

He had believed her.

Not at once. Not generously. Not without all the bitter little trials by which a proud man attempts to prove a truth false because it is inconvenient.

But he had believed her.

And now, in rooms she had left, the consequences continued to present themselves for signature.

He bent to the letter.
