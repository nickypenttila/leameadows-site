# BSP — Chapter 14: The Lower Walk

By Thursday morning, Elizabeth had learned that a second invitation to Pemberley could trouble a person more than the first.

A tempered sun lay over Lambton. A little wind moved through the inn yard. The hills, lush with green, looked ready to reward any party sensible enough to be pleased with them.

Over another fine breakfast at the inn, Mrs. Gardiner read the latest letter from Jane. “The children are well, Mrs. Bennet nervous, and Lydia injured beyond all previous female suffering by the continued absence of Brighton."

“Then all is as we left it," said Mr. Gardiner.

“Not quite," said Mrs. Gardiner. “Mary writes a postscript."

Elizabeth looked up. “Mary?"

“On the subject of self-command."

“That is new in subject only," said Mr. Gardiner. “Mary has always been very ready to command herself in public, and others in private."

Mrs. Gardiner smiled and folded the sheet. “She praises Kitty, praise be. Kitty, it seems, has been practicing."

Elizabeth’s spoon stilled in her tea. “Practicing?"

“The pianoforte. Jane says she plays twenty minutes every morning, and Lydia declares no punishment in the family has ever been so severe."

Mr. Gardiner laughed. “A noble beginning. Most improvements are first recognized by the suffering of others."

Elizabeth smiled, but the image remained with her. Kitty at the instrument, awkward perhaps, resentful perhaps, but staying. Kitty, who had once drifted through every room in Lydia’s wake, now sitting for twenty whole minutes where Jane had persuaded her to sit, making some sound that was not merely echo. Perhaps she was even displacing Mary from the piano stool. The image came with them into the carriage and took a seat next to Elizabeth.

At Pemberley, the house, cool even in the sultry heat, did not trouble itself to be new.

Stone, lawn, and water held their places. Elizabeth had no time to resent them for it, for Miss Darcy was already in the morning room with her brother and came forward at once.

Her soft blue muslin gown was plain enough for walking, fine enough to put Jane’s second-best to shame; her gloves were in one hand, as if she had come a little before she was quite ready and would not retreat to correct herself now.

“I am very glad you could come," she said, and then looked at Elizabeth with a quickness that warmed and discomposed them both.

“So are we," Mrs. Gardiner replied. “You promised us shade, Miss Darcy. In such weather, that is no small recommendation."

“The lower walk is shaded nearly all the way," Miss Darcy said. “And there is a place where the water is cooler. I mean — it sounds cooler. I do not know whether water can sound cool."

“It can in August," said Mrs. Gardiner. “I have heard it do so myself."

Miss Darcy smiled before she could prepare for it.

Mr. Darcy bowed to the Gardiners, then to Elizabeth. Elizabeth met his eyes while she was still smiling. His expression faltered a moment, and then settled back into propriety. 

The small morning room, on the garden side, was pale-paneled and bright with reflected green from the lawn. The windows stood open, and the air carried the smell of warm boxwood and cut grass.

The room might have been all summer ease, had Miss Bingley not been deeper within it, yet seated where no one could fail to discover her. A small book lay open and unread in her hand; a shawl, too light to be necessary, rested about her shoulders. She looked better than the day before, which was to say less ill and more dangerous.

“My dear Georgiana," she said, rising with languid care, “you are very good to exert yourself for the lower walk. I am sure Miss Bennet and Mrs. Gardiner will forgive me if I do not attempt Derbyshire heroics today."

Miss Darcy’s fingers tightened around her gloves. “It is not so difficult a walk," she said, soft. “And it is shaded."

“How fortunate," said Miss Bingley. “Shade is always a consolation to those who are determined upon exertion."

The words were small. Their effect was not. They made a pleasure sound like a defect of judgment.

Mrs. Gardiner’s answer came before Elizabeth’s indignation could arrange itself into speech. “Then we shall be doubly obliged to Miss Darcy for choosing so well. I have always thought the best walks are those that make one forget they are exercise."

Miss Darcy’s eyes lifted gratefully.

Mr. Darcy nodded toward his sister. “If you are ready, Georgiana."

Miss Darcy led the way back into the hall, where gloves and hats were donned. Miss Bingley, having made the walk rustic and herself elegant in one economy of effort, remained within. Elizabeth discovered, as they crossed the hall, that the prospect of the lower walk had improved.

They set out by a side door that opened toward the rear garden.

For the first few minutes the sun lay full on the gravel, bright enough to make the pale house behind them seem whiter when Elizabeth glanced back. Warmed stone scented the air; bees worked somewhere among the borders with a noisy industry that made conversation feel less necessary. 

Mrs. Gardiner lifted her face to the breeze. Mr. Gardiner seemed to be already listening for water. Miss Darcy, who had been careful in the hall, seemed to take easier possession of herself with every step away from it. When the path slipped beneath the beeches, the shade closed over them cool and green, and Elizabeth felt the whole party breathe easier.

Mr. Gardiner, with the instinct of a man who knew that rivers and gentlemen improved on acquaintance, asked almost immediately whether the lower bend he had fished yesterday belonged to the same stream they could just hear through the trees.

“It does," said Mr. Darcy. “The path divides a little way ahead. If you would like to see the turn from above—"

Mr. Gardiner looked a question at his wife.

“My dear," said Mrs. Gardiner, “do not pretend reluctance for our sake." She smiled at Mr. Darcy. “We shall keep Miss Darcy with us, if she will permit it."

Miss Darcy looked from her brother to Mrs. Gardiner, and then to Elizabeth.

“I should like that," she said.

Mr. Darcy’s gaze rested on her for one brief instant. “Then we shall meet you by the lower bridge."

It was correctly said, and nothing more than the arrangement required. Yet Elizabeth felt the cost in it, as one feels a current beneath a smooth piece of water. He would have preferred, perhaps, to remain near enough to interpret every silence and prevent every embarrassment. Instead he bowed, turned with Mr. Gardiner toward the side path, and left his sister to them.

Miss Darcy watched him until the turn of the path took him from view. Then she faced the lower walk with an expression Elizabeth had not yet seen from her: not relief exactly, but pleasure.

Mrs. Gardiner began to speak of the trees. Her kindness took the form of interest in beech roots, path stones, a bird’s nest, the turn of a hill. By the time they had gone twenty paces, she had asked Miss Darcy whether that copper beech had always leaned so, whether the path flooded in winter, and whether the lower walk had been much frequented when Miss Darcy was a child.

“Oh no," Miss Darcy said, and then corrected herself. “That is — I came here sometimes. With my brother, or Mrs. Reynolds. But not often alone."

“Were you forbidden?" Elizabeth asked lightly. The question had risen before discretion could smooth it.

Miss Darcy considered this with great seriousness. “Not forbidden. Only there was generally some reason not to go. Lessons, or weather, or someone thinking another place more suitable."

“Suitable," said Elizabeth, “is a powerful word. It can close more doors than a key."

Miss Darcy looked startled, then smiled in understanding. “My brother is never unkind. But he is sometimes careful before I have known I wished for anything."

The words came softly. The loyalty in them was plain; so was the care with which she had permitted herself to say even that much.

Mrs. Gardiner did not answer too quickly. “Those who love us are often tempted to do our fearing for us."

“Yes," said Miss Darcy. “And my cousin too."

“Colonel Fitzwilliam?" Elizabeth asked.

Miss Darcy nodded. “He laughs at my brother, which is a comfort. But then he generally decides my brother is right."

Mrs. Gardiner laughed gently. Elizabeth could not help smiling.

Miss Darcy seemed surprised at having said so much. Her eyes went at once to the path, and her fingers moved over the seam of one glove.

“I do not mean that I am not grateful."

“No," said Mrs. Gardiner, with a warmth that made denial unnecessary. “Of course you do not."

Under the beeches, the path curved downward. Gravel gave way to packed earth, dry at the center and soft with moss at the edges. Ferns grew in the cooler hollows. The air changed as they descended: less garden sweetness, more damp stone and leaf shade. Somewhere below, water moved over rock, not loudly, but with a steady murmur that seemed to gather the heat from the day and carry it away.

Miss Darcy’s step altered. She began to look ahead rather than at her own feet.

“There is a place," she said, and stopped. She flushed faintly. “It is nothing. Only stones by the water. When the stream is low, one can cross there. Or almost cross. My brother would say it is not a crossing if one must still turn back at the last stone."

“Brothers," said Mrs. Gardiner, a world of annoyance and love in one word.

Miss Darcy’s smile returned. “This way."

She led them from the main path down a smaller one, made perhaps for gardeners or children and tolerated by the estate as a harmless irregularity. A hazel branch leaned across it; Miss Darcy lifted it with an unconscious familiarity. The stream appeared at once — narrow here, bright over shallow stones, with a ferny bank on one side and, on the other, three flat stones set crookedly from the shore.

The place was very small.

That was its charm.

No prospect opened from it. No guest would be brought there to admire Pemberley’s consequence. The house was unseen; the lawn forgotten. The grand water above and below had reduced itself here to a child’s problem and a girl’s secret.

Miss Darcy looked at it as if she feared it might not be worth the showing.

Mrs. Gardiner stepped closer. “Oh, this is delightful."

“Do you think so?"

“Absolutely. When I was young, we had a place nearer town where the water crossed the lane after rain. We considered it a matter of honor to pass without wetting our shoes, which we never did. Or at least, we never told our mother we did."

Miss Darcy’s laugh came lightly, and this time did not disappear at once.

Elizabeth crouched to look at the stones. Spray had darkened the middle of the nearest one; the far edge was green where moss had taken advantage of neglect.

“Is this the famous crossing?" Elizabeth asked.

“Only in a very generous sense," Miss Darcy said. “The third stone is too far unless the water is low."

“Then it is a crossing with principles. It refuses to be attempted at the wrong season."

Miss Darcy considered this, then laughed. “My brother would approve of that description."

Mrs. Gardiner looked from one young woman to the other and wisely interested herself in a fern.

Elizabeth rose, brushing a speck of moss from her glove. “Longbourn has very few tests of this kind. Our dangers are more often chairs left where they ought not be, or sisters practicing scales with more resolution than mercy."

“Your sisters play?"

“Some of them do. Some attempt it. Some consider music an excellent thing in other people."

Miss Darcy smiled. “And you?"

“I perform only when affection, duty, or a shortage of alternatives requires it."

“That sounds very sensible."

“It is cowardice with a better name."

Miss Darcy laughed again — not loudly, but with a quickness that seemed to surprise her. She looked almost at once toward the path by which Mr. Darcy had gone, then checked herself, as if even laughter required permission when it arrived unexpectedly.

Elizabeth saw it.

She did not like how much she minded it.

Miss Darcy was loved. That was beyond question. Protected, instructed, cherished, considered. Two guardians, a companion, masters, servants, a brother whose attention gathered around her at the first sign of unease. Yet here she was, showing two almost-friends a stone by a stream as if it were a pleasure that had to be smuggled out.

No. Not almost-friends. Not yet.

But not strangers.

Mrs. Gardiner had the enviable art of becoming familiar without presuming on it. Elizabeth had been admitted, somehow, not to Georgiana’s confidence, but to the threshold of it; and she found, with a discomfort that was not all discomfort, that she wished to be allowed further in.

Miss Darcy stepped onto the first stone.

“Take care," Elizabeth said, and regretted it at once.

Miss Darcy looked back, not offended, but rueful. “You see? Everyone says that."

Mrs. Gardiner’s eyes met Elizabeth’s with the smallest glint of humor.

Elizabeth made her voice solemn. “Then I withdraw it. I meant to say: advance with all the recklessness proper to a heroine of the lower walk."

Miss Darcy’s lips parted in astonishment and pleasure. She stepped to the second stone.

“That is the easy one," she said. “It is so wide."

“I am relieved to find even heroines are allowed easy stones."

Miss Darcy set the toe of one half-boot carefully on the easy stone. “The third is only possible if the water is lower."

“Then the third is not failure. It is deferred glory."

Miss Darcy laughed, and this time the sound carried above the stream.

The pleasure of it struck Elizabeth. It was not that Miss Darcy became another creature. She did not suddenly chatter, or grow bold, or throw off the years of careful teaching like a shawl too warm for the weather. She was still shy. Still careful. Still glancing back at propriety as if it might have followed her down the path.

But something had opened.

Mrs. Gardiner asked whether the stream froze in winter. As she stepped back safely to shore, Miss Darcy answered that it did, or partly did. Mrs. Reynolds disliked anyone coming down when the bank was hard. Once Colonel Fitzwilliam had said he would teach her to slide and then been reproved by her brother before he had advanced three steps in the plan.

“Did he obey?" asked Elizabeth.

“Colonel Fitzwilliam? No. But he changed the subject so amusingly that my brother was obliged to stop being displeased before he had finished."

“A useful talent."

“Yes," Miss Darcy said. “I have often wished to learn it."

The simplicity of the admission silenced Elizabeth.

She pictured Longbourn. Lydia’s unchecked noise, Kitty’s echoing giggles, Mary’s solemn interruptions, Jane’s gentleness, herself saying too much because she could not bear to say nothing. There was no want of foolishness there. No shortage of contradiction. No careful male authority had ever stood between a Bennet daughter and a ridiculous opinion. Elizabeth had often wished for more restraint in her family. She had not considered that restraint, in abundance, might become another kind of poverty.

A girl such as Miss Darcy did not need another lesson. She needed someone who could laugh first and explain afterward.

And with that thought, absurdly and unmistakably, came Kitty.

Kitty, who was still too easily led, and yet not only led. Kitty, who had begun, however grudgingly, to practice when Jane asked it. Kitty, who would have stepped onto the first stone, squealed at the second, declared the third impossible, attempted it anyway, and then described the whole adventure at dinner with more drama than accuracy.

Kitty would know what to do with a place like this.

The thought was so unexpected that Elizabeth nearly smiled at the stream.

They returned slowly to the main path. Miss Darcy did not talk all the way; that would have been too much to ask of one afternoon. But her silences had changed. They no longer seemed empty spaces waiting to be filled by someone more practiced. They were pauses in which she might, at any moment, decide to offer something.

At the turn by the lower bridge, voices reached them.

Mr. Gardiner appeared first, hat in hand, speaking animatedly, somehow, about water. Mr. Darcy came beside him, attentive, composed, and carrying in his expression a sort of grave satisfaction.

Miss Darcy turned at the sound.

A moment earlier, she had been smiling at something Mrs. Gardiner said about wet shoes and childhood grandeur. The smile had been unguarded. Not brilliant; not noisy; only hers.

When she saw her brother, it gathered itself back into propriety.

Not vanished. Only folded.

Mr. Darcy saw it. His step faltered.

For one breath, brother and sister looked at one another across the little distance where the paths met. Miss Darcy seemed about to apologize for something — having delayed, or wandered, or been pleased beyond the approved measure — but her brother spoke first.

“You found the stepping-stones," he said.

Miss Darcy’s color rose. “They are not truly a crossing."

“No," he replied, with something almost dry in his voice. “But they have always made a very determined attempt."

Miss Darcy looked down, smiling.

Elizabeth, who had meant to enjoy only the kindness of it, found herself enjoying his tone as well.

They walked back toward the house together, the party re-forming by degrees: Mr. Gardiner and Mr. Darcy speaking of the water; Mrs. Gardiner beside Miss Darcy; Elizabeth near enough to hear, not near enough to intrude. The house appeared through the trees in portions — a line of stone, a high window, the pale flash of steps. The formal world reassembled itself with every yard.

Near the garden door, Miss Darcy slowed.

“Perhaps you might stay—" she began, then stopped, as if the words had outrun her training. “Forgive me. You have already given us so much of the afternoon."

Mrs. Gardiner’s smile warmed. “Then we must be careful not to spend all our pleasure at once."

Mr. Darcy looked from his sister to Mrs. Gardiner. “If you would allow us another day, Miss Darcy would be very happy to show you more of the grounds."

Miss Darcy’s eyes flew to him. The brightness returned before she could prevent it.

Elizabeth felt, absurdly, as if he had given the permission to her as well.

Mrs. Gardiner accepted with all proper gratitude. Mr. Gardiner declared himself entirely at Pemberley’s disposal. Elizabeth said what was required, and what was true.

“I should like it very much."

It was true; and on the road back to Lambton, when Kitty returned to her thoughts, Miss Darcy returned with her.
