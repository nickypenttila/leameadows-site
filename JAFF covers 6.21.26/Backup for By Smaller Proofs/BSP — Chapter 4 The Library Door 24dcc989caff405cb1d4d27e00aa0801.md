# BSP — Chapter 4: The Library Door

Elizabeth had taken the stone bench at the end of the gravel walk, where the hedge screened her from the house and the April sky pressed in too fine for brooding. She was brooding anyway. A wren quarreled somewhere in the hawthorn, a bee fumbled among the early primroses.

Hill found her there. Hill, with her unerring instinct for locating members of the family who meant not to be found, came down the path at her usual unhurried pace, hands folded before her apron, and waited the half-beat she always waited before speaking.

"Your father asks for you, miss. In the library."

Elizabeth set down the trowel she had not been using. She had brought it outside for the purpose of appearing occupied, and it had served this function admirably for the better part of an hour while she sat on the stone bench and stared at the hedge and tried not to think about the letter that had followed her out of Kent, and followed Hill inside.

The library door was shut. It had been shut, with varying degrees of finality, since the morning Kitty had spoken at breakfast. In the days since, it had opened for trays, for Hill, and once for Mary, who had knocked to request a volume of Fordyce and been told through the oak that she would find it improving to locate the book herself. It had not opened for Mrs. Bennet. It had not opened for Lydia. It had opened for Kitty, once, on that first morning, and whatever had passed between father and daughter in that quarter of an hour had sent Kitty back upstairs with red eyes and a set to her jaw that Elizabeth had not seen there before.

Elizabeth knocked. "Papa, it is Lizzy."

"Come."

The library was Mr. Bennet's country, and he had arranged it with the care of a man who understood that the chief pleasure of domestic life was the possibility of retreating from it. Lather and old paper scented air that has not been disturbed by an open window in several days. Books lined three walls from floor to ceiling, not decoratively, but with the disordered density of a collection that had been read. His desk stood against the wall beside the window, angled so that he could see the garden from his left and the door from his right without committing his full attention to either. On the desk, beside the inkstand and a cup of cold tea with a skin forming on its surface, lay a letter.

Mr. Bennet was in his reading chair — a deep, worn leather armchair drawn up to the desk at that same considering angle, its arms darkened at the elbows from forty years of use. He had his spectacles on and a book open on his knee, but the book was face-down in the manner of a volume that had been set aside some time ago and not returned to, and his spectacles sat at the end of his nose in the position that meant he was looking over them rather than through them.

"Ah, Lizzy. Do sit."

She sat. The chair opposite his desk was hard-backed and upholstered in faded green, and it had the particular discomfort of a seat not designed for lingering. Elizabeth had been in this room a thousand times — to borrow books, to share a passage, to trade an amused glance at the rest of the household's expense. She had sat in this chair, across from him, perhaps a dozen times: always summoned, never invited to stay.

"Your uncle Gardiner has written." Mr. Bennet picked up the letter between two fingers, as though it were a curiosity brought to him by the post rather than a piece of family correspondence. "He and your aunt propose a tour. Not the Lakes, as originally discussed — your uncle's business will not permit the full excursion. A shorter journey. The north, he says, without troubling to be more specific. I believe your aunt mentioned the scheme when you were in London."

"She did."

"And you are inclined to go."

It was not a question. Elizabeth looked at her father and saw that he already knew the answer — had known it, perhaps, before sending Hill to fetch her. He was watching her with the expression she had always privately enjoyed: the one that said *I see you, Lizzy, and I find you the most tolerable member of this household, and that is as close to a declaration of love as I am prepared to make before noon.*

She had always been flattered by that look. She was not certain she was flattered now.

"I should like it very much," she said. "If you can spare me."

"Spare you." He repeated the words with the faintest inflection. Not quite irony, not quite weariness, but something that lived in the territory between. "I believe the household will survive your absence. Your mother will have one fewer audience member, which may shorten the performances. Lydia will have one fewer sister to blame, though I expect she will redistribute the surplus with her customary efficiency. And I shall have one fewer person whose company I can tolerate, which is a loss I feel keenly in advance."

Elizabeth did not smile. She would have smiled, a week ago. She would have matched him quip for quip and felt the warm, conspiratorial pleasure of being the daughter who understood the joke. But the joke, this morning, had a shape she had not noticed before — or had noticed and chosen not to examine. 

Her father made wit out of his family's disorder because wit was cheaper than governance, and funnier, and did not require him to open the library door.

*Occasionally even by your father.*

Mr. Darcy’s words arrived as they had been arriving for days — unbidden, unwelcome, precise as a surgeon's notation in the margin of a case he had diagnosed correctly.

"You are very quiet, Lizzy."

"I am thinking."

"A dangerous occupation. I have largely given it up myself." He set the letter down and removed his spectacles, folding them with the deliberate care that was his particular form of punctuation — the gesture that meant the pleasantries were concluded and the real conversation was about to begin. "You have been quiet since Hunsford. Quieter than Kent would seem to warrant. Your cousin Mr. Collins is tedious, but he is a familiar tedium, and Charlotte is sensible enough to dilute it. Lady Catherine, I am told, is magnificent in her certainty. None of this should produce the expression you have been wearing at meals for the past week."

"What expression is that?"

"The expression of a woman who has been served a dish she cannot politely return to the kitchen."

Elizabeth's hands went still in her lap. She had not expected this. She had expected the sardonic commentary, the dry observations, the performance of paternal attention that substituted cleverness for care. She had not expected him to see her.

"I am merely tired," she said. "The journey was long, and Longbourn has been... eventful."

"Eventful." He tasted the word. "Yes. Your sister Kitty has supplied us with more excitement than I had budgeted for this quarter. Kitty came to me. Sat in that very chair. She had folded a note multiple times before she came in, and she could not stop folding it while she talked. She began her first sentence four times before she reached the end of it; thereafter the words came out in a rush, as water will when a pipe has been too long stopped. Six minutes, she spoke. I timed her. The longest ever, for her. At the end she asked whether I was cross with her. I told her I was not. I daresay that was the most useful sentence I have spoken to any of my daughters this season."

He paused. Elizabeth waited.

"She did a brave thing, your sister. I am not certain I have done a brave thing since the century turned. Though in fairness, the opportunities for bravery in a gentleman's library are limited. One can only refuse to answer one's wife's knock with so much heroism before the gesture loses its luster."

It was a clever joke. It was the kind one laughed at before one understood what it cost, and by the time one understood, the laughter had already spent itself, and what remained was not laughter at all.

He knew, Elizabeth saw. He knew he had hidden. He knew Kitty had done what he should have done months ago, and he was making it amusing because the alternative was letting it stand.

"She was brave," Elizabeth said.

"She was. And now she is miserable, because Lydia has made it her particular project to ensure that courage is punished as thoroughly as cowardice in this household. I have spoken to Lydia. Speaking to Lydia is rather like speaking to the weather. One feels the effort has been made, and the weather continues unaltered. Your mother has spoken to me. Your mother speaks to me with the regularity of a tide, and with approximately the same expectation of being heeded." He picked up the cold tea, looked at it, set it down again. "The regiment leaves this week. I presume you know."

"We saw the preparations in Meryton yesterday."

"And did you encounter anyone of note?"

Elizabeth's pulse did something she would rather it had not.

"We met Mrs. Forster," she said. "And several of the officers. The street was full of bustle."

"Mrs. Forster. The colonel's young bride. A woman of — what shall we say — considerable vivacity and limited reflection. Rather in the mold of your sister Lydia, if Lydia had the advantage of a husband with an income and a commission." He paused. "And Mr. Wickham? You will have had your last sight of him, I suppose."

The name sat in the room between them.

"We saw him," Elizabeth said.

She heard the flatness in her own voice. A week ago she would have said *Mr. Wickham* with a warmth she now understood to have been manufactured, not by the man alone, but by her own eagerness to believe in a version of events that flattered her judgment and confirmed her worst opinions of a better man whom she had decided to despise. She would have said something clever about his charm, or his manners, or the general injustice of his situation. She would have performed, for her father's amusement, exactly the kind of confident misreading that Wickham's whole career depended upon.

She could not do so now.

"You saw him," Mr. Bennet repeated. "And?"

"And he was civil. He carried Mrs. Forster's parcels. He asked to be remembered to Lydia — as any officer might, taking his leave."

"As any officer might." Mr. Bennet's eyebrows rose a fraction — the movement so slight it might have been a trick of the light, except that Mr. Bennet's eyebrows never moved by accident. "A careful phrase, Lizzy. You have not always been so careful on the subject of Mr. Wickham."

The air in the library went thin.

Elizabeth knew — she *knew* — that the correct response was a light one. A quip about how a girl who has been proved wrong about one man's character might grow cautious about another's. A wry deflection that would satisfy her father's curiosity without feeding it. She had a dozen such responses available, and she reached for one, and what came out instead was:

"I would not trust his good nature as far as Meryton trusts it."

The sentence fell into the room and lay there.

It was, on its surface, the kind of thing anyone might say about anyone — a mild expression of skepticism, the sort of hedged opinion that belonged in drawing rooms and cost nothing. But Elizabeth heard, a half-second after speaking, the weight the words carried: not the weight of casual doubt but of specific knowledge, and the specificity was audible.

Her father was not a man who missed such things.

The look lasted three seconds. Elizabeth counted them. In the first second he was merely attentive — the sharpened attention of a man who has heard something unexpected from the person he least expected to surprise him. In the second, something shifted behind his eyes: a narrowing, a focus, the look of a reader who has turned a page and found the paragraph he was not prepared for. In the third, he made a decision.

He did not ask.

He picked up the letter from Mr. Gardiner, smoothed a crease in the paper with his thumb, and said, in the tone of a man returning to safer ground: "Your uncle writes well. He has a steady hand and a talent for making the practical sound inviting. 'A tour of the north' — as though the north were a garden one might stroll through between breakfast and dinner. I daresay you will see cathedrals, Lizzy. And hills. And sheep. The sheep of the north are said to be superior to ours, though I have never troubled to verify the claim."

Elizabeth exhaled, making the retreat mutual. As they both stepped back from the edge of something, the relief was so immediate and so complete that it frightened her. She had nearly said more. *He is not what he seems* or *You should enquire more closely into his history* or — worst of all — *I have it on authority that he attempted to elope with a girl of fifteen for her fortune.*

She had even, sitting on the stone bench in the hour before Hill came down the path, considered the half-measure. *I heard at Rosings, from a source I cannot name, that he ran out on his debts and had attempted, by less honourable means, to secure a fortune.* She had turned the sentence over twice. She had seen what was wrong with it.

*At Rosings* placed the source within a small, knowable circle. *A young girl with a fortune* was not a generic phrase in a world that contained Mr. Darcy's sister. Her father might not draw the line; but a warning, once given, must take some shape in the world — a letter to Colonel Forster, an instruction to Hill, a refusal at the door — and any of these, in a county the size of theirs, would be observed. Wickham, finding himself observed, would draw the line within a sentence. And Mr. Bennet — who had answered his daughter's direct testimony with the closing of a door — would not be moved by hearsay smuggled out of Kent. The half-measure would purchase her nothing, and would cost a girl in Ramsgate a name she had nearly lost once already. The authority was a letter she could not produce from a man she could not name, and the wreckage that declaration would have caused was incalculable.

"I should enjoy the hills," she said. "And the sheep. Though I make no promises about the cathedrals."

"Then you shall go." Mr. Bennet replaced his spectacles. The gesture was the reverse of their removal — the conversation was being closed, the punctuation applied, the door about to shut. "Write to your aunt and confirm it. I shall tell your mother, which I expect will produce either raptures at having one fewer mouth to feed or despair at losing a daughter to the wilderness. The odds are even."

Elizabeth rose. The chair creaked as she left it — a small, sharp sound in the quiet room.

She stood in the middle of the library and looked at her father and felt something tear. Quietly, cleanly, like a seam giving way along a line that had been weakening for some time. Not love. The love was intact; she did not think it would ever be otherwise. But something adjacent to love — the uncomplicated admiration of a daughter who had always believed her father's wit was wisdom, his detachment was philosophy, his closed door was the reasonable boundary of a reasonable man.

*Occasionally even by your father.*

The man who had written those words had never met Mr. Bennet. He had observed the family at Netherfield, at the Meryton assembly, at one ball. He had watched from across the room with the cold, cataloging eye of a man who judged freely and spoke what he saw, and he had been — in this, as in so many things Elizabeth was only now beginning to tally — correct.

She wanted to say: *You are right. You have been that sort of father. And I have been the daughter who laughed at your jokes instead of seeing what they cost, and we are more alike than I can bear to examine, and I am leaving for the north because this house has become a mirror I do not wish to stand in front of.*

She said: "Thank you, Papa."

Mr. Bennet glanced at her over his spectacles. He didn’t smile. The look held for a moment longer than it should have. Long enough for Elizabeth to understand that he heard what she had said and what she had not said, and that he would accept the lie, as he accepted most things, because accepting it was easier than opening the door.

Elizabeth crossed the library. The room was not large, but the distance felt different today, longer or shorter, she could not tell which. The books watched her from their shelves. The cold tea sat on the desk with its skin of neglect. Her father's pen lay uncapped beside the inkstand, and a drop of ink had dried on the blotter in a shape that looked, from certain angles, like a small closed fist.

She reached the door. She turned the handle. She stepped into the hallway, where the air was cooler and the light came from the window above the stairs rather than the single pane behind her father's desk, and the smell was beeswax and laundered linen instead of leather and cold tea.

She pulled the door shut behind her.

The click of the latch was small and definite. Elizabeth stood with her hand on the handle for a moment longer than was necessary, feeling the brass warm under her palm. 

Jane was in the hallway.

She was standing by the window at the top of the first flight, half in the light and half out of it,  her hands folded and her face carrying the patient, unsurprised expression of a woman who had been waiting without appearing to wait. She did not ask what had happened in the library. She did not ask why Elizabeth had been summoned or what their father had said. She simply stood there, steady and present, offering her company the way she always offered it — without conditions, without questions, without the demand that Elizabeth explain herself before being allowed to feel whatever she felt.

"Lizzy," Jane said. "Shall we walk?"

Dear, constant Jane. Who had asked no question and would ask none. Who was willing, as she had always been willing, to walk beside whatever her sister carried, and wait to be told, or not told, as the days required.

"Yes," Elizabeth said. "I think we must."
