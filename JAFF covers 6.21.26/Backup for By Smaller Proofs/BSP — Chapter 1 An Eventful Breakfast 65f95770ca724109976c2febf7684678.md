# BSP — Chapter 1: An Eventful Breakfast

The breakfast room at Longbourn was lit by the kind of thin April sunshine that promises nothing and keeps its word — a theater in which Mrs. Bennet performed and Mr. Bennet declined to attend — and Elizabeth Bennet was not listening to her mother.

This was neither new nor remarkable. Mrs. Bennet's morning conversation operated on the same principle as a church bell: it rang at intervals, it carried across distances no one had requested, and it required no response from the congregation beyond the occasional murmur of acknowledgment. Elizabeth had been supplying these murmurs since childhood and could produce them now without the involvement of her higher faculties, which were otherwise engaged.

They had come home yesterday evening, she and Jane together, after three days at the Gardiners' in Gracechurch Street. Before that had been the journey from Hunsford — the roads dry, Maria Lucas talking the whole way to London with a cheerful vigor that required nothing of Elizabeth beyond a traveling companion's face turned in her direction. Elizabeth had been grateful for this. She had needed the hours. She had needed the motion of the carriage and the hedgerows sliding past the window and the long, flat stretches of road where there was nothing to look at and therefore nothing to prevent her from looking, again and again, at the contents of her own mind.

*The situation of your mother's family, though objectionable, was nothing in comparison of that total want of propriety —*

She reached for the butter dish.

*— so frequently, so almost uniformly betrayed by herself, by your three younger sisters, and occasionally even by your father —*

The butter knife was cold in her hand. She applied it to her toast with the careful attention of a woman lettering an address she did not wish to smudge.

"— and I said to Mrs. Long, I said, there is no point in despairing, for where there are officers there is always hope, and where there is hope there is — Jane, you are not eating."

Jane looked up from her plate. She had been arranging a piece of ham with the fork in a pattern that suggested occupation without appetite. "I am eating, Mama. I am only a little slow this morning."

"You are pale. You have been pale since London. I do not understand why London should make a girl pale when there are so many shops and so much to see, but there it is. You must take more air. Fresh air and a new bonnet — that is my prescription for paleness."

Jane smiled. It was the smile that Elizabeth had cataloged since London with growing precision: the one that meant *I have put this away where it cannot be seen, and I will keep it there, and if the lock does not hold, that is my concern and not yours.* She had worn it at breakfast in Gracechurch Street while the Gardiners spoke of their summer plans. She had worn it yesterday evening, stepping down from the carriage at Longbourn with her bonnet ribbons straight and her composure immaculate and her eyes not red at all. She wore it now over her uneaten ham, and Elizabeth watched it and felt the sting of knowing exactly who had put that lock in place.

*He separated them because he thought Jane did not care enough. Because he thought the match was beneath his friend. Because he judged her family —*

And had he been wrong?

Elizabeth set down her toast. She had not, she discovered, any appetite either.

As always, Mr. Bennet occupied the far end of the table behind the *Courier*, which he held at an angle suggesting that the news of the nation was of considerably more interest than the news of his household. He had not spoken since sitting down, beyond a grunt that might have been good morning or might have been directed at the coffee. The newspaper formed a wall between himself and the room, a wall he had been constructing daily for as long as Elizabeth could remember, and which the room had learned to navigate around rather than through.

Kitty sat opposite Elizabeth, crumbling a piece of toast into fragments that were growing steadily smaller. She had the look of a kettle approaching the boil, a restless energy in her fingers, a tightness around her mouth, a way of glancing at Mrs. Bennet and then away again that Elizabeth  filed under *Kitty is being Kitty* and thought no more about.

Mary, to Kitty’s left, sat upright, her teacup precisely centered on its saucer. Her face wore the drawn expression of a woman composing a sermon and awaiting the appropriate pause in which to deliver it.

"Lydia is still abed, I suppose," Mrs. Bennet said, not as a question but as a fact she wished to introduce into evidence. "She was out late at Mrs. Forster's again — such a favorite she is with Harriet Forster, you would think they were sisters. So lively! So popular with the regiment. I do not think there is a girl in the neighborhood half so admired. Certainly none of the Lucas girls can touch her for liveliness. When I was her age—"

"Mama."

The word dropped into the room like a stone into still water.

Kitty's voice was not loud. It was not sharp. But it carried the fizzy quality of a person who has been working up to something without knowing it and has arrived at the edge before recognizing the drop. Her toast crumbs lay in a small heap on her plate, demolished beyond any hope of reconstruction.

"Mama, Lydia has been writing to Mr. Wickham."

The *Courier* rustled. Mr. Bennet's page did not turn.

"Writing to — writing letters? To an officer?" Mrs. Bennet's face performed a complicated transit from surprise to calculation. "Well, and what of that? If Harriet Forster allows it, I am sure there can be nothing improper. Mr. Wickham is a very pleasant young man, and Lydia is —"

"She believes herself in love with him."

The sentence landed differently from the first. The first had been a stone. This was a key, turning in a lock that no one at the table had known existed. Elizabeth's hand went still on her teacup.

"In love!" Mrs. Bennet's transit completed itself: she arrived at rapture. "In love with Mr. Wickham! Well, I cannot say I am astonished. He is very handsome, and she has such a way with men. Oh, but this is excellent news! Kitty, why did you not say so before? A match with an officer — not the most advantageous, to be sure, but a uniform is a uniform, and he is so very —"

"Mrs. Bennet." Mr. Bennet's voice came from behind the *Courier*. He had not lowered it. The newspaper remained where it was, a barrier that made his tone — even, clipped, and entirely without amusement — more arresting for being disembodied. "That will do, Mrs. Bennet."

Mrs. Bennet was not done. Mrs. Bennet was constitutionally incapable of being done when instructed to be so; the instruction itself operated as a kind of bellows. "I will not be quiet when my own daughter may be on the verge of an engagement — an officer, Mr. Bennet! — and you sit there behind your paper as though —"

The *Courier* came down.

Elizabeth had seen her father irritated. She had seen him sardonic, dismissive, bored, and — on rare occasions — disappointed in a way that expressed itself as a particularly devastating sentence delivered over the top of his spectacles. She had not often seen him look the way he looked now: at Kitty, with his spectacles pushed up and his face stripped of its usual armor of wit.

"Kitty," he said. He removed his spectacles, folded them, and set them on the table beside his plate — an operation he performed with such deliberate care that it silenced even Mrs. Bennet for the span of three seconds. "Tell me what you know."

Kitty's hands were in her lap, twisting at the napkin she had pulled from the table. She did not look at her mother. She looked at her father, and Elizabeth saw in that look something she had not expected to find there: not defiance, not self-importance, but a plea. *Pay attention to me. I am telling you something that matters.*

"They write to each other. He — he writes to her through Harriet, so the letters do not come to the house. Lydia showed me. She would not stop showing me. She says he has — that he has particular affection for her, and that when the regiment goes to Brighton —" Kitty stopped. Swallowed. Started again. "She says I am jealous and stupid and that I do not understand what it is to be admired."

The last words came out fast and uneven, and Elizabeth understood — with the clarity of a woman who had spent the better part of three days turning over her own failures — that Kitty had not planned this. This was not strategy. This was a girl who had been carrying a secret that was too heavy for her, and whose mother had, at precisely the wrong moment, praised the sister who had pressed that weight upon her.

"Mr. Bennet," Mrs. Bennet began, her voice climbing toward a register that the household knew well and dreaded universally, "if Lydia has an understanding with Mr. Wickham, then I see nothing —"

"There is no understanding." Mr. Bennet's voice was quiet now, which was worse. "There will be no understanding."

The room shifted. Mrs. Bennet drew breath for a response that promised to be operatic. Mary, who had been waiting with visible patience for a silence large enough to accommodate moral instruction, seized the moment.

"Vanity and pride are different things," Mary said, "though the words are often used synonymously. A young woman may be allowed to be proud of her connections to an officer; but vanity in the display of that pride before an attachment is settled —"

"Thank you, Mary," Mr. Bennet said.

"— exposes the family to censure and the young woman herself to —"

"*Thank* you, Mary." 

Elizabeth sat very still.

She should speak. She was Elizabeth — the clever one, the brave one, the one who said what she thought and bore the consequences with a laugh and a lifted chin. She should say something. She should tell her father that he was right, that Brighton was impossible, that Wickham was —

That Wickham was what?

The word she needed was *dangerous*, and she could not say it. To say it was to explain how she knew, and to explain how she knew was to unspool a letter in a man's close hand. And the letter contained a sister's name that was not hers to speak. Georgiana. Just fifteen. A fortune nearly stolen and a reputation nearly destroyed, and the only reason Elizabeth knew any of it was that the man who had written that letter had trusted her with the knowledge in a moment of wounded pride, and she could not — she *would not* — betray that trust, however she might feel about the man himself.

And how did she feel about the man himself?

She picked up her teacup and found it empty.

*I had not known you a month before I felt that you were the last man in the world whom I could ever be prevailed on to marry.*

Had she really said that? She had. She had stood in Mr. Collins's parlor at Hunsford and said those words to his face, and she had meant every one of them. She had been — she could admit this now, in the privacy of her own mind, at this breakfast table where no one was watching her because a larger catastrophe had swallowed the room — she had been *wrong*. 

Not about all of it. Not about his pride, or his cruelty at the assembly, or the wounding formulation of his proposal. But about Wickham, about Jane, about the shape of her own judgment. She had believed herself an excellent judge of character. 

She had been a fool.

Mrs. Bennet had reached the stage of lamentation that Elizabeth privately cataloged as *Act Three,* the one that began with nerves, progressed through the injustice of the entail, and arrived, by a route that defied all logic, at the certainty that she would be turned out of her home to die in a hedgerow if her daughters were not permitted to marry as they chose. 

Mr. Bennet endured two full iterations of the hedgerow. Then he was on his feet, the *Courier* folded under his arm with the finality of a man closing a book he did not intend to reopen, moving toward the door with the set expression that meant the library — the only room in the house with a lock — and a decanter.

"Kitty," he said from the doorway, "you will come to me in the library in  one quarter of an hour. You have done right to speak."

Now Kitty's face did something Elizabeth had never seen it do. It crumpled — not with tears, but with relief so large it had no composure to contain it. She nodded once, hard, and looked down at her plate of toast crumbs.

Jane laid her hand over Kitty's. She did not say anything. She did not need to. It was the same hand she had extended to Elizabeth a hundred times — steady, warm, unbearably generous.  Elizabeth watched it and thought: *I do not deserve you. Neither of us deserves you. And the man who separated you from your happiness thought he was doing the right thing, and I cannot decide whether that makes it better or worse.*

"A secret shared too late is scarcely better than one kept forever," Mary said, reaching for the marmalade. "The damage is done in the interval."

Elizabeth’s gaze locked on Mary, pulled by the awful precision of the words. The way they had landed on the exact bruise that the morning had spent an hour pressing. Mary looked back. Her face held the calm satisfaction of a woman who had found  *le mot juste* and delivered it cleanly and had no notion — not the faintest whisper of a notion — that she had said anything that could wound.

*Wickham.*

Elizabeth's hand tightened on the empty teacup. The porcelain was smooth and cold and told her nothing.

She knew what he was. She could not say what she knew. And sitting in this room full of her family's noise and grief and oblivion, she understood for the first time that knowing the truth and being unable to act on it was its own kind of captivity — and that the man who had given her this knowledge had also, whether he intended it or not, given her the bars.

The door to the breakfast room burst open.

Lydia swept in with her bonnet already on, her pelisse buttoned crooked, and the youthful energy of a girl with news she could not contain for the length of a staircase. She was flushed with exertion and self-importance, and she spoke before she had fully crossed the threshold.

"Mama! The most wonderful thing — Harriet has written — I am invited to Brighton! I am to go to Brighton with the Forsters when the regiment removes! And Wickham, of course. *Brighton!* Is it not the most delicious — why is everyone looking at me like that?"

She stopped. She looked at her mother, who was mid-lamentation with a handkerchief pressed to her temple. She looked at Kitty, whose face was blotched and whose eyes were fixed on the tablecloth. She looked at the empty chair where her father had been.

"What has happened?" Lydia's voice lost its music. "What has Kitty *done*?"
