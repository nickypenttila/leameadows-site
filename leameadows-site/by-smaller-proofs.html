What if Lydia’s trip to Brighton never happened — and Darcy had to prove himself without a dramatic rescue?

When Kitty Bennet speaks before Brighton, the scandal is prevented. But without Lydia’s elopement, Darcy loses the grand proof that might have shown Elizabeth he has changed. At Pemberley, love must be earned by patience, honesty, and smaller acts of character — and Elizabeth must learn that trusting what she sees is harder than knowing she was wrong.

Chapter One — An Eventful Breakfast

The breakfast room at Longbourn was lit by the kind of thin April sunshine that promises nothing and keeps its word — a theater in which Mrs. Bennet performed and Mr. Bennet declined to attend — and Elizabeth Bennet was not listening to her mother.

This was neither new nor remarkable. Mrs. Bennet's morning conversation operated on the same principle as a church bell: it rang at intervals, it carried across distances no one had requested, and it required no response from the congregation beyond the occasional murmur of acknowledgment. Elizabeth had been supplying these murmurs since childhood and could produce them now without the involvement of her higher faculties, which were otherwise engaged.

They had come home yesterday evening, she and Jane together, after three days at the Gardiners' in Gracechurch Street. Before that had been the journey from Hunsford — the roads dry, Maria Lucas talking the whole way to London with a cheerful vigor that required nothing of Elizabeth beyond a traveling companion's face turned in her direction. Elizabeth had been grateful for this. She had needed the hours. She had needed the motion of the carriage and the hedgerows sliding past the window and the long, flat stretches of road where there was nothing to look at and therefore nothing to prevent her from looking, again and again, at the contents of her own mind.

The situation of your mother's family, though objectionable, was nothing in comparison of that total want of propriety —

She reached for the butter dish.

— so frequently, so almost uniformly betrayed by herself, by your three younger sisters, and occasionally even by your father —

The butter knife was cold in her hand. She applied it to her toast with the careful attention of a woman lettering an address she did not wish to smudge.

"— and I said to Mrs. Long, I said, there is no point in despairing, for where there are officers there is always hope, and where there is hope there is — Jane, you are not eating."

Jane looked up from her plate. She had been arranging a piece of ham with the fork in a pattern that suggested occupation without appetite. "I am eating, Mama. I am only a little slow this morning."

"You are pale. You have been pale since London. I do not understand why London should make a girl pale when there are so many shops and so much to see, but there it is. You must take more air. Fresh air and a new bonnet — that is my prescription for paleness."

Jane smiled. It was the smile that Elizabeth had cataloged since London with growing precision: the one that meant I have put this away where it cannot be seen, and I will keep it there, and if the lock does not hold, that is my concern and not yours. She had worn it at breakfast in Gracechurch Street while the Gardiners spoke of their summer plans. She had worn it yesterday evening, stepping down from the carriage at Longbourn with her bonnet ribbons straight and her composure immaculate and her eyes not red at all. She wore it now over her uneaten ham, and Elizabeth watched it and felt the sting of knowing exactly who had put that lock in place.

He separated them because he thought Jane did not care enough. Because he thought the match was beneath his friend. Because he judged her family —

And had he been wrong?

Elizabeth set down her toast. She had not, she discovered, any appetite either.

As always, Mr. Bennet occupied the far end of the table behind the Courier, which he held at an angle suggesting that the news of the nation was of considerably more interest than the news of his household. He had not spoken since sitting down, beyond a grunt that might have been good morning or might have been directed at the coffee. The newspaper formed a wall between himself and the room, a wall he had been constructing daily for as long as Elizabeth could remember, and which the room had learned to navigate around rather than through.

Kitty sat opposite Elizabeth, crumbling a piece of toast into fragments that were growing steadily smaller. She had the look of a kettle approaching the boil, a restless energy in her fingers, a tightness around her mouth, a way of glancing at Mrs. Bennet and then away again that Elizabeth filed under Kitty is being Kitty and thought no more about.

Mary, to Kitty's left, sat upright, her teacup precisely centered on its saucer. Her face wore the drawn expression of a woman composing a sermon and awaiting the appropriate pause in which to deliver it.

"Lydia is still abed, I suppose," Mrs. Bennet said, not as a question but as a fact she wished to introduce into evidence. "She was out late at Mrs. Forster's again — such a favorite she is with Harriet Forster, you would think they were sisters. So lively! So popular with the regiment. I do not think there is a girl in the neighborhood half so admired. Certainly none of the Lucas girls can touch her for liveliness. When I was her age—"

"Mama."

The word dropped into the room like a stone into still water.

Kitty's voice was not loud. It was not sharp. But it carried the fizzy quality of a person who has been working up to something without knowing it and has arrived at the edge before recognizing the drop. Her toast crumbs lay in a small heap on her plate, demolished beyond any hope of reconstruction.

"Mama, Lydia has been writing to Mr. Wickham."

The Courier rustled. Mr. Bennet's page did not turn.

"Writing to — writing letters? To an officer?" Mrs. Bennet's face performed a complicated transit from surprise to calculation. "Well, and what of that? If Harriet Forster allows it, I am sure there can be nothing improper. Mr. Wickham is a very pleasant young man, and Lydia is —"

"She believes herself in love with him."

The sentence landed differently from the first. The first had been a stone. This was a key, turning in a lock that no one at the table had known existed. Elizabeth's hand went still on her teacup.

"In love!" Mrs. Bennet's transit completed itself: she arrived at rapture. "In love with Mr. Wickham! Well, I cannot say I am astonished. He is very handsome, and she has such a way with men. Oh, but this is excellent news! Kitty, why did you not say so before? A match with an officer — not the most advantageous, to be sure, but a uniform is a uniform, and he is so very —"

"Mrs. Bennet." Mr. Bennet's voice came from behind the Courier. He had not lowered it. The newspaper remained where it was, a barrier that made his tone — even, clipped, and entirely without amusement — more arresting for being disembodied. "That will do, Mrs. Bennet."

Mrs. Bennet was not done. Mrs. Bennet was constitutionally incapable of being done when instructed to be so; the instruction itself operated as a kind of bellows. "I will not be quiet when my own daughter may be on the verge of an engagement — an officer, Mr. Bennet! — and you sit there behind your paper as though —"

The Courier came down.

Elizabeth had seen her father irritated. She had seen him sardonic, dismissive, bored, and — on rare occasions — disappointed in a way that expressed itself as a withering sentence delivered over the top of his spectacles. She had not often seen him look the way he looked now: at Kitty, with his spectacles pushed up and his face stripped of its usual armor of wit.

"Kitty," he said. He removed his spectacles, folded them, and set them on the table beside his plate — an operation he performed with such deliberate care that it silenced even Mrs. Bennet for the span of three seconds. "Tell me what you know."

Kitty's hands were in her lap, twisting at the napkin she had pulled from the table. She did not look at her mother. She looked at her father, and Elizabeth saw in that look something she had not expected to find there: not defiance, not self-importance, but a plea. Pay attention to me. I am telling you something that matters.

"They write to each other. He — he writes to her through Harriet, so the letters do not come to the house. Lydia showed me. She would not stop showing me. She says he has — that he has particular affection for her, and that when the regiment goes to Brighton —" Kitty stopped. Swallowed. Started again. "She says I am jealous and stupid and that I do not understand what it is to be admired."

The last words came out fast and uneven, and Elizabeth understood — with the clarity of a woman who had spent the better part of three days turning over her own failures — that Kitty had not planned this. This was not strategy. This was a girl who had been carrying a secret that was too heavy for her, and whose mother had, at precisely the wrong moment, praised the sister who had pressed that weight upon her.

"Mr. Bennet," Mrs. Bennet began, her voice climbing toward a register that the household knew well and dreaded universally, "if Lydia has an understanding with Mr. Wickham, then I see nothing —"

"There is no understanding." Mr. Bennet's voice was quiet now, which was worse. "There will be no understanding."

The room shifted. Mrs. Bennet drew breath for a response that promised to be operatic. Mary, who had been waiting with visible patience for a silence large enough to accommodate moral instruction, seized the moment.

"Vanity and pride are different things," Mary said, "though the words are often used synonymously. A young woman may be allowed to be proud of her connections to an officer; but vanity in the display of that pride before an attachment is settled —"

"Thank you, Mary," Mr. Bennet said.

"— exposes the family to censure and the young woman herself to —"

"Thank you, Mary."

Elizabeth sat very still.

She should speak. She was Elizabeth — the clever one, the brave one, the one who said what she thought and bore the consequences with a laugh and a lifted chin. She should say something. She should tell her father that he was right, that Brighton was impossible, that Wickham was —

That Wickham was what?

The word she needed was dangerous, and she could not say it. To say it was to explain how she knew, and to explain how she knew was to unspool a letter in a man's close hand. And the letter contained a sister's name that was not hers to speak. Georgiana. Just fifteen. A fortune nearly stolen and a reputation nearly destroyed, and the only reason Elizabeth knew any of it was that the man who had written that letter had trusted her with the knowledge in a moment of wounded pride, and she could not — she would not — betray that trust, however she might feel about the man himself.

And how did she feel about the man himself?

She picked up her teacup and found it empty.

I had not known you a month before I felt that you were the last man in the world whom I could ever be prevailed on to marry.

Had she really said that? She had. She had stood in Mr. Collins's parlor at Hunsford and said those words to his face, and she had meant every one of them. She had been — she could admit this now, in the privacy of her own mind, at this breakfast table where no one was watching her because a larger catastrophe had swallowed the room — she had been wrong.

Not about all of it. Not about his pride, or his cruelty at the assembly, or the wounding formulation of his proposal. But about Wickham, about Jane, about the shape of her own judgment. She had believed herself an excellent judge of character.

She had been a fool.

Mrs. Bennet had reached the stage of lamentation that Elizabeth privately cataloged as Act Three, the one that began with nerves, progressed through the injustice of the entail, and arrived, by a route that defied all logic, at the certainty that she would be turned out of her home to die in a hedgerow if her daughters were not permitted to marry as they chose.

Mr. Bennet endured two full iterations of the hedgerow. Then he was on his feet, the Courier folded under his arm with the finality of a man closing a book he did not intend to reopen, moving toward the door with the set expression that meant the library — the only room in the house with a lock — and a decanter.

"Kitty," he said from the doorway, "you will come to me in the library in one quarter of an hour. You have done right to speak."

Now Kitty's face did something Elizabeth had never seen it do. It crumpled — not with tears, but with relief so large it had no composure to contain it. She nodded once, hard, and looked down at her plate of toast crumbs.

Jane laid her hand over Kitty's. She did not say anything. She did not need to. It was the same hand she had extended to Elizabeth a hundred times — steady, warm, unbearably generous. Elizabeth watched it and thought: I do not deserve you. Neither of us deserves you. And the man who separated you from your happiness thought he was doing the right thing, and I cannot decide whether that makes it better or worse.

"A secret shared too late is scarcely better than one kept forever," Mary said, reaching for the marmalade. "The damage is done in the interval."

Elizabeth's gaze locked on Mary, pulled by the awful precision of the words. The way they had landed on the exact bruise that the morning had spent an hour pressing. Mary looked back. Her face held the calm satisfaction of a woman who had found le mot juste and delivered it cleanly and had no notion — not the faintest whisper of a notion — that she had said anything that could wound.

Wickham.

Elizabeth's hand tightened on the empty teacup. The porcelain was smooth and cold and told her nothing.

She knew what he was. She could not say what she knew. And sitting in this room full of her family's noise and grief and oblivion, she understood for the first time that knowing the truth and being unable to act on it was its own kind of captivity — and that the man who had given her this knowledge had also, whether he intended it or not, given her the bars.

The door to the breakfast room burst open.

Lydia swept in with her bonnet already on, her pelisse buttoned crooked, and the buoyancy of a girl with news she could not contain for the length of a staircase. She was flushed with exertion and self-importance, and she spoke before she had fully crossed the threshold.

"Mama! The most wonderful thing — Harriet has written — I am invited to Brighton! I am to go to Brighton with the Forsters when the regiment removes! And Wickham, of course. Brighton! Is it not the most delicious — why is everyone looking at me like that?"

She stopped. She looked at her mother, who was mid-lamentation with a handkerchief pressed to her temple. She looked at Kitty, whose face was blotched and whose eyes were fixed on the tablecloth. She looked at the empty chair where her father had been.

"What has happened?" Lydia's voice lost its music. "What has Kitty done?"

Chapter Two — The Siege of Longbourn

The shrieking began at half past nine.

Elizabeth was in the morning room with Jane and her embroidery. Or rather, with her embroidery frame balanced on her lap and her needle making no progress whatsoever, because she had not set a stitch since sitting down and had no immediate plans to begin. The morning room faced east, and the April light came in thin and provisional through the tall windows, laying pale squares on the carpet that would shift and shrink as the hours passed. The fire had not yet been built up; the grate held last night's ash and a chill that the sunshine could not quite reach.

She had chosen the room for its distance from the rest of the household, its view of the garden where nothing of consequence had ever happened, and the fragile hope that she might pass thirty minutes without the contents of a certain letter arranging themselves behind her eyes. She had achieved none of these aims. But the morning room was quiet, and Jane was beside her stitching away, the silk whispering through the linen in small, regular strokes, and for a quarter of an hour, at least, this had been enough.

Then came Lydia.

Not Lydia herself — not yet — but the sound of Lydia, which always preceded her person by some margin, in the same way that thunder can precede the rain. The cry began somewhere upstairs, gathered force along the corridor, and arrived at the first floor landing as a single, sustained note of operatic outrage.

"I will not! I will not stay here! Papa cannot — Mama! Mama!"

Jane looked up, toward the doorway. Elizabeth looked down, pinning her needle into the fabric. Neither of them moved.

The next ten minutes proceeded with the inevitability of a natural disaster observed from the road. Lydia appeared in the morning room doorway, her face blotched and her fists at her sides and her voice at a volume that caused the window casements to vibrate in sympathy. Kitty followed at her heels, drawn or dragged — it was not clear which — her face carrying the hunted look of a girl who knows the storm is about to turn on her. Their mother came from the other direction, most likely the kitchen, still holding a ring of household keys that jingled with each agitated step.

The facts, as Elizabeth pieced them together over the next quarter of an hour, were these: Mr. Bennet had spent his promised fifteen minutes with Kitty in the library, had emerged long enough to send Hill upstairs with a message for Lydia — Your father wishes you to know that you will not be going to Brighton, and that no further discussion is required — and had then locked the door behind him. Lydia, receiving this intelligence from Hill while still in her dressing gown, had dressed with a speed she normally reserved for balls, descended the stairs in a fury, found the library door immovable, and turned her campaign upon the rest of the household.

She produced an argument anyway.

It was, in its way, magnificent. Lydia mounted her case with the passionate incoherence of a barrister who has not read the brief but feels strongly that justice is on her side. Brighton was promised. Harriet was expecting her. The regiment would think her a nobody. She would be the only girl in Hertfordshire — in England — who had ever been denied a trip to the seaside by a father who cared nothing for her happiness and a sister — here she wheeled on Kitty, who had been trying to make herself invisible behind the curtain — a jealous, spiteful, stupid sister who had ruined everything because she could not bear to see anyone else admired.

Kitty, who had been brave once that morning and had used up the whole of her supply, burst into tears.

"Oh, do not cry," Lydia said, with the contempt of a person who had been crying without interruption for twenty minutes and considered it her exclusive privilege. "You have got what you wanted. I hope you are satisfied."

Kitty was not satisfied. Kitty was red-eyed and shaking and pressed against the curtain as though she wished the fabric would swallow her. Jane rose and went to her. Elizabeth did not. Her hands had gone still in her lap, and the words came anyway.

He had called them wanting in propriety. He had written it in his own hand and she had read the words in the lane at Hunsford and she had been so angry — so blisteringly, righteously angry — that she had walked for an hour without seeing the hedgerows. He had called her family improper, unguarded, lacking in every social grace that could recommend them to the world, and she had thought him proud and cruel and blind.

And here was Lydia, who was now informing the morning room at large that she would speak to her father herself, that no locked door in Christendom could prevent her, that she would make him listen.

And here was Mrs. Bennet, who had arrived in the doorway already in a state of high agitation and a lace cap that had come unpinned on one side, and who was already promising Lydia that she would go to Mr. Bennet herself, that she would reason with him, that a mother's feelings must carry some weight even in a household governed by a man who had no heart.

He had not been blind. He had been, if anything, mercilessly precise.

Mrs. Bennet's embassy to the library door was conducted with all the tactical subtlety of a cavalry charge. Elizabeth heard it from the morning room — the knock, the entreaty, the invocation of nerves, the rising complaint about daughters who were denied their natural rights, the observation that Mrs. Long's daughters had been to Ramsgate twice, and the final, immovable silence from the other side of the door that indicated Mr. Bennet was in no danger of being moved.

Lydia had claimed the fainting couch, displacing Kitty to its farthest end without troubling to disguise the shove, and was producing groans of despair at a volume suggesting she wished them to carry through the library door.

Mrs. Bennet returned with the air of a diplomat who has been refused at the gates and wishes everyone to understand that the fault lies entirely with the gates.

"He will not listen," she announced. "He never listens. Twenty-three years I have been married to that man and he has not listened to a single word I have said — not one! — and now poor Lydia is to be punished for her sister's jealousy, and I am to have no say in the matter, and my nerves — my nerves— you cannot imagine what this is doing to my nerves."

Elizabeth could, in fact, imagine with considerable precision.

The morning room had grown close. Too many bodies, too many tears, the windows shut against the April wind, and Mrs. Bennet's lavender water hanging in the air like a reproach. The pale squares of light on the carpet had crept halfway across the floor by the time Elizabeth escaped to the hallway, where the air was cooler and carried only the distant sound of Mary practicing her arpeggios with grim determination.

By noon, Lydia had adopted a new strategy.

"I shall not eat," she declared, positioning herself in the dining room chair with the posture of a martyr preparing for the stake. "I shall not eat a single bite until Papa relents. You may tell him so. You may tell him I shall waste away entirely, and when I am dead he may put Here lies Lydia Bennet, who was denied Brighton on the stone, and I hope he will be sorry."

"Oh, Lydia!" Mrs. Bennet pressed her handkerchief to her eyes. "Mr. Bennet, do you hear? She will not eat! She will starve herself, and I shall have to bury my own daughter, and all because —"

Mr. Bennet was not present to hear. Mr. Bennet had not emerged from the library since nine o'clock. A tray of cold meat and bread had been sent in at eleven; Hill had reported that the door opened precisely wide enough to admit the tray and closed again before any human face could follow it. This intelligence Mrs. Bennet received as further evidence of her husband's depravity.

The hunger strike held through the first course with admirable commitment. It held through the laying of the cold chicken with visible effort. It did not survive the arrival of Hill's stewed apples — the smell of them warm and brown-sugared, filling the dining room with the warm sweetness of the Longbourn kitchen — which Lydia had loved since childhood and which reached her with the merciless timing of an army flanking a weakened position. The first spoonful was taken in defiant silence. The second was taken with less defiance. By the third, the strike had been abandoned with no formal announcement of its conclusion, and Lydia had moved on to her next campaign: the letter.

She wrote to Harriet Forster at the dining table, in a hand that Elizabeth — passing behind her chair on the way to the sideboard — could not help but observe. Every third word was underlined. Exclamation marks appeared in clusters. The letter appeared to contain, in its first paragraph alone, the words cruel, unjust, Papa, impossible, and what looked very much like prison.

"Kitty." Lydia did not look up from her composition. "Kitty, bring me the sealing wax."

Kitty, who had been eating her dinner in the careful silence of a person hoping to be overlooked, set down her fork. "It is in the writing desk. In the —"

"I know where it is. I asked you to bring it. Since you have taken everything else from me, you may at least make yourself useful."

Kitty's eyes filled. She pushed back her chair and left the room, and Elizabeth heard her footsteps on the stairs — quick, uneven, with the speed of someone retreating before the tears could arrive in company.

"Lydia," Elizabeth said.

"What?"

Elizabeth opened her mouth. What she wanted to say was this: You are behaving exactly as he described. Every word he wrote about this family is being staged for my particular edification, and I cannot bear it, and I cannot say why, and the man whose judgment I despised has turned out to be a better observer of my own household than I have ever been.

What she said was: "The sealing wax is in the left drawer."

Mary, who had been waiting with her customary patience for a silence large enough to accommodate her, found one.

"The observation of the individual is a lesser duty than the governance of the family," she said. "When parents are unable or unwilling to check the follies of a child —"

"Nobody asked you, Mary," Lydia said.

Mary took a measured sip of her water and returned to her plate with the composure of a woman accustomed to preaching into the wind and finding the acoustics perfectly adequate.

Elizabeth looked at her father's empty chair. The dining room was warm and airless, the windows fogged at their lower edges, the remains of dinner growing cold on plates no one had troubled to clear. Afternoon light pressed flat and white against the glass. He had written occasionally even by your father. She thought of the library door, shut against the household's noise, and the decanter on the second shelf, and Mr. Bennet behind his wall of books and silence, governing nothing, correcting nothing, retreating from the disorder he had permitted to flourish for sixteen years. He had called it a want of propriety. She might have called it something less polite, had she been writing the letter. But she had not written the letter. He had. And he had not been wrong about a single word.

By evening, the house had the exhausted quiet of a field after the armies have withdrawn.

Lydia had retreated upstairs to compose, Elizabeth suspected, a second letter to Harriet and possibly a third to the other officers' wives, detailing her persecution with the industry of a war correspondent. Mrs. Bennet had retired to her room with her salts and a grievance that showed no signs of diminishing before morning. Mary was practicing scales on the pianoforte in the back parlor — the same four bars, repeated with a mechanical precision that suggested penance rather than pleasure. Kitty was somewhere. Elizabeth was ashamed not to know where, and doubly ashamed that this was the first time all day she had given Kitty a thought uncomplicated by her own distress.

The drawing room was empty. The fire had burned low to a red line along the last log, giving off a dry, papery heat that did not reach the corners of the room. The April light was fading in the windows — long amber slants that caught the dust still suspended in the air from the day's commotion — and the room had the particular stillness that comes when too many people have been shouting in it and have finally stopped. It smelled of cold tea and spent candle wax and, faintly, of Mrs. Bennet's lavender water, which had soaked into the furnishings like smoke.

Jane was there, a ghost.

She sat by the window with her hands folded in her lap and her face turned toward the garden, and when Elizabeth came in she looked up with an expression that was not quite a smile but was, Elizabeth thought, the nearest Jane could manage these days.

"Shall we walk?" Jane said.

It was not a question of exercise. Elizabeth knew it and Jane knew it, and neither of them needed to say so. It was the same offer Jane had been making since they were girls, the quiet provision of company without the demand for explanation, the open door that could be walked through or left alone as Elizabeth chose.

"Yes," Elizabeth said. "Please."

They took the lane behind the garden, the one that led past the meadow and toward the copse where they had played as children and confided as young women and where the hedgerows, at least, had the decency not to offer their opinions.

The first breath of outside air hit Elizabeth like cold water. After an evening sealed indoors with Lydia's grief and Mrs. Bennet's lavender and the close, overheated rooms, the evening was a shock of green and wet earth and the mineral coolness of air that had been moving freely across open fields. A blackbird was singing from the top of the hawthorn by the gate — a liquid, unhurried sound that knew nothing of Brighton or Wickham or locked library doors. The gravel crunched under their boots. The meadow grass, still damp from the morning, darkened the hems of their skirts. The light was the thin gold of a spring evening that has not yet decided whether it is finished, and it lay across the lane in long bars that stretched and shifted as the sun dropped toward the treeline.

Jane walked beside her without speaking, and for the first hundred yards Elizabeth let the silence and the open air do their work.

Was it a punishment, to see your family through a stranger's eyes and find the portrait accurate? Or was it something else — unwanted, unwelcome, delivered without tenderness — but honest? And did the man who had forced that clarity upon her know what he had done, or had it been merely the excess of his wounded pride, spilling truths he had no right to offer and no expectation she would keep?

She did not know. She was not certain he had meant it as anything at all.

"Lizzy," Jane said, after they had passed the meadow gate and the house had fallen out of sight behind them. "You have been very quiet today."

"It has been a loud day. I thought someone ought to balance the account."

Jane's mouth curved. Not quite a laugh — Jane did not laugh at her family's distress, it was not in her composition — but an acknowledgment that the observation was fair.

"You are troubled," Jane said. "And not only by Lydia."

Elizabeth looked at her sister. Dear, steady Jane, who had carried her own heartbreak back from London like a parcel she refused to unwrap in public, who smiled her careful smile over uneaten ham, who asked shall we walk instead of what is wrong because she understood that some questions are better put by offering your company than your curiosity.

"I am thinking," Elizabeth said carefully, "about how well one can know the people one has lived with all one's life, and how badly one can judge the people one has only just met."

Jane considered this. "That sounds like the beginning of a very long walk."

"It may be."

Jane took her arm. "Then we had best keep going."

Chapter Three — An Errand to Meryton

The walk to Meryton was Jane's idea, and like most of Jane's ideas it arrived so wrapped in quiet reasonableness that Elizabeth had agreed before thinking to examine why.

"I ought to post my letter to Aunt Gardiner," Jane said, standing in the hallway with her bonnet already in hand and her pelisse buttoned, as though the decision had been made some time ago and she was merely informing Elizabeth of its existence. "And the morning is fine. Will you come?"

The morning was, in fact, fine — aggressively, unhelpfully fine, the kind of April morning that insists on warmth and birdsong and a sky of flawless blue when one would rather it had the decency to rain. Elizabeth had spent the past three days inside Longbourn's walls, and Longbourn's walls had begun to feel like the interior of a clock: the same mechanisms turning at the same intervals, producing the same sounds. Lydia wept. Mrs. Bennet lamented. The library door stayed shut. The household revolved around the absence of Brighton with a grim constancy that would have been admirable had it been directed toward any useful purpose.

"Yes," Elizabeth said. "Gladly."

Kitty came too — not gladly, but with the restless need of a girl who had been trapped indoors with a sister who blamed her for everything and a mother who had not yet forgiven the messenger. She said little on the lane, walking a half-step behind Elizabeth and Jane with her shawl pulled close despite the warmth, and Elizabeth thought: the cost of courage, it seemed, did not end with the spending of it.

The road to Meryton ran between hedgerows just coming into leaf — hawthorn and blackthorn, white blossom caught in the branches like scraps of linen snagged on a line. The air smelled of turned earth from the fields beyond, and of something sweeter beneath it, May blossom or early honeysuckle. Elizabeth had enjoyed the fragrance of a Hertfordshire spring since childhood, but today it carried an unwelcome sharpness. A cart passed them going the other direction, the driver touching his hat. Birdsong threaded through the hedgerows — a wren, insistent and close, and further off a cuckoo repeating its two notes with the self-satisfaction of a bird that has never been contradicted.

They heard Meryton before they saw it.

The high street, on an ordinary morning, held its ordinary traffic: the butcher's boy, the postman, a scattering of women and servants on their errands, and perhaps two or three officers strolling between the draper's and the coffeehouse with the unhurried air of men whose chief occupation was being admired. But today the street had the disordered energy of a household packing to move. Wagons stood at angles outside the inn, half loaded with trunks and equipment cases. A pair of soldiers carried a rolled tent between them with the careful coordination of men who had done this before and resented doing it again. Orders were being given somewhere — Elizabeth could hear the bark of a sergeant's voice from behind the assembly rooms — and the whole scene had the dusty, restless air of a place about to lose its reason for notice.

The regiment was leaving. Elizabeth had known this in the abstract — the removal to Brighton had been the fixed point of Lydia's universe for weeks — but seeing the physical evidence of departure on the high street made it real in a way that Lydia's tantrums had not. The officers would go. The red coats would disappear from Meryton's lanes and shop doors and evening parties. All would return to dreary dullness, as Lydia put it. What was leaving would not be coming back — not this regiment, not these officers, not quite this arrangement of a town.

And with them would go Mr. Wickham.

Elizabeth had not allowed herself to think about encountering him again.

That was not quite true. She had thought of little else for three days, in the way that one thinks about an approaching interview with the tooth-drawer: with the steady, low-grade dread of a person who knows the appointment is necessary and wishes profoundly that it were not. She had rehearsed her face. She had rehearsed her voice. She had reminded herself, with the stern internal discipline of a governess addressing a particularly willful charge, that she knew exactly what Mr. Wickham was and that this knowledge, however painful, was also armor.

She had told herself she was ready.

Jane posted her letter at the receiving office with the quiet efficiency of a woman who completes tasks while others are still considering them. Elizabeth waited outside with Kitty, her eyes on the street. A dog slept in a patch of sun outside the saddler's. The smell of fresh bread reached her from the bakery two doors down — warm, yeasty, indifferent to crisis.

Then Kitty's hand closed on her arm.

Elizabeth followed her sister's gaze up the high street and saw them at once. Mrs. Forster, in a walking dress of sprigged muslin and a bonnet trimmed with silk flowers that nodded with each step, coming down from the direction of the draper's with two bandboxes and the animated expression of a woman who has been shopping with great success. Beside her, carrying a third bandbox and a paper-wrapped parcel with the easy grace of a man performing a favor he wished to be noticed, walked Mr. Wickham.

He looked well. He always looked well. That was the machinery of it, Elizabeth saw with a clarity that sat in her chest like a swallowed stone. The uniform sat perfectly. The smile was already in place — not the full smile, not yet, but the preliminary version, the one that warmed his features into an expression of open, uncomplicated friendliness before he had any reason to be friendly. She had once found that smile charming. She had once thought it the natural overflow of an amiable disposition. He shifted Mrs. Forster's parcel to his other hand so that he could touch his hat to a passing shopkeeper's wife, and she saw the gesture for what it was: a performance so practiced that the performer no longer distinguished it from sincerity.

He told his story with such an air of openness.

The words from the letter arrived without invitation, as they had been arriving for days — at breakfast, in the garden, in the dark of her bedroom ceiling — inserting themselves between Elizabeth and the world like a pane of tinted glass she could not remove.

Denny was with them, a few steps behind, talking to a younger officer Elizabeth did not recognize. He spotted the Bennet sisters first and raised a hand in greeting. The gesture caught Wickham's attention. As Wickham spotted them, his smile completed itself.

"Miss Bennet! Miss Elizabeth! What a welcome sight on a morning of dust and drudgery." He shifted the bandboxes with theatrical resignation. "As you see, I have been pressed into service. Mrs. Forster's shopping will not carry itself, and the colonel has abandoned me to my fate."

Mrs. Forster laughed — a bright, silly laugh that reminded Elizabeth, with a pang she had not anticipated, of Lydia. "Oh, Mr. Wickham is too obliging. I told him I could manage perfectly well, but he would insist."

"I am a man of deep principle," Wickham said. "Chief among them: never allow a lady to carry her own bandboxes when an officer with two free hands is available."

Jane smiled at this with the serene goodwill she extended to all the world. "Good morning, Mr. Wickham. Mrs. Forster — how nice to see you."

Jane curtseyed to Wickham, and the sting of it caught Elizabeth like vinegar on a paper cut — small, precise, and entirely private. He did not deserve it. But Jane's kindness was indiscriminate — it fell on the worthy and the unworthy with the same gentle constancy, like rain. Elizabeth had always admired this quality, had always considered it the purest expression of her sister's character.

She did not admire it now. She envied it. Jane could be kind to Wickham because Jane did not know what Elizabeth knew, and ignorance, in this instance, was not merely bliss but a species of freedom that Elizabeth would have traded half her wit to possess.

"But where is Lydia?" Mrs. Forster looked past them down the street, as though Lydia might be trailing behind at her customary delay. "I have not seen her these three days. She has not answered my letter. I was beginning to think she had been carried off by pirates — though I told Colonel Forster, the pirates would bring her back within the hour, for Lydia would exhaust even a pirate's patience."

She laughed at her own joke. Kitty did not laugh. Kitty had gone pale — the color leaving her face as visibly as water draining from a basin. Her hand on Elizabeth's arm tightened.

"Lydia is at home," Jane said, with a smoothness that betrayed no effort. "She has been a little unwell. Nothing alarming — merely a headache that has kept her indoors. She will be sorry to have missed you."

The lie was so clean, so perfectly shaped to the occasion, that Elizabeth felt a complicated admiration. Jane had not planned it. Jane did not plan lies; she simply produced the kindest available version of reality and delivered it with such conviction that it became, for the duration of the conversation, true.

"Oh, poor Lydia!" Mrs. Forster pressed a gloved hand to her collarbone. "She will be devastated to miss the packing — she did so want to see the regiment off. You must tell her I asked after her."

"What a pity," Wickham said. He was looking directly at Elizabeth. Not at Jane, not at Kitty, not at the street. "I had hoped for the pleasure of your company before we depart, Miss Elizabeth. We have had so little opportunity to talk since your return from Kent."

The word Kent landed on the conversation like a coin on a counter. Elizabeth heard it ring.

"Kent was uneventful," she said. "Mr. and Mrs. Collins are very well. Lady Catherine is in excellent health and excellent voice. I believe that covers the substance of my visit."

Wickham's smile did not falter. That was the thing about his smile; it had the resilience of a well-engineered structure, built to absorb shocks and redistribute the weight. "And did you encounter anyone else of note at Rosings? I understand Mr. Darcy is often there at Easter. A great favorite of Lady Catherine's, as I am sure you know."

Georgiana. Ramsgate. Thirty thousand pounds and a girl of fifteen.

The words pressed against the backs of Elizabeth's teeth. She swallowed them.

"Yes, Mr. Darcy was there," she said. "We exchanged the usual civilities. I found the park very fine."

"The park," Wickham repeated, with the faintest emphasis — an emphasis so slight that only someone listening for it would have caught it. "Yes, Pemberley's grounds are said to be superior, but Rosings has its merits. And Darcy? Was he his usual charming self?"

The irony was laid on like varnish — thin, smooth, inviting her to share it. A month ago she would have laughed. A month ago she would have met his eye and said something clever about Darcy's deficiencies and felt the warm complicity of a shared joke. And she would have been wrong, and she would not have known it.

"Mr. Darcy was perfectly civil," Elizabeth said. "I have no complaint."

Something moved behind Wickham's eyes. A recalculation. Elizabeth watched it happening: the rapid, invisible adjustment of a man who has expected one response and received another and is now determining what the discrepancy means. She had always spoken freely about Mr. Darcy. She had always been willing — eager, even — to hear and echo Wickham's version of Mr. Darcy's character. And now she had called him perfectly civil and offered no complaint, and the flatness of these words, their careful neutrality, was louder than any criticism.

"Well," Wickham said lightly. "I am glad to hear it. Darcy can be — when he chooses — capable of tolerably good manners. Though one wonders what prompts the choice."

Elizabeth said nothing. The silence was a mistake — she knew it even as it formed, knew that her old self would have filled it with something pointed and witty, would have risen to the bait, but she could not produce the words. Every clever remark she might have made about Mr. Darcy now tasted of the lane at Hunsford and the mortifying collapse of her own judgment.

Wickham let the silence sit almost to the point that the others noticed. Then he turned his attention — easily, smoothly, with the practiced pivot of a man accustomed to steering conversations toward more agreeable ground — to Kitty.

"And Miss Kitty, you are very quiet today. I hope you are well?"

Kitty made a sound that might have been affirmation. Her arm was now pressed against Elizabeth's. She had not looked directly at Wickham since his approach, and she did not look at him now. Her gaze was fixed on the paving stones with the desperate concentration of a girl attempting to disappear into the ground by force of will.

"She is quite well," Jane said, with a warmth that covered Kitty's silence like a blanket laid over a shivering child.

"And Miss Lydia not with you? I had half expected to see her." Wickham's tone was light, conversational, and Elizabeth heard beneath it a thread of specific interest that had nothing to do with headaches.

He shifted one of Mrs. Forster's bandboxes higher on his arm and turned to Jane. "Pray remember me to Miss Lydia," he said, with the easy grace of any officer taking leave of a family; and then, as an afterthought too neatly placed to be one: "She was always very kind to me."

Elizabeth heard her own voice before she had decided to use it. "I am sure she thought of you as one of the family."

It was the past tense that was wrong. Thought. She had meant thinks, and some truer self had intervened to correct her; and Wickham, who had been smiling at Jane, now turned the smile upon Elizabeth and let it rest there a moment longer than smiles ordinarily rested.

"Oh, but you must let me carry her a word, Miss Bennet —" Mrs. Forster broke in brightly, her bandbox hitched against her hip. "I shall write to her directly we are back, and she will think herself forgotten if none of you send anything. A line only."

Jane smiled, and her smile was very kind, and very composed, and very slightly late. "You are good to think of her. But Lydia is resting, Mrs. Forster — best not, I think."

Best not. Elizabeth had never heard Jane refuse a kindness with those two words. She did not look at her sister. She did not need to.

"Well, you must come this evening at any rate," Mrs. Forster went on, undeflected. "Colonel Forster insists on a farewell party before we leave for Brighton — we shall be a small, merry company. The Harringtons, of course, and Captain Carter, and Miss King — who grows prettier by the fortnight, I declare. Ten thousand pounds will do wonders for a young woman's complexion."

It was the smallest thing. Wickham, who had been smiling still, did not stop smiling. But something behind his eyes fixed, briefly, upon the name, and then released it, and then smiled on.

Elizabeth felt Kitty move at her elbow — a small, instinctive half-step back, as though from a hot thing on a stove — and Kitty did not look at Wickham again after that.

"You are very kind," Jane said of the invitation, with the same serene grace. "I am afraid we cannot. Our father has particular plans for the family this week, and we are not at liberty. But we are grateful for the invitation, and please give our regards to Colonel Forster."

Mrs. Forster accepted with a pout that did not quite conceal her relief at having fewer places to set.

Another soldier called to Wickham from up the street — something about the quartermaster and a missing inventory — and Wickham shifted the bandboxes with a rueful shrug.

"Duty calls, I am afraid. Mrs. Forster, shall I leave these at the inn?" He turned to the sisters with a bow that included all three of them and excluded none and that Elizabeth recognized, with a specificity that made her jaw ache, as the exact bow he had given her on the evening they first met in Meryton. The bow that said I am the most agreeable man in this room and I have chosen to direct that quality at you. "Miss Bennet. Miss Elizabeth. Miss Kitty. I hope we shall meet again before we must leave you."

He walked away up the high street with Mrs. Forster's parcels balanced on one arm and Denny falling into step beside him. The dust from a passing wagon rose around his boots and settled on the hem of his coat. He said something to Denny that made Denny laugh.

He looked back.

Just once — a glance over his shoulder as he reached the corner by the saddler's, so brief that Elizabeth might have missed it had she not been watching. It was not the look of a man bidding farewell. It was not the look of a man admiring a woman he had once flattered. It was the look of a man reading a card table: quick, careful, marking what he had observed and keeping it for later use. His face held the same easy pleasantness it always held. But his eyes, in that half-second before he turned the corner, were the eyes of a man who had noticed a lock where there used to be an open door, and who was already considering what that meant.

Five minutes. It had taken five minutes to prove that carrying a secret and carrying it well were not the same thing.

They walked in silence past the milliner's, past the bakery, past the turn where the Meryton road began; and behind them the regiment's noise went on — a cart-wheel, a shouted order, a laugh — and each sound was a little farther off than the last, and none of them was a sound that would be there next week, or the week after, or ever again in quite that arrangement.

Elizabeth did not look back. She had looked her fill.

At the turn by the hedgerow, Kitty spoke. It was not a sentence; it was the beginning of one. "Lizzy, do you think—" and she did not finish it. She pressed her lips together and shook her head once, as though to unask the question, and walked on.

Ahead of them the road bent toward Longbourn, and the hedgerows closed quietly behind.

Chapter Four — The Library Door

Elizabeth had taken the stone bench at the end of the gravel walk, where the hedge screened her from the house and the April sky pressed in too fine for brooding. She was brooding anyway. A wren quarreled somewhere in the hawthorn, a bee fumbled among the early primroses.

Hill found her there. Hill, with her unerring instinct for locating members of the family who meant not to be found, came down the path at her usual unhurried pace, hands folded before her apron, and waited the half-beat she always waited before speaking.

"Your father asks for you, miss. In the library."

Elizabeth set down the trowel she had not been using. She had brought it outside for the purpose of appearing occupied, and it had served this function admirably for the better part of an hour while she sat on the stone bench and stared at the hedge and tried not to think about the letter that had followed her out of Kent, and followed Hill inside.

The library door was shut. It had been shut, with varying degrees of finality, since the morning Kitty had spoken at breakfast. In the days since, it had opened for trays, for Hill, and once for Mary, who had knocked to request a volume of Fordyce and been told through the oak that she would find it improving to locate the book herself. It had not opened for Mrs. Bennet. It had not opened for Lydia. It had opened for Kitty, once, on that first morning, and whatever had passed between father and daughter in that quarter of an hour had sent Kitty back upstairs with red eyes and a set to her jaw that Elizabeth had not seen there before.

Elizabeth knocked. "Papa, it is Lizzy."

"Come."

The library was Mr. Bennet's country, and he had arranged it with the care of a man who understood that the chief pleasure of domestic life was the possibility of retreating from it. Leather and old paper scented air that had not been disturbed by an open window in several days. Books lined three walls from floor to ceiling, not decoratively, but with the disordered density of a collection that had been read. His desk stood against the wall beside the window, angled so that he could see the garden from his left and the door from his right without committing his full attention to either. On the desk, beside the inkstand and a cup of cold tea with a skin forming on its surface, lay a letter.

Mr. Bennet was in his reading chair — a deep, worn leather armchair drawn up to the desk at that same considering angle, its arms darkened at the elbows from forty years of use. He had his spectacles on and a book open on his knee, but the book was face-down in the manner of a volume that had been set aside some time ago and not returned to, and his spectacles sat at the end of his nose in the position that meant he was looking over them rather than through them.

"Ah, Lizzy. Do sit."

She sat. The chair opposite his desk was hard-backed and upholstered in faded green, and it had the discomfort of a seat not designed for lingering. Elizabeth had been in this room a thousand times — to borrow books, to share a passage, to trade an amused glance at the rest of the household's expense. She had sat in this chair, across from him, perhaps a dozen times: always summoned, never invited to stay.

"Your uncle Gardiner has written." Mr. Bennet picked up the letter between two fingers, as though it were a curiosity brought to him by the post rather than a piece of family correspondence. "He and your aunt propose a tour. Not the Lakes, as originally discussed — your uncle's business will not permit the full excursion. A shorter journey. The north, he says, without troubling to be more specific. I believe your aunt mentioned the scheme when you were in London."

"She did."

"And you are inclined to go."

It was not a question. Elizabeth looked at her father and saw that he already knew the answer — had known it, perhaps, before sending Hill to fetch her. He was watching her with the expression she had always privately enjoyed: the one that said I see you, Lizzy, and I find you the most tolerable member of this household, and that is as close to a declaration of love as I am prepared to make before noon.

She had always been flattered by that look. She was not certain she was flattered now.

"I should like it very much," she said. "If you can spare me."

"Spare you." He repeated the words with the faintest inflection. Not quite irony, not quite weariness, but something that lived in the territory between. "I believe the household will survive your absence. Your mother will have one fewer audience member, which may shorten the performances. Lydia will have one fewer sister to blame, though I expect she will redistribute the surplus with her customary efficiency. And I shall have one fewer person whose company I can tolerate, which is a loss I feel keenly in advance."

Elizabeth did not smile. She would have smiled, a week ago. She would have matched him quip for quip and felt the warm, conspiratorial pleasure of being the daughter who understood the joke. But the joke, this morning, had a shape she had not noticed before — or had noticed and chosen not to examine.

Her father made wit out of his family's disorder because wit was cheaper than governance, and funnier, and did not require him to open the library door.

Occasionally even by your father.

Mr. Darcy's words arrived as they had been arriving for days — unbidden, unwelcome, precise as a surgeon's notation in the margin of a case he had diagnosed correctly.

"You are very quiet, Lizzy."

"I am thinking."

"A dangerous occupation. I have largely given it up myself." He set the letter down and removed his spectacles, folding them with the deliberate care that was his particular form of punctuation — the gesture that meant the pleasantries were concluded and the real conversation was about to begin. "You have been quiet since Hunsford. Quieter than Kent would seem to warrant. Your cousin Mr. Collins is tedious, but he is a familiar tedium, and Charlotte is sensible enough to dilute it. Lady Catherine, I am told, is magnificent in her certainty. None of this should produce the expression you have been wearing at meals for the past week."

"What expression is that?"

"The expression of a woman who has been served a dish she cannot politely return to the kitchen."

Elizabeth's hands went still in her lap. She had not expected this. She had expected the sardonic commentary, the dry observations, the performance of paternal attention that substituted cleverness for care. She had not expected him to see her.

"I am merely tired," she said. "The journey was long, and Longbourn has been... eventful."

"Eventful." He tasted the word. "Yes. Your sister Kitty has supplied us with more excitement than I had budgeted for this quarter. Kitty came to me. Sat in that very chair. She had folded and refolded a note before she came in, and she could not stop folding it while she talked. She began her first sentence four times before she reached the end of it; thereafter the words came out in a rush, as water will when a pipe has been too long stopped. Six minutes, she spoke. I timed her. The longest ever, for her. At the end she asked whether I was cross with her. I told her I was not. I daresay that was the most useful sentence I have spoken to any of my daughters this season."

He paused. Elizabeth waited.

"She did a brave thing, your sister. I am not certain I have done a brave thing since the century turned. Though in fairness, the opportunities for bravery in a gentleman's library are limited. One can only refuse to answer one's wife's knock with so much heroism before the gesture loses its luster."

It was a clever joke. It was the kind one laughed at before one understood what it cost, and by the time one understood, the laughter had already spent itself, and what remained was not laughter at all.

He knew, Elizabeth saw. He knew he had hidden. He knew Kitty had done what he should have done months ago, and he was making it amusing because the alternative was letting it stand.

"She was brave," Elizabeth said.

"She was. And now she is miserable, because Lydia has made it her particular project to ensure that courage is punished as thoroughly as cowardice in this household. I have spoken to Lydia. Speaking to Lydia is rather like speaking to the weather. One feels the effort has been made, and the weather continues unaltered. Your mother has spoken to me. Your mother speaks to me with the regularity of a tide, and with approximately the same expectation of being heeded." He picked up the cold tea, looked at it, set it down again. "The regiment leaves this week. I presume you know."

"We saw the preparations in Meryton yesterday."

"And did you encounter anyone of note?"

Elizabeth's pulse did something she would rather it had not.

"We met Mrs. Forster," she said. "And several of the officers. The street was full of bustle."

"Mrs. Forster. The colonel's young bride. A woman of — what shall we say — considerable vivacity and limited reflection. Rather in the mold of your sister Lydia, if Lydia had the advantage of a husband with an income and a commission." He paused. "And Mr. Wickham? You will have had your last sight of him, I suppose."

The name sat in the room between them.

"We saw him," Elizabeth said.

She heard the flatness in her own voice. A week ago she would have said Mr. Wickham with a warmth she now understood to have been manufactured, not by the man alone, but by her own eagerness to believe in a version of events that flattered her judgment and confirmed her worst opinions of a better man whom she had decided to despise. She would have said something clever about his charm, or his manners, or the general injustice of his situation. She would have performed, for her father's amusement, exactly the kind of confident misreading that Wickham's whole career depended upon.

She could not do so now.

"You saw him," Mr. Bennet repeated. "And?"

"And he was civil. He carried Mrs. Forster's parcels. He asked to be remembered to Lydia — as any officer might, taking his leave."

"As any officer might." Mr. Bennet's eyebrows rose a fraction — the movement so slight it might have been a trick of the light, except that Mr. Bennet's eyebrows never moved by accident. "A careful phrase, Lizzy. You have not always been so careful on the subject of Mr. Wickham."

The air in the library went thin.

Elizabeth knew — she knew — that the correct response was a light one. A quip about how a girl who has been proved wrong about one man's character might grow cautious about another's. A wry deflection that would satisfy her father's curiosity without feeding it. She had a dozen such responses available, and she reached for one, and what came out instead was:

"I would not trust his good nature as far as Meryton trusts it."

The sentence fell into the room and lay there.

It was, on its surface, the kind of thing anyone might say about anyone — a mild expression of skepticism, the sort of hedged opinion that belonged in drawing rooms and cost nothing. But Elizabeth heard, a half-second after speaking, the weight the words carried: not the weight of casual doubt but of specific knowledge, and the specificity was audible.

Her father was not a man who missed such things.

The look lasted three seconds. Elizabeth counted them. In the first second he was merely attentive — the sharpened attention of a man who has heard something unexpected from the person he least expected to surprise him. In the second, something shifted behind his eyes: a narrowing, a focus, the look of a reader who has turned a page and found the paragraph he was not prepared for. In the third, he made a decision.

He did not ask.

He picked up the letter from Mr. Gardiner, smoothed a crease in the paper with his thumb, and said, in the tone of a man returning to safer ground: "Your uncle writes well. He has a steady hand and a talent for making the practical sound inviting. 'A tour of the north' — as though the north were a garden one might stroll through between breakfast and dinner. I daresay you will see cathedrals, Lizzy. And hills. And sheep. The sheep of the north are said to be superior to ours, though I have never troubled to verify the claim."

Elizabeth exhaled, making the retreat mutual. As they both stepped back from the edge of something, the relief was so immediate and so complete that it frightened her. She had nearly said more. He is not what he seems or You should enquire more closely into his history or — worst of all — I have it on authority that he attempted to elope with a girl of fifteen for her fortune.

She had even, sitting on the stone bench in the hour before Hill came down the path, considered the half-measure. I heard at Rosings, from a source I cannot name, that he ran out on his debts and had attempted, by less honourable means, to secure a fortune. She had turned the sentence over twice. She had seen what was wrong with it.

At Rosings placed the source within a small, knowable circle. A young girl with a fortune was not a generic phrase in a world that contained Mr. Darcy's sister. Her father might not draw the line; but a warning, once given, must take some shape in the world — a letter to Colonel Forster, an instruction to Hill, a refusal at the door — and any of these, in a county the size of theirs, would be observed. Wickham, finding himself observed, would draw the line within a sentence. And Mr. Bennet — who had answered his daughter's direct testimony with the closing of a door — would not be moved by hearsay smuggled out of Kent. The half-measure would purchase her nothing, and would cost a girl in Ramsgate a name she had nearly lost once already. The authority was a letter she could not produce from a man she could not name, and the wreckage that declaration would have caused was incalculable.

"I should enjoy the hills," she said. "And the sheep. Though I make no promises about the cathedrals."

"Then you shall go." Mr. Bennet replaced his spectacles. The gesture was the reverse of their removal — the conversation was being closed, the punctuation applied, the door about to shut. "Write to your aunt and confirm it. I shall tell your mother, which I expect will produce either raptures at having one fewer mouth to feed or despair at losing a daughter to the wilderness. The odds are even."

Elizabeth rose. The chair creaked as she left it — a small, sharp sound in the quiet room.

She stood in the middle of the library and looked at her father and felt something tear. Quietly, cleanly, like a seam giving way along a line that had been weakening for some time. Not love. The love was intact; she did not think it would ever be otherwise. But something adjacent to love — the uncomplicated admiration of a daughter who had always believed her father's wit was wisdom, his detachment was philosophy, his closed door was the reasonable boundary of a reasonable man.

Occasionally even by your father.

The man who had written those words had never met Mr. Bennet. He had observed the family at Netherfield, at the Meryton assembly, at one ball. He had watched from across the room with the cold, cataloging eye of a man who judged freely and spoke what he saw, and he had been — in this, as in so many things Elizabeth was only now beginning to tally — correct.

She wanted to say: You are right. You have been that sort of father. And I have been the daughter who laughed at your jokes instead of seeing what they cost, and we are more alike than I can bear to examine, and I am leaving for the north because this house has become a mirror I do not wish to stand in front of.

She said: "Thank you, Papa."

Mr. Bennet glanced at her over his spectacles. He did not smile. The look held for a moment longer than it should have. Long enough for Elizabeth to understand that he heard what she had said and what she had not said, and that he would accept the lie, as he accepted most things, because accepting it was easier than opening the door.

Elizabeth crossed the library. The room was not large, but the distance felt different today, longer or shorter, she could not tell which. The books stood close around her, shelf on shelf. The cold tea sat on the desk with its skin of neglect. Her father's pen lay uncapped beside the inkstand, and a drop of ink had dried on the blotter in a shape that looked, from certain angles, like a small closed fist.

She reached the door. She turned the handle. She stepped into the hallway, where the air was cooler and the light came from the window above the stairs rather than the single pane behind her father's desk, and the smell was beeswax and laundered linen instead of leather and cold tea.

She pulled the door shut behind her.

The click of the latch was small and definite. Elizabeth stood with her hand on the handle for a moment longer than was necessary, feeling the brass warm under her palm.

Jane was in the hallway.

She was standing by the window at the top of the first flight, half in the light and half out of it, her hands folded and her face carrying the patient, unsurprised expression of a woman who had been waiting without appearing to wait. She did not ask what had happened in the library. She did not ask why Elizabeth had been summoned or what their father had said. She simply stood there, steady and present, offering her company the way she always offered it — without conditions, without questions, without the demand that Elizabeth explain herself before being allowed to feel whatever she felt.

"Lizzy," Jane said. "Shall we walk?"

Dear, constant Jane. Who had asked no question and would ask none. Who was willing, as she had always been willing, to walk beside whatever her sister carried, and wait to be told, or not told, as the days required.

"Yes," Elizabeth said. "I think we must."
