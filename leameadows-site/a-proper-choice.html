A Proper Choice

A Pride and Prejudice Sequel

Coming September 21, 2026

Anne de Bourgh has learned, at last, to want things. Now comes the harder lesson: to choose them.

With her health her own and her future no longer decided for her, Anne must weigh the safety of the life she was given against the uncertain promise of one she might build herself — and learn whether a happiness chosen by her own judgment can be trusted to hold.

Chapter One — Winter's End

The ponies took the last rise to the house at a walk, which was all Anne had asked of them; and being Pemberley ponies, and therefore reasonable, they gave her exactly that and not a step more.

The thaw was well set in now. All winter, the country had lain muffled; now it had got its voice back, and Anne, who knew the world chiefly by its sounds, had been listening to it return with interest. Water ran wherever water could find a way — in Mr. Chelmsworth's ditches, along the ruts, off the eaves of the keeper's cottage, under the road in places the road did not own to. Behind her, a blackbird tried a phrase from a bare elm, broke off, and tried it again with more conviction. The ponies' hooves came soft and wet on the gravel; the harness kept up its small, regular remark. The air smelled of turned earth that had only lately remembered it was earth under sunlight that fell long and thin across the park.

At Rosings she had driven a low phaeton along the same circuit of the same park every day for years and had scarcely ever got down. Here, in Pemberley's larger phaeton, she got down a dozen times in a morning, wherever Mr. Chelmsworth wished to look at the ground, and got up again, and drove on, and no one sent a man after her to ask whether she was tired.

She was not tired. That was the morning's single uninteresting fact, and she had stopped marking it. A year ago a morning like this would have been the best part of the day, and she had never troubled to ask why. The motion of the carriage, she had supposed; or only the plain relief of being, for an hour, past the reach of her mother's expectations, which weighed more than any weather.

Out of doors she did well enough. Indoors, in time, she did not — the dull head by evening, the heaviness she had long since filed under her own constitution and ceased to question. She had kept the account for years and never once balanced it; here there was no account to keep. The body that had once been her chief subject of study had become, by slow degrees, merely the thing that drove the phaeton.

Beside her, Mr. Chelmsworth had his notebook open on his knee and was not writing in it. This was his way when a thing was still telling him something; he wrote only once it had done. He had filled two such books since the thaw, and had been paid for none of them. Sir Thomas's third son, with Ashcombe to go home to and no estate of his own to answer for, had no need to spend a winter reading other men's wet ground; he did it because the water interested him, which Anne had come to think was the only respectable reason for doing anything.

"The home wood will do," he said at last, and shut the book. "It is the long meadow I cannot like."

"You have not been able to like the long meadow since Tuesday."

"The long meadow has given me no encouragement." He looked back over his shoulder at it, the way another man might look at a guest who had said something unforgivable at dinner. "There is water in it that is on none of the formal estate maps. Water that keeps its own counsel is water I do not trust."

The long meadow lay placid and blameless below them, betraying nothing of the disloyalty he accused it of. But she had driven the estate at his elbow since the ground first opened, and had learned to see a field the way he saw it — not as a spread of green, but as an argument between water and the men who had tried for a hundred years to tell it where to go. She now read the managed surface of Pemberley as she could read the managed surface of a drawing room: by what it was at pains to keep smooth, and where, despite all pains, it would not lie flat.

Mr. Chelmsworth had mud to the ankle on one boot and none at all on the other, which was the whole record of his morning: he had gone down the wet side of every ditch and kept the dry side for the notebook.

They came level with the far paddock. Captain — a colt grown leggy and disreputable over the winter, a foal still in no one's opinion but Georgiana's — left off insulting a fence post to come and insult the ponies instead.

Mr. Chelmsworth's hand went, without his attending to it, to the breast of his coat: to the pocket where a watch would sit, and where instead there rode a horse no bigger than his thumb, with worse legs than Captain's and, Anne had always held, a worse temper. He had carried it out of Pemberley at Christmas and carried it back again at the thaw. She had noticed. She noticed most of his unguarded habits.

"Has he forgotten you?" Anne asked.

"He has not. He is pretending, to punish me for the winter." Captain put his nose over the rail and lipped hopefully at nothing. "Hope, in Captain, is very little instructed by experience."

"No," Anne agreed. "In that he is constant."

She drew the ponies up, though the house was in plain sight and there was no reason to stop but the one she had settled on some days ago and had been waiting to find a decent moment for.

"I have been considering," she said, "that 'Mr. Chelmsworth' has become inaccurate."

He turned to her. Not quickly — he never turned quickly when the matter concerned her, but as though he must first set down whatever he was holding before he could take up the person. Now, he set down the long meadow.

"Inaccurate?" he said.

"It is the name of a man who has been called about a ditch. You have been called about a great many ditches, through a frost and out the far side of it, and are now the only soul at Pemberley who knows where its water means to go. The acquaintance has altered. The name has not."

He weighed this with the gravity he gave to questions of fall and flow. "You will understand," he said, "that I am wary of a correction I have not been permitted to check."

"You may check it. It is Arthur."

She said it plainly, as she said most things now, and watched it land. At Rosings, the offer of a Christian name between two people neither related nor engaged would have been a scandal, or a claim, or a piece of evidence to be entered against her. Here, in an open phaeton, on the first of April, with a groom on the seat behind affecting a profound interest in the hedgerow, it was none of those things. It was merely accurate. She had decided, some days since, that she was tired of inaccuracy, and that she was allowed, now, to mend such of it as came within her reach.

Mr. Chelmsworth tapped the side of his jaw with his thumb, thinking. Deciding.

"Then you must allow me the same economy," he said, "or I shall stand at a disadvantage I cannot afford."

"That seems just."

"Anne." He tried it once, low. He did not make a ceremony of it. He did not make it into a little medal to hang about her neck for them both to admire. He received it, and used it, and let it be ordinary. "Anne — your near pony has put a foot wrong."

"He has not."

"He is thinking of it."

"That is not the same as doing it. You would condemn the whole meadow on such reasoning."

"I would. I have." He opened the notebook again. "Drive on, before I am proved right about something and made insufferable."

She drove on.

The phaeton came round to the front of Pemberley. The groom went to the ponies' heads, and Arthur got down first and then did not hand her down, because he had learned, somewhere in the course of the winter, that she would rather manage the step herself and be met at the foot of it than be lifted over it. He stood at the right distance and let her arrive.

Inside, the house was at its afternoon.

In the hall, Mrs. Reynolds was giving an order to a footman in the tone of a woman whose household obeyed because her instructions were clear, and not because she required continual proof of her authority. She had a great ring of keys that she never seemed to consult and never failed to produce. "Welcome in, Miss de Bourgh, Mr. Chelmsworth," she said, and to the footman, without a change of key, "the long passage, and mind the corner," and the thing was done.

A maid took Anne's boots and cloak, while a footman relieved Arthur of his hat and the worst of his outdoor dirt. Thus restored to the house, they turned toward the music room. Beckoning them was the pianoforte, clear, practiced, and exact, and under it, finding its place by degrees, the harp. Georgiana had the command of the passage; Kitty had, as yet, only a treaty with it.

As Anne came into the music room, Kitty looked up. "Oh — Anne, do come and hear — no, wait, you must hear the beginning properly — Georgiana, not so quick, I lose the — there." She caught the string, and her face cleared, and for the length of a phrase she was entirely competent. Kitty seemed always to begin every sentence three or four times and arrive, in the end, at precisely the place she had meant to reach, like a hound that overruns the scent and circles back to it.

"Is Captain very disgraceful?" Georgiana asked, without lifting her hands from the keys.

"Very," said Anne.

"Good," said Georgiana, comfortably.

By the window sat Mrs. Jenkinson with her workbasket and a shawl folded over the arm of her chair. The shawl was always there; it was no longer always deployed. There had been a time — the whole of her life until the past autumn — when the sight of Anne coming in flushed from the cold would have brought Jenkinson up out of her chair with the shawl already opening in her two hands, and a question about her chest, and a second question that did not wait upon the answer to the first.

Today Jenkinson took in the high color the wind had put in Anne's face, and read it rightly, for once, as weather and not as warning. Her hands moved on her knitting; went toward the shawl, out of twenty years' habit; and then, with a small private effort Anne had learned to honor by not appearing to see it, stayed where they were.

"You will have driven past the long meadow," she said — not you will be chilled, not you will lie down before dinner — "Mr. Chelmsworth and his meadow." And she smiled: at Anne first, and then, as he came in at Anne's shoulder, at Arthur, with something in it that was very nearly conspiracy.

Arthur excused himself to find Darcy with the long meadow's bad news. Anne crossed the music room and found Elizabeth in the small parlor beyond with paper, pencil, and the expression of a woman conducting a campaign.

"Anne. You are come in good time. Tell me you have an opinion about the ivory bedroom, for Mrs. Reynolds and I have between us only a disagreement, and one of us must be outnumbered."

"It smokes," Anne said.

"It smokes when the wind is in the east, which Mrs. Reynolds holds to be the chimney's fault and I hold to be the wind's." Elizabeth set down the pencil. "William says it has smoked since his grandfather's time and may be allowed to go on, which is the answer of a man who does not sleep in the ivory room."

Anne tapped the edge of the table three times, which was how she counted, and considered the question as she would have considered it for Rosings: the room, the flue, the prevailing wind, the use the room was put to.

"Move Kitty to the green room until the east winds are done," she said, "and have the flue swept now, while the house is thin of company. You will not mend an east wind. You may at least stop feeding it guests."

Elizabeth looked at her for a moment with frank pleasure. "There," she said, taking up the pencil again. "Outnumbered after all, Mrs. Reynolds, and by my own cousin. — Anne, you grow alarmingly useful."

It was the kind of thing that, said to her at Rosings, had always carried a hook in it. Said here, it carried none. Anne had spent a good part of the winter learning to tell the one from the other, and had got, she thought, tolerably quick at it.

Darcy came as far as the doorway of the small parlor and no farther, Arthur beside him. Her cousin had a way of filling a threshold without crossing it. "You found water in the long meadow," he said.

"He found water everywhere," Anne said. "He suspects the lawn."

"I suspect the lawn of nothing it has not earned," said Arthur.

Darcy's mouth moved toward a smile and stopped a little short of one, which was as far as he generally took it before company; he kept the rest for Elizabeth. He stepped into the parlor. "Then you may tell me what it has earned after tea," he said.

Elizabeth reached for the bell. "A defeat for the ivory room, a conspiracy in the long meadow, and no tea. Pemberley is declining."

In the passage beyond, two housemaids were carrying a corded travel trunk toward the back stairs. Anne saw it pass, heard the scrape of it fade, and let it go by. There would be time enough later to think about trunks.

After tea, she and Arthur went up together as far as the top of the stairs, where the gallery divided: the guest rooms one way, her own the other.

"The long meadow will keep you from dinner one of these days," she said.

"The long meadow will keep me from a great many things. It is the most interesting thing on the estate, and the most ungrateful." He paused, his hand on the newel. "After Pemberley, I am due at my father's estate for a fortnight, and then —" He did not finish it, which was its own kind of accuracy; the whole house knew where they were all of them bound after that, and by whose gracious invitation.

"Then," Anne agreed.

"Until dinner," he said.

"Until dinner. — Arthur." She tried it as he had, once, to keep. It fit the mouth better than she had expected; or perhaps she had simply expected it to be heavier than it was.

He inclined his head — not deeply, not as though she had granted him anything absurd — and went his way along the gallery, and she went hers.

At the turn of the passage another corded trunk had come to rest against the wall, waiting on someone to carry it down. Easter was two weeks off, and Kent was at the end of a long road, and at the end of Kent stood a house that had spent eight-and-twenty years teaching everyone in it not to hear her.

Chapter Two — The Road South

By the second hour out of Pemberley, Elizabeth had made three resolutions, abandoned one, and discovered that the abandoned one was the only one that signified.

The traveling carriage had been long enough on the road for every part of it to have declared an opinion. The glass rattled softly in its frame; the leather cushions held the morning cold where the sun had not reached them; now and then a wheel found a rut and sent the whole party into a brief, collective adjustment of shoulders, skirts, and patience. Elizabeth sat with her gloved hands folded in her lap, the taste of road-dust already at the back of her throat, and discovered that there was very little to be done in a carriage except endure one's own thoughts with as much grace as the springs allowed.

Elizabeth had resolved to be civil at Rosings — which required no resolution at all, civility toward Lady Catherine being less a virtue than a form of stamina. She had resolved to be useful, though usefulness, once shut into a traveling carriage, reduced itself by degrees to keeping her elbows to herself, her alarm out of her voice, and her feet from finding the same patch of carpet as Mrs. Jenkinson's. And she had resolved not to begin dreading Rosings until Rosings was at least in the same county as herself.

That last she lost somewhere short of Matlock.

It was not that she feared her husband's aunt. Fear was too simple, and too flattering to Lady Catherine. What Elizabeth felt, watching the green country unspool past the carriage glass, was nearer to the wariness of a person who has once been let inside a great machine and shown precisely how it works, and who must now go and stand within it again knowing that she had never been permitted near the levers.

She had hosted the woman, after all. Lady Catherine had declined to come to the wedding, and had then arrived at Pemberley in the autumn to inspect what her absence had failed to prevent. Elizabeth had given her the south wing, the largest chair, and four days of her own life, and had come away with the single piece of intelligence that mattered: that Lady Catherine's weapon was not her temper. It was her certainty. She did not contradict you. She relabeled you, and then proceeded as though the new label had always been the truth.

And now they were driving south to be relabeled at Rosings.

She had no intention of arriving with the label already pinned. She was not a guest at Rosings, to be approved or found wanting. She was Mrs. Darcy, married into the family, and the distinction was exactly the one Lady Catherine would spend a fortnight declining to observe. Very well. Elizabeth would observe it for them both — quietly, immovably, and without the satisfaction of a single contradiction. One did not defeat certainty by arguing with it. One defeated it by being, at the end of the fortnight, still oneself.

Georgiana and Kitty followed in the second carriage with Mrs. Annesley, a disposition Elizabeth had approved at once. Georgiana required no managing, but she did better when no one made a subject of her not requiring it; Kitty required, if not managing, then the company of someone who could distinguish alarm from intelligence. The maids and heavier baggage traveled behind them, under arrangements which appeared to Elizabeth to belong less to domestic comfort than to military logistics.

Longbourn was left for the return, if Rosings left any of them fit for visiting.

Beside her, Anne de Bourgh sat with her face turned to the window, and was, Elizabeth thought, the only person in the carriage entirely content.

It was a thing Elizabeth had learned to notice without announcing — announcing it being the very error she had spent the autumn learning not to make. Through the long ordinary winter, while Anne stayed on at Pemberley and the house slowly stopped treating her as an event, Elizabeth had watched Miss de Bourgh be wearied by warmth, by attention, by the plain social labor of being considered. On the road she was none of those things. There was color along the top of her cheek that no fever had put there. Her hands lay loose in her lap rather than folded for inspection. Her eyes moved over the country with the steady, cataloguing attention of a woman taking an inventory that she alone would ever read.

"You are not tired by the motion?" Elizabeth asked — and heard, a half-second too late, how nearly the question resembled all the other questions that were forever being asked in Anne's vicinity and answered above her head.

But Anne only considered it, in her unhurried way, as though it were a question of some interest and not mere solicitude.

"No," she said. "I like it." And then, because Anne's plain statements had a way of acquiring a second story if one waited for it: "A carriage is the one room that cannot be arranged around me. It goes where the road goes. No one can make it warmer."

Elizabeth laughed before she had decided whether she was permitted to. Anne did not smile, quite; but something moved at the edge of her mouth and was gone, which Elizabeth had come to understand was the same thing, in Anne's currency, as laughter.

Opposite them, Mrs. Jenkinson stirred. Her hand went toward the shawl folded on the seat beside her — went, and then, with a small visible effort, did not arrive. She drew it back into her lap and pressed it flat. The shawl had been, all autumn, a kind of punctuation: deployed at the first cloud, the first opened window, the first unscheduled breath of air. That she could now leave it folded was the nearest thing to a revolution that soft, anxious, dutiful woman had yet made, and she made it as she made everything, by halves and with apology. She caught Elizabeth watching, and colored.

"She does very well in a carriage," Mrs. Jenkinson offered — and then, hearing herself speak for Anne in the old way, stopped, and began again, more bravely, to Anne herself: "You do."

"I do," Anne agreed.

It was going to be the chief labor of the coming weeks, Elizabeth thought — not to undo Rosings, which no one could undo from a carriage, but to keep these small reclamations from being quietly folded back up the moment they passed under Lady Catherine's roof.

They lay the first night at a clean, indifferent inn beyond Leicester, where the beds were aired and the supper was honest and Lady Catherine was, blessedly, a hundred miles and two days to the south and unaware of any of it.

In a private parlor with a fire that drew perfectly well, Elizabeth took the measure of her younger charges.

Kitty was to lodge at the parsonage. This had been arranged between Elizabeth and Charlotte by letter — Rosings could not comfortably absorb the whole party, and Charlotte, with the particular generosity of a woman who understood exactly what she was offering, had written that she would be glad of the company. Kitty had received the intelligence in three distinct stages, each louder than the one before.

"I am sure Mrs. Collins is very kind," she began, "only — that is, I had thought — when one is asked to a great house one does not expect to be — and Mr. Collins is so very —" She stopped, having reached the end of her courage before the end of her sentence, and looked to Elizabeth to be told what she had meant.

"Mr. Collins is so very," Elizabeth agreed gravely. "You will find it an education, Kitty. There is no better school than a parsonage for learning the precise market price of a compliment."

Georgiana, who had been quietly dreading Rosings since Derbyshire and saying nothing of it, smiled into her cup.

"You are very silent," Elizabeth said to her.

"I had hoped," Georgiana said, in the low voice she kept for confessions, "that my aunt might have forgotten the pianoforte." A pause, while she gave the thought a little more time than it strictly required. "She has never once forgotten the pianoforte."

"Then we shall give her a great deal of it," said Elizabeth, "early, and all at once, and be done. A thing dreaded is simply a thing not yet survived." It was the sort of remark she made chiefly to find out whether she believed it.

Darcy came in then from speaking with the landlord, bringing the cold with him, and paused in the doorway long enough for the room to feel smaller.

"The roads tomorrow?" Elizabeth said.

"Better than my aunt believes them, and worse than I should like."

Later, when Mrs. Annesley had taken Georgiana and Kitty up, and Anne had gone with Mrs. Jenkinson — early, but by her own announcement, which Elizabeth had been careful not to turn into an occasion — she and Darcy were left to the fire.

"You are quiet," she said.

"I am calculating."

"Distances?"

"Tempers." He turned the poker once in the grate, which did not need it. "My aunt will have arranged everything before we come. The rooms, the hours, the order of the table. She will have arranged Anne most of all."

"And you mean to unarrange it."

There was a silence, in which Elizabeth heard her husband decline to promise her something.

"I mean," he said at last, "not to make it worse by managing it differently. If I take Anne's part too loudly, my aunt will only hold her the tighter, and call it love." He looked at the fire rather than at Elizabeth. "I have done that. Held a thing too close and given it the wrong name, and been certain I was right."

Elizabeth's answer came readily to her, and then did not. There were some confessions one could not reward with cleverness, however neatly cleverness offered itself.

"You have improved," she said at last.

"I have had instruction." The corner of his mouth did the thing it did, which on Darcy passed for open laughter; and then it stilled. "Promise me you will not fight her on my account. Nor on Anne's. Anne does better when no one fights for her — I have watched it all winter. It is the strangest thing."

"It is not strange in the least," Elizabeth said. "You are describing a woman who would like to be allowed to answer her own door."

She had meant it lightly. It did not come out lightly, and they sat with it a while, the fire ticking, the inn settling its boards around them. Somewhere above, a tread crossed a room and crossed it again — too light for a servant, too unhurried for Kitty — and Elizabeth thought, without quite meaning to: Anne, awake, walking the length of a strange room simply because she could, in a house that had not yet been told what she was.

"Two more days," Darcy said.

"Two more days of road," Elizabeth said, "or two more days before Rosings begins to improve us?"

"A fortnight," he said, "and Easter, and my aunt upon her own ground."

"Then we had better sleep," said Elizabeth, "while the ground is still ours."

The third day gave them Kent, and Kent gave them, by slow degrees, a different quality of green.

The country grew tamer the further south they came. Hedges squared, gates lately painted, verges cut to a width that suggested someone had an opinion about verges. Even the country appeared to have been instructed.

Anne had gone silent some miles back. Not the silence of fatigue — Elizabeth had learned that one too — but a guarded quiet that had come over her by degrees. She sat very straight. Her hands had folded themselves in her lap without, Elizabeth thought, Anne quite noticing it; the loose ease of the first morning had been put away somewhere along with the open country.

"There," said Georgiana, softly.

Rosings Park, handsome and high upon its rising ground, every window of that famous front catching the late light and giving none of it back — the whole façade arranged. There was no other word for it; Lady Catherine's houses, like her sentences, were arranged, to be seen precisely thus, from precisely here, by travelers who had been permitted to arrive.

The carriage turned in at the lodge. The light changed first: it went grayer beneath the avenue of limes, giving the air that came in at the window a tint of green.

But Anne had turned her face to the glass again — not as she had on the open road, taking the country in, but the way one turns toward a sound. She was listening to something. Elizabeth heard nothing but the wheels changing their note on the swept gravel. Ahead, the great door began to open to receive them.

Lady Catherine did not come out to the steps. One did not, Elizabeth understood, descend to the gravel for guests one had oneself summoned. One waited, indoors, in the largest chair, for them to be brought.

Elizabeth set her hand to the carriage door before the footman could reach it. A small thing; she let it be a small thing. But she would go in on her own feet and as family — not brought, but arrived.

Chapter Three — By the Front Door

Anne saw the famous front as Elizabeth must have seen it from the road: windows catching the late light, stone made handsome by distance, consequence arranged for the eye of a person arriving. But Rosings never stopped at the eye. The wheels changed their note on the raked gravel; the light went gray-green under the limes; and the house began its old work of gathering wind, rooks, wheels, every sound of arrival, into one thick, upholstered hush. Then the carriage stopped.

Before the footman could come round to it Elizabeth moved first: one gloved hand to the latch, one small forward lean, and then the click of the catch giving. Anne saw it and heard it — the sound of a woman letting herself out of a carriage rather than waiting to be let. That was the difference, and she did not even know she was making it.

Through the open door the day came in, and with it the smell: wet stone, old polish, cold ashes, old leaves. Five months at Pemberley had scoured it from her so completely that its return felt almost intimate. Her throat knew it. Her hands, lying quiet in her lap, knew it. At once, her body accepted the old name it had been given: home. What else would it be? She had breathed it in for all her eight-and-twenty years.

She let the footman hand her down. There was no method by which she might decline it without making an event of declining, and she had resolved, somewhere past Bromley, to spend nothing on events today that she might want later for better purposes. So she was handed down — received, set upon the gravel like a parcel correctly directed — while three feet off her cousin's wife stood already arrived, having brought herself.

Darcy was out and at the work of it before the rest of them had found their feet. He gave the postilions their orders and a word to the horses, who deserved it more; he told a footman which trunks were wanted tonight and which might wait until morning. It was the plain competence Anne had watched run Pemberley all winter without seeming to run anything. Here it met a soft and total resistance. A Rosings man in Rosings livery took each instruction with a bow, and then carried on as Rosings had always carried on, which was not in every case what Mr. Darcy had said. He did not appear to notice the correction.

The second carriage gave up its passengers more slowly. Mrs. Annesley descended first and turned back to the two girls with the patience of a woman who had crossed England with both of them and meant to deliver them entire. They came down into the same gray-green air, and took it, Anne saw, in opposite directions.

Georgiana had gone quiet. Not the easy quiet of the music room at Pemberley, where her silences were only the spaces she left around a musical phrase. This was the other kind: a careful, folded, anticipatory quiet; a person making herself small before anyone has required it of her. She had been here before. She knew already what the house would ask of her, and had begun to pay it in advance.

Kitty had never been here at all, and met it with her whole startled face. "Oh —" she said, to no one, to the front of the house, which was a great deal of front. "Oh, it is — Georgiana, it is so very —" and lost the end of the sentence somewhere up among the windows. The two of them sounded as two instruments set down in a cold room and going out of tune by different routes.

The great door gaped open above them.

They were brought through the hall and into the drawing room, where Rosings had already arranged itself around the largest chair. Every line of the room pointed toward the chair by the fire.

Her mother sat there with the fire built high behind her, and did not rise.

"So," she said. "You are come."

She looked them over in order of precedence, which was her own order and not the world's. Her eye reached Elizabeth and stopped, and performed the maneuver Anne had been dreading.

"You are thin, Mrs. Darcy. Thinner than at Pemberley. Derbyshire does not agree with you, or your table does not. I shall write to your housekeeper; there is no occasion for the mistress of such a house to arrive looking as though she had walked to it."

It was done so smoothly one might miss the violence in it. Elizabeth had come three days in good health and good looks; she had stepped down from the carriage arrived; and in a single sentence she had been re-entered in her aunt's books as a woman insufficiently fed by a husband insufficiently attended to by his wife. Her mother did not contradict a person. She misnamed them, and the room obeyed. Anne had watched it done to herself in rooms like this, and had never — until she saw it done from the outside, at Pemberley, in the autumn — understood it as misnaming.

And here her cousin moved.

He had been standing just behind Elizabeth, as courtesy required. Now he moved forward — only a few paces, but enough to set himself between his wife and the chair by the fire.

"She looks as I like her to look, madam," he said. "And she keeps the best table in Derbyshire. I am the evidence of it, and I am satisfied with the verdict." He said it pleasantly. He had known this house since boyhood; he knew its one navigable channel as a riverman knows his water, and he steered it without seeming to lay a hand to the tiller. Lady Catherine made the small sound of a woman declining to pursue a point she had nonetheless entered in the ledger, and passed on.

The small movement gave Anne two understandings at once, and the second was colder than the first. The first was that he would move for his wife — that the man who had filled doorways all winter and not come through them had, after all, a threshold he crossed without thinking, and that it was Elizabeth. The second came a breath later: in moving, he had not once turned toward her.

"And my dear Anne." Her mother's eye arrived at her last, as it always had, and did not so much see her as confirm her. "You are tired. Three days in a carriage. You will not own to it — you never do — but I know your constitution better than you have ever troubled to know it yourself. You will go up directly and lie down before dinner."

Anne was not tired. She had been nearer well on the road than she had any memory of being inside a house. She had said as much to Elizabeth, somewhere in Bedfordshire. She was not tired now. She opened her mouth to say so — and did not.

The old silence had been a door shut from the far side. This silence she chose, with her own hand on the latch: she heard the correction stand ready on her tongue — I am not tired; I have never in my life felt better; it is only the house that tires me, and I have begun to wonder whether it was ever anything else — and she set it down, deliberately, unspent.

Across a carpet built to carry her mother's voice and swallow hers, the truth would only be spent too soon. She would want it later, whole, and at a better range. She tapped the side of her knee three times, once for each day of the road, counting herself back into her own keeping, and gave the smallest nod she could.

Beside her, she felt Mrs. Jenkinson begin to move.

"Mrs. Jenkinson." Her mother had not finished arranging the people in the room. "Anne will take something warm before she lies down. See to it. And the shawl — has she no shawl? She has come all this way with nothing about her shoulders?"

Something close to grief moved through Anne as Jenkinson's hand went toward the shawl folded over her arm — the autumn's whole patient education undone in an instant by the voice that had trained her for twenty years. Her hand went out, and then, with the cost of the effort crossing her face, did not quite arrive. Jenkinson looked, for one helpless moment, from the chair by the fire to Anne and back again: a servant of two houses, asked to face both ways at once.

"She does very well without it, your ladyship," Jenkinson managed. It came out frightened, and far too quiet for the room, and was the bravest sentence Anne had heard since they passed the gates.

Her mother did not hear it, or did not choose to. "Nonsense. Put it about her."

The shawl was unfolded and laid about Anne's shoulders.

Then her mother turned to the younger ones, and Anne watched them play their two different airs.

"Georgiana." Warmth, of a kind — the warmth of ownership. "You will play for us. You have been let run wild at Pemberley, I make no doubt, but we shall soon have you in order. After dinner; I have had the instrument tuned against your coming." It was offered as a compliment and settled like a command. Georgiana said, "Yes, Aunt," in the smallest of her voices, and folded the last of Pemberley away.

Then the eye moved to Kitty, and changed — cooled, narrowed, priced. "And this is the sister." Not your sister, to Elizabeth; the sister, to the room, a specimen entered into the record. "Miss Catherine Bennet. You will be more at your ease at the parsonage, I think. Mrs. Collins keeps a comfortable house, and you will find more society at the parsonage."

It was an eviction dressed as kindness, and Kitty, already overmatched by the room, accepted it. "Oh — yes — thank you, your ladyship." She had agreed herself out of the great house before she had been ten minutes inside it.

And then, because she could not bear a silence and had not yet learned what Rosings did with the things one said into its silences, she went on. "It is so very grand," she said, looking up and around her, "ever so much grander than Pemberley — only Pemberley is —" and stopped.

Stopped dead, on the very edge of it, having all but said into Lady Catherine de Bourgh's own drawing room that Pemberley, for all it was not Rosings, was the happier house — warmer, kinder, the place where a person might be glad. The truth of it hung there, half out, an honest thing flushed into the open; and the room did not know what to do with it. No one did. Kitty went scarlet to the ears.

"Pemberley is large," said Lady Catherine, "and ill-governed. Yes."

❦

They led her up to her old rooms.

No one asked. The room was hers because it had always been hers; the old decision had never required a fresh one. Up the stairs, along a gallery she could have walked blind, to the door she had known all her life. And into the rose room: warm, small, close, chosen long ago by someone who had decided once and for all what Anne de Bourgh was, and had never since troubled to revise the finding.

The air felt closer than the warmth could account for. The fire was high. The bed-curtains were drawn back just so. Beside the chair, the fire-screen had already been set; at its feet waited the footstool. A folded rug lay over the chair's back. The furniture of an invalid.

She went to the window.

At Pemberley, in the blue room — the airy one, the one she had chosen — there was a place at the window where the frame did not quite meet the sill; fresh air trickled in as a thin cold thread. She had reached for that thread more than once, privately, for the small comfort of finding it still there: not enough air to change the room, only enough to prove that the room was not wholly closed. She had thought of it more than once on the road south.

Here she put her fingers to the sash and lifted, and it did not give. She tried the second window. It did not give either. They were not locked. They were simply settled — painted into their frames over years of being shut and meant to stay shut, the wood swollen, the joints grown over.

She laid her hand flat against the wall beside the dead window, and read the room as Arthur had shown her how to read a field: by old permissions, by changes in temperature and texture, by the signs that marked where water had kept its route. The chill was in this wall. She could feel it through the paper: plaster that never seemed dry, loose at the seams. She could not yet say what it meant. Only that it was worth noticing.

Behind her Mrs. Jenkinson had come in with the shawl and was arranging it over the back of the chair. "There," she said, to the shawl, unhappily. "There, now." She did not offer it to Anne. That much of the autumn held. But her hands had remembered the dance, and were dancing it.

From the gallery came the sounds of the others being bestowed — a door, voices, her cousin's among them, low and satisfied. Anne could not make out the words, but she knew what that tone meant: Mrs. Darcy comfortable, the family well-housed. It did not seem to have occurred to him to ask which room they had put his cousin in, or whether the windows in it opened.

When Mrs. Jenkinson had gone, and the room was at last her own, Anne did not lie down. She sat in the invalid's chair, because it was the chair, and took from the small case she had carried up herself — not let a maid carry — the papers she kept. Lady Anne Darcy's green book itself was safe at Pemberley, where it belonged: a record of dinners, visits, weather, all the house had thought worth keeping. Anne had not brought it. She had brought the little book she had made in answer to it: folded sheets stitched together in green thread, small enough for her case, plain enough to escape notice. Not a record of Pemberley's events, but of her own. She opened to the page she wanted.

Water remembers where it was allowed. She had written that down in the autumn, after Arthur had said something near it about Stony Close, and had made it hers by writing it. And it was true: you may pave a thing over and call it dry, and the water will go on keeping its old appointments underneath, and one wet spring it will tell you the truth of where it had always meant to be.

She said nothing. Words unspoken pressed toward her mother, toward her cousin, toward the frightened woman smoothing a shawl no one had asked for. She kept them banked, deliberately, the way one banks a fire to carry it through a long night rather than letting it roar itself out at dusk.

After a while, the light began to go. Behind the swollen sash the thin cold air went on moving where the room could not admit it.

Then came Mrs. Jenkinson's soft knock.

"Miss de Bourgh? Her ladyship says they are assembling before dinner."

Anne closed the little book and put it back in her case. When she rose from the invalid's chair, the bed was still untouched.

❦

Chapter Four — Dinner

Dinner at Rosings was not a meal but a muster, and Elizabeth had dressed for it as for a field she meant to hold.

She had learned the ground last year, in another spring, as a guest of the parsonage — had sat at this very table when she was nobody her ladyship need trouble over, and had been troubled over exhaustively all the same. She came down to it now by a different stair and under a different name, and she was curious, in the cool way she had taught herself, to see which of the two facts Rosings would consent to notice.

The drawing room had been lit and arranged and was waiting to be entered correctly. Her husband gave her his arm at the foot of the stairs. "Courage," he said, very low, with the particular gravity that in him was a joke; and she pressed his sleeve once — William — and let him take her in.

Mr. Collins was there before them. Of course he was; he had very likely been there an hour, rehearsing his readiness. He came at them across the carpet in a series of bows that began too early and arrived too late. Elizabeth received her cousin's compliments upon her appearance, her journey, her husband, her husband's house, and the singular condescension of her ladyship in receiving them all, before she had got fully into the room.

"Mrs. Darcy. Mrs. Darcy. We are honored. I said to Mrs. Collins only this morning — I said, Rosings has not seen such a party in years. One does not like to make comparisons," said Mr. Collins, who liked nothing in the world better, "but one feels them."

"Mr. Collins. I hope Charlotte is well."

"Exceedingly. Exceedingly. She did not come up tonight — the child, you understand — but she sends every — that is, she would send, were she aware —" He recollected that she was not aware, not having been asked, and covered the gap with a fresh bow.

So Charlotte was at the parsonage, and Kitty with her — dispatched there this afternoon and, Elizabeth suspected, a good deal happier for it than she yet understood. She felt the small loss of her friend's face at the table and set it down among the evening's other economies.

Lady Catherine — Elizabeth must remember to call her Lady Catherine, even in the privacy of her own head. The woman had a way of making even kinship feel like something granted at her pleasure. She directed them in to dinner as a general directs a column: by name and in order, brooking no error in the precedence and committing one or two herself with perfect confidence. Mrs. Darcy here. Darcy there. Georgiana within reach of her eye. Mr. Collins wherever Mr. Collins might be most useful and least audible, which proved not to be the same chair.

And Anne she placed beside herself, where she had always been, where she might be reached and spoken for without the inconvenience of turning the head.

Anne came in last and quietly, on Mrs. Jenkinson's steadying company, and took the place that had been hers since girlhood with no sign of finding it either welcome or strange. Elizabeth, who had spent a winter learning to read that smooth countenance, could make nothing of it tonight, and found the failure unsettling. Across the table Georgiana had gone pale and dutiful, a girl waiting for the part of the evening she dreaded — which would come, her aunt had promised it, after dinner.

They sat. The courses were served. And Lady Catherine, having arranged the people to her satisfaction, turned her attention to the larger and more disobliging matter of her land.

It began, as Elizabeth would afterward remember it, with drainage.

"My low fields are wet," Lady Catherine announced, in the tone of a woman reporting an insolence. "Too wet by half this spring, and the high ground above the church too dry, which any person will tell you is contrary to reason, and yet there it is. I have spoken to Spencer, and Spencer has told me a great deal in return and mended nothing. I begin to think there is not a man in Kent who understands water."

"It is a particular study," Darcy said.

"Everything is a particular study, to the man who would rather not undertake it."

Darcy let that pass. He had the look of a man long practiced in letting his aunt's remarks spend themselves without assistance. But then, helpfully, fatally, he set down his glass and offered the one thing he had that might be of use.

"There is a man who would understand it," he said. "Chelmsworth — Arthur Chelmsworth, Sir Thomas's third son, in Derbyshire. He drained Stony Close for me last year, ground everyone had given up as sour, and did it by reading the fall of the land rather than fighting it. If anyone could tell you why your high field is dry and your low one drowning, it is he. I could not recommend a man more warmly."

He said it pleasantly, usefully, with no notion on earth of what he was doing. He was helping his aunt with her fields. He was a riverman pointing out to the flood a convenient breach in his own bank.

It took Elizabeth a moment, and then it took her all at once.

Arthur Chelmsworth. The name landed in the middle of Lady Catherine's table like a stone into a still pond. Elizabeth — who knew what the name had come to mean at Pemberley, though Darcy had spoken it only as a matter of ditches and drains — felt the ripple of it go out, and turned, without meaning to, to watch it reach Anne.

For one unguarded instant Anne's careful surface broke, and what came up through it was not the small private gladness Elizabeth had half expected from a friend soon to be near. It was a great deal closer to fear. Anne's head lifted; across the table, the shock in her struck Elizabeth like a hand laid hard upon the wrist — an appeal, a warning, a plea that something not be said aloud — and then the surface closed over again, Anne bent to her plate, and the moment was gone.

She had no leisure to puzzle it, for Mr. Collins had heard a gentleman's name spoken with approval by Mr. Darcy, and rose to it like a trout.

"Chelmsworth!" he cried. "But I am acquainted with Mr. Chelmsworth — I had the honor, during the inspection at Pemberley in the winter. A most superior young man, your ladyship; very searching in his inquiries, very exacting in his notions, a gentleman of philosophical pursuits, of the first respectability, who would, I am persuaded, esteem it the very greatest privilege to be made known to the grounds of Rosings Park. Indeed I have seldom met a young man who spoke so feelingly of soil." He pronounced the word as though it were itself a kind of distinction. "Your ladyship could not do better. Your ladyship could assuredly not do better."

Lady Catherine considered it with the gravity she brought to all things that had been her own idea. That a younger son of a Derbyshire baronet should be brought the breadth of England to look at her fields struck her, Elizabeth saw, as no more than the fields deserved.

"A third son," she said, weighing it. "But of a good house, and recommended by my nephew. Mrs. Jenkinson, you mentioned the name, I believe, in one of your very particular accounts from Pemberley?"

At the far end of the table, Mrs. Jenkinson's fingers closed once on the edge of her napkin. Her eyes went, helplessly, toward Anne, and away again before they could betray her. She inclined her head.

"And known to my clergyman," Lady Catherine continued. "Yes. I see no objection — indeed it is precisely what ought to be done; I wonder it has not been done before. I shall write. He shall come and see what a properly managed estate asks of a man, and we shall have the low fields right by midsummer." She inclined her head, conferring the favor upon herself. "I am very good to have thought of it."

"Your ladyship is goodness itself," said Mr. Collins.

The door had opened, and Lady Catherine had opened it herself. She had made the summons her own notion; and the only two people at the table who understood that anything had been set in motion were Elizabeth, who did not understand the half of it, and Anne, who would not raise her eyes from her plate.

❦

After dinner the ladies withdrew, and the evening proceeded to its appointed pleasures, which were her ladyship's pleasures, and compulsory.

Georgiana was put to the instrument. She played with her usual brilliance, every difficult passage cleanly met and mastered; but tonight there was a carefulness in it, a small tenderness almost like apology. Lady Catherine, who had never in Elizabeth's hearing once allowed music to interrupt her, talked across the whole of it.

She talked, chiefly, of Anne.

"Anne would have been a true proficient," she informed the room, over a passage Georgiana was taking with great care, "had her health ever allowed of her applying. There was never anything wanting in her taste — I have always found it precisely what it ought to be. She has not the strength for performance, which is a great pity, but there it is, and she does not repine." Anne, seated near, was not asked in any manner that required her to answer, and did not; her mother had supplied the answer along with the question, as she supplied Anne with all things, pre-decided and cut to the correct size. "She is best as she is," Lady Catherine concluded, of her grown daughter, in her grown daughter's hearing. "Listening."

Elizabeth's own hands lay quiet in her lap, and she kept them so by an act of will. There was a sentence in her — there were several — and she could feel her husband, across the room, not looking at her in a way that was its own request. Anne does better when no one fights for her. He had said it to her by the fire at Pemberley, and she had not entirely believed him then, and did not entirely believe him now. But she had promised, and she held the sentence behind her teeth, and found it cost her a good deal more than she had bargained for to sit in a warm room and let a woman misname her own daughter to her face, and say nothing.

The card-tables were the last of it. At Pemberley, Anne had played with the whole party all winter, quadrille and whist, badly and cheerfully and entirely as one of them; Elizabeth had watched her win, and lose, and reach across to argue a trick. Here her mother arranged the tables as she arranged all else. Georgiana was claimed for the grown table, pale and obedient and within reach of praise or correction; Anne was set down to cassino: the children's game, the invalid's game, the game one gives a person who cannot be trusted with consequences — with Mrs. Jenkinson and Mrs. Annesley for company and the stakes kept low enough to do no harm.

Elizabeth, two tables off and pretending to attend her own cards, waited to see what it would do to her.

It did, at first, nothing: Anne took her demotion as she took everything, without comment. And then came a small thing, so slight Elizabeth almost missed its consequence. Dealt her hand, Anne did not play it idly, drifting, the way the table plainly expected of her. She gathered it, and ordered it, and played it to win — quietly, precisely, with a small private attention, keeping her own count under her breath, taking a trick she had no need to take and taking it clean. It was nothing. A hand of a child's game at a side table; no one of consequence would have marked it. But she had been put somewhere small and told what she was, and instead of becoming it she had sat down and kept her own account; and Elizabeth took an unreasonable quantity of heart from a won trick at cassino.

The hand finished; Mrs. Annesley gathered the cards and dealt again, and Mrs. Jenkinson kept the score aloud, half a point at a time, and Anne let her. At the grown table a pronouncement of Lady Catherine's rose and subsided like weather.

Elizabeth turned back to her own cards, which she had been neglecting shamefully — her partner wore the patient look of a man tallying his losses — and played a hand, and lost it, and let the side table alone. She might not have caught the other thing at all. She caught it only because she glanced back once more, eased and off her guard on the strength of that small victory.

Anne set down a card, and reached for the next, and between the one motion and the other she stopped. Only for a breath. Her hand came to rest on the edge of the table, the fingers flattening against the wood as though she were, very briefly, making sure of it; and something passed through her and was gone — the smallest banking of a fire against a draft that Elizabeth could not feel. Then she took up the card and played it, and Mrs. Jenkinson said something, and Anne answered it, and the game went on.

No one else registered it. There was almost nothing to register. A woman had paused over a card at the end of a long day of travel and reception; fatigue would have explained it neatly. Elizabeth told herself so, and did not believe herself. Only that something had crossed Anne that had not crossed her in the months at Pemberley; and Elizabeth did not like it, and had no name to give it, and no one to whom she might say it.

The evening ended as Rosings evenings ended, by her ladyship's clock and not the company's. There was no drive home now in which to be honest — they were under the one roof — only the stairs, and the candle, and her husband's low question in their own room: whether it had been very bad.

"No," Elizabeth said, slowly. "No. It was a dinner."

Mr. Chelmsworth's name remained with her, and so did Anne's face when she heard it. So did the card table: the small won trick, the smaller pause, the hand pressed flat a moment against the wood.

Elizabeth lay awake with them long after the candle was out. Below stairs, no doubt, the house had settled into its proper order; above stairs, Lady Catherine's decision had settled just as firmly. A letter would be written in the morning. By this time next week, perhaps sooner, Arthur Chelmsworth would be at Rosings.

Elizabeth did not know whether Anne dreaded his coming, wished for it, or both.
